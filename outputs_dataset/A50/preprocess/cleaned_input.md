# A B S T R A C T

The mechanical properties and deformation behaviors of a low-carbon low-density steel of Fe35Mn6Al0.1C are studied under both room temperature (RT) and liquid nitrogen temperature (LNT). It is found that the yield strength, tensile strength, total elongation are 251 MPa, 580 MPa and 52 % at RT, which increase 497 MPa, 945 MPa and to 75.5 % at LNT. The Charpy V-notch impact energy is 331±4 J at RT, which remains an excellent value of 254 ± 6 J at LNT, indicating an excellent combination of strength and ductility at LNT. The microstructural evolutions are examined by EBSD in SEM and TEM, it is shown that the RT tensile deformation is mainly controlled by the dislocation substructure refinement accompanied by deformation twinning just before the necking. However, the LNT deformation is controlled by both dislocation substructure refinement and deformation twinning with twinning started just after yielding. The multiple strain hardenings are divided into four stages with strain increasing at LNT, rather than two stages phenomena at RT. Deformation twinning is found in the specimen after \~28 % tensile strain at RT, whereas it is found in the specimen after \~10 % tensile deformation at LNT, which is seldom reported in low-density steel in literature. Based on the analysis of the relation between work hardening behaviors and microstructure evolution, the high stacking fault energy (SFE) twinning and its orientation dependence are mainly attributed to the stress concentration induced by planar dislocation substructure. The exceptional combination of strength and toughness at LNT makes the low-carbon low-density steel of Fe35Mn6Al0.1C to be a promising cryogenic steel.

# 1. Introduction

Light weight steel as one of the new generation materials has been paid much more attention in recent two decades due to its combination of excellent mechanical properties and low-density, with the aim to understand the deformation mechanism in scientific research and explore the possibility of industry application [1–6]. However, much work has been focused on the chemical design [3,7–10], the deformation mechanism [11–14] and the strengthening and toughening behaviors of the low-density steel [3,15–18], such as the austenitic low-density steel with high carbon, high manganese and high aluminum, the dual-phase low-density steel with relative lower contents of carbon, manganese and aluminum, and the ferritic low-density steel with lowest content of carbon, manganese and aluminum. Amongst these three kinds of low-density steel, the austenitic low-density steel is widely studied due its lowest density and the excellent combination of strength and ductility [5,6,18–20].

The austenitic low-density steel with chemical composition of 0.5–1.5%C, 25–35%Mn and 6–11%Al has been thoroughly demonstrated in literature for the application in the automobile industry [2,5, 15,19,21]. It is shown that the yield strength varied from 400 to 1000 MPa, tensile strength varied from 600 to 1000 MPa with total elongation in the range of 40–80 %. The excellent mechanical properties are mainly attributed to the thinning of the slip band spacing enclosed by the planar dislocations in the easy gliding plans [2,14,21]. It is reported that the tensile deformation behavior of austenite-based duplex Fe-Mn-Al-C steels is primarily dominated by the behavior of the austenitic matrix [5,12]. Due to the deformation incompatibility between the two phases, the main crack sources originate from the FCC/BCC interfaces in duplex steels [4,18]. In addition, it is reported that the austenite grains can be refined by a small quantity of ferrite [1] and the mechanical properties of austenite-based duplex low-density steels can be improved through solution treatment [1,2,5]. It is reported that the addition of carbon and aluminum would results in a significant increase of stacking fault energy (SFE) [22–24], which is usually beneficial to the formation of the dislocation cell by the cross glide of the screw component of [25]. In order to resolve this contrary, the term “glide plane softening” is proposed and well interpreted by the interaction between dislocation and short-range order (SRO) or clustering [14,26]. Another interesting phenomenon is the twinning in the austenitic low-density steel, which however usually take places in the austenite steel with relative lower SFE $\mathrm { p f } 2 0 { - } 4 5 \mathrm { m J } { \cdot } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } ,$ , such as the TWIP steel. It is reported [13,27] that deformation twinning is found in the region near the grain boundary at high strain during the tensile deformation of the austenitic low-density steel with SFE about $7 9 { - } 1 0 8 \ \mathrm { m J { \cdot } m ^ { - 2 } }$ , which is explained by the local stress concentration enabling the critical stress for dislocation gliding larger than that of twinning. However, other factors could also result in the formation of the deformation twinning, such as deformation temperature, grain orientation, Schmid factor, SRO and planar slip [27–33].

Comparing with the extensive work has been done in the low-density steel, few studies has been found for the low-density steel with lowcarbon content. This research trend may be explained by the fact that high carbon content could result in much more reduction of the density of the low-density. Low temperature mechanical behavior of microalloyed and controlled-rolled Fe-Mn-Al-C-X alloys steel and also the relative high strength and ductility as demonstrated in literature. However, with increasing of the carbon content, the tendency of the precipitation of κ-carbide is enhanced, which would impair the toughness and be detrimental to the welding properties of the high-carbon low-density steel. It is reported that a very high toughness about 200 J [34] in the cryogenic temperature could be obtained in the low-density steel with a carbon content of 0.3 %, which making these low-carbon low-density steel suitable to the application as a cryogenic steel. However, no detailed research on the microstructure evolution and mechanical responses have been done, which is essential to the application of the low-carbon low-density steel in the cryogenic temperature.

In this study, the austenite-based duplex low-density steel with of 0.1%C, 35%Mn and 6.0%Al with SFE $( \gamma _ { S F E } )$ of 62.64 mJ⋅m− 2 is designed and fabricated. The mechanical properties and the microstructure at both room temperature and cryogenic temperature are explored for this austenitic low-density steel. It could be expected that both extensive deformation twinning and dislocation gliding could take place due to the high stack fault energy and short-range ordering as found in the high carbon low-density steel. The deformation microstructure of deformation twinning and dislocation structure will be characterized by the EBSD, TEM and XRD and the mechanical response of both tensile properties and impact toughness will be examined by the tensile test and Charpy impact test. Our objective is to reveal the microstructure evolution during tensile deformation process at both ambient temperature and cryogenic temperature and its effects on the mechanical properties. Both strengthening and toughening mechanism of the low-carbon lowdensity steel will be discussed in this study.

The phases fraction was determined by X-ray diffraction analysis (Bruker D8 Focus) to confirm the stability of austenite after different tensile strain. The microstructure was characterized in details by scanning electron microscope (SEM) and electron back-scattered diffraction (EBSD) in a scanning electron microscope (JSM-7800 F). The geometrically necessary dislocation density can be estimated with Eq. (1) from the EBSD data of the specimens after tensile deformation with different tensile strain [33]:

$$
\rho^ {G N D} = 2 \theta / u b \tag {1}
$$

in which u is the unit length for EBSD test, b is the Burgers vector, and θ is the average KAM value of the selected area.

In order to measure the dislocation density in the specimens after tensile deformation with different tensile strain, the average values of crystallite size D and micro-strain $\langle \varepsilon ^ { 2 } \rangle ^ { 1 / 2 }$ were estimated according to the Williamson–Hall equation [36,37] by using the profiles of $( 1 1 1 ) _ { \gamma } ,$ (200) and (220) diffraction peaks after Gaussian curve fitting. The dislocation densities ρ could be estimated by the following equation [36, 37]:

$$
\rho = \frac {3 \sqrt {2 \pi} \langle \varepsilon^ {2} \rangle^ {1 / 2}}{D b} \tag {2}
$$

where ε is the average micro-strain, D is average crystallite size, b is the Burgers vector (0.26 nm).

Tensile tests were carried out on standard tensile specimens, with gauge length of 25 mm and diameter of 5 mm, cut from the rods of the water quenched steel samples with the tensile axis direction parallel to the rod axis. The stress strain responses and corresponding strain hardening rates of the solution-treated specimens were investigated from tensile tests carried out at an initial strain rate of $1 . 0 \times 1 0 ^ { - 3 } \bar { \mathrm { s } } ^ { - 1 }$ 1 at room temperature (RT) and liquid nitrogen temperature (LNT) using an MTS tensile machine. At least three tensile specimens were deformed until fracture to confirm reproducibility. To reveal the microstructural evolution during straining, interrupted tensile tests to engineering strains of 3 %, 10 %, 20 %, 28 %, 38 % and 55 % were performed. The toughness of the samples was measured by the Charpy V-notch impact test on the standard samples (55 mm in length, 10 mm in width, 10 mm in thickness, and a V-notch depth of 2 mm) and the test recording the force-displacement curves and energy-displacement curves at RT and LNT.

To further understand the differences in impact toughness at RT and LNT, the instrumented impact test was applied to reveal the different impact energy contribution from the crack initiation and crack propagation. The results were displaced in Fig. 2(c) by the impact force-displacement curves and energy-displacement curves, in which the impact energy was calculated by the integration of the forcedisplacement curve, which was taken as the impact energy. It is very interesting that crack initiation is almost the same of \~80–100 J for both room temperature deformation and cryogenic temperature deformation. However, the crack propagation energy is changed significantly from 251 J at room temperature to 156 J at cryogenic temperature. This means that the Charpy impact at both temperature is governed by crack propagation energy.

# 3.3. EBSD analysis of tensile microstructural evolution

The texture evolutions of austenite along tensile axis (tensile axis is shorten as TA) during tensile deformation at RT and LNT are examined by EBSD and the results shown in Fig. 3(a) and (b). The IPF of the tensile samples at room temperature are calculated from the orientation data measured by an in-situ EBSD tension experiments. It can be seen that a weak texture of the austenite as shown in Fig. 3(a) before the tensile test, but the texture strength increases gradually with increasing of the tensile deformation strain. Finally, a very strong <111>//TA texture and weak ${ < } 1 0 0 { > } / / \mathrm { T A }$ texture were observed in the samples tensile deformed at RT as shown in Fig. 3(a). Similar texture evolution is found in the samples tensile deformed at cryogenic temperature as shown in Fig. 3(b). It should be pointed out, the texture strength of <111>//TA texture component at room temperature is slightly higher than that deformed at cryogenic temperature. However, the texture strength of <111>//TA texture component at the maximum uniform elongation at cryogenic temperature is significantly higher than that deformed at room temperature, which is clearly resulted from the higher tensile deformation strain at cryogenic temperature.

Fig. 4 displays the black, red, blue and green lines representing the evolution of the average {111} ${ < } 1 1 0 > _ { \gamma }$ Schmid factor and {111} ${ < } 1 1 2 { > } _ { \eta }$ Schmid factor with deformation at RT, {111} ${ < } 1 1 0 > _ { \gamma }$ Schmid factor and {111} $< 1 1 2 > _ { \gamma }$ Schmid factor with deformation at LNT, respectively. It can be seen that the average $\{ 1 1 1 \} < 1 1 0 > _ { \gamma }$ Schmid factor at RT achieved its maximum value near 3 % tensile strain, while the average $\{ 1 1 1 \} < 1 1 2 > _ { \gamma }$ Schmid factor at RT showed a decrease at the same strain level. This means that in the early stage of deformation, the activation of dislocation slip is easier than that of twinning. Upon increasing the strain, the average {111} ${ < } 1 1 2 { > } _ { \eta }$ Schmid factor surpassed the {111} ${ < } 1 1 0 > _ { \tau }$ average Schmid factor at RT, which attributed to the development of ${ < } 1 1 1 { > } / / \mathrm { T A }$ and ${ < } 1 0 0 { > } / / \mathrm { T A }$ textures. It is very interesting that both the average {111} ${ < } 1 1 0 > _ { \gamma }$ Schmid factor and the $\{ 1 1 1 \} < 1 1 2 > _ { \gamma }$ Schmid factor at LNT reach their maximum values at approximately 10 % engineering strain. This means that dislocation slip and twinning are easier to occur in the early stage of deformation at LNT.

Fig. 5 shows the orientation dependence of deformation mechanism of our studied steel deformed at both RT and LNT. It can be seen that the deformation mechanisms are strongly dependent on the crystal orientation, such as ${ < } 0 0 1 { > } / / \mathrm { T A }$ orientation with angle deviation of approximately 15◦ (type I), <111>//TA orientation with angle deviation of approximately 15◦ (type III) and the rest apart from type I and III (type II) as defined by I. Gutierrez-Urrutia [40]. Fig. 5(a1) illustrates the correlation between deformation twinning and grain orientation after 38 % tensile deformation at room temperature. Similar to the study by I. Gutierrez Urrutia et al., twins are more likely to occur in the type II and type III grains. Due to the very small fraction of the grains with deformation twinning, the tensile deformation of our studied steel at RT is controlled by dislocation gliding.

Fig. 5(b1, c1, d1) illustrates the correlation between deformation twinning and grain orientation at 28 %, 38 %, and 55 % engineering strain at LNT, respectively. Similar to room temperature deformation, twins are more readily formed in type II and type III grains. As shown in Fig. 5(b1, b2, c1, c2, d1, d2), plenty deformation twins could be found in the microstructure when the studied steel is deformed at LNT. And the deformation twinning fraction increased with the increasing of the deformation strain. Thus, the deformation twinning plays an important role in the deformation behaviors of our studied low-carbon low-density steel at LNT temperature, which is strongly dependent on the grain orientations. It can be observed in Fig. 5(c2) that type II grains tend to form single orientation twinning, while type III grains tend to form multiple orientation twinning.

Fig. 6 further illustrates the relationship between glide traces and grain subdivision. Interestingly, two types of dislocation glide traces as shown in Fig. 6(a–c) and (d-f), double dislocation glide traces and single dislocation slip traces, are formed. It can be seen that type II grains exhibit a propensity for the single slip traces, Type III grains demonstrate a tendency to develop double glide traces, whereas only a small fraction of type I grains exhibit slip traces. The study of Fe-30.5Mn-2.1Al-1.2C (wt.%) steel [41] at 0.1 true strain demonstrates that grains with orientations close to <111>//TA and <001>//TA are prone to forming a Taylor lattice, while grains with orientations oriented along the line between ${ < } 0 0 1 { > } / / \mathrm { T A }$ and ${ < } 1 1 1 { > } / / \mathrm { T A }$ directions are inclined to form high-density dislocation walls. However, in low-carbon low-density steel, the deformation microstructure of grains close to ${ < } 0 0 1 { > } / / \mathrm { T A }$ is featured by the formation of the dislocation cells. This microstructure difference between present steel and Fe-30.5Mn-2.1Al-1.2C (wt.%) steel may be resulted from low-carbon content and high deformation rate, which promote the cross glide in grains with ${ < } 1 0 0 { > } / / \mathrm { T A } .$ .

# 3.4. TEM analysis of tensile microstructural evolution

For the understanding of the deformation behaviors of our studied steel at RT, the deformation microstructures are examined by TEM after different deformation strain and the results are displayed in Fig. 7. It can be seen, when the strain reaches to 3 %, complex dislocation configurations are formed, such as dislocation nodes, multiple dislocations and dislocation pairs (Fig. 7(a)), which are supposed to be related to planar slip [21]. After 10 % deformation, Lomer-Cottrell (LC) locks and the high-density dislocation walls (HDDWs) are formed (Fig. 7(b and c)), which are regularly observed in other FCC materials [21,42]. After 20 % strain, the so-called Taylor lattices, cell blocks (CBs) and dislocation cells (DCs) are formed along {111} slip plane (Fig. 7(d–f)). It should be noted that Taylor lattice is not popular compared to dislocation cell and cell block. This implies that dislocations are more prone to cross slip. Furthermore, grains (type I) oriented close to ${ < } 1 0 0 { > } / / \mathrm { T A }$ are more prone to forming dislocation cells (Fig. 7(f)), while grains (type II and III) oriented away from ${ < } 1 0 0 { > } / / \mathrm { T A }$ are more likely to develop cell blocks (Fig. 7(e)). Fig. 7(g–j) shows that the CBs and DCs are refined as the strain increases to 28 %. The refinement of cell blocks also has been observed in TWIP and lightweight steels [40,41]. A small amount of deformation twinning has been formed along the cell block walls (CBWs), as shown in Fig. 7(g and h). Furthermore, a few microbands (Fig. 7(j)) are found, which is an indication of stress localization.

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Advantages to be one of the cryogenic steels and stacking fault energy assessment of the low-carbon low-density steel

The cryogenic steel is a special steel applied under the condition with very low temperature, specially the LNT. The selection of the cryogenic steel is very conservative, requiring not only the excellent cryogenic toughness but also the high ductility to assure the safety of the cryogenic equipment during shipping and storage [45]. Thus, the asthenic steel such as the high-Mn TWIP-steel rather than the conventional 9Ni steel is much more welcome due to its high cryogenic toughness and excellent ductility at LNT [45,46].

For the revealing of the advantages of our designed low-carbon lowdensity steel, the properties of the cryogenic steel were collected from literature and compared with our new designed steel as shown in Fig. 12. It can be seen from Fig. 10(a) that the Charpy impact energy and specific yield strength of the steel at liquid nitrogen temperature and those of other steels (converted to full size $1 0 \times 1 0 \times 5 5 \mathrm { m m } )$ . It can be observed that, compared to high carbon lightweight steel [47,48] and TWIP steel [46], the designed low-carbon lightweight steel exhibits a higher Charpy impact energy. In contrast to low-carbon high manganese steel [29] and 304-stainless steels [49], the designed low-carbon lightweight steel demonstrates a superior specific yield strength. 304-stainless steel is mechanically unstable and undergoes martensitic transformation at low temperatures, leading to a significant reduction in its impact energy. Although the duplex structure plays a crucial role in the fracture behavior of steel, the presence of a small amount of ferrite can significantly refine the austenite grains, which substantially enhances its impact toughness [29]. Moreover, compared to martensite, ferrite is more readily deformed, which may alleviate stress concentration at the $\gamma / \alpha$ interface to some extent. The strength and toughness of our designed lightweight steel are comparable to those of the Al0.1CoCrFeNi high-entropy alloy [50]. The ductility and the specific yield strength of the steel at liquid nitrogen temperature and those of other steels are shown in Fig. 10(b). The ductility of our studied steel is in the same level comparing with the austenite TWIP steel, but nearly 3 times higher than that of the 9Ni-steel, indicating a strong resistance of deformation induced rupture. Furthermore, although 9Ni-steel has been well recognized as a cryogenic alloy [51], the price of our designed low-carbon low-density steel is much lower that of the conventional 9Ni-steel, due to its low-cost elements alloying and simple high temperature solution heat treatment as revealed in the experiment section in this research. There are still challenges of high Ni alloy cost and complex heat treatment processes. Similarly, other stainless steel and high-entropy alloy is more expensive because of their high Co or Ni alloying design. Based on above comparison, the advantages of low-density, excellent cryogenic toughness and ductility and low-cost of our designed low-carbon low-- density steel are very impressive comparing with other cryogenic steels. These advantages make this steel to be one of the promising materials as cryogenic application.

It is well recognized that the addition of aluminum into the low-density steel could increase the stack fault energy significantly, which in turn suppressed the occurrence of twins in the low-density steel [22,24].

# 4.2. Deformation twinning mechanism of low-density steel with high SFE

Austenitic steel with medium SFE $( 2 0 { - } 4 5 \mathrm { m J { \cdot } m } ^ { - 2 } )$ is typically prone to the formation of deformation twins [2,24]. The spontaneous formation of deformation twinning is unlikely occurred in the low-density steel because of its high stacking fault energy, especially at room temperature [11,12,14]. Interestingly, our designed high-stacking fault energy lightweight steel, with a room temperature SFE of 62.64 mJ⋅m− 2 and a liquid nitrogen temperature SFE of 49.46 $\mathrm { m J } { \cdot } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 }$ , has been generated deformation twins at both room temperature and liquid nitrogen temperature. The deformation twinning from this study is demonstrated strongly dependent on the deformation temperature and crystal orientation. The grain orientation dependence of twinning has also been observed in TWIP steel and high carbon low aluminum lightweight steel $[ 2 8 , 4 0 , 4 1 ]$ . Deformation twinning of type III grain is commonly explained via Schmid law of twin dislocation slip [58]:

However, they did not consider the influence of dislocation structures on the flow stress. In fact, type II grains, which generate twins, are scarcely impacted by the twins of adjacent grains, as displayed in Fig. 5(c1-c3).

Such twinning observed in this high SFE steel is similar to that found in Cu–10Mn (at. %) alloy [31], Fe-22Mn-x $\left( \mathbf { x } = 0 , 3 , 6 \right) \mathrm { A l } \mathbf { - } 0 . 6 \mathrm { C }$ [62] and Fe30Mn11Al1.2C(wt.%) steels [27]. Twinning cannot be activated for copper in traditional deformation processes, but the incorporation of 10 at. % Mn can induce twin formation. The authors attributed this to the presence of SRO, which promotes planar slip causing that critical twinning stress can be easily reached by stress concentration [31]. Park et al. [62] also attributed planar slip to the glide softening associated with SRO. In addition, they have qualitatively proved that the interaction between dislocation and SRO can lower the critical stress for twinning in the Fe-22Mn-x $( \mathbf { x } = 3 , 6 ) \ : \mathrm { A l } – 0 . 6 \mathrm { C }$ steel [62]. Ren et al. [27] obtained similar findings that the high flow stress induced by dislocation planar slip and the reduction in critical twinning stress due to the presence of SRO may be the causes of the twinning phenomenon. In current work, the microstructure contains SRO (Fig. 1(d)). Furthermore, no typical mechanical antiphase domain boundaries (APBs) bounded by two partials are observed in this steel. The short-range order in our designed steel is similar to that of Fe-22Mn-x (x = 3,6) Al-0.6C steel [62]. Thus, the short-range order in our steel may reduce the stacking fault energy and critical twinning stress rather than increasing them. Type II and III grains form cell block walls with planar slip characteristics, which lead to stress concentration required for twinning nucleation and growth (Fig. 7(g–k)). Type I grains form uniform dislocation cells, which are not conducive to the formation of the high stress required for twinning growth. The shear stress arising from stress concentration at these cell block walls significantly exceeds the macroscopic shear stress. The homogeneous dislocation cell structure in type I grains effectively disperses the macroscopic shear stress. Thus, type II and III grains, which are not favorable for twin activation, paradoxically generate a substantial number of twins. Conversely, type I grains, which are conducive to twin initiation, only produce a small number of twins at the grain boundaries, which are governed by the twin nucleation stress. Therefore, the high stacking fault energy (SFE) twinning and its orientation dependence are mainly attributed to the stress concentration induced by planar dislocation substructure.

# 4.3. Multistage hardening behaviors and flow stress contribution

As it is shown in Fig. 2(b), the work hardening rate in stage A is sharply decreased with increasing of tensile strain at both RT and LNT. This sharp decreasing phenomenon could be easily interpreted by the dislocation accumulation and recovery [30,61]. However, the work hardening rate at LNT is much higher than that at RT, which could be attributed to the increased lattice friction stress with decreasing of deformation temperature as reported found in TWIP steel [30]. This explanation is consistent with our calculation that the lattice friction stresses are 143 MPa for RT deformation but 389 MPa for LNT deformation as revealed in subsequent calculations.

Stage B hardening at LNT is characterized by almost constant in the strain hardening rate. For low aluminum, high carbon lightweight steels, the Taylor lattice observed at low strains is ascribed to the solute carbon effect on diminishing the frequency of dislocation cross-glide [41]. Fig. 8 (a–c) shows that the cross-slip of dislocations is notably enhanced, which diminishes the propensity for the formation of a Taylor lattice. Therefore, the formation of a Taylor lattice is not favored in the designed lightweight steel. Consequently, the approximately constant strain hardening rate exhibited during this phase cannot be explained by the Taylor lattice. At 3 % engineering strain, distinct stacking faults are produced at the annealed twin boundaries (Fig. 8(b)). Upon reaching 10 % engineering strain, pronounced deformation twins are evident at the annealed twin boundaries (Fig. 8(c)), a result of the previously formed stacking faults (Fig. 8(b)). As detailed in Section 4.2, the true stress at 10 % engineering strain has surpassed the calculated lower bound of the critical twinning stress. Furthermore, during this phase, an increasing trend in the {111} ${ < } 1 1 2 { > } _ { \eta }$ Schmid factor is observed, which augments the twinning partition stress and favors twin activation. Consequently, the nearly constant high strain hardening rate observed in this stage is primarily attributed to the activation of twin at the annealed twin boundary.

Stage C is characterized by a continuous decrease in strain hardening rate associated with the refinement of the dislocation substructure. As discussed in Section 4.2, typical dislocation cells are formed in type I grains, while dislocation blocks are produced in Type II and Type III grains. This hardening mechanism, driven by the refinement of the dislocation substructure, manifests as a sustained reduction in the strain hardening rate [41]. Although this stage has reached the lower limit of critical stress under liquid nitrogen condition, the continuous decrease in the {111} <112> γ Schmid factor results in less active twinning development. Consequently, the strain hardening behavior in this stage is primarily governed by the refinement of the dislocation substructure.

During the initial phase of Stage D, the true stress at 38 % engineering strain under liquid nitrogen temperature (LNT), reaching 1280 MPa, has substantially surpassed the critical twinning stress of Taylor model. As a result, primary twins are substantially activated under these elevated stress conditions. Upon further straining to 55 %, the primary deformation twins are further enhanced, and the secondary twins are also significantly activated. The twin boundaries serve as effective barriers to dislocation motion [28,30,33], significantly contributing to the sustained hardening response observed during Stage D. Therefore, the continuous strain hardening in Stage D is ascribed to the extensive activation of primary and secondary twins.

It is widely known that during the deformation process, twin boundary effectively acted as barriers to dislocation glide by reducing their mean free path, known as the “dynamic Hall Petch effect” [28,30, 33,59]. However, Liang and Zhi et al. [63,64] demonstrated that dislocation evolution dominated the work hardening rate of TWIP steels instead of twins. Tang et al. [30] held that dislocation hardening and TWIP effects worked synergistically. Therefore, it is necessary to further clarify the contribution of twins and dislocations to work hardening. # 5. Conclusion

In this research, the low-carbon low-density steel of Fe-35Mn-6Al-0.1C (wt.%) was designed and its microstructure evolution and mechanical response at both RT and LNT were examined systematically. Different from the extensively studied low-density steel with a high carbon content around $1 . 0 \% ,$ massive deformation twinning was found not only in the tensile deformation mode but in the impact deformation mode, which gives the combination of excellent strength, ductility and V-notch impact toughness at both RT and LNT. Based on the study of the microstructure evolution at both RT and LNT, the factors affecting the deformation twinning, including SFE, temperature, crystallographic orientation, Schmid factor, and planar glide were analyzed thoroughly. The main conclusions can be summarized as follows.

1 In present low-carbon low-density steel, a two-stage deformation mechanism is observed for RT deformation and four-stage deformation mechanism is revealed for LNT deformation. Its RT behavior is dominated by dislocation planar slip, cells or cell blocks forming and refinement, followed with twinning occurring just before the necking at RT. However, its LNT deformation behavior is found to be started with the dislocation planar slip firstly, primary twinning secondly, cells or cell blocks forming and refinement and finally second deformation twinning at LNT, indicating a strong deformation twinning dependence on deformation temperature and deformation strain.
