# Evaluation of the elastic modulus of steels

Ulrich Bohnenkamp, Rolf Sandström

Literature data on the physical properties of steels have been collected and put into a database. The elastic modulus of steels has been analyzed as a function of composition. An overview over former studies is given. The steels have been investigated in three groups, martensitic and ferritic steels, ferritic steels separately, and austenitic steels. For the last two groups, a thermodynamic analysis with Thermo-Calc has been performed. Regression analysis on the influence of composition on the elastic modulus was then carried out. The results for ferritic steels reveal that cementite has no effect on the elastic modulus, whereas Cr, Mo, Si, Mn, and Cu increase it. The elastic modulus of austenitic steels is reduced by Ni and Mo and increased by N, NbC, TiC, and Cr. $Cr_{23}C_{6}$ , while statistically significant in the analysis, has no effect on the elastic modulus of austenitic steels. The regression coefficients found can be used to predict the elastic modulus of steels with known composition.

Berechnung des Elastizitätsmoduls von Stählen. Daten über physikalische Eigenschaften von Stählen wurden aus dem allgemein zugänglichen Schrifttum gesammelt und in eine Datenbank eingegeben. Der Elastizitätsmodul von Stahl wurde in Abhängigkeit von der chemischen Zusammensetzung analysiert. Ein Überblick über frühere Studien wird gegeben. Die Stähle wurden in drei Gruppen untersucht, martensitische und ferritische Stähle, ferritische Sorten getrennt, und austenitische Stähle. Für die letzten beiden Gruppen wurde eine thermodynamische Analyse mit Thermo-Calc durchgeführt. Anschließend wurde der Einfluß der Zusammensetzung auf den Elastizitätsmodul mit einer Regressionsanalyse untersucht. Das Ergebnis für ferritische Stähle zeigt, daß Zementit keinerlei Wirkung auf den Elastizitätsmodul hat, wohingegen Cr, Mo, Si, Mn und Cu ihn vergrößern. Der Elastizitätsmodul von austenitischen Stählen wird durch Ni und Mo reduziert, durch N, NbC, TiC und Cr vergrößert. Obwohl $Cr_{23}C_{6}$ in der chemischen Analyse von Bedeutung ist, zeigt es keinen Einfluß auf den Elastizitätsmodul austenitischer Stähle. Die ermittelten Regressionskoeffizienten können zur Betimmung des Elastizitätsmoduls von Stählen mit bekannter Zusammensetzung herangezogen werden.

Improvement of the materials properties of steels usually involves an empirical process. Where the qualitative understanding often is given, the quantitative understanding of the improvements is not well developed. Therefore, an attempt will be made to quantify them. Available data and models for steels are analyzed and relationships between composition, production, microstructure, property, and performance are established. These results form the basis of a systematic approach to materials design. The behaviour of new steels can be predicted and optimized for different applications. Accurate information about the elastic modulus is essential in all structural engineering design. The purpose of this paper is to give an overview over existing results and to present an analysis based on these results. The elastic modulus has been investigated with statistical methods.

# Former studies

On the effect of solid solutes in bcc iron on the elastic modulus, extensive studies have been made by preparation of (virtually) binary alloys. Takeuchi evaluated the elastic and shear modulus of single crystals of the six alloy systems Fe-Be, Fe-Al, Fe-Si, Fe-V, Fe-Ge and Fe-Mo [1]. Vanadium slightly increases the elastic modulus and the other elements reduce it.

Leslie's work on the effect of dilute substitutional solid solutions in fcc iron on mechanical and physical properties includes the elastic constants [2]. Speich et al. determined an increase in the elastic and shear modulus for the alloy-

ing elements Co, Re, Cr and Ir and a decrease for Rh, Pt, C, Mn and Ni; and they showed the influence of the composition of the binary alloys on Poisson's ratio. They split the effect of alloying elements on the elastic constants in two parts, first, the effect due to changes in the lattice parameters and second, the electronic contribution. They showed that the latter could be related to the position of the solute element in the periodic table for the case that the elements are in substitutional solid solution [3]. To properly evaluate the effect of carbon on the elastic constants of Fe-Ni-C martensite, Speich and Leslie corrected both retained austenite content and nickel content and found that the elastic and shear moduli decrease with increasing carbon content [4].

The understanding of solid solution strengthening was the motivation of the former studies. The effect of substitutional atoms in the fcc iron lattice on the strength is small. For this reason similar studies on austenitic steels have been carried out mainly for interstitials, i.e. carbon and nitrogen. Richter investigated the physical properties of steels including heat resistant and stainless steels $[5...7]$ . In contrast to the other authors, Richter investigated the properties of steels from the regular commercial production. Here, the weaker influences of most elements on the investigated properties remain covered by the effects of the elements that strongly affect these properties in solid solution with iron. Only tendencies can be given for the most important components. On the other hand, the results are more relevant for commercial steels.

Fink et al. studied physical properties on twelve steels, focussing on the temperature dependence and giving scatter bands for the different steel classes (low-alloy, high-Cr and austenitic) [5]. Richter covered several classes of steels with in total 52 alloys in [7]. Like in the former work, the focus is on the temperature dependence of the physical properties. Some influence of composition and microstructure is visible in the graphs but has not been further investigated.

Later, Richter graphically determined factors for variations of the elastic constants with composition and temperature [6]. For plain carbon and low alloy steels up to a mass content of 12 % chromium, Cr increases the elastic modulus at room temperature but the effect decreases at higher temperatures. Two high-carbon austenitic steels showed a somewhat smaller than average elastic modulus at RT (room temperature) but a significantly larger than average modulus at higher temperatures (>400°C). The N-content of these steels was not given. Poisson's ratio of austenitic steels is shown to increase with increasing nickel content. The elastic properties are sensitive to crystal textures. Since high chromium steels with a mass content of more than 12 % Cr usually show strong textures, Richter did not give values for their elastic constants.

Richter investigated physical properties including the elastic constants for several melts of two heat-resistant low-alloy steels. He studied the scatter in the properties on different samples of one melt and on samples of different melts and stated that the influence of changing composition on the physical properties of these steels, especially in annealed condition, dominates over changes in microstructure [8]. He studied the influence of composition on the properties separately for each steel grade. The results for the elastic constants seem not to be useful since they contradict each other. In a later study on two austenitic stainless steels Richter provided a similar amount of data on the sample- and melt induced scatter of the physical properties but did not carry out any regression analysis [9].

# Data collection

Data from the open literature have been collected and the data have been put into a database. The data for multi-component steels from the papers discussed above have been used, among others [10...14].

Most data on properties of steels in literature are given for the standard compositions of the steels. It has been tried to use such data for these computations, hoping that the mass of data would overcome the lack of precision, with no success. So only data that included the exact composition for the investigated samples could be considered. For the elastic modulus Richter indicates an accuracy of $\pm0.3\%$ which equals $\pm0.6$ GPa for a typical value of about 200 GPa for steels. He gives the same relative error for the shear modulus. Poisson's ratio is determined to $\pm3\%$ which is equivalent to $0.3\pm0.01$ .

A general problem in this investigation of the elastic modulus of steels is its strong dependence on the texture and the relatively weak influence of changes in the composition. The only information one could obtain about the texture in the samples was from Poisson's ratio. Poisson's ratio of untextured pure iron is known to be 0.291; deviations indicate texture. In engineering work Poisson's ratio of untextured steels is often taken as 0.3, independent of composition and microstructure. However, this is not necessarily a good approximation. Speich et al. showed for binary alloys that the influence of the alloying element on elastic and shear modulus is in principle the same [3]. So in their case Poisson's ratio varies for untextured samples between 0.26 (atomic mass fraction of $10\%$ Ir or $10\%$ Mn) and 0.33 (atomic mass fraction of $10\%$ Ni). Data of steels with a Poisson's ratio in that region are considered to be untextured. If Poisson's ratio was not given it had to be computed from the elastic and the shear modulus.

Since the dynamic measurement of the elastic modulus produces the most reproducible results and requires only small sample sizes most authors use this method, though the values slightly differ from the results of static measurements. Results of dynamic tests were used only, which are calculated from the sonic speed at high frequencies. The temperature dependence of the elastic modulus has been investigated in all sources. The present work was related to a room temperature of $20^{\circ}$ C. There was not always a data point on exactly that temperature and due to the temperature dependence of the elastic modulus it was necessary to correct even small deviations like $5^{\circ}$ C. Interpolated or extrapolated values were used where three or four neighbouring points were fitted to a line or a second order polynomial.

# Data analysis

For ferritic steels, two analyses are provided. First, a study where the phase composition was neglected and second, a study where the amount of cementite and ferrite in the steels and the concentrations of elements in solid solution with these phases have been computed in a thermodynamic analysis. Regression analysis on the influence of composition on the elastic modulus was performed. The regression formula $E_{Steel} = \Sigma r_{i} C_{i}$ has been used, where $C_{i}$ is the mass content of a component i in %, and $r_{i}$ is the regression coefficient for that component. Both elements in solid solution with ferrite and the total amount of cementite have been treated as components i. The microstructure of the steels, i.e., the shape and size of precipitates, was neglected due to lack of data, as was the composition of the cementite. For case one the regression coefficient of C can be converted to a coefficient for cementite if cementite is assumed to consist of pure $Fe_{3}C$ in all investigated steels. A stepwise regression analysis yields the regression coefficients in the order of their significance. For austenitic steels the amount of the phases $Cr_{23}C_{6}$ , NbC, and TiC has been calculated in a thermodynamic analysis. Steels forming other phases have been excluded from the study. The three carbides have been taken as components i in the regression formula, neglecting variations in their composition.

# Discussion

Influence of composition on the elastic modulus of ferritic steels. Cr, Mo, and Si significantly increase the elastic modulus of ferritic and martensitic steels. For Cr it is a contributing factor that it can be added in much higher concentrations than Mo or Si. Ni is insignificant in study II because there it was only present with a maximum mass content of 1.64 %. With Ni mass contents of up to 9.25 % in study I, the coefficient for Ni is only valid for higher concentrations above a mass content of 3 %, because at this point there is a phase border in the Fe-Ni-phase diagram. The higher value for the coefficient for Cr in study I is an indicator for the role of the martensitic microstructure in some steels.

The regression coefficients can be used to predict the elastic modulus of ferritic steels if the composition is known. For use of the model II (table 2) and for austenitic steels containing carbides (table 3), a thermodynamic analysis has to be performed. The regression coefficients are valid in the concentration range they were determined in; this range is given in tables 1 to 3. Reasonable estimates can be expected for compositions exceeding the range of our analysis but remaining within the region of the phase diagram in which the coefficients were determined, i.e. the $\alpha$ -region for Cr, Mn, and Mo, and the 2-phase region for C, Ni, and Si. A linear relationship between the elemental concentration and its influence on the elastic modulus is assumed. This is fulfilled for most elements.

A reasonable agreement is found with the literature data obtained in experiments on multicrystalline binary alloys at RT. Deviations are found from estimates of the elastic modulus from measurements on single crystals. Ni has been known to reduce the elastic modulus and Cr to increase it. C is reported to reduce the elastic modulus of steel progressively with increasing mass contents up to 0.5% [3]. Mitsubayashi et al. reported an elastic modulus of single-phase cementite film of 177 GPa and cited values of 160 GPa for single-phase cementite film and 170 and 200 GPa for cementite in cast iron [16]. Unidirectionally solidified cementite has been reported to have a low elastic modulus of 138 GPa transversal to crystal growth direction but 227 GPa in growth direction [17]. For arbitrary orientation in steel, some effective value lower than the average of 183 GPa will apply. A mass content of 7% Cr in cementite raises the elastic modulus to 222 respectively 235 GPa in the two directions. All values were extrapolated from directionally solidified Fe-C-Cr-alloys with C mass contents of between 1 to 4% [17]. Since the lattice parameter of cementite shows a minimum at a mass content of 10% Cr [18], it is likely that its elastic modulus shows a maximum at that concentration. For the steels in this study, a mass content of 20% Cr and 4% Mn on average has been computed. Experimental data for that concentration range of Cr and for the effect of Mn were not available. The regression coefficient of cementite closely matches the coefficient of iron in the present study. For that reason it was not attempted to study the interaction of the two phases with parallel or series models or alike. The increasing effect of Mo and Si could not be verified against literature data of experiments on polycrystalline samples.

Regression analysis of the influence of composition on the elastic modulus of austenitic steels. Nitrogen as an interstitial solute in austenite deforms the lattice and increases the elastic modulus. Probable cause of the strong effect might be clustering to Cr dissolved in the lattice. It has been demonstrated in [19] that the elastic modulus increases with increasing N-content while the composition otherwise was held virtually constant, indicating a coefficient of 34 GPa/N mass content in % in austenite at RT. This result is reproduced within the large standard deviation in our regression analysis. The small amount of data on N-containing steels in the study (4 batches, 3 batches from source [19]) causes this deviation. The effect of Cr and Ni on the elastic modulus of austenite has been qualitatively known. NbC has a reported elastic modulus of 350-500 GPa and TiC of 460 GPa. Elastic modulus data for $Cr_{23}C_{6}$ was not available. The effect of precipitates like carbides on the elastic modulus must be related to the modulus of the precipitate, so this regression result seems to be reasonable. Conclusions from the elastic modulus data of pure elements to the effect of that element when in solid solution with iron can not be drawn.

# Conclusions

Literature data on the physical properties of steels have been collected from the open literature and put into a database. The steels have been investigated in three groups, martensitic and ferritic steels, ferritic steels separately with a smaller composition range, and austenitic steels. For ferritic and austenitic steels, a thermodynamic analysis has been performed. The influence of the composition on the elastic modulus of steels has been studied by regression analysis.

The results obtained by the present regression analysis are:

- if the phase composition and microstructure of the investigated steels is neglected and regression analysis on the effect of elemental composition on the elastic modulus of martensitic and ferritic steels is performed, then
- Cr, Mo, and Si are found to increase and
- C and Ni are found to decrease the elastic modulus of these steels;
- if the phase composition and the elemental compositions of the phases in ferritic steels are computed in a thermodynamic analysis, then
- Cr, Mo, Si, and Mn are found to increase the elastic modulus,
- cementite, hardened by solutes in the steels investigated in this study, has the same regression coefficient as $\alpha$ -iron and shows no effect on the elastic modulus;
- for austenitic steels, the phase compositions and the elemental compositions of the phases were computed in a thermodynamic analysis;
- for pure $\gamma$ -iron at room temperature, the elastic modulus of $195\mathrm{GPa}$ is found to be lower than that of pure $\alpha$ -iron,
- N, Cr, NbC, and TiC increase and
- Ni and Mo reduce the elastic modulus of austenitic steels;
- $\mathrm{Cr_{23}C_6}$ appears with the same regression coefficient as $\gamma$ -iron in the study, thus having no effect on the elastic modulus of austenitic steels.
