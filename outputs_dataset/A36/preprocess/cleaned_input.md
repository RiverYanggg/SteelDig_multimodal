# A B S T R A C T

In this investigation, a Fe–20Mn–9Al-1.5C lightweight austenitic steel has been developed through strategic alloying with Ni and Cr elements coupled with the employment of a thermo-mechanical processing technique featuring ultra-fast cooling. This composition and treatment synergistically transcend the conventional trade-off between strength and ductility, positioning the mechanical performance of this steel notably above that of comparable low-density austenitic steels previously reported. The coordinated incorporation of Ni and Cr is key to modulating the precipitation threshold for nanoscale κ-carbides and LRO domains, achieving an optimal size for precipitate-induced strengthening. The incorporation of Cr specifically contributes to a dual enhancement mechanism: it fosters the generation of high-density dislocation arrays (HDDA) and refines the dynamic slip band (DSBR) during deformation, which in turn, elevates the strain hardenability and ductility. The remarkable synergy in strength and ductility is attributed to a combination of mechanisms including grain boundary reinforcement, solid solution hardening, the precipitation strengthening effect of nanoscale precipitates, and the activation of secondary slip system dislocations. This synergy is quantitatively reflected in the material’s yield strength (YS) exceeding 1100 MPa, ultimate tensile strength (UTS) surpassing 1200 MPa, and a total elongation (TEL) exceeding 45%. The insights gained from this research provide critical guidance for the design and creation of austenitic low-density steels that do not compromise between strength and ductility, thereby offering superior overall performance.

# 1. Introduction

In the domains of automobile, maritime, and aerospace engineering, the pursuit of lightweight metal materials is a persistent scholarly focus. Offering significant weight savings contributing to fuel efficiency [1–3], Fe–Mn–Al–C steel emerges as a material of choice compared with conventional Al alloys [4], Ti alloys [5], dual-phase steel [6] and stainless steel [7], etc, exhibiting superior noise, impact damping capabilities [8] and high strength-ductile in lightweight applications [9–18]. Based on the constituent phases, these lightweight steels are categorized into three types, i.e., ferritic steels, austenitic steels, and dual-phase ferritic-austenitic steels [19]. The austenitic variant of this steel displays commendable mechanical properties, including a tensile strength of over 800 MPa, ductility with elongations exceeding 10%, and a modest density range of 6.8–7.4 g/cm3 [19]. Nevertheless, while the base yield strength of these steels typically spans 300–800 MPa [19–21], it is important to note that higher yield strengths can be achieved through subsequent aging treatments or by the incorporation of additional alloying elements, as demonstrated in Refs. [19,22,23].

Extensive research into the Fe–Mn–Al–C quaternary system has elucidated that adding Al element can not only efficaciously reduce the density of steel but also strengthen it through the genesis of nanoscale κ-carbides. Incrementing Al content in Fe–Mn–Al–C-based steels typically yields formidable specific strengths, attributing to a synergistic effect of diminished density and augmented strain hardening [24]. Each 1 wt% increment of Al can cause a 1.3% density reduction [19]. Additionally, Al catalyzes the formation of the κ-carbides, which manifests as both intragranular κ and intergranular $\kappa ^ { * } .$ -carbides, with the former playing a pivotal role in strengthening austenitic low-density steels [20, 25,26]. At the atomic scale within γ-austenite, a mix of Fe, Mn, and Al atoms randomly populate a face-centred cubic lattice. When the preferential occupation of lattice vertex points by Al and the centre of the face by Fe and Mn, an ordered γ-austenite phase with $\mathbf { L } 1 _ { 2 }$ structure is formed, sometimes referred to as a short-range ordered phase [27]. When C takes over octahedral interstices, the body centre $( 1 / 2 , 1 / 2 ,$ , 1/2), κ-carbides are formed [28]. κ-carbide characterized by a perovskite crystal structure, also known as an $\operatorname { E 2 } _ { 1 }$ structure, has an ideal stoichiometric of $( \mathrm { F e } , \mathrm { M n } ) _ { 3 } \mathrm { A l C }$ [29]. A same structure as the κ-carbides, but partial octahedral interstices position without carbon occupancy is called metastable (Fe, $\mathbf { M n } ) _ { 3 } \mathbf { A l C _ { x } } ( \mathbf { x } \ < \ 1 )$ phase formed by spinodal decomposition, known as κ′ or $\mathbf { L } ^ { \prime } \mathbf { 1 } _ { 2 }$ phase [27,30]. Y. F. An et al. [11] investigated the precipitation and transformation pathways of the coherent nano κ-carbides in austenitic low-density steels with adding Ni, revealing that nano-sized κ′-carbides significantly bolster yield strength. M. J. Yao et al. [21] have posited that ductility remains largely unimpaired due to the coherence between κ-carbides and austenite and the shear ability of κ-carbides by dislocation. References $[ 1 0 , 3 1 , 3 2 ]$ show that adding Cr mitigates intergranular coarse $\kappa ^ { * } .$ -carbide precipitation in γ-austenite, which is beneficial for elongation [33–35]. The effect of Ni and Cr elements in controlling the κ-carbides remain largely uncharted.

G. Frommeyer et al. [36]discussed high-strength austenite lightweight steels with a general composition of Fe-(18–28)Mn-(9–12) Al-(0.7–1.2)C steel, distributed by nanoscale κ′-carbide and α-Fe(Al, Mn) ferrite. They delivered a harmonious balance of low density and robust mechanical properties, including high strength and significant ductility. The main deformation mechanism of these steels is the shear-zone-induced plasticity (SIP) effect. Z. Q. Wu et al. [37] investigated the microstructure evolution and deformation mechanism of Fe–12Mn–8Al-0.8C steel, revealing a gradual transition from Taylor lattice to highly dense dislocation walls and microstrips is observed in austenite related to changes in the substructure of such dislocations as the strain increases. The deformation behavior and strengthening mechanism of Cr and Ni alloyed Fe–Mn–Al–C steels have not been studied deeply.

Hence, this work endeavors to prepare low-density steel with a comprehensive excellent property and a density below $6 . 8 ~ \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 3 }$ with exemplary attributes by alloying with 9 wt% Al. Through rigorous EBSD, TEM, and computational analyses, this research comprehensively scrutinizes the microstructural and mechanical properties of Fe–Mn–Al–C steel by Ni and Cr alloying, elucidating the growth mechanisms of nanoscale κ-carbides and examining their strain hardening and deformation behaviors under different cumulative strains. Consequent discussions will dissect the deformation and strengthening mechanics of Fe–Mn–Al–C steel according to the experimental results and the theoretical models, particularly in the context of Ni and Cr alloying, aiming to unravel the mechanisms underpinning their contribution to steel strengthening and ductility effect. This research is a significant contribution to the development of Fe–Mn–Al–C-xNi-yCr austenitic lightweight steels to become more competitive among the widespread lightweight structural materials.

# 2. Materials and experimental details

Four ingots with nominal compositions of Fe–20Mn–9Al-1.5C-xNiyCr (x = 0, 2 wt%, $\mathbf { y } = 0 , 3 ,$ , 5 wt%) were prepared by vacuum induction melting under a high-purity Ar atmosphere. The measured compositions of these as-cast ingots are shown in Table 1. The as-cast ingots were homogenized at $1 2 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 h. The initial and finishing rolling temperature is $1 0 4 0 { \mathrm { - } } 1 0 6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $9 5 0 { - } 9 7 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ respectively. The ingots were hot-rolled (HR) to 4 mm-thick sheets with 95% thickness reduction. The plate was online ultrafast cooled (UFCed) to ambient temperature with a cooling rate of ${ \sim } 4 0 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } / s$ , based on the efficient-use-water ultrafastcooled device (ADCOS-PM) developed in-house by our laboratory.

The tensile specimens were cut by electrical discharge machining (EDM) along the rolling direction (RD) of the steel plate with a gauge length of 25 mm, a thickness of 3 mm and a width of 7 mm. Tensile tests were performed at a crosshead speed of 3 mm/min in a Gotech electronic universal testing machine, where a laser extensometer was used to monitor the tensile strain. At least three tensile specimens of each steel were deformed to fracture to ensure reproducibility. To reveal the microstructural evolution during straining, interrupted tensile tests to engineering strains of 3% (or 5%) and 10% were performed.

The microstructure was analyzed by scanning electron microscopy (SEM), electron back-scatter diffraction (EBSD) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). The possible precipitation structures and their energies for the Fe–20Mn–9Al-1.5C steel were calculated by the Materials Project database (https://legacy.materialsproject.org/). The Materials Project database employs density functional theory (DFT) calculations using the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) with the Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) functional [38]. EBSD was performed in a Zeiss Ultra55 scanning electron microscope, and specimens were prepared by vibration polishing with 50 nm colloidal silica for 2.5–24 h in a Buehler-VibroMet 2. TEM was performed on a FEI Tecnai G2 F20 instrument operating at 200 kV. The TEM specimens were cut by EDM with a thickness of 1 mm and then first ground into foils with a thickness of <80 μm. Subsequently, the foils were further thinned using Struers TenuPol-3 electrochemical double-jet equipment, which was formed into an electrolyte of 10 vol% perchloric acid and 90 vol% ethanol at a voltage of 20 V and a temperature of $- 3 0 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ .

These four steels have similar grain sizes, but these steels show an increase in elongation with an increase in Cr content and an increase in yield strength with an increase in Ni, indicating that a strength-ductility combination can be achieved by alloying with Ni and Cr. # 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Formation of an $E 2 _ { 1 }$ -type ordered structure promoted by Cr or Ni alloying

Mechanical properties are closely related to the evolution of the microstructure. In this study, TEM characterization of Fe–20Mn-9A1- 1.5C-xNi-yCr lightweight steel austenite is shown in Fig. 1. The formation of κ-carbides in austenite is in accordance with the nucleation and growth mechanism, and the different degrees of the ordered phases and the ordered domains of LRO and κ-carbides lead to different yield strengths [20,53]. Previous studies have shown that the addition of Al can promote the formation of ordered phases [19,20,53]. In this study, the steel under investigation contains not only the elements Fe, Mn, Al, and C but also Ni and $\mathrm { C r } ,$ which are added to influence the formation process of the κ-carbides. As depicted in Fig. 1, these two elements indeed affect the phase transition process of the κ-carbides.

For studying phase transformation and thermodynamic properties, formation energy is a crucial parameter as it relates to the stability and equilibrium of the κ-carbides. This study analyzed the effect of Ni and Cr on LRO and κ-carbides in Fe–20Mn-9A1-1.5C steel by first-principle calculations for the Fe–20Mn-9A1-1.5C lightweight steel studied. The possible structures and their energies for the Fe–20Mn–9Al-1.5C steel were calculated by the Materials Project database (https://legacy.ma terialsproject.org/). The Materials Project database employs density functional theory (DFT) calculations using the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) with the Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) functional [38]. Fig. 9 shows the crystal structures of (a) disordered $\gamma ,$ (b) ordered γ, (c) κ-carbide, and (d) AlNi C (one of the Ni-rich κ-carbide). In Fe–20Mn-9A1-1.5C-xNi-yCr lightweight austenitic steels, the Fe, Mn, and Al atoms randomly occupy the vertex and face-centre positions of the face-centred cubic lattice (Fig. 9a). When the Al element occupies the position of the vertex and the Fe and Mn elements occupy the face centre positions of the cubic lattice, the ordered γ-austenite phase is formed (Fig. 9b). When elemental C gradually occupies the body-centred (1/2, 1/2, 1/2) octahedral interstitials, κ-carbides are formed. κ-carbides are perovskite crystal structures, also known as $\operatorname { E 2 } _ { 1 }$ structures [29]. The ideal stoichiometry of κ-carbides is (Fe, Mn) AlC [29]. The results of the first-principle calculations for the possible formation of κ-carbides structure are shown in Table 3.

With the addition of the Cr element into the κ-carbides, the formation energy of the κ-carbides increases, requiring greater energy for phase transition, thus making it more difficult for κ-carbides nucleation and growth. Thus, the size of the κ-carbides decreases. Conversely, with the addition of Ni element into the κ-carbides, the formation energy of the κ-carbides decreases, requiring less energy for phase transition, thus making it easier for κ-carbides nucleation and growth. In the calculated results, as shown in Table $^ { 3 , }$ the addition of Cr into the steels has the potential to form the carbides with $\mathrm { C r } _ { 2 1 } ( \mathrm { F e C } _ { 3 } ) _ { 2 }$ structure in the γ-matrix. According to the calculated formation energy of the $\mathrm { C r } _ { 2 1 } ( \mathrm { F e C } _ { 3 } ) _ { 2 }$ structure, which is − 0.049 eV/atom, being negative indicates that the $\mathrm { C r } _ { 2 1 } ( \mathrm { F e C } _ { 3 } ) _ { 2 }$ structure of the carbides can form in nature. However, compared to the most commonly formed (Fe, Mn) AlC structure, it is higher by 0.111 eV/atom, increasing the nucleation energy barrier.

This phenomenon can also be found in 3D APT characterization [14] and first-principles calculations [24]. Factors such as the increase in the nucleation energy barrier are the main reasons for the decrease in the size of κ-carbides in Cr-containing Fe–Mn–Al–C lightweight steels. For 2Ni steels, since Ni has an atomic radius of 1.24◦A, Fe atoms have a radius of 1.27◦A, and Mn atoms have a radius of 1.32◦A. They have equivalent atomic radii, and Ni atoms preferentially displace Fe/Mn atoms to form Ni-rich κ-carbides with AlNi C structure (with a formation energy of − 0.181 eV/atom). The formation energy of Ni-rich κ-carbides with AlNi3C structure is lower than that of ideal κ-carbides. The addition of Ni atoms in κ-carbide reduces the formation energy of κ-carbide, lowers the critical formal energy of κ-carbide, and promotes the formation of κ-carbide. The reduction in the nucleation energy barrier is the main reason for increasing the size of κ-carbide in Ni-containing lightweight steels. The results are consistent with those of the dark field images of Fig. 1i–1l in Section 3.1. Since the nucleation energy of the Fe NiC structure is $> 0 ,$ it is less likely for the $\mathrm { F e _ { 2 } N i C }$ structure of the κ-carbides to form in nature.

# 4.2. The contribution increasement in yield strength after Ni–Cr alloying

We can predict that the yield strength of steels in this study is primarily contributed by LRO nanodomains and κ-carbides. The yield strength can be represented as a linear combination of strengthening mechanisms, as illustrated in Equation (1) [14]. The influence of dislocation strengthening in fully recrystallized Fe–Mn–Al–C steel is minimal and is therefore disregarded.

$$
\sigma_ {\mathrm{ys}} = \sigma_ {0} + \sigma_ {\mathrm{ss}} + \sigma_ {\mathrm{gb}} + \sigma_ {\text { prep }} \tag {1}
$$

here, $\sigma _ { 0 }$ can be readily calculated based on the shear stress of dislocation motion(τ0): $\sigma _ { 0 } = 3 \tau _ { 0 }$ [55], and represents the sum of the frictional stress components. According to Honeycombe [56], $\tau _ { 0 } = 2 \times 1 0 ^ { - 4 } G ,$ where G is the shear modulus. Given that the G value for Fe– $\mathbf { \partial } _ { - } \mathbf { M } \mathbf { n } { - } \mathbf { A l - C }$ steel is $^ { 7 0 }$ $\mathrm { G P a , }$ , σ0 here is calculated to be 42 MPa. The Hall-Petch (HP) relationship $[ 5 7 , 5 8 ] , \sigma _ { \mathrm { g b } } = K _ { \mathrm { H P } } d _ { \mathrm { e f f } } ^ { - 0 . 5 } ;$ , is employed to fit the experimental data and to represent the grain boundary strengthening component $\sigma _ { \mathrm { g b } } .$ Here, KHP represents the Hall-Petch coefficient for steels in this study (KHP = 446.8 MPa μm0.5 [59]). $d _ { \mathrm { e f f } }$ represents the grain sizes containing twins for the ${ } ^ { 3 \mathrm { C r } , }$ , 2Ni, 2Ni3Cr, and 2Ni5Cr steels as described in Section 3.1, and we obtained the $d _ { \mathrm { e f f } }$ values from EBSD analysis as 3.51, 3.73, 3.72, and $2 . 2 \ \mu \mathrm { m } ,$ respectively. As shown in $\mathrm { F i g . ~ 1 } _ { : }$ , the effects of Ni and Cr elements on the grain sizes of Fe–Mn– $_ { - \mathrm { A l - C } }$ austenite steels are minimal. Therefore, the calculated strengthening component results for the ${ } ^ { 3 \mathrm { C r } , }$ 2Ni, 2Ni3Cr, and 2Ni5Cr specimens are quite similar, amounting to 238.5, 231.3, 231.7, and 301.2 MPa, respectively. In Equation $( 1 ) , \sigma _ { \mathrm { s s } }$ represents the solute strengthening component expressed as an empirical formula: $\sigma _ { s s } = 2 7 9 [ \mathrm { C } ] \cdot 1 . 5 [ \mathrm { M n } ] + 2 0 . 5 [ \mathrm { A l } ] + 2 . 9 [ \mathrm { N i } ] + 3 . 9 [ \mathrm { C r } ] [ 6 0 ] ,$ , where [C], [Mn], [Al], [Ni] and [Cr] represent the weight percentages of these elements dissolved in the matrix. The solute strengthening components for the 3Cr, 2Ni, 2Ni3Cr, and 2Ni5Cr samples are 515.4, 509.5, 557.2 and 554.1 MPa, respectively.

In Equation (1), $\sigma _ { \mathrm { p r e p } }$ represents precipitation strengthening components, which can be expressed in various ways depending on the type of dislocation movement, such as shearing and Orowan bypass mechanisms [61]. In this study, as shown in $\mathrm { F i g . } 8 ,$ moving dislocations shear the precipitates, thus, shear strengthening is the predominant mechanism. The shearing mechanism involves three strengthening processes: ordering strengthening $( \sigma _ { \mathrm { o r d e r } } ) _ { : }$ , coherency strengthening $( \sigma _ { \mathrm { c o h } } ) ,$ , and modulus mismatch strengthening $( \sigma _ { \mathrm { m o d } } )$ . These contributions to the yield strength of Fe–Mn–Al–C steel from each mechanism can be predicted using the following equations:

$$
\sigma_ {\text { order }} = \sum_ {r = 0} ^ {n} A _ {S} M 0. 8 1 \frac {\gamma_ {A P B}}{2 b} \left(\frac {3 \pi f _ {i}}{8}\right) ^ {1 / 2} \tag {2}
$$

$$
\sigma_ {c o h} = \sum_ {r = 0} ^ {n} A _ {S} M \alpha_ {\varepsilon} (G \varepsilon_ {c}) ^ {3 / 2} \left(\frac {r _ {i} f _ {i}}{0 . 5 G b}\right) ^ {1 / 2} \tag {3}
$$

$$
\sigma_ {m o d} = \sum_ {r = 0} ^ {n} A _ {S} M 0. 0 0 5 5 (\Delta G) ^ {3 / 2} \left(\frac {2 f _ {i}}{G}\right) ^ {1 / 2} \left(\frac {r _ {i}}{b}\right) ^ {(3 m / 2) - 1} \tag {4}
$$

Here, $A _ { s }$ represents a factor for the strong obstacle precipitation phase, and κ-carbide is considered a strong obstacle since the precipitation fraction of κ-carbides in $\mathrm { F e { \mathrm { - } } M n { \mathrm { - } } A l { \mathrm { - } } C }$ steels is significantly higher than that of traditional steels. In this study, we set $A _ { s }$ to 0.64 [59]. Additionally, $M \approx 3 . 0 6$ represents the Taylor factor for fcc matrix steels [62]. G is the shear modulus of austenite, and $\Delta G \approx 2 3 . 3$ GPa represents the difference in shear modulus between the austenite matrix and κ-carbides [61]. b = 0.264 nm represents the magnitude of the Burgers vector for dislocations in the austenite phase [53]. $\alpha _ { \mathrm { { g } } } \approx 2 . 6$ and $m \approx 0 . 8 5$ are constants for fcc metals [61]. Additionally, $f _ { \mathrm { i } }$ and $r _ { \mathrm { i } }$ respectively represent the radius and fraction of precipitates. $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { c } } = 0$ .9% is the lattice parameter mismatch parameter between the austenite matrix and κ-carbides [61]. Finally, $\gamma _ { \mathrm { A P B } } \approx 0 . 3 5 \mathrm { ~ J } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ represents the antiphase boundary energy per unit area generated when the ordered κ-carbide is sheared [63].

Here, we assume that the larger of the two factors $( \sigma _ { \mathrm { o r d e r } }$ and σcoh+σmod) contributes to the precipitation strengthening [64]. Among these, $\sigma _ { \mathrm { c o h } } + \sigma _ { \mathrm { m o d } }$ peaks when shear dislocations approach the interface between the matrix and the precipitate, while σ peaks when half of the precipitate is sheared by the dislocation [62]. We calculate $\sigma _ { \mathrm { o r d e r } }$ and $\sigma _ { \mathrm { c o h } } \mathrm { + } \sigma _ { \mathrm { m o d } }$ using the size and volume fraction of nanodomains and κ-carbides, taking the maximum value as $\sigma _ { \mathrm { p r e p } } $ . We ultimately predict the yield strength using size distribution $\left( r _ { \mathrm { i } } \right)$ and the volume fraction (f ) of κ-carbides of different sizes, comparing it with the experimental results of tensile tests. The calculated $\sigma _ { \mathrm { p r e p } }$ is shown in Fig. 10, where the red dashed line represents the transition radius, at which the dominant strengthening mechanism shifts from $\sigma _ { \mathrm { o r d e r } }$ to $\sigma _ { \mathrm { c o h } } + \sigma _ { \mathrm { m o d } } .$ . For the ${ } ^ { 3 \mathrm { C r } , }$ , 2Ni, 2Ni3Cr and 2Ni5Cr steels, the strength contribution of $\sigma _ { \mathrm { c o h } } + \sigma _ { \mathrm { m o d } }$ is dominant. However, in the 3Cr and 2Ni5Cr samples with LRO nanodomains, $\sigma _ { \mathrm { o r d e r } }$ also contributes to $\sigma _ { \mathrm { p r e p } }$ because some of the nanodomains are smaller than the critical size.

Based on Equation (1), we summarize the strengthening effects for the 3Cr, 2Ni, 2Ni3Cr, and 2Ni5Cr samples. Fig. 11 compares the results with the measured yield strengths, illustrating the contribution to the yield strength of each strengthening mechanism for each sample. There is a correlation between the calculated and experimental values of yield strength and the predicted and measured yield strengths, which are in very good agreement. Solution strengthening is evidently the primary strengthening mechanism for these samples, with the enhancement in yield strength for traditional Fe–Mn–Al–C steels being closely associated with a carbon content as high as 1.5%. Precipitation strengthening also plays a significant role in these samples, with the contribution from Ni and Cr elements synergistically in Fe–Mn–Al–C steel providing an increase in the size of the precipitate phase, leading to a gradual increase in precipitation strengthening as the precipitate size grows.

# 4.3. Evolution of deformed substructures during the strain process

According to the experimental results, the evolution of the deformation-induced microstructure with increasing strain for 2Ni and 3Cr steels is schematically shown in Fig. 12. Fig. 12a–c show the case of 3Cr steel. At the initial stage of plastic deformation, the steel shows planar dislocation activity. For dislocations that initially appear slide in a planar feature, the activity of subsequent dislocations tends to be constrained within the planar slip bands (Fig. 6a–c and Fig. 12a), and the strain hardening rate decreases rapidly, followed by an increase with increasing strain (Figs. 6b and 12a), which is mainly attributed to the larger mean free path of the dislocations. A limited number of dislocation annihilation events at grain boundaries are also expected to lead to a decrease in the strain hardening rate. When entering strain-hardening stage II (Fig. 6d–f), plastic deformation is characterized by the appearance and proliferation of HDDA (Fig. 6e–f and Fig. 12b). With a further increase in strain, the microstructural evolution is characterized by mutual annihilation and cross-slip through sliding dislocations (Fig. 6e–f and Fig. 12b) and by refinement of the slip bands (Figs. 6d and 12b). This corresponds to strain-hardening stage III (Fig. 4c), where the strainhardening rate decreases with increasing strain and is dominated by a high density of dislocations (Figs. 6g and 12c) and very fine slip bands (Figs. 6h and 12c). It should also be noted that stacking fault and deformation twins also occur in 3Cr steels (Fig. 7i–l and Fig. 12c), but only at very high strain levels near ultimate failure.

As shown in Fig. 12d–f, the deformation mechanisms of 2Ni steel are mainly mediated by plane slip. In this steel, the slip bands are relatively uniformly distributed (Fig. 7a–b and Fig. 12d), such slip bands multiply with increasing strain, and the space of the slip band decreases (Fig. 7d–f and Fig. 12f). It results that strain hardening rate increase with the strain increasing, and the strain hardening stage is shortened than 3Cr steel. At strain-hardening Stage II, the strain-hardening rate increases with increasing strain. When entering strain-hardening Stage III, it is expected to be due to accelerated dynamic recovery and hard-to-dynamic slip band refinement (Fig. 7i). The dynamic recovery corresponds to dislocations on planar slip bands that are close enough or sufficiently high stresses for dislocation annihilation to occur during cross-slip. A small number of stack faults were found at very high strain levels close to final failure (Fig. 7j–l and Fig. 12e). Overall, Cr alloying of Fe–Mn–Al–C steels promotes the DSBR and HDDA effects of plastic deformation, which can increase elongation.

# 5. Conclusion

Focusing on austenitic low-density steels with compositions of Fe–20Mn–9Al-1.5C-xNi-yCr (where x = 0, 2 wt% and y = 0, 3, 5 wt%), this study leverages first-principle computational analysis, precipitation kinetics, deformation, and fracture mechanism investigations to successfully design a material that harmonizes exceptional strength and ductility with superior overall performance. Furthermore, it elucidates the intrinsic connection between microstructural features and macroscopic deformation behavior, with the following specific conclusions:

(1) The Fe–20Mn–9Al-1.5C-xNi-yCr steels exhibit superior YS, UTS, and TEL compared to other steels in the Fe–Mn–Al–C series that benefit from effective Ni and Cr alloying. This alloying strategy mitigates the traditional compromise between YS, UTS, and TEL, as demonstrated by 2Ni3Cr steels with YS exceeding 1100 MPa, UTS surpassing 1200 MPa, and TEL greater than 45%.
(2) The strategic addition of Ni and Cr plays a pivotal role in controlling the precipitation dynamics of nano-sized κ-carbides and LRO domains, optimizing their dimensions for enhanced precipitate-induced strengthening. Ni element adding reduces nucleation energy barriers and facilitates the growth and stabilization of κ-carbides, enhancing their efficacy in strengthening. On the contrary, Cr elevates the nucleation energy barrier.
(3) These improvements are attributed to a synergy of grain boundary strengthening, solid solution strengthening, and precipitation strengthening. Additionally, the progressive evolution of the microstructure under strain leads to the repeated formation and multiplication of HDDA and DSBR duel mechanisms.
(4) The enhancement of both strength and strain hardening properties through Ni and Cr alloying is a key contribution of this study. This approach not only elucidates the mechanisms behind the improved properties but also sets a foundational strategy for designing next-generation austenitic lightweight steels with superior mechanical performance.
