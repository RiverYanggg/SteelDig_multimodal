# Material System

The study investigates Fe-Mn-Al austenitic alloys, designated as Fermanal type, designed for lightweight, high-strength applications. The nominal composition window spans Mn 5–45 wt%, Al 2–14 wt%, and C 0.05–1.5 wt%. Specific alloys characterized include G30Yu9 (nominally 30% Mn, 9% Al) and several alloys with documented compositions: an alloy containing 26.1% Mn, 10.2% Al, and 1.02% C; an alloy with 27.5% Mn, 9.1% Al, and 0.92% C; an alloy with 30% Mn and 8% Al; and an alloy with 10% Al (Mn and C content unspecified). The material system at 1150 °C with approximately 1% C comprises γ-solid solution (austenite), α-solid solution (ferrite), carbides, and a Mn-type brittle phase. Reference materials include steel 1Kh17N2 and boiler steel 20KhM for oxidation resistance comparison.

# Processing Route and Variables

All alloys were melted in a laboratory induction furnace under a helium atmosphere and processed via investment casting. The primary solution treatment consisted of quenching from 1150 °C after a 1.5-hour hold, followed by water quenching. Aging treatments were conducted at 400, 500, 550, 600, and 700 °C for durations ranging from 30 minutes to 100 hours. Oxidation tests were performed at 600, 700, and 800 °C in air for 500 hours. Tensile specimens were machined with a 5 mm diameter and 25 mm gauge length. Density was determined by hydrostatic suspension.

# Microstructure and Phase Evolution

The ternary Fe-Mn-Al phase diagram at 1150 °C with approximately 1% C establishes a phase boundary separating single-phase austenite from two-phase ferrite-austenite regions. In the quenched condition from 1150 °C, an alloy containing 25% Mn, 10% Al, and 1% C exhibits single-phase austenite, whereas an alloy with 20% Mn, 10% Al, and 1% C displays a duplex structure of austenite and ferrite. Aging the 25% Mn alloy at 600 °C for 50 hours induces dispersed phase precipitation at grain boundaries with enhanced etchability. The 20% Mn alloy after the same aging treatment shows retained austenite with acicular morphology surrounding ferrite particles, accompanied by carbide precipitation at austenite grain boundaries. Above an aging temperature of 550 °C, coalescence of the dispersed precipitate phase is observed, consistent with overaging behavior.

# Processing-Structure-Property Chain

The quench from 1150 °C establishes the baseline phase constitution: alloys with sufficient Mn content (near 25%) and Al near 10% with 1% C solidify into a single-phase austenitic structure, whereas lower Mn content (near 20%) at the same Al and C levels introduces ferrite, yielding a duplex structure. Subsequent aging at 600 °C for 50 hours transforms the microstructure through γ-phase decomposition and carbide precipitation at grain boundaries, irrespective of the initial phase constitution. This microstructural evolution produces a sharp hardness increase from HRB 86–88 in the quenched state to HRC 35–37 after aging, confirming precipitation hardening. For the Fe-27.5Mn-9.1Al-0.92C alloy aged for 16 hours, ultimate tensile strength and yield strength increase with aging temperature, both peaking at 550 °C (σb ≈ 95 kg/mm², σ0.2 ≈ 85 kg/mm²), while elongation and reduction of area reach minimum values (δ ≈ 7–8%, ψ ≈ 10%) at the same temperature. Above 550 °C, strength decreases and ductility partially recovers, linked to coalescence of the precipitate dispersion.

# Mechanistic Interpretation

The microstructural observations support a precipitation hardening mechanism driven by decomposition of the supersaturated γ-solid solution during aging. In the 25% Mn alloy, the single-phase austenite matrix, which is supersaturated after quenching, undergoes precipitation at grain boundaries during aging at 600 °C, causing the observed hardness increase. The 20% Mn alloy, initially duplex, experiences additional carbide precipitation at austenite grain boundaries while retaining acicular austenite around ferrite. The property trends in the Fe-27.5Mn-9.1Al-0.92C alloy aged for 16 hours indicate a classical age-hardening response: increasing aging temperature accelerates precipitation kinetics, raising strength at the expense of ductility up to the peak-aging temperature of 550 °C. Beyond this temperature, precipitate coalescence (overaging) reduces the barrier to dislocation motion, leading to strength decrement and ductility restoration. The identification of a Mn-type brittle phase in the system suggests a compositional constraint to avoid embrittlement in service.

# Key Quantitative Findings

- An alloy with 25% Mn, 10% Al, 1% C, quenched from 1150 °C, possesses a single-phase austenitic structure; reducing Mn to 20% at otherwise identical composition introduces ferrite.
- Aging at 600 °C for 50 hours raises hardness from HRB 86–88 (as-quenched) to HRC 35–37.
- For Fe-27.5Mn-9.1Al-0.92C aged 16 hours, peak strength occurs at 550 °C with σb ≈ 95 kg/mm² and σ0.2 ≈ 85 kg/mm², accompanied by a ductility minimum (δ ≈ 7–8%, ψ ≈ 10%).
- Overaging above 550 °C reduces strength and partially restores ductility due to precipitate coalescence.
- Oxidation tests were conducted at 600–800 °C for 500 hours; density was measured via hydrostatic suspension, though quantitative oxidation and density values are not provided in the available data.
