Austenitic alloys of the "Fermanal" type (Fe—Mn—Al) have recently come into use abroad in the aviation and automotive industries for critical and heavily loaded parts (springs, gears, landing gears). These alloys have a relatively low density (12-15% below that of iron), are capable of precipitation hardening, and have high mechanical properties and good oxidation resistance.

It was shown in [1-3] that these alloys can be processed by hot and cold working and their machinability is similar to that of 18/8 steels. The mechanical properties of the forged and heat treated alloy G30Yu9 [2] and the alloy with $26.1\%$ Mn, $10.2\%$ Al, and $1.02\%$ C are given in Table 1.

In the alloys with about 10% Al the optimal mechanical properties are obtained by aging at lower temperatures (475-500°C). Aging temperatures of 550-575°C substantially reduce the ductility and strength. There are no data on alloys of the "Fermanal" type in Soviet literature.

The purpose of this work was to determine the optimal compositions of Fe-Mn-Al alloys and the effect of heat treatment on the structure and mechanical and physicochemical properties.

Over 100 heats were prepared with 5-45% Mn, 2-14% Al, and 0.05-1.5% C. The alloys were melted in a laboratory induction furnace in a helium atmosphere (previously evacuated to $10^{-3}$ mm Hg) and in a 30-kg commercial furnace. Samples 65 mm long and 11 mm in diam. were produced by investment casting. The alloys were investigated as cast, after quenching in water from 1150°C (held 1.5 h), and after quenching + aging at 400, 500, 550, 600, and 700°C for 30 min to 100 h.

Dispersed phases are precipitated intensely in aging at 550-600°C, while at 400-500°C the amount of phase precipitated is negligible.

In alloys with 1% C, 10% Al, and about 10% Mn the γ-phase decomposes into α-phase and carbides after quenching + aging at 600°C. In the alloy with 15% Mn one observes separate sections of austenite in the microstructure that are retained after aging. With 20% Mn the amount of retained austenite increases and the structure consists of austenite and ferrite with carbides in the austenite grain boundaries. Austenite with an acicular structure is sometimes observed around the ferrite (Fig.2,b, II). With 25% Mn the structure after aging consists of austenite and dispersed phase precipitated in the grain boundaries, which is manifest in the higher etchability of the grain boundaries (Fig.2b). The hardness of the alloy increases sharply after aging — from HRB 86-88 to HRC 35-37.

A new brittle phase appears in the alloy with 30% Mn, mainly in the austenite grain boundaries, the amount increasing with the manganese concentration. X-ray structural analysis showed that this phase has a crystal lattice similar to manganese, which agrees with the data in [3].

The mechanical properties were determined on samples 5 mm in diam. and 25 mm long after quenching and aging 16 h at different temperatures, depending mainly on the aluminum concentration and less on the manganese concentration.

The strength characteristics of the alloys with 30% Mn and 8% Al change little after aging, the plasticity and ductility decreasing somewhat. In alloys with \~9% Al the ultimate strength and yield strength increase with the aging temperature up to 550°C, but the plasticity and ductility decline sharply (Fig.3). Above 550°C the strength begins to decrease and the plasticity to increase due to coalescence of the dispersed phase.

Alloys with 12-14% Al have low plasticity and ductility both after quenching and after aging.

From the values of permeability it was established that the alloys with an austenitic structure after quenching are completely nonmagnetic. After aging, the permeability increases, particularly in the alloys with over 10% Al (Table 2). The increase of permeability after aging may be due to the precipitation of iron—aluminum carbides, the amount of which increases with the aluminum concentration of the alloy.

The oxidation resistance of the alloys was tested in air at 600, 700, and 800°C and determined from the weight increase after 500 h. The oxidation resistance depends mainly on the aluminum concentration and very little on the concentration of manganese, which impairs it slightly. After 500 h at 700°C the weight increase was 6-12.5 g/m² for the alloys with over 8% Al, 6.2 g/m² for control samples of steel 1Kh17N2, and 118 g/m² for boiler steel 20KhM.

The density of the alloys was determined by hydrostatic suspension. With increasing aluminum concentrations from 6 to 10% the density decreased from 7.24 to 6.68 g/cm $^{3}$ . Alloys with 9% Al and 25-30% Mn had a density of 6.75-6.85 g/cm $^{3}$ .
