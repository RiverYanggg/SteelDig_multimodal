# Material System
Fe–28Mn–10Al–0.8C (wt%) low-density steel system with controlled nitrogen additions, producing two compositional variants: a low-nitrogen steel (LN, 0.0025 wt%N) and a high-nitrogen steel (HN, 0.1326 wt%N). The alloy is designed as a duplex austenite–ferrite low-density steel, with nitrogen acting as a potent austenite stabilizer and AlN inclusion former.

# Processing Route and Variables
Both LN and HN ingots were subjected to identical thermo-mechanical processing: homogenization at 1423 K for 2 h, followed by multi-directional hot forging in the temperature range 1123–1323 K, and air cooling. The sole deliberate processing variable is the bulk nitrogen content—0.0025 wt%N in LN versus 0.1326 wt%N in HN—introduced during smelting. Tensile testing was performed at a strain rate of 1×10⁻³ s⁻¹ (crosshead speed 3 mm/min) using cylindrical specimens with 10 mm gauge diameter and 50 mm gauge length; Charpy V-notch impact testing used standard 10×10×50 mm³ specimens with a 45° notch and 8 mm remaining ligament.

# Microstructure and Phase Evolution
**Grain structure and phase constitution (Figure 3):** After homogenization and hot forging, both steels exhibit a duplex microstructure consisting of an austenite matrix with parallel ferrite strips aligned along prior austenite grain boundaries. LN steel (0.0025 wt%N) shows an average austenite grain size of 20.5 μm and a ferrite area fraction of 5.08%, appearing as coarse, elongated δ-ferrite bands. HN steel (0.1326 wt%N) shows a refined austenite grain size of 12.1 μm and a reduced ferrite area fraction of 1.84%, with more abundant deformation twins visible in the austenite grains.

**Thermodynamic prediction (Figures 8, 9):** An AlN stability diagram (Figure 8) with equilibrium curves for smelting temperature (1873 K), liquidus (1587 K), and solidus (1493 K) demonstrates that LN steel lies in the “No AlN” dissolution regime at all three temperatures, while HN steel is deep within the “AlN saturation” regime. A Thermo-Calc pseudobinary Fe–28Mn–10Al–0.8C–N phase diagram (Figure 9) confirms that increasing nitrogen mass fraction progressively expands the austenite (γ) phase field, suppresses δ-ferrite and α-ferrite stability, and stabilizes AlN across a wide temperature range from the liquid state down to approximately 1000 K.

**Inclusion population (Figures 4–7):** In LN steel (Figure 4), inclusions are primarily elliptical Mn(Mg)S sulfides and isolated polyhedral AlN particles, with occasional composite AlN–Mn(Mg)S inclusions in which sulfide precipitates epitaxially on pre-existing AlN. In HN steel (Figure 5), AlN dominates the inclusion population, appearing as polyhedral particles (cubic/angular habit) with straight edges and sharp facets; some are intact (single crystals ~2–3 μm, Figure 5a), while others are visibly fragmented because of brittle fracture during hot forging (Figure 5b). The inclusion size distribution shifts dramatically with nitrogen addition (Figures 6, 7): LN steel shows a broad distribution—39% <2 μm, 39% 2–4 μm, 12% 4–6 μm, 10% >6 μm; HN steel shows 84% of inclusions are <2 μm, 11% are 2–4 μm, and only 4% are 4–6 μm. Total inclusion number density increases from 73 mm⁻² (LN) to 133 mm⁻² (HN), while the inclusion area fraction rises from 0.212% to 1.005%.

# Processing-Structure-Property Chain
**Nitrogen addition → Inclusion evolution → Grain refinement cascade:** High nitrogen content in HN steel (0.1326 wt%N) places the composition into the AlN saturation region at smelting temperature (Figure 8), driving copious precipitation of fine AlN during solidification and subsequent cooling. These AlN particles—84% smaller than 2 μm—act as heterogeneous nucleation sites for austenite during thermo-mechanical processing, reducing the average austenite grain size from 20.5 μm (LN) to 12.1 μm (HN) (Figure 3).

**Nitrogen addition → Austenite stabilization → Ferrite suppression:** Thermodynamic calculations (Figure 9) show that increasing nitrogen expands the γ-phase field; experimentally this manifests as a reduction in ferrite area fraction from 5.08% (LN) to 1.84% (HN) (Figure 3). Because ferrite is intrinsically softer, its suppression contributes to the net mechanical response.

**Microstructure → Tensile properties (Figure 2):** LN steel yields at ~685 MPa with ultimate tensile strength ~832.5 MPa and fracture strain ~60–65%. HN steel yields at a lower ~647 MPa but reaches a comparable/higher UTS of ~841.5 MPa and significantly higher fracture strain of ~75–80%. The HN engineering stress–strain curves display larger necking regions and maintain higher flow stress at large strains, indicating superior uniform elongation and total elongation.

**Microstructure → Impact toughness (Figures 10, 11):** Charpy V-notch impact energy increases from 81 J (LN) to 130.5 J (HN). LN fracture surfaces exhibit a 45° shear fracture with smaller, more numerous dimples indicating localized stress concentration; HN fracture surfaces show a pronounced crater-shaped fracture with larger, deeper, uniformly distributed equiaxed dimples, a higher shear-lip area fraction, and maximum profile height of 9.95 mm vs. 7.53 mm for LN.

**Inclusion role duality:** Fine, dispersed AlN (84% <2 μm) promotes grain refinement and property enhancement; however, fragmented or aggregated AlN inclusions (Figures 5b, 12b) found within dimples on HN fracture surfaces serve as crack initiation sites, introducing a competing degradation mechanism.

# Mechanistic Interpretation
**Grain refinement by particle-stimulated nucleation:** The high number density of sub-2 μm AlN particles (133 mm⁻² in HN vs. 73 mm⁻² in LN) provides abundant heterogeneous nucleation sites, pinning austenite grain boundaries during hot forging and suppressing grain growth. This reduces the average austenite grain size from 20.5 μm to 12.1 μm.

**Yield strength decrease mechanism:** Despite grain refinement (which would normally increase yield strength via Hall–Petch strengthening), HN steel exhibits lower yield strength (~647 MPa vs. ~685 MPa). This is attributed to the reduced ferrite content (1.84% vs. 5.08%), as the softer ferrite phase contributes to composite strengthening at yield in the duplex structure. The loss of ferrite reinforcement outweighs the grain-boundary strengthening increment.

**Ductility and toughness enhancement:** Refined austenite grains and increased twin density in HN steel promote more homogeneous plastic deformation by distributing strain more uniformly, delaying microvoid coalescence. The larger, deeper, equiaxed dimples on HN fracture surfaces (Figures 10, 11) confirm greater plastic energy absorption before fracture, consistent with the 60% increase in Charpy impact energy (from 81 J to 130.5 J).

**Crack initiation by aggregated AlN:** Brittle, faceted AlN particles—especially those fragmented during forging (Figure 5b) or present as aggregates—debond from the austenite matrix under tensile stress, forming microvoids that coalesce into dimples (Figure 12b). These aggregated AlN paradoxically lower the energy barrier for crack initiation, partially offsetting the toughening benefits of grain refinement.

# Visual Evidence
**Figure 1:** Documents specimen geometries for tensile (ϕ10×50 mm gauge) and Charpy V-notch (10×10×50 mm, 45° notch) tests, confirming standardized property measurement protocols.

**Figure 2:** Engineering stress–strain curves for three LN and three HN replicates directly demonstrate the yield strength depression in HN (~647 vs. ~685 MPa), comparable UTS, and substantially enhanced ductility with fracture strain shift from ~60–65% to ~75–80%.

**Figure 3:** Optical micrographs provide direct microstructural evidence correlating nitrogen addition with austenite grain refinement (20.5→12.1 μm) and ferrite suppression (5.08→1.84 area%).

**Figure 4:** SEM micrographs establish baseline inclusion characteristics in LN steel: elliptical Mn(Mg)S, polyhedral AlN, and composite AlN–sulfide particles, supporting the identification of multiple inclusion types at low nitrogen.

**Figure 5:** SEM images reveal AlN morphology in HN steel—intact polyhedral particles (2–3 μm) and fragmented particles—directly linking nitrogen-driven AlN precipitation with brittle inclusion behavior during forging.

**Figure 6:** Stacked bar chart quantifying the pronounced shift in inclusion size distribution toward sub-2 μm population with nitrogen addition (39%→84% <2 μm), supporting the grain-refinement mechanism.

**Figure 7:** Bar chart of inclusion number density vs. size range confirms HN steel’s higher density across all size bins, with peak density of ~72 mm⁻² in the 1–2 μm range, underpinning the particle-stimulated nucleation mechanism.

**Figure 8:** AlN stability diagram (N content vs. Al content) explains why LN steel falls in the “No AlN” regime while HN steel is in “AlN saturation” at smelting temperature, providing the thermodynamic rationale for the observed inclusion populations.

**Figure 9:** Pseudobinary Fe–28Mn–10Al–0.8C–N phase diagram confirms nitrogen’s role as austenite stabilizer and AlN former, supporting the experimentally observed ferrite reduction and inclusion evolution.

**Figure 10:** Tensile fracture surface comparison—3D profilometry, low-mag SEM, and high-mag dimple imaging—shows LN steel’s 45° shear fracture with smaller dimples vs. HN steel’s crater-shaped fracture with larger, deeper, uniformly distributed dimples, linking microstructure to enhanced plasticity.

**Figure 11:** Impact fracture surfaces with 3D topography (max height 7.53 mm LN vs. 9.95 mm HN), radiation zone morphology, and dimple characteristics correlate grain refinement and reduced ferrite with higher impact toughness (81→130.5 J) and slower crack propagation.

**Figure 12:** High-magnification SEM images of inclusions within fracture dimples identify Mn(Mg)S (deformable) as the primary crack initiator in LN steel and aggregated AlN (brittle) as crack initiation sites in HN steel, demonstrating the dual role of AlN in property enhancement and degradation.
