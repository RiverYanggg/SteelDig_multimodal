# Material System
The study investigates two duplex low-density Fe-Mn-Al-C steels based on a nominal Fe-15Mn-10Al-0.8C (wt.%) composition, one without Ni and one with 5 wt.% Ni addition. The matrix consists of austenite (γ, face-centered cubic) and ferrite (α, body-centered cubic). Key ordered precipitate phases include κ-carbide (L'1₂ perovskite-type, ordered fcc with Fe, Mn, Al, C) and B2 intermetallic. In the Ni-free variant, the B2 is FeAl-type (with mention of an Fe₃Al-L1₂ phase); in the Ni-containing variant, the B2 is NiAl-type. Morphological forms observed: cuboid κ in γ, needle-like κ in α, B2 stringer bands (continuous bands replacing α-ferrite), nano-sized B2 discs in α, and micron-sized B2 particles at grain boundaries and within γ.

# Processing Route and Variables
Alloys were subjected to annealing heat treatments at four temperatures: 500 °C, 700 °C, 900 °C, and 1050 °C, establishing a processing window to modulate precipitation. The primary composition variable is the presence (5 wt.%) or absence of Ni in the base Fe-15Mn-10Al-0.8C alloy. Nanoindentation experiments were performed using a Berkovich tip with a constant loading/unloading rate of 500 µN/s, a maximum load of 10 000 µN, and a hold time of 20 s to extract nanohardness.

# Microstructure and Phase Evolution
Temperature- and composition-dependent microstructural evolution is synthesized from SEM and TEM observations (Figures 1, 2).

- **500–700 °C (Ni-containing steel):** Original α-ferrite transforms into B2 stringer bands, embedded in a γ matrix that contains nano-sized cuboid κ-carbides. Coarse grain-boundary B2 particles also appear at 500 °C.
- **500–700 °C (Ni-free steel):** Instead of B2 stringer bands, needle-like κ-carbides precipitate within α-ferrite, while γ carries cuboid κ.
- **900–1050 °C (Ni-containing steel):** B2 adopts two morphologies: micron-sized particles at grain boundaries and within γ, and nano-sized disc-shaped precipitates dispersed inside α-ferrite. The γ matrix continues to host cuboid κ. Here B2 is NiAl-type.
- **900–1050 °C (Ni-free steel):** B2 appears as FeAl-type precipitates (no NiAl-type); microstructural descriptions are analogous but the intermetallic chemistry differs.

Thus, the processing–structure link is: low annealing temperatures (500–700 °C) yield B2 stringer bands in Ni-containing grades and needle-like κ in Ni-free grades; high temperatures (≥900 °C) produce nano-disc B2 in ferrite and coarsened grain-boundary B2, while cuboid κ in austenite persists across the whole temperature range.

# Processing-Structure-Property Chain
Annealing temperature and Ni addition jointly determine the type, chemistry, and morphology of ordered precipitates, which in turn govern the nanomechanical response measured by nanoindentation.

- **Ni addition effect:** In the Ni-containing alloy Fe-15Mn-10Al-0.8C-5Ni, the B2 is NiAl-type, which intrinsically possesses stronger Ni–Al bonds (compared to Fe–Al bonds in the Ni-free FeAl-type B2). This bond-strength difference raises the hardness of Ni-bearing samples relative to Ni-free samples for comparable microstructural configurations.
- **High annealing temperature (≥900 °C) effect:** The nano-sized disc-like B2 precipitates finely dispersed in the α-ferrite matrix act as effective hardening agents, leading to higher ferrite hardness. This is attributed to the combination of strong bonding in NiAl-type B2 and a morphology (nano-discs) conducive to dislocation–precipitate interaction.
- **Low annealing temperature (500–700 °C) effect:** The formation of B2 stringer bands (in Ni-containing alloy) or needle-like κ (in Ni-free alloy) results in a lower hardness compared to the high-temperature condition. The stringer-band morphology does not provide the same degree of strengthening as finely dispersed discs, and the needle‑like κ in ferrite of the Ni-free alloy is associated with relatively modest hardening.
- **Composite phase hardness trends:** Hardness varies spatially depending on the phase constitution (γ, α, γ + cuboid κ, α + needle κ, B2-rich regions). The nanoindentation grid mapping (50–70 indents per region) captures this heterogeneity, confirming that B2-rich domains (especially nano‑disc B2) show higher hardness, whereas regions dominated by coarse stringer bands or needle κ exhibit lower hardness.

# Mechanistic Interpretation
Density functional theory (DFT) calculations using PBE-GGA in Quantum ESPRESSO provide the underlying atomic-scale rationale. The hardness trends are correlated with interatomic bonding energies:
- The Ni–Al bond in NiAl-type B2 is significantly stronger than the Fe–Al bond in FeAl-type B2, directly explaining why the Ni-containing alloy exhibits higher measured nanohardness.
- Mn substitution in κ-carbide (replacing Fe or Al sites) modifies the elastic properties and bonding, but the primary hardness contrast between the two alloys originates from the B2 phase chemistry, not from κ.
- At high annealing temperatures, the fine dispersion of strong-bonded nano-disc B2 provides maximum resistance to plastic flow, whereas at low temperatures the coarser stringer-band morphology and the presence of softer needle-like κ phases reduce the overall strengthening efficiency. The nanoindentation results are thus a direct consequence of the interplay between process‑dictated precipitate morphologies and the inherent bond strength of the ordered phases.

# Key Quantitative Findings
No absolute nanohardness values are reported in the available core facts; however, the following qualitative and semi‑quantitative trends emerge from the study:
- The Ni-containing alloy (Fe‑15Mn‑10Al‑0.8C‑5Ni) shows higher nanoindentation hardness than the Ni‑free alloy (Fe‑15Mn‑10Al‑0.8C) for the same annealing condition, due to stronger Ni–Al bonds in NiAl‑type B2.
- In the Ni‑containing alloy, annealing at ≥900 °C (nano‑disc B2) yields higher hardness than annealing at ≤700 °C (B2 stringer bands).
- In the Ni‑free alloy, the absence of NiAl‑type B2 and the presence of needle‑like κ in ferrite at low temperatures lead to the lowest hardness among the investigated conditions.
- Hardness contributions are phase‑specific: B2‑rich nano‑disc domains > coarse B2 particles > γ + cuboid κ composite > α + needle κ composite.
