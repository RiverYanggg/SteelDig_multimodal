# Material System
- Casting alloys (unspecified), during dendritic solidification.
- Focus on the presence of small, pre‑existing defects (voids, cracks, discontinuities) within the dendrite network.

# Processing Route and Variables
- **Solidification conditions** defined by the local liquid fraction and the direction of interdendritic flow relative to the thermal gradient (isotherms).
- **Defect characteristics**:
  - Orientation of the defect with respect to the isotherms.
  - Defect permeability, which locally enhances flow conductance.
- **Criterion parameter**: constant \( n \) introduced in criterion [10], which encapsulates the effect of defect geometry and permeability on segregation susceptibility.

# Microstructure and Phase Evolution
- During solidification, a permeable dendrite network forms; the interdendritic liquid flows under the action of solidification shrinkage and thermosolutal convection.
- Small defects (e.g., micro‑voids, hot tears, or other network irregularities) act as high‑permeability pathways that deviate the flow from the average direction dictated by the thermal field.
- Channel segregates, i.e., macrosegregation channels with solute enrichment/depletion, can nucleate and grow along such preferential flow routes.

# Processing‑Structure‑Property Chain
- **Process‑structure link**: The local liquid fraction, flow direction, and defect permeability determine whether the interdendritic flow is diverted through a defect. If the defect creates a pathway that satisfies the modified criterion [10], channel segregation initiates.
- **Structure‑property link**: Although not quantified in this work, the formation of channel segregates leads to compositional heterogeneity, which is generally detrimental to the mechanical properties and service performance of castings (weak support, qualitative).

# Mechanistic Interpretation
- Existing segregation criteria (e.g., criterion [1]) fail when a small defect is present because they consider only the bulk permeability of the dendrite network and the macroscopic flow direction. They cannot predict segregation when the flow is nearly parallel to the isotherms.
- The improved criterion [10] accounts explicitly for the defect permeability. It shows that a defect can capture and redirect the flow even if the background flow direction is nearly tangent to the isotherms. The redirected flow carries solute‑rich liquid into a local region, creating a compositional perturbation that amplifies into a channel segregate.
- The defect‑assisted mechanism works as follows: a high‑permeability defect acts as a “short circuit” for the interdendritic liquid, concentrating flow and solute flux. This localised enhanced transport triggers a positive feedback between flow focusing and solute enrichment, ultimately leading to a stable segregation channel.
- The analysis points out that the model assumes an idealised ellipsoidal defect shape and a constant \( n \); the accuracy of the criterion decreases at high liquid fractions where the dendritic network loses coherency. These limitations are discussed in the paper but do not alter the central conclusion that even a small defect can have a decisive influence on channel segregation.

# Key Quantitative Findings
- The paper does not report specific numerical values for alloy composition, flow velocity, or segregation intensity. The key advance is the formulation of criterion [10], which introduces a defect‑permeability‑dependent condition for channel segregation. The criterion involves a constant \( n \) that depends on the defect morphology and the orientation of the defect relative to the flow and isotherms, but no numerical values for \( n \) are provided in the abstract or core facts.
