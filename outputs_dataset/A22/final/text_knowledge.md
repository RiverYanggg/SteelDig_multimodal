# Material System

- **Fe–15Al binary alloy** (A2 disordered bcc matrix)
- **Fe–26Al binary alloy** (D0₃/B2 order–disorder transitions with temperature)
- **Fe–15Al–2M–1C alloys** (M = Ti, V, Nb, Ta; MC carbides: TiC, VC, NbC, TaC)
- **Fe–26Al–2Ti–1C** and **Fe–26Al–4Ti–1C** (TiC precipitates)
- **Fe–26Al–2V–1C** (VC precipitates)
- **Fe–26Al–2Nb–1C** (NbC precipitates)
- **Fe–26Al–4Nb–1C** (NbC + (Fe,Al)₂Nb Laves phase)
- **Fe–26Al–2Ta–1C** (TaC precipitates)

All carbide‑containing materials were produced with nominal 1 at.% C. The Fe‑15Al base provides an A2 disordered bcc matrix; the Fe‑26Al base undergoes D0₃↔B2 order–disorder transitions in the elevated‑temperature range.

# Processing Route and Variables

- **Casting**: Vacuum induction melting and copper mould casting; no subsequent hot working or heat treatment reported for the carbide‑containing variants in this work.
- **Test geometry**: Mechanical testing was performed in both uniaxial compression and uniaxial tension.
- **Deformation rate**: Constant strain rate of 10⁻⁴ s⁻¹ for all tests.
- **Temperature range**: Room temperature (RT) up to 800 °C; specimens were held for 30 min at the testing temperature before loading.
- **Hardness measurements**: Brinell hardness of the matrix was recorded at RT (comparison with yield stress shown in Fig. 1).

The critical processing variables are the identity of the carbide‑forming element (Ti, V, Nb, Ta), its concentration (2 or 4 at.%), and the Al level (15 at.% vs. 26 at.%), while the carbon content is fixed at 1 at.%.

# Microstructure and Phase Evolution

- **Binary Fe–15Al**: A2 disordered bcc single phase; no carbide or intermetallic precipitates.
- **Binary Fe–26Al**: A2 (high‑T) → B2 → D0₃ (low‑T) ordered structures; no second‑phase precipitates.
- **Fe–15Al–2M–1C series**: MC‑type carbides (TiC, VC, NbC, TaC) precipitate during solidification. **NbC and TaC** provide the largest volume fraction of hard, stable particles.
- **Fe–26Al–2Ti/4Ti/2V/2Nb/2Ta–1C**: In all these alloys MC carbides form (TiC, VC, NbC, TaC). **Fe–26Al–4Nb–1C** additionally contains the hexagonal Laves phase (Fe,Al)₂Nb, which exhibits higher thermal stability than the MC carbides.
- Fractography indicates that **TiC** often appears as cube‑like particles; **VC** can form plate‑like precipitates that influence the fracture path.

The microstructure of all carbide‑containing alloys consists of the Fe‑Al matrix (A2 or D0₃‑ordered) with hard, micron‑ to submicron‑scale MC carbides (and, in the case of 4Nb, Laves phase) dispersed throughout.

# Processing–Structure–Property Chain

1. **Alloy design → MC carbide formation**:  
   Addition of 2 at.% Nb or Ta with 1 at.% C to Fe–15Al generates a high number density of NbC/TaC precipitates during casting. These carbides are thermodynamically stable and resist coarsening.  
   → *Room‑temperature compressive yield stress*:  
     - Fe–15Al binary: ~356 MPa  
     - Fe–15Al–2Nb–1C: ~827 MPa  
     - Fe–15Al–2Ta–1C: ~868 MPa  
   (Fig. 1a, Fig. 2a)

2. **Al content and matrix order → yield anomaly and hardening**:  
   Fe‑26Al‑based alloys show a **yield stress anomaly** (peak near 550 °C for Ti‑ and V‑containing compositions, ~400 MPa; peak shifted to 474 °C at 619 MPa for Fe‑26Al–4Nb–1C). This anomaly is linked to the D0₃/B2 order‑disorder transition and cross‑slip locking of superdislocations.  
   Despite **lower initial yield stress** compared to the binary in some cases, the carbide‑containing Fe‑26Al alloys exhibit **strong work hardening** with nearly constant hardening rate before a parabolic stage (Fig. 2b).

3. **Laves phase formation → superior high‑temperature strength**:  
   Only Fe‑26Al–4Nb–1C contains (Fe,Al)₂Nb Laves phase, which is more resistant to coarsening than MC carbides at high temperature.  
   → At 800 °C, this composition achieves the highest compression yield stress among all tested alloys (~136 MPa in compression, ~82 MPa in tension; Fig. 8). The yield stress anomaly peak is also the highest (619 MPa at 474 °C), demonstrating the advantage of Laves phase + NbC over only MC strengthening.

4. **Carbide‑type dependent room‑temperature ductility and fracture mode**:  
   - Binary Fe–15Al: ~16% ductility, fine transgranular cleavage (Fig. 4a).  
   - Fe–15Al–2Ti–1C: ~3.3% ductility, coarse transgranular cleavage (Fig. 4b).  
   - Fe–15Al–2Nb/2Ta–1C: sharply yielding but low ductility; coarse transgranular cleavage.  
   - Fe‑26Al‑based alloys: Most show limited ductility. Fe–26Al–2Nb–1C stands out with ~1.4% elongation and fine facetted transgranular cleavage (Fig. 4j). Ti‑ and Ta‑containing Fe‑26Al alloys show mixed intergranular + transgranular fracture (Fig. 4g, h, l), indicating grain boundary weakening by precipitates.  
   Thus, **precipitation strengthening by NbC/TaC increases yield stress but sacrifices ductility, shifting fracture from fine cleavage to coarse cleavage or intergranular decohesion**.

5. **Tension–compression asymmetry (all alloys, all temperatures)**:  
   In both Fe‑15Al and Fe‑26Al systems, compression yield stress consistently exceeds tensile yield stress by 30–100 MPa at 650 °C and 800 °C (Fig. 7, Fig. 8). This asymmetry is intrinsic to the ordered/disordered matrix and persists even after carbide‑strengthening. The magnitude depends on the carbide former: Nb‑ and Ta‑containing alloys not only show the highest strengths but also the largest absolute differences.

6. **Hardness vs. yield stress correlation**:  
   - For Fe‑15Al‑based alloys, Brinell hardness and compressive yield stress correlate well (Fig. 1a).  
   - For Fe‑26Al‑based alloys, **no correlation exists**; matrix hardness fails to predict the compressive yield stress (Fig. 1b), likely because the yield anomaly and work‑hardening behaviour are controlled by order‑related dislocation mechanisms rather than simple indentation flow.

# Mechanistic Interpretation

- **Room‑temperature strengthening**: The high compressive yield stress of Nb‑ and Ta‑containing Fe‑15Al alloys (~827–868 MPa) results from **Orowan‑type dislocation looping and/or shearing of fine MC carbides**. TiC and VC provide less strengthening, probably because of lower volume fraction or softer shearing characteristics.
- **Yield stress anomaly in Fe‑26Al**: The anomalous peak at intermediate temperatures (~474–550 °C) is attributed to the **Kear–Wilsdorf cross‑slip locking mechanism** of ⟨111⟩ superdislocations in the B2/D0₃ ordered matrix. The shift toward lower peak temperature and higher peak stress with Nb/Ta additions indicates that the high density of thermally stable carbides (and Laves phase) hinders dislocation climb and cross‑slip, intensifying the anomaly.
- **High‑temperature strength retention**: NbC, TaC, and especially the (Fe,Al)₂Nb Laves phase possess high thermal stability and resist Ostwald ripening. This preserves a fine dispersion of obstacles to dislocation motion up to 800 °C, thereby maintaining yield strengths of ~100–136 MPa in compression for the best alloys, compared with only ~32–55 MPa for the binary counterparts.
- **Tension–compression asymmetry**: Arises from the non‑Schmid behaviour of screw dislocations in bcc‑based ordered structures, where the Peierls stress and dislocation core transformations depend on stress state. The asymmetry remains pronounced in carbide‑strengthened variants, showing that the matrix deformation modes dominate, although the precipitates increase the absolute flow stress levels.
- **Brittle fracture**: Coarse MC carbides act as fracture initiation sites. Intergranular decohesion in some Fe‑26Al‑based alloys is promoted by grain boundary precipitation of carbides (e.g., cube‑like TiC on grain boundaries, Fig. 4g,h). Coarse transgranular cleavage replaces fine cleavage when plastic flow is restricted by strong precipitation hardening.

# Key Quantitative Findings

- **Room‑temperature compressive yield stress (0.2% offset)**:  
  Fe–15Al: ~356 MPa; Fe–15Al–2Nb–1C: ~827 MPa; Fe–15Al–2Ta–1C: ~868 MPa.  
  Fe–26Al–4Nb–1C: ~792 MPa (highest among Fe‑26Al alloys at RT).
- **Yield stress anomaly peak**:  
  Fe–26Al–4Nb–1C: 619 MPa at 474 °C.  
  Ti‑/V‑containing Fe‑26Al alloys: peak ~400 MPa at ~550 °C.
- **800 °C compression yield stress**:  
  Fe–26Al–4Nb–1C: ~136 MPa; binary Fe–26Al: ~55 MPa.  
  Fe–15Al–2Ta–1C: ~104 MPa; binary Fe–15Al: ~32 MPa.
- **Tensile ductility at RT**:  
  Fe–15Al binary: ~16% elongation; Fe–15Al–2Ti–1C: ~3.3%; Fe–26Al–2Nb–1C: ~1.4%.
- **Tension–compression asymmetry at 650 °C**: yield strength in compression surpasses that in tension by 30–100 MPa across all alloys, with Fe–26Al–4Nb–1C showing one of the largest absolute differences.
- **Hardness correlation**: Good in Fe–15Al alloys; absent in Fe–26Al alloys.

- **Figure 1 (bar charts)**: Demonstrates that Nb and Ta additions give the highest compressive yield stresses and Brinell hardness in both 15Al and 26Al base alloys. Panel (b) reveals a lack of correlation between hardness and yield strength for Fe‑26Al‑based alloys.
- **Figure 2 (RT compression stress–strain curves)**: Confirms superior strengthening by Nb/Ta carbides; reveals strong work hardening and nearly constant hardening rate in Fe‑26Al alloys even when initial yield is low.
- **Figure 3 (RT tensile stress–strain curves)**: Shows the trade‑off between strength and ductility. Nb‑ and Ta‑containing alloys exhibit sharp yielding but limited ductility; Fe‑26Al–2Ti–1C shows the highest tensile strength in the Fe‑26Al series.
- **Figure 4 (SEM fractographs after RT tension)**: Correlates fracture mode (fine cleavage → coarse cleavage → intergranular) with composition and ductility. Direct evidence of cube‑like TiC on intergranular fracture surfaces in Fe‑26Al‑based alloys and fine facetted cleavage in Fe‑26Al–2Nb–1C.
- **Figure 5 (compression yield stress vs. temperature, Fe‑15Al)**: Monotonous decrease of yield stress with temperature; Nb‑ and Ta‑containing alloys maintain higher strength at all temperatures, retaining ~104 MPa (Ta) and ~83 MPa (Nb) at 800 °C.
- **Figure 6 (compression yield stress vs. temperature, Fe‑26Al)**: Reveals the yield stress anomaly. Subfigure (b) shows the peak for Fe‑26Al–4Nb–1C shifted to 474 °C with a maximum of 619 MPa, attributed to Laves + MC strengthening.
- **Figure 7 (650 °C yield stress, compression vs. tension)**: Provides direct evidence of tension–compression asymmetry; Nb and Ta additions provide the greatest high‑temperature strengthening (up to ~330–430 MPa in compression).
- **Figure 8 (800 °C yield stress, compression vs. tension)**: Confirms that Fe‑26Al–4Nb–1C possesses the best 800 °C performance (~136 MPa compression, ~82 MPa tension) among all alloys, underscoring the thermal stability of the Laves phase + NbC microstructure.
