Material System  
The studied alloys belong to the Fe–26Mn–xAl–1C system (wt.%), with aluminum contents x = 2.59, 5.84, 9.92, and 11.58 wt.%, designated as 3Al, 6Al, 10Al, and 12Al, respectively. Depending on the Al level and processing, the steels may contain austenite (γ), ferrite (α), and ordered nanosized κ‑carbides ((Fe,Mn)₃AlCₓ).  

Processing Route and Variables  
All alloys were prepared by induction melting and then hot‑rolled after soaking at 1150 °C, with initial rolling at 1050 °C to achieve an 85% total reduction down to a 3 mm thick strip. The primary heat treatment was a solution treatment at 1100 °C for 1 h followed by water quenching. For the 6Al steel only, additional heat‑treatment schedules (1000 °C, 1100 °C, and 1200 °C for 1 h, water quenched) were applied to vary the austenite grain size and isolate grain‑boundary strengthening. Mechanical testing included room‑temperature tensile tests at a strain rate of 10⁻³ s⁻¹ and microhardness measurements (FM‑700 tester, 5 kg load). Microstructural characterization employed optical microscopy after etching (1 ml HCl, 100 ml methanol, 4 g picric acid) and TEM with samples prepared by twin‑jet electropolishing in 30% nitric acid in methanol at –30 °C.

Microstructure and Phase Evolution  
After the standard 1100 °C solution treatment and quenching, the 3Al and 6Al steels are fully austenitic with similar grain sizes of ~80 μm (excluding twin boundaries). The 10Al steel remains fully austenitic but shows extensive annealing twins, and weak superlattice reflections in selected‑area electron diffraction (SAED) indicate the onset of (Fe,Mn)₃AlCₓ κ‑carbide ordering. The 12Al steel, in contrast, exhibits a duplex structure containing approximately 7 vol% ferrite (small white blocky phase) and a refined austenite grain size of ~40 μm (~16 μm when including twin boundaries); strong superlattice spots and modulation contrast in TEM confirm substantial coherent κ‑carbide precipitation already present in the as‑quenched state. Thus, increasing Al above ~10 wt% not only introduces ferrite but also accelerates κ‑carbide formation, producing a triplex microstructure (γ + α + κ) at 12Al.

Processing‑Structure‑Property Chain  
The variation in Al content drives the microstructural evolution and directly controls the mechanical properties.  
- 3Al (2.59 wt% Al) → fully austenitic, 80 μm grains → yield strength ≈ 321 MPa.  
- 6Al (5.84 wt% Al) → fully austenitic, 80 μm grains → yield strength increases modestly. The Hall–Petch effect was quantified on this alloy by varying grain size between ~5.4 μm and 52 μm through different solution temperatures, yielding a linear relationship between yield strength and d⁻¹/², with yield strengths of ~340 MPa, 378 MPa, and 447 MPa for d⁻¹/² ≈ 0.14, 0.20, and 0.26 μm⁻¹/², respectively.  
- 10Al (9.92 wt% Al) → austenitic with incipient κ‑carbide ordering → yield strength 470 MPa (from the stacked bar chart analysis).  
- 12Al (11.58 wt% Al) → 7 vol% ferrite, 40 μm austenite grains, abundant coherent κ‑carbides → yield strength ≈ 952 MPa, a nearly three‑fold increase over the 3Al baseline.  
The strengthening contributions from base strength, solid‑solution hardening (by Al), ferrite formation, grain refinement, and κ‑carbide precipitation were quantitatively deconvoluted. For the 12Al steel, κ‑carbide precipitation hardening contributed approximately 362 MPa, which is 1.78 times the solid‑solution hardening effect from Al.

Mechanistic Interpretation  
Aluminum plays a dual role in this series. In the 3Al and 6Al alloys, it acts primarily as a solid‑solution strengthener, while the Hall–Petch effect from grain boundaries remains an important contributor. At higher Al levels (10Al, 12Al), the alloy’s thermodynamic phase balance shifts, promoting the formation of ferrite and, critically, coherent nanosized κ‑carbides of the (Fe,Mn)₃AlCₓ type. These precipitates provide a potent precipitation‑strengthening increment, exceeding 360 MPa in the 12Al steel. Simultaneously, the formation of ~7 vol% ferrite triggers austenite grain refinement (from ~80 μm to ~40 μm), adding grain‑boundary strengthening. The combined contributions of precipitation hardening (~362 MPa), grain refinement, ferrite strengthening, and solid‑solution hardening raise the yield strength from 321 MPa in 3Al to 952 MPa in 12Al, establishing κ‑carbide precipitation as the dominant mechanism.

Key Quantitative Findings  
- Yield strength as a function of Al content (all after 1100 °C/1 h/WQ): 3Al ≈ 321 MPa; 10Al ≈ 470 MPa; 12Al ≈ 952 MPa.  
- In the 12Al steel, κ‑carbide precipitation hardening contribution: ≈ 362 MPa, which is 1.78 × the solid‑solution hardening effect.  
- 12Al microstructure: ≈ 7 vol% ferrite, austenite grain size ~40 μm (or ~16 μm including twins).  
- For the 6Al steel, Hall–Petch data yield: σ_y ≈ 340 MPa at d⁻¹/² ≈ 0.14 μm⁻¹/², 378 MPa at 0.20 μm⁻¹/², and 447 MPa at 0.26 μm⁻¹/², covering grain sizes from ~5.4 μm to 52 μm.
