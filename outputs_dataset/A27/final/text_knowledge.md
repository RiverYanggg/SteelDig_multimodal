# Material System
- Binary Fe–Al alloys with Al content from 4 to 18 at.%, plus a nominally pure Fe reference.
- Impurity levels controlled: total interstitial content <100 wt ppm for most alloys; carbon specifically <20–60 wt ppm C depending on the batch.
- Selected monocrystalline samples: Fe‑16 at.% Al and Fe‑17 at.% Al, oriented along [112] or (111) for compression and microhardness, respectively.
- Polycrystalline as‑cast samples comprise the full composition series Fe‑4Al, Fe‑6Al, Fe‑7Al, Fe‑8Al, Fe‑9Al, Fe‑10Al, Fe‑11Al, Fe‑12Al, Fe‑13Al, Fe‑14Al, Fe‑15Al, Fe‑16Al, Fe‑17Al, Fe‑18Al (all at.%), plus Fe‑0Al polycrystal as reference.

# Processing Route and Variables
- Starting condition: as‑cast polycrystals; monocrystals grown from melt.
- Primary heat‑treatment window:
  - Quenching from 1100 °C (water quench, denoted Q*), which traps excess vacancies.
  - Furnace cooling (FC) from 1100 °C to room temperature, permitting vacancy equilibration and some degree of short‑range ordering.
  - Interrupted furnace cooling to 600 °C followed by water quench (FC 600 °C Q).
  - Quench + annealing: 1100 °C Q followed by isothermal annealing at 250, 300, 320, 400, or 600 °C for durations from 6 hours to 223 days.
- Cooling rate thus becomes a key variable for vacancy concentration and ordering state.

# Microstructure and Phase Evolution
- The alloys are single‑phase ferritic (bcc) solid solutions across the whole composition range under investigation; no phase transformation is reported.
- Grain size and precipitate content are noted but not systematically quantified; paper states low impurity levels and absence of interfering second phases.
- TEM evidence (Figures 6, 7) shows that the deformation‑induced dislocation substructure is strongly influenced by prior cooling rate:
  - Water‑quenched Fe‑16Al (<20 ppm C): straight dislocations confined to glide bands after 5.5 % compressive strain at room temperature (10 ⁻⁴ s ⁻¹).
  - Furnace‑cooled Fe‑16Al: dense dislocation tangles between glide bands under identical strain.
  - Water‑quenched Fe‑12Al (60 ppm C): distinct glide bands and tangles.
  - Furnace‑cooled Fe‑12Al: organized cell structures with high‑density walls and clear interiors.

# Processing–Structure–Property Chain
- **Effect of Al content (as‑cast polycrystals, room‑temperature compression, 10 ⁻⁴ and 10 ⁻² s ⁻¹):**
  - Compressive yield stress increases linearly with Al content from ≈50 MPa (pure Fe) to >400 MPa (Fe‑18Al).
  - Solid‑solution hardening coefficients: ≈23 MPa per at.% Al at 10 ⁻⁴ s ⁻¹; ≈21 MPa per at.% Al at 10 ⁻² s ⁻¹ (Figure 3).
- **Effect of Al content on tensile properties (as‑cast polycrystals, 10 ⁻⁴ s ⁻¹, room temperature vs −100 °C):**
  - Yield stress and ultimate tensile strength both increase linearly with Al content; values at −100 °C are about 80 MPa higher than at room temperature (Figure 5).
  - Ductility (strain before necking, fracture strain) decreases with increasing Al; the drop becomes steep above ~15 at.% Al at room temperature and above ~12 at.% Al at −100 °C (Figure 5).
  - Deformation twinning initiates at −100 °C for Al contents ≥11 at.%; the stress for twin initiation is tracked in Figure 5(b).
- **Effect of heat treatment on hardness (Fe‑16Al monocrystal, (111), room temperature):**
  - Microhardness varies by at most ~10 % across conditions: as‑grown, furnace‑cooled, interrupted cooled, and quenched + annealed.
  - Highest hardness ≈2100 N/mm² (HU) occurs in the water‑quenched (Q*) condition, attributed to quenched‑in excess vacancies.
  - Extended annealing at 300 °C and above reduces hardness slightly; short‑range ordering is inferred to have no major impact (Figure 1).
- **Effect of heat treatment on compressive yield stress (Fe‑16Al [112] monocrystal, room temperature, 10 ⁻⁴ s ⁻¹):**
  - As‑grown, furnace‑cooled, and FC 600 °C Q specimens yield at 400–430 MPa with similar strain‑hardening.
  - The 1100 °C Q + 300 °C/14d anneal yields at 450–480 MPa with consistently higher flow stress (Figure 2).
  - Thus only heat treatments involving temperatures above ~600 °C notably alter strength, primarily through vacancy concentration rather than ordering.
- **Dislocation‑substructure linkage:**
  - Furnace cooling promotes dislocation cell formation (dynamic recovery), while quenching suppresses cell organization and preserves planar slip bands, correlating with the hardening effect of excess vacancies and their influence on dislocation mobility (Figures 6, 7).

# Mechanistic Interpretation
- Solid‑solution hardening from Al is the primary strengthening mechanism; the linear increase of yield stress with Al content fits Suzuki’s theory for bcc alloys.
- Quenched‑in excess vacancies act as additional hardening agents by reducing dislocation mobility; this is the source of the ~10 % hardness increment in quenched Fe‑16Al and the elevated flow stress in the quenched‑plus‑annealed monocrystal.
- Short‑range ordering is demonstrated to play a minor role – annealing treatments that would promote ordering (e.g., 300 °C for 14 days) do not produce hardening beyond that attributable to vacancies.
- Yield drops and serrated flow (most prominent in Fe‑18Al at room temperature and Fe‑4Al at −100 °C) are linked to deformation twinning and to vacancy‑assisted dislocation unlocking rather than ordering effects.
- At −100 °C, increasing Al content progressively suppresses dislocation mobility; when glide becomes too difficult, deformation twinning is triggered (observed for Al ≥11 at.% at −100 °C). This twin‑mediated deformation leads to a macroscopic ductile‑to‑brittle transition above 12–15 at.% Al.

# Key Quantitative Findings
- Linear solid‑solution hardening rate for polycrystalline as‑cast Fe–Al at room temperature: 21–23 MPa per at.% Al (Figure 3).
- Temperature‑dependent offset of tensile properties: yield stress (and UTS) at −100 °C is ≈80 MPa higher than at room temperature for the same composition (Figure 5).
- Maximum microhardness change across heat treatments: ≤10 %, from ≈1870 to ≈2100 N/mm² HU (Figure 1).
- Yield stress range for [112] Fe‑16Al monocrystals: 400–480 MPa, depending on cooling‑rate history (Figure 2).
- Deformation twinning threshold at −100 °C: emerges at Al ≥11 at.% (Figures 4, 5).
