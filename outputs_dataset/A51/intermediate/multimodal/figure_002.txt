Subfigures (a) and (b) are scanning electron microscopy (SEM) micrographs revealing the banded morphology along the rolling direction, where (a) shows the AC sample with alternating δ-ferrite and (γ+α*) bands (α* representing martensite within prior austenite), while (b) displays the FC sample with δ-ferrite and austenite (γ) bands. Subfigures (c) and (d) are electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) maps combining kernel average misorientation (KAM) of austenite with image quality (IQ) data, overlaid with grain boundaries (black lines: high-angle grain boundaries >15°; red lines: low-angle grain boundaries >2°). The EBSD maps quantitatively distinguish the defect structures: the AC sample exhibits a higher KAM value of 0.48° in austenite compared to 0.43° in the FC sample, indicating greater geometrically necessary dislocation (GND) density introduced by the martensitic transformation. Caption states the AC sample contains 26 vol% martensite and 42 vol% austenite, whereas the FC sample contains only 2.8 vol% martensite and 51 vol% austenite. The IQ maps further reveal darker regions in the AC sample corresponding to highly distorted martensite. These observations demonstrate that faster air cooling stabilizes more martensite and introduces higher internal stresses, whereas furnace cooling produces a softer microstructure with higher austenite stability and lower dislocation density, establishing the critical role of cooling path in tailoring the initial microstructure for subsequent annealing treatments and mechanical property optimization.", "confidence": "high"}
