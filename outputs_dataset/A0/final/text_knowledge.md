# Material System

The investigation focuses on austenitic low-density steels: Fe–28Mn–10Al–1C–3Cu (designated 3Cu steel) and a Cu-free reference Fe–28Mn–10Al–1C (0Cu steel). The 3Cu steel contains nanoscale κ-carbide (perovskite-type) and FCC Cu-rich phase precipitates embedded in a γ-austenite matrix.

# Processing Route and Variables

- Solution treatment: 1000 °C for 1 h, applied to both 0Cu and 3Cu steels to dissolve constituents and produce a supersaturated austenitic matrix.
- Aging treatment: 550 °C for 3 h, applied to the 3Cu steel after solution treatment to induce precipitation.
- Primary composition variable: Cu content – 0 wt% vs. 3 wt% Cu.
- Lattice misfit parameter (δ) between precipitates and austenite matrix and the critical austenite lattice parameter (~0.3670 nm) are identified as process-structure indicators.

# Microstructure and Phase Evolution

- In the solution-treated condition, both steels are single-phase austenite.
- After aging the 3Cu steel at 550 °C for 3 h, two precipitate types form:
  - κ-carbide: dot-like or bar-shaped precipitates, coherent with the austenite.
  - Cu-rich phase (FCC): exhibits irregular oval morphology (~10 nm diameter) and bar-shaped morphology (~30 nm length), also coherent.
- Both precipitate types maintain a cube-on-cube orientation relationship with the austenite matrix: [011]γ//[011]κ//[011]Cu and (111)γ//(111)κ//(111)Cu. SAED patterns from the aged 3Cu steel show no additional spots beyond those of κ-carbide and Cu-rich phase, confirming full coherency.
- Elemental partitioning (TEM‑EDS):
  - κ-carbide: enriched in Fe, Mn, Al; depleted in Cu.
  - Cu-rich phase: enriched in Cu; depleted in Fe, Mn, Al.
  - This partitioning indicates that κ-carbide precipitation rejects Cu atoms into the surrounding austenite.
- The co-precipitation sequence: κ-carbide precipitates first, locally enriching the adjacent austenite in Cu; subsequently, FCC Cu-rich phase nucleates adjacent to existing κ-carbide particles.

# Processing‑Structure‑Property Chain

- Solution treatment at 1000 °C/1 h dissolves all solutes into a single-phase supersaturated γ matrix.
- Aging at 550 °C/3 h drives decomposition: κ-carbide precipitation initiates, followed by Cu-rich phase formation.
- The resulting nanoscale dispersion of coherent κ-carbide and Cu-rich precipitates is a direct consequence of the solute rejection–concentration mechanism, which can influence the mechanical response (strengthening) of the austenitic steel, although specific mechanical properties are not reported in this study.

# Mechanistic Interpretation

- Cu addition promotes κ-carbide precipitation; concurrently, κ-carbide facilitates the formation of the FCC Cu-rich phase.
- The lattice misfit calculations reveal that the Cu-rich phase has a lower nucleation barrier in the austenite matrix compared to other potential sites, favoring precipitation adjacent to pre-existing κ-carbide.
- The austenite lattice parameter is a critical index: when it reaches a value around 0.3670 nm, the misfit δ becomes favorable for κ-carbide precipitation.
- The mechanism operates through Cu rejection: as κ-carbide grows, it excludes Cu atoms, raising the local Cu supersaturation to the point where coherent Cu-rich phase precipitates on the {111} planes of the austenite, maintaining cube-on-cube orientation.

# Key Quantitative Findings

- Precipitate sizes in aged 3Cu steel:
  - Cu-rich phase (irregular oval): ≈ 10 nm
  - Cu-rich phase (bar‑shaped): ≈ 30 nm length
  - κ-carbide: nanoscale (dot- or bar-shaped)
- Lattice fringe spacing in Cu-rich phase: 0.182 nm (from HRTEM)
- Critical austenite lattice parameter for κ-carbide precipitation: ~0.3670 nm
- Ageing temperature and time: 550 °C, 3 h

# Visual Evidence

**Figure 001** (TEM + EDS mapping, aged 3Cu steel)  
Provides direct visualization of precipitate morphology and elemental partitioning. Irregular oval Cu-rich phases (~10 nm) and bar-shaped variants (~30 nm) are observed alongside dot-like/bar-shaped κ-carbide. EDS maps confirm κ‑carbide is enriched in Fe, Mn, Al and depleted in Cu, while Cu-rich phase regions show strong Cu enrichment and depletion of matrix elements. This pattern supports the sequence where Cu is expelled from κ‑carbide and subsequently enriches the nearby matrix.

**Figure 002** (HRTEM + SAED, aged 3Cu steel)  
HRTEM lattice images distinguish γ+κ and γ+Cu regions. SAED patterns along [011] zone axis show indexed spots for κ‑carbide and Cu-rich phase; no extra reflections appear, verifying both precipitates are fully coherent with the austenite. The patterns confirm a cube-on-cube orientation relationship: [011]γ//[011]κ//[011]Cu, (111)γ//(111)κ//(111)Cu. This evidence solidly links the coherent interface to the low nucleation barrier of Cu-rich phase.

**Figure 003** (TEM, EDS, HRTEM, aged 3Cu steel)  
Panel (a) shows the dense distribution of nanoscale precipitates after aging. EDS mapping in (b) spatially correlates κ-carbide (Fe/Mn/Al rich, Cu poor) and Cu-rich phase (Cu rich) as distinct domains. Collectively, this figure reinforces the co-precipitation mechanism driven by solute rejection and local enrichment.
