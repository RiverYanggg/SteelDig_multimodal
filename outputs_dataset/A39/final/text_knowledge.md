# Formation of precipitates and their effects on the mechanical properties in a Fe–Mn–Al–C austenitic low-density steel

## Material System

The study investigates two austenitic low-density steel variants within the Fe–Mn–Al–C system:
- **M1 (Reference steel, Ti/V-free)**: Fe–Mn–Al–C base composition without Ti or V microalloying additions.
- **M2 (Ti/V-modified steel)**: Fe–20.04Mn–8.05Al–0.85C–0.62V–0.09Ti (wt%), incorporating strong carbide-forming elements Ti and V to modify precipitation behavior.

Both steels are fully austenitic with high stacking fault energy (~70–78 mJ/m²), favoring planar dislocation slip during deformation.

## Processing Route and Variables

The processing route comprises two principal stages applied to both M1 and M2 alloys:

1. **Solution Treatment**: Performed at 1000 °C for 30 min followed by water quenching. For M1-SS, this produces a clean, single-phase austenitic matrix devoid of visible precipitates (TEM evidence, Figure 3). For M2-SS, the same treatment leads to the precipitation of nanoscale (Ti,V)C carbides (average size: 35.8 ± 20.1 nm) dispersed in the austenite matrix, as observed via bright-field TEM (Figure 3b, blue arrows). These carbides form at high temperature due to the strong carbide-forming tendency of Ti and V.

2. **Aging Treatment**: Conducted at 550 °C for 10 h or 30 h. For M1-30, aging produces a high number density (~7 × 10²² m⁻³) of homogeneously distributed κ-carbides throughout the matrix (TEM dark-field imaging and SAED, Figure 5a–c). For M2-30, aging promotes heterogeneous nucleation of κ-carbides preferentially at the edges of pre-existing (Ti,V)C carbides, with κ-carbide mean radius of 9.7 ± 8.5 nm and volume fraction of 3.2 ± 2.8% after 30 h (Figure 5g–j, embedded table).

Samples are designated as M1-SS, M1-30, M2-SS, M2-10, and M2-30 according to composition and thermal history.

## Microstructure and Phase Evolution

**Grain Structure**:
- Optical microscopy (Figure 1) and EBSD IPF maps (Figure 2) reveal equiaxed austenite grains with random crystallographic orientations and no significant texture in all conditions.
- Ti/V microalloying refines the grain size: M2-SS exhibits an average grain size of 32.3 ± 10.4 μm, notably smaller than M1-SS (42.4 ± 7.8 μm). After aging, M2-30 shows slight grain growth to 35.8 ± 12.7 μm, while M1-30 reaches 45.3 ± 9.3 μm.
- M2-SS displays a bimodal grain size distribution (small grains embedded around larger ones), transitioning to a unimodal distribution after aging in M2-30.

**Precipitate Phases**:
- **(Ti,V)C carbides** (M2 only): Formed during solution treatment; mean radius of 35.8–37.1 nm; volume fraction ~2.3–2.4%; non-shearable particles that impede dislocation motion via the Orowan bypass mechanism. EDS mapping (Figure 6) confirms V enrichment in these carbides, while EPMA (Figure 9) shows intense carbon enrichment at the carbide cores with a carbon concentration gradient extending into the surrounding matrix.
- **κ-Carbides**: In M1-30, these form homogeneously with a high number density (~7 × 10²² m⁻³) and are uniformly distributed throughout the matrix (Figure 5a–c). In M2 steels, κ-carbides nucleate heterogeneously at the carbon-enriched edges of (Ti,V)C particles (HRTEM and FFT, Figure 7), confirmed by orientation relationship (002)(Ti,V)C || (001)κ observed along the Z = [110] zone axis. No apparent Mn or Al enrichment is observed at κ-carbide locations via EDS (Figure 6), suggesting an ordering mechanism rather than compositional partitioning for κ-carbide formation.

## Processing-Structure-Property Chain

**Ti/V addition → (Ti,V)C formation during solution treatment → Carbon sink effect → Altered κ-carbide precipitation mode → Modified mechanical properties**

1. **Composition → Microstructure**: Ti and V additions (0.62 wt% V, 0.09 wt% Ti) in M2 steel cause (Ti,V)C precipitation during solution treatment at 1000 °C. In M1 (no Ti/V), the matrix remains precipitate-free after solution treatment.

2. **Microstructure → Precipitation behavior during aging**:
   - M1-30: Homogeneous κ-carbide precipitation throughout the austenite matrix, high number density (~7 × 10²² m⁻³), uniform distribution.
   - M2-30: Heterogeneous κ-carbide precipitation at (Ti,V)C edges; the (Ti,V)C particles act as carbon sinks, locally depleting matrix carbon and creating carbon concentration gradients (EPMA evidence, Figure 9) that drive preferential κ-carbide nucleation at carbide peripheries (schematic mechanism, Figure 10).

3. **Precipitation mode → Mechanical properties**:
   - M1-30: Yield strength (YS) = 860 MPa, ultimate tensile strength (UTS) = 1004 MPa, elongation = 40%, but with significantly reduced work-hardening rate compared to M1-SS (YS ~430 MPa). The work-hardening degradation is attributed to glide plane softening caused by uniformly distributed shearable κ-carbides.
   - M2-30: YS = 884 MPa, UTS = 1096 MPa, while maintaining a work-hardening capacity comparable to M2-SS (YS ~660 MPa). The heterogeneous κ-carbide distribution avoids glide plane softening, preserving the strain hardening rate.

4. **Yield strength contributions** (Figure 11):
   - M1-SS: Total YS ~430 MPa, minimal precipitation strengthening.
   - M1-30: Total YS ~860 MPa, dominated by κ-carbide shearing contribution (~430 MPa).
   - M2-SS: Total YS ~660 MPa, includes Orowan bypass contribution from (Ti,V)C.
   - M2-30: Total YS ~884 MPa, achieved through a hybrid mechanism: κ-carbide shearing contribution (~220 MPa, reduced vs. M1-30) supplemented by (Ti,V)C Orowan bypass (~120 MPa).

## Mechanistic Interpretation

**Carbon Sink Mechanism** (Figure 10 schematic):
The strong carbide-forming elements Ti and V consume matrix carbon to form (Ti,V)C during high-temperature solution treatment, thereby reducing the carbon available for homogeneous κ-carbide precipitation during subsequent aging. Carbon concentration gradients around (Ti,V)C particles (directly imaged by EPMA, Figure 9) drive the heterogeneous nucleation of κ-carbides at the carbon-enriched carbide edges rather than in the bulk matrix. This spatial redistribution from homogeneous to heterogeneous precipitation is the core microstructural design principle.

**Work-Hardening Preservation**:
In M1-30, the high density of uniformly distributed, shearable κ-carbides leads to dislocation glide plane softening: dislocalizations cut through κ-carbides, localizing slip into widely spaced planar slip bands, which causes a rapid decrease in work-hardening rate (Crussard-Jaoul analysis, Figure 12a: M1-30 shows only two work-hardening stages vs. three stages for M1-SS). Post-deformation TEM (Figure 13a) confirms wide slip-band spacing in M1-30.

In M2-30, the presence of non-shearable (Ti,V)C particles and the heterogeneous κ-carbide distribution hinder dislocation motion more effectively, resulting in finer, more densely packed slip bands (Figure 13b, yellow dashed lines; higher-magnification Figure 13c shows dislocation interactions with (Ti,V)C carbides). The work-hardening index curves for M2-SS and M2-30 nearly coincide (Figure 12b), demonstrating that work-hardening capacity is retained despite the precipitation strengthening from heterogeneously distributed κ-carbides.

**Planar Slip Deformation**:
Both M1-30 and M2-30 exhibit planar dislocation slip, consistent with their high stacking fault energy (~70–78 mJ/m²). The key difference lies in slip band spacing and density, which is controlled by the precipitate distribution.

## Visual Evidence

- **Figure 1 (Optical micrographs)**: Confirms equiaxed austenitic grain structures across all four conditions (M1-SS, M1-30, M2-SS, M2-30) and demonstrates grain refinement by Ti/V additions (42.4 ± 7.8 μm vs. 32.3 ± 10.4 μm). The bimodal-to-unimodal grain size distribution evolution in M2 is visually evident.
- **Figure 2 (EBSD IPF maps)**: Validates random crystallographic orientations, absence of texture, and the grain size refinement in M2 steel. Supports the microstructural baseline for subsequent precipitation analysis.
- **Figure 3 (TEM bright-field)**: Provides direct evidence that M1-SS is precipitate-free while M2-SS contains dispersed (Ti,V)C particles (35.8 ± 20.1 nm), establishing the initial microstructural difference before aging.
- **Figure 5 (TEM BF/DF/SAED)**: Comprehensive precipitate characterization showing homogeneous κ-carbides in M1-30 (~7 × 10²² m⁻³) versus heterogeneous coexistence of κ-carbides and (Ti,V)C in M2-10 and M2-30. The SAED patterns confirm orientation relationships and crystal structures.
- **Figure 6 (TEM-EDS mapping)**: Shows V enrichment at (Ti,V)C carbides and no Mn/Al enrichment at κ-carbide locations, supporting an ordering-based κ-carbide formation mechanism.
- **Figure 7 (HRTEM and FFT)**: Resolves the intergrowth of κ-carbides at (Ti,V)C edges at atomic scale and confirms the orientation relationship (002)(Ti,V)C || (001)κ.
- **Figure 8 (Engineering and true stress-strain curves)**: Quantitatively demonstrates that M2-30 achieves higher UTS (1096 MPa vs. 1004 MPa for M1-30) while maintaining work-hardening capacity comparable to M2-SS, in contrast to M1-30’s work-hardening decay relative to M1-SS.
- **Figure 9 (EPMA carbon mapping)**: Directly visualizes carbon enrichment at (Ti,V)C cores and the surrounding carbon-rich region, experimentally validating the carbon sink mechanism.
- **Figure 10 (Schematic)**: Illustrates the proposed precipitation evolution: (Ti,V)C carbides create carbon gradients that drive heterogeneous κ-carbide nucleation at particle edges during aging.
- **Figure 11 (Yield strength decomposition)**: Quantifies individual strengthening contributions (σ₀, σ_G, σ_Or, σ_S), revealing the hybrid strengthening mechanism in M2-30 vs. the κ-carbide shearing-dominated strengthening in M1-30.
- **Figure 12 (Crussard-Jaoul plots)**: Shows the work-hardening index evolution: M1-30 loses one work-hardening stage vs. M1-SS, while M2-30 and M2-SS curves nearly coincide, confirming preserved work-hardening capacity via heterogeneous precipitation.
- **Figure 13 (Post-deformation TEM)**: Compares dislocation structures: wider slip-band spacing in M1-30 vs. finer, denser slip bands in M2-30, with direct imaging of dislocations interacting with (Ti,V)C carbides, explaining the differential work-hardening behavior.
