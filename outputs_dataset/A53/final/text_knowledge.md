# High Temperature Oxidation of Iron–Manganese–Aluminum Based Alloys

## Material System
- **Ternary alloys**: Fe–Mn–Al with 0–40 wt% Mn and 0–15 wt% Al (high purity, levitation melted).
- **Binary alloys**: Fe–Mn.
- **Quaternary alloys**: Fe–Mn–Al–Cr (e.g., Fe–10Mn–4Al–5Cr, Fe–15Mn–5Al–5Cr, Fe–7.5Mn–4Al–5Cr).
- **Phase regions** investigated: single-phase ferrite (α), single-phase austenite (γ), duplex α+γ, and duplex α+β-Mn.
- **Optimum oxidation‑resistant compositions**: Fe–(5–10)%Mn–(6–10)%Al, fully ferritic structure.

## Processing Route and Variables
- **Alloy preparation**: Levitation melting of high‑purity elements → as‑cast discs (~7 mm diameter, 1 mm thickness).
- **Isothermal oxidation**:
  - Temperatures: 600 °C, 800 °C, 1000 °C.
  - Atmosphere: static pure oxygen at 200 torr.
  - Oxidation time: 24 h (selected tests up to 100 h).
- **Post‑oxidation quenching**: for phase confirmation.
- **Characterization**: weight‑gain kinetics, SEM/EDS, X‑ray mapping, EPMA, metallography, X‑ray diffraction.

## Microstructure and Phase Evolution
- **As‑cast microstructures** determined by Mn and Al contents: ferritic (low Mn, moderate Al), austenitic (high Mn), or duplex α+γ; β‑Mn appears in high‑Mn (e.g., 30% Mn) compositions.
- **During high‑temperature oxidation**:
  - In duplex alloys, **austenite (γ) plates nucleate and grow beneath the initial alumina scale**, then promote oxide breakthrough.
  - Mn depletion and oxygen ingress convert affected substrate layers back to ferrite; e.g., Fe‑20Mn‑6Al develops a ferrite surface layer beneath bulky Mn‑rich oxides.
  - In fully ferritic compositions (Region III), the substrate remains single‑phase α throughout oxidation.

## Processing–Structure–Property Chain
- **800 °C, 200 torr O₂, 24 h**: oxidation behavior mapped into four distinct regions (Figure 3 oxide map) governed by Al and Mn contents and the resulting substrate phase constitution.
  - **Region I** (low Al, <6%): Alumina scale interspersed with large Fe‑rich oxide nodules; rapid breakaway oxidation → high weight gain.
  - **Region II** (duplex α+γ, e.g., Fe–15Mn–6Al, Fe–20Mn–6Al): Alumina scale disrupted by bulky Mn‑rich oxides and wormlike Mn₂O₃ threads growing above γ plates; weight gain 0.223 mg cm⁻² for Fe–15Mn–6Al, >1 mg after 20 h for Fe–20Mn–6Al.
  - **Region III** (fully α, 5–10% Mn, 6–10% Al): Continuous protective α‑Al₂O₃ scales, minimal weight gain (e.g., Fe–10Mn–9Al follows near‑parabolic kinetics with n=2.9; Fe–15Mn–8Al weight gain ~0.116 mg cm⁻²).
  - **Region IV** (high Al, >9%): Protective alumina scales locally penetrated by small Mn‑rich nodules; spallation on cooling.
- **Effect of Mn content at 800 °C (Fig. 2)**:
  - For fixed Al (6–9%), weight gain minimum at 5–10% Mn.
  - <5% Mn: large Fe‑rich nodules accelerate oxidation.
  - >10% Mn: Mn‑rich scale formation, increased nodule density.
- **Effect of Al content**: weight gain minimum at 6–9% Al for fixed Mn (2.5–20%).
- **Temperature influence**:
  - Fe–10Mn–6Al (Region III) retains protective Al₂O₃ at 800 °C but at 1000 °C after 100 h shows thick, convoluted, partially detached scales with localized oxide growth.
- **Cr additions** (5 wt%):
  - Reduce critical Al for protective scale formation; e.g., Fe–10Mn–4Al–5Cr after 24 h shows only hollow oxidized mounds, but long‑term (100 h) spallation and Mn‑oxide threads occur.
  - Duplex Fe–15Mn–5Al–5Cr suffers severe breakaway oxidation, weight gain 2.663 mg cm⁻² in 24 h; thick convoluted Mn‑rich scale.
  - Fully ferritic Cr‑containing alloys can maintain Al₂O₃ protection.

## Mechanistic Interpretation
- **Protection mechanism**: In fully ferritic Region III alloys, Al selectively oxidizes to form a continuous, adherent α‑Al₂O₃ scale, limiting cation/anion transport.
- **Austenite‑induced breakdown** (Region II, six‑stage model, Fig. 21):
  1. Initial α matrix covered by thin Al₂O₃.
  2. γ plates precipitate beneath the scale during heating.
  3. Wormlike Mn₂O₃ threads grow through scale defects above γ.
  4. Threads merge into bulky Mn‑rich nodules; substrate beneath becomes Mn‑depleted, reverting to α.
  5. Healing layer of Al₂O₃ or (Fe,Mn)Al₂O₄ forms at nodule‑alloy interface, but scale integrity is lost.
- **Mn vapor transport mechanism** (Region IV and high‑Mn alloys, Fig. 23):
  - Voids form beneath Al₂O₃ scale; defects allow O₂ ingress and Mn evaporation.
  - Mn vapor diffuses through voids, oxidizes to Mn₂O₃/Mn₃O₄, forming small nodules that penetrate the scale.
  - Subsequent internal oxidation of Al creates healing layers at nodule base, limiting growth.
- **Effect of Mn on nodule structure** (800 °C, Fig. 20):
  - <5% Mn: large Fe‑rich nodules (outer Fe₂O₃, inner (Fe,Mn)₃O₄, discontinuous Al₂O₃ at base).
  - 5–7.5% Mn: mixed Fe‑ and Mn‑rich nodules; partial (Mn₂O₃ in Fe₂O₃) and partial healing layers.
  - ≥7.5% Mn: small Mn‑rich nodules with continuous outer Mn₂O₃, continuous Al₂O₃ beneath, and complete (Fe,Mn)Al₂O₄ healing layer → lower oxidation rate.

## Key Quantitative Findings
- **Oxidation kinetics at 800 °C (24 h)**:
  - Fe–10Mn–9Al: weight gain follows power‑law exponent n=2.9 (near‑parabolic).
  - Fe–30Mn–11Al: n=3.7; Fe–5Mn–8Al: n=10.6; Fe–20Mn–10Al: n=4.6; Fe–1Mn–8Al: n=5.6.
  - Breakaway/healing kinetics in Fe–25Mn–7Al, Fe–5Mn–6Al, Fe–25Mn–6Al, Fe–7.5Mn–5Al, Fe–20Mn–6(7)Al, Fe–1Mn–6Al.
- **Weight gain data (800 °C, 24 h)**:
  - Fe–15Mn–6Al (Region II): 0.223 mg cm⁻².
  - Fe–20Mn–6Al: >1 mg after 20 h.
  - Fe–15Mn–8Al (Region III): ≈0.116 mg cm⁻² (small Mn‑rich protrusions persist).
  - Fe–15Mn–5Al–5Cr (duplex): 2.663 mg cm⁻².
- **Critical composition window**: Fe–(5–10)%Mn–(6–10)%Al, fully ferritic, yields protective Al₂O₃ scales with minimal weight gain.
- **Cr effect**: 5% Cr lowers minimum Al from ~6% to ~4–5% for ferritic alloys; austenite negates the benefit.
- **Phase correlations**: Austenite (γ) or γ precipitates are essential for nodular breakdown; fully ferritic α avoids catastrophic oxidation.
