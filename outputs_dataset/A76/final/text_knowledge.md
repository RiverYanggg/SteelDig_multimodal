# Material System
- Base alloy: Fe-12Mn-9Al-3Cr-1.4C (wt.%) low-density steel  
- Nb-microalloyed variants:  
  - 0.02Nb: Fe-12Mn-9Al-3Cr-1.4C-0.02Nb  
  - 0.04Nb: Fe-12Mn-9Al-3Cr-1.4C-0.04Nb  
- Phases present: austenite (γ, FCC), δ-ferrite (BCC), κ-carbide, NbC  

# Processing Route and Variables
- Melting: induction melting  
- Casting: centrifugal casting with near-rapid solidification → as-cast condition  
- Aging treatments:  
  - Temperature: 723 K, 773 K, 823 K  
  - Soaking time: 3 h for all aging conditions  
- Samples examined: as-cast and aged conditions for both 0.02Nb and 0.04Nb steels  

# Microstructure and Phase Evolution
- As-cast and aged microstructures are dual-phase: irregular δ-ferrite islands dispersed in a γ-austenite matrix.  
- XRD (Figure 1) confirms the BCC δ-ferrite and FCC γ-austenite peaks remain stable across all aging temperatures for both Nb levels, indicating no detectable additional phase transformation or new precipitate peaks. Minimal difference in phase constitution between 0.02Nb and 0.04Nb steels.  
- δ-ferrite volume fraction: approximately constant at 16–18 vol.% across aging temperatures (SEM observations, Figure 2).  
- NbC precipitates: submicron particles observed by SEM (Figure 2) and TEM (Figure 3).  
  - In 0.02Nb steel, a NbC particle is confirmed by TEM/SAED with orientation relationship [100]γ // [100]NbC.  
  - Higher Nb content (0.04Nb) results in more frequent NbC particles and a reduction of austenite grain size by approximately a factor of two compared to 0.02Nb steel.  
  - NbC particles undergo slight coarsening with increasing aging temperature.  
- κ-carbide: Nb addition increases the formation energy of κ-carbide and impedes its coarsening (inferred from paper focus). No direct κ-carbide TEM evidence is provided in the visual evidence, but its behavior is linked to the mechanical property discussion.  

# Processing-Structure-Property Chain
- Nb addition during melting → NbC precipitation → pinning of austenite grain boundaries during casting/aging → grain refinement.  
- Increasing Nb from 0.02 to 0.04 wt.% → stronger grain refinement (grain size halved) and higher NbC number density → greater contribution from fine-grain strengthening.  
- Ageing temperature elevation (723 K → 823 K) → slight NbC coarsening, but κ-carbide coarsening suppressed by Nb → maintenance of precipitation strengthening and delay in over-aging.  
- The yield strength of 0.04Nb steel is ~71 MPa higher than that of 0.02Nb steel. This increment is primarily attributed to enhanced fine-grain strengthening and dislocation strengthening, as deduced from quantitative analysis of strengthening mechanisms. (Exact yield strength values not reported, but difference is stated in the abstract.)  

# Mechanistic Interpretation
- Nb in solid solution and as NbC precipitates provides Zener pinning pressure on austenite grain boundaries, restricting grain growth during solidification and subsequent aging. This is the origin of austenite grain refinement.  
- The increase in formation energy of κ-carbide by Nb delays its nucleation and growth kinetics, thereby impeding κ-carbide coarsening and maintaining a finer distribution of strengthening precipitates even after prolonged or elevated-temperature aging.  
- The 71 MPa strength difference between 0.04Nb and 0.02Nb steels arises from:  
  - Grain-size strengthening: Δσ ∝ k_y(d^(-½) difference) due to halved grain size in 0.04Nb.  
  - Dislocation strengthening: likely enhanced by NbC-related dislocation storage and Orowan-type resistance, especially if NbC particles are nanometric.  

# Key Quantitative Findings
- δ-ferrite content: 16–18 vol.% across all aging conditions, relatively insensitive to Nb content and aging temperature.  
- Austenite grain size reduction: 0.04Nb steel austenite grains approximately half the size of 0.02Nb steel.  
- Yield strength difference: σ_y(0.04Nb) − σ_y(0.02Nb) ≈ +71 MPa.  
- NbC orientation relationship: [100]γ // [100]NbC (TEM/SAED for 0.02Nb steel).  
- NbC particles coarsen slightly with increasing aging temperature from 723 K to 823 K.  

# Visual Evidence
- **Figure 1 (XRD)**: Shows the same set of γ and δ peaks across as-cast and all aged conditions (723–823 K, 3 h) for both 0.02Nb and 0.04Nb steels. There is no indication of new phase formation or dissolution. This confirms phase stability and supports that Nb does not alter the fundamental ferrite/austenite balance, only refining existing phases and introducing fine precipitates.  
- **Figure 2 (SEM)**: Panels (a-d) for 0.02Nb and (e-h) for 0.04Nb in as-cast and aged states. Demonstrated key facts:  
  - Austensite grain size visibly refined in 0.04Nb compared to 0.02Nb (approximately halved).  
  - NbC particles (purple arrows) more abundant in 0.04Nb.  
  - δ-ferrite fraction roughly constant (16–18 vol.%).  
  - Slight coarsening of NbC with aging temperature, yet overall grain refinement remains.  
- **figure_003 (TEM)**: Bright-field image of a NbC particle in austenite matrix in 0.02Nb steel. SAED inset reveals cube-on-cube orientation relationship [100]γ // [100]NbC. This crystallographic information confirms the identity and coherent/direct formation of NbC within the γ phase, supporting the pinning mechanism for grain boundary control and providing direct evidence of NbC precipitation.
