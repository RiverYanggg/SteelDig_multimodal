# Microstructural Evolution and Mechanical Properties of Nb-Containing Medium-Mn Low-Density Steels

Chen Ma, Jianlei Zhang,\* Fenghui An, Gang Chen, Junye Zhou, Changjiang Song, and Qijie Zhai

Microalloying of Nb has been commonly utilized to enhance the structure and mechanical properties of advanced high-strength steel (AHSS), but its application in medium-Mn low-density steel has been relatively understudied. This study aims to investigate the evolution of nano-scale precipitates and mechanical properties of Fe-12Mn-9Al-3Cr-1.4C-0.02/0.04Nb low-density steels. The findings reveal that the austenite grain size of 0.04Nb steels is approximately half that of 0.02Nb steels due to the precipitation of NbC particles. Moreover, the addition of Nb is found to elevate the formation energy of κ-carbide, thereby impeding its growth and coarsening. Consequently, the intragranular κ-carbides in 0.04Nb steels are consistently smaller than those in 0.02Nb steels, with no coarsened intergranular κ-carbides detected in either steel variant after aging treatment at 723–823 K. Despite a slight reduction on precipitation strengthening of κ-carbides with higher Nb content, the yield strength of 0.04Nb steels exceeds that of 0.02Nb steels by ≈71 MPa, mainly due to additional fine grain strengthening and dislocation strengthening. The strengthening mechanisms in the Nb-containing low-density steels are analyzed quantitatively. The role of Nb in regulating the microstructure and mechanical properties of medium-Mn low-density steels is comprehensively discussed, offering valuable insights for the alloy design of lowdensity steel.

# 1. Introduction

Due to the good comprehensive mechanical properties, medium-Mn steels have become highly interesting for automotive structure applications and are recognized as the third-generation advanced highstrength steels (AHSS).[1–4] Through utilizing various strengthening mechanisms and triggering transformationinduced plasticity (TRIP) effect or twinning-induced plasticity (TWIP) effect in retained austenite, the medium-Mn steels could achieve an excellent product of strength and elongation (PSE) up to 80 GPa%.[1,4] Recently, as automobile lightweighting is increasingly valued, a significant amount of Al is added into the medium-Mn steel to develop low-density steel.[5–7] It has been reported that adding 1 wt% Al could decrease the steel density by 1.3–1.5%.[8,9] Moreover, the addition of Al could facilitate the formation of κ-carbide, a nano-scale strengthening precipitate in austenite, thereby substantially improving the mechanical properties of steels.[10,11] Our previous work has reported that, a κ-carbidestrengthened Fe-12Mn-9Al-1.4C-3Cr (wt%) steel, which underwent cold rolling, exhibited an impressive tensile strength of 1.5 GPa while maintaining a total elongation of 15%.[12]

The precipitation behaviors of κ-carbide have a significant impact on the mechanical properties and strain-hardening behaviors of the medium-Mn low-density steels.[6,13,14] The increase in both quantity and size of κ-carbides can enhance their strengthening effect, resulting in a strength contribution of more than 400 MPa.[12] However, Liu et al. reported that aging at 550 °C for 60 min resulted in the formation of a large amount of intragranular and intergranular κ-carbides in Fe-11Mn-10Al-1.2C (wt%) steel, achieving a high yield strength of 1407 MPa but with a low elongation of 7.4%.[6] Additionally, the growth of κ-carbides could hinder dislocation glide and slip band formation, leading to decreased strain-hardening ability.[11,14,15] Compared to high-Mn low-density steels, the regulation of κ-carbides in medium-Mn low-density steels appears to be more challenging. The higher Mn content in steels could slow down the formation rate of κ-carbides,[16,17] which allows for more regulation space, whereas in the medium-Mn low-density steels, it is easier to form coarse κ-carbides.[13] Further exploration of

The addition of microalloying elements, such as Ti, V, and Nb, has been shown to effectively control the microstructure and performance of medium-Mn steel.[18–20] A study by Park et al. demonstrated that the addition of 0.2 wt% V could trigger the formation of VC precipitates in a Fe-8Mn-0.2C-3Al (wt%) steel, leading to significant grain refinement in austenite and a strength enhancement of 246 MPa.[18] Similar effects on grain refinement and strength enhancement also observed in Ti-containing and Nb-containing medium-Mn steels, due to the formation of TiC and NbC particles.[19,20] The addition of 0.05 wt% Nb to a Fe-5.6Mn-1.2Al-0.17C steel could refine the austenite grain by forming nano-scale Nb(C, N) particles.[20] Additionally, the smaller average austenite grain caused by the precipitation of Nb(C, N) particles was proven to improve the stability of austenite.[20] However, there are few research on how microalloying elements regulate the microstructure and performance of medium-Mn low-density steels, especially for the precipitation behaviors of κ-carbides.

Park et al. have reported that the addition of 0.1 wt% Nb resulted in the formation of NbC carbides in a Fe-20Mn-8Al-1.0C (wt%) steel, which reduced the C content in austenite, decreasing the precipitation of κ-carbides.[21] The Nb(C, N) carbides typically possess a hard characteristic, contributing to significant strain-hardening potential.[22] But it was questionable whether the limited formation of NbC could significantly reduce the C content in austenite and thus affect the formation of κ-carbides. Consequently, the influence of Nb microalloying on the microstructure and mechanical properties of medium-Mn low-density steel is worth exploring in detail. This work designed two Fe-12Mn-9Al-3Cr-1.4C-0.02/0.04Nb (wt%) low-density steels to explore the microstructural evolution and mechanical properties of the Nb-containing medium-Mn low-density steels. Considering the influence of aging treatment on the nanoprecipitation behavior in low-density steels, especially κ-carbides,[8,23] the studied steels were further aged at 723, 773, and 823 K. The microstructure evolution was examined using a scanning electron microscope (SEM) equipped with electron back scatter diffraction (EBSD) system and a transmission electron microscope (TEM). Additionally, the influence of Nb addition on the nano-precipitation behavior was elucidated through thermodynamic calculations and first-principles calculations.

# 2. Experimental Section

The Fe-12Mn-9Al-3Cr-1.4C (wt%) low-density steels with 0.02 wt% and 0.04 wt% Nb addition in this work were prepared using a centrifugal casting method under near-rapid solidification. Initially, the ingot was prepared by induction melting in a water-cooled copper crucible. Subsequently, the ingot was re-melted and swiftly poured into a rotating copper mold at a high speed to obtain steel strips with dimensions of 60 70 2.5 mm through near-rapid solidification. Aging treatment was performed on a portion of these steel strips in a tubular vacuum furnace at temperatures of 723, 773, and 823 K for 3 h. Subsequently, an appropriate aging temperature was selected to investigate the structure evolution and mechanical properties of steels under different aging periods. For simplicity, hereafter, the steels containing 0.02 wt% Nb and 0.04 wt% Nb are referred to as 0.02Nb steels and 0.04Nb steels, respectively.

The actual measured chemical compositions of steel strips are presented in . X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis for the Table 1steel strips was conducted using a D/max-2500 instrument at 40 kV, scanning in the range of 30°–100° with a speed of 4°/min. An Apollo 300 thermal field emission SEM equipped with an EBSD detector was used for the microstructure characterization. The scanning step size for EBSD characterization was set as 300 nm. Prior to SEM observation, the samples underwent electropolishing with 10 vol% perchloric acid alcohol solution under 25 V for a duration of 10 s. To analyze the elemental distribution, an electron probe microanalysis (EPMA) was carried out using a JXA-8050G instrument at an electron beam voltage of 15 kV. The TEM characterization for the nano-scale precipitates was conducted using a JEOL JEM-2100 TEM operating at 200 kV. For the preparation of TEM specimen, a double-jet electropolishing technique was utilized with a solution composed of 10% perchloric acid and 90% alcohol acid at a temperature of ≈ 30 °C and a voltage of 35 V. The microhardness of the strips was measured using a Vickers hardness tester (MH-5L) with an indentation load of 200 gf and a holding time of 20 s. The tensile property tests were carried out using a MTS Criterion 44 universal testing machine at ambient temperature. The strain rate during the tests was set as $2 . 5 \times 1 0 ^ { - 4 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ . The test specimens had a gauge width of 4 mm and a gauge length of 12 mm to ensure consistent measurements. To ensure accurate data analysis, each steel strip underwent a minimum of four repetitions during the testing process.

To explore the influence of Nb addition on the formation of κ-carbide, this study employed spin-polarized density functional theory (DFT) calculations within the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) utilizing the first-principles Perdew–Burke– Ernzerhof (PBE) recipe, which was implemented in Vienna Ab Initio Simulation Package (VASP).[24–27] The ion nuclei were characterized by projected enhanced wave (PAW) potentials,[28,29] while the valence electrons were treated with a plane-wave basis set and a kinetic energy cutoff of 500 eV. To accurately compensate for the partial occupation of the Kohn–Sham orbitals, the Gaussian smearing approach was utilized with a width of 0.05 eV. The convergence of the geometry optimization process was determined by ensuring that the energy change was below 0.03 eV Å1 . The U scheme with a value of 2.59 eV was applied for Fe atoms. The slab structure had a vacuum spacing of 15 Å in the direction perpendicular to the structure plane. Brillouin zone integration of the structure was achieved using 6 6 6 Monkhorst–Pack k-point sampling.
