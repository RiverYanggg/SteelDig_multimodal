# a b s t r a c t

The microstructure evolution and tensile properties of the Fe–10Mn–10Al–0.7C low-density steel annealed at the temperature range from $7 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $1 2 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h were investigated in the present study. The phases in the annealed specimens mainly included ferrite (α), austenite (γ) and κ-carbide (κ). The dissolution temperature for κ-carbide is between 850 °C and 900 °C. At the annealing temperature of $7 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ all the γ phase transformed to α phase and κ-carbide by eutectoid reaction. As the annealing temperature rose from 700 °C to 900 ${ } ^ { \circ } C , \alpha$ phase and κ-carbide fraction decreased and γ phase fraction increased, and ultimate elongation increased, while ultimate strength and yield strength had a decreasing trend. The γ phase fraction in the specimen dominated the ultimate elongation, and the κ-carbide strengthened the specimen and weakened ductility. As the annealing temperature rose from 900 °C to 1200 °C, the phase fraction change was small but the grain size increased greatly. The rapid growth of the grains was the main reason why ultimate tensile strength and ultimate elongation decreased with the increase of annealing temperature over 900 °C. The steel designed here possessed a density of 6.80 g/cm3 , which is about 13% lower than pure iron.

# 1. Introduction

The demand for increasing the efficiency of fuel and decreasing the emission of exhaust gas has become more and more prominent in automotive industry, as considered from both environment and energy source aspects. Reducing the weight of automobiles is a good idea for the demand and much effort has been done to improve the strength and ductility of steels used for automotive industry, which will reduce the weight of automobiles by reducing the necessary thickness of steels [1,2]. Twinning induced plasticity (TWIP) steel and transformation induced plasticity (TRIP) steel are representative high-strength steels, containing a high amount of Mn [3–12]. Adding or increasing the Al content in the steel will decrease the density of the steel as Al has a low density. Since low-density high-strength steels can reduce the weight of automobiles more efficiently, Fe–Mn–Al–C steel with a high amount of Al has attracted much attention [1,13,14].

The phases in Fe–Mn–Al–C steels mainly include ferrite, austenite, and κ-carbide [1,15–20]. The κ-carbide has a perovskite structure, and can form by decomposition of austenite or the ordering process in austenite [2,21,22]. The structure of κ-carbide is based on the face-centered cubic crystal lattice, with Fe or Mn atoms occupying the face-centered positions, Al atoms occupying the cornered positions and C atoms occupying the body-centered positions. The space group of κ-carbide is Pm3m. In addition, B2 and $\mathsf { D } 0 _ { 3 }$ ordered phases may also appear in the steel [23]. The space groups of B2 phase and $\mathsf { D } 0 _ { 3 }$ phase are Pm3m and Fm3m, respectively. B2 phase is based on body centered cubic crystal lattice, Fe or Mn atoms occupying cornered positions, and Al atoms occupying centered positions (the positions of Fe or Mn atoms and the positions of Al atoms are equivalent). For the structure of $\mathsf { D } 0 _ { 3 }$ phase, it can be regarded as that every other position occupied by Al atom in the B2 structure is replaced by Fe or Mn atom. Sketches of the structures of the ordered phases are given by Ha et al. [23]. Mn and C are austenitic stabilizing elements, while Al is a strong ferrite forming element. Ferrite is inevitable in the steel with a high Al concentration [1]. Temperature may largely affect the microstructure of the steel, and then affect the mechanical properties. Frommeyer et al. studied the steel Fe–26/ 28Mn–10/12Al–1.0/1.2C composed of ferrite, austenite and nano-sized κ-carbide dispersed in the austenite, whose enhanced ductility is attributed to the homogeneous shear band formation, and the existence of nano-sized κ-carbide coherent to the austenitic matrix [24]. In the steel Fe–27Mn–12Al–0.8C studied by Park, there are B2 and D0 ordered phases in ferrite and κ-carbide in austenite, which are considered to contribute to the high work hardening rate by acting as obstacles for dislocation glide [23]. Another steel with degraded Mn, Al and C contents, Fe–20Mn–9Al–0.6C, studied by Park et al. reveals no or undetectable ordered phase in the duplex microstructure and has lower ultimate strength than Fe–27Mn–12Al–0.8C [23,25]. Ding et al. investigated steels Fe–12Mn–8Al–0.8C and Fe–18Mn–10Al–(0.8–1.2)C, which contain Mn content less than 20 wt.% and Al content more than 8 wt.%, and results show that, the ductility of Fe–18Mn–10Al–0.8C which composed of ferrite and austenite is less than that of Fe–18Mn–10Al–1.2C which composed of austenite and dispersed κ-carbide when temperature is higher than $9 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ while Fe–12Mn–8Al–0.8C composed of ferrite, austenite and dispersed κ-carbide exhibits similar ultimate strength and ductility with Fe–18Mn–10Al–0.8C [26,27]. The ductility of the fully austenitic steels usually seems to be superior to that of the steels containing ferrite, and the nano-size κ-carbide dispersed in the austenite may promote the ductility [17,28–30].

Although lots of investigations have been done on the microstructure and mechanical properties of low-density high-strength Fe–Mn– Al–C steels, most of them are focused on the steels containing Mn content near or more than 20 wt.%, and the microstructure and mechanical properties of the steels containing a high Al and a relative low Mn and C contents should be studied. Thus, a low-density steel Fe–10Mn–10Al– 0.7C is designed and the microstructure and tensile properties after annealed at various temperatures of the steel are investigated in the present study.

# 2. Experimental

A 20 kg ingot of a Fe–10Mn–10Al–0.7C steel was prepared by vacuum induction melting method. The ingot was heated to $1 2 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and then forged to forging blank with cross section of about 40 mm × 60 mm. After homogenized at $1 2 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h, the forging blank was hot-rolled to the plate of 3 mm thickness, with the finishing rolling temperature around $8 8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } _ { \scriptscriptstyle 1 }$ and then water quenched to about 300 °C. The hot-rolled plate was annealed at various temperatures ranging from $7 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $1 2 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for the same time, 1 h, followed by water quenching. The hot rolling and annealing conditions are shown in Fig. 1.

The chemical composition of the hot rolled steel was measured to be 10.4 wt.% Mn, 9.7 wt.% Al, 0.70 wt.% C, and Fe balance. The density of the steel was measured by a densitometry based on Archimedes Principles. The density of the present steel is about $6 . 8 0 \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 3 }$ , which is about 13% lower than pure Fe.

The constituent phases were identified at first by the X-ray diffraction (XRD, Rigaku, D/Max-RB) analysis with a Cu target operated at 40 kV and 150 mA. Microstructures were examined by optical microscope (OM, Zeiss AxioCam MRc5), field-emission scanning electron microscopy (FE-SEM, FEI Quanta 450) operated at an acceleration voltage of 20 kV and transmission electron microscopy (TEM, JEOL 2100) operated at an acceleration voltage of 200 kV. The SEM is equipped with an energy dispersive spectrometer (EDS), which can be used to examine the chemical composition of the points or areas of interest. The specimens for OM and SEM observations were mechanically polished and then etching by 8% Nital. Thin foils for TEM observation were prepared by twin-jet polishing in a mixture of 10% perchloric acid and 90% alcohol $\tt a t - 3 0 ^ { \circ }$ C at an applied potential of 30 V. The volume fraction of phases was determined from XRD data using direct comparison method [31, 32]. The grain sizes of specimens were measured from SEM micrographs with the aid of the ImageJ software.

Specimens with a gage length of 50 mm and a gage width of 12.5 mm for tensile test were machined from the annealed and water quenched plate. Room temperature tensile test were performed by a universal testing machine (CMT 4105) at the initial strain rate of $3 \times 1 0 ^ { - 4 } \varsigma ^ { - 1 }$ , and the tensile axis was parallel to the rolling direction.

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Equilibrium phase diagram

The phase diagram of the Fe–10Mn–10Al–0.7C steel was calculated by Thermo-Calc software with database TCFE7, and fractions of the equilibrium phases (ferrite (α), austenite (γ) and κ-carbide (κ)) when the temperature is over $5 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ are shown in Fig. 2. According to Fig. 2, ferrite is expected to form first during solidification, and then austenite forms by peritectic reaction [33]. After solidification, the phase fraction of ferrite will decrease and that of austenite will increase with the decrease of temperature until the temperature reaches around $1 0 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ where the fraction of ferrite is lowest. When the temperature is lower than about $1 0 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ the fraction of ferrite decreases with the decrease of temperature. However, the phase fractions do not change much until temperature becomes lower than $8 2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , where κ-carbide appears by eutectoid reaction $\gamma  \alpha + \kappa .$ Apparently, a part of body-centered cubic (BCC) ferrite will always exist without transforming to facecentered (FCC) austenite, and this part of ferrite is often referred to as δ-ferrite [34,35]. The δ-ferrite usually exhibits coarse grains in the microstructure [13,34]. In the present study, δ-ferrite is not separately treated with α-ferrite. There is difference in the phase fractions and the eutectoid temperature for κ-carbide between the result calculated by Thermo-Calc and that measured from the steel, which will discussed later. The differences may come from that, the steel in the study is not in equilibrium state, and that the calculated fractions are based on the atomic fraction, while the measured fraction from XRD results is based on the volume fraction [34].

# 3.2. Microstructure evolution

The microstructure of hot rolled specimen (marked as “HR”) is shown in Fig. 3. Fig. 3(a) and (b) is OM micrographs of RD–ND (rolling direction–normal direction) plane, RD–TD (rolling direction–transverse direction) plane, respectively. As shown in Fig. 3(a), typical banded structure is presented, and coarse ferrite formed during the early stage of solidification steel is obviously observed. It is easy to see that the microstructure is very inhomogeneous in Fig. 3(b), and, large α phase area (the continuous area that mainly contains α phase, while there are also some γ phase or κ-carbide dispersed) and γ phase area (the continuous area that mainly contains γ phase, while there are also some α phase or κ-carbide dispersed) can be used to describe the microstructure. SEM micrograph in Fig. 3(c) clearly shows the κ-carbide along ferrite/austenite (α/γ) and ferrite/ferrite (α/α) boundaries. EDS is used to confirm the α phase, γ phase, and κ-carbide, since α phase is Al-rich, γ phase is Mn-rich and C-rich, while κ-carbide is Mn-rich and Al-rich [15,18,34].

XRD results of hot rolled and annealed specimens are shown in Fig. 4. The peaks of κ-carbide are seen in specimen annealed at $7 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ 800 °C and $8 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ and no obvious peaks of γ phase can be seen in the specimen annealed at $7 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . In the hot rolled specimen, peaks of κ- carbide are not seen, probably due to the low volume fraction of κ- carbide, or the coherency of κ-carbide and α matrix [36]. Lattice parameters can be calculated from the XRD profiles, and results show that the lattice parameter of α phase changes in the range 2.89–2.91 Å, the lattice parameter of γ phase changes in the range 3.65–3.67 Å, and the lattice parameter of κ-carbide changes in the range 3.76–3.79 Å. The lattices of α phase and γ phase have been slightly expanded by Mn, Al and C atoms, and being larger than the lattices of α phase and γ phase in Fe–3.5Mn–5.8Al–0.35C, respectively [34]. It is said that the lattice of κ-carbide is mainly governed by the interstitial element, C, while the increment of Al would also bring expansion to the κ lattice and because Mn has a larger atomic radius than Fe, the substitute of Fe by Mn in κ- carbide would also cause slight expansion in the κ-carbide lattice [15, 34]. The lattice expansion of α phase and γ phase, which are the main phase constituents in the steel, will contribute to the low density of the steel.

A $t 9 0 0 ^ { \circ } C ,$ the γ phase fraction is about 60%. After the dissolution of κ-carbide, the volume fraction of γ phase first increase slightly and then decrease slightly with the increment of annealing temperature, and the fraction of γ phase reaches largest around the annealing temperature of $1 0 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ The volume fraction of γ phase changes only in the small range of 57% to 63% when the annealing temperature is between $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and $1 2 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . The boundary κ- carbide that was seen in Fig. 10 cannot be detected by XRD, probably due to that the fraction of boundary κ-carbide is too small. In addition, TEM analysis (not shown here) indicates that no obvious superlattice spots can be seen in the SADPs of α phase or γ phase in all the annealed specimens, and although some weak superlattice spots can be seen in the SADPs of some grains; they are all too weak to obtain the DF images. So ordering of atoms in α phase or γ phase is not expected during annealing at the temperature range $7 0 0 { - } 1 2 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h, while ordering may occur during quenching, whereas the time is too short for the ordering to well develop.

# 4.1. Microstructure evolution during annealing at various temperatures

The γ phase has almost totally decomposed to α phase and κ- carbide by eutectoid reaction $\gamma \to \alpha + \kappa$ after annealing at $7 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h, and the N–W orientation relationship is found mainly between some lamellar κ-carbide and the α matrix (Fig. 7). When annealed at $8 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ or $8 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ the γ phase volume fraction is about 29% and 39%, respectively. The κ-carbide and α phase fraction decreases, while the γ phase fraction increases with annealing temperature rising from 700 $^ \circ \mathsf { C }$ to $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ The N–W orientation relationship has been hardly found between κ-carbide and α matrix when annealing temperature reaches $8 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \left( \mathrm { F i g . } 8 \right)$ . In the annealing temperature range from 700 to 850 ${ } ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } ,$ , the γ phase becomes more stable with the increase of temperature, so the γ phase fraction increases with the increase of temperature. The morphology of κ-carbide tends to be larger and closer to globular shape (Fig. 6(a) through (c)), which originates from the higher diffusion speed of elements and growth speed of κ-carbide at higher temperatures. For the sake of lowering the strain energy, the growth of κ- carbide favors to be along the plane of boundary between κ-carbide and α matrix with orientation relationship at first, and with the grow up of κ-carbide, the lattice coherency and orientation relationship will be destroyed. This is why the orientation relationship is easy to found between the lamellar κ and α matrix in the specimen annealed at 700 ${ } ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } } ,$ and becomes harder to be found or has disappeared when the annealing temperature rises from $7 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to $8 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ .

Among α phase, γ phase and κ-carbide in the same specimen, the Mn and Al contents in κ-carbide are the highest. Compared with in α phase, Mn content is higher, while Al content is lower in the γ phase (Fig. 13). There are competitions for Mn and C between neighboring α phase and κ-carbide, and for Al between neighboring γ phase and κ- carbide. With the increase of annealing temperature, the κ-carbide distributed at or near the boundary of α phase and γ phase dissolves first and the κ-carbide along $\alpha / \alpha$ boundaries of large grains dissolves last. It indicates that the dissolution temperature of κ-carbide along α/α boundary is higher than that along $\alpha / \gamma$ boundary in the present steel, which is relatively lean in Mn content and rich in Al content. This may mainly come from that the Al content in the κ-carbide at α/α boundary is slightly higher than that at $\alpha / \gamma$ boundary due to the nonuniform of the microstructure, and it has been pointed out that the effect of Al on raising the solvus of κ-carbide is larger in austenitic Fe–Mn–Al–C steels [42].

# 4.2. Correlation between tensile properties and microstructure

The tensile strength of γ phase is usually higher than that of α phase, and a high content of α phase is often detrimental to mechanical properties $\left[ 1 7 , 4 3 , 4 4 \right]$ . The grain sizes and morphologies of phases have a great effect on the tensile strength of annealed steels [34,45]. The ultimate tensile strength and yield strength of the steel in the present study has a trend of decrease with the increment of annealing temperature, and the ultimate elongation first increase with the increment of annealing temperature when temperature is lower than 900 °C and then decrease when annealing temperature is over $9 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Fig. 16), and the trend of the product of strength and ductility of the annealed specimens against temperature resembles the trend of ultimate elongation. The tensile properties are closely related with the microstructure of the annealed specimens.

Yield phenomenon, which is usually seen in the ferrite steels [46], only appears in the specimen annealed at $7 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ In the specimen annealed at $7 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , the γ phase has almost totally transformed into α phase and κ-carbide, and the microstructure is composed of large α grains, continuous κ-carbide along α grain boundaries, and areas containing lamellar κ-carbide within γ matrix (Fig. 6(a)). Although the lamellar κ-carbide in the α matrix may contribute to the strength and ductility by secondary phase strengthening and by preventing the prolongation of microcracks that initiated from α/κ interface where strain would concentrate, the continuous distributed κ-carbide along the grain boundaries of large α grains are readily cracked and preferred to crack by cleavage fracture during tensile deformation [47]. So the ultimate strength of the specimen annealed at $7 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ is relatively high and the ultimate elongation is low (Fig. 16). When annealing temperature is $8 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ the microstructure is composed of α phase, γ phase and κ-carbide (Fig. 6(b)). Compared with the specimen annealed at $7 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Fig. 6(a)), the fraction of κ-carbide has decreased, and the size of κ- carbide has coarsened, which indicates that the strengthening and ductility from κ-carbide will be weakened in the specimen annealed at 800 ${ } ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } .$ The κ-carbide still continuously distributed along the large α grains will decrease the ductility. However, the γ phase in the microstructure can increase the ductility. Additionally, at higher annealing temperature, the elimination of dislocations inherited from the hot rolling process is better developed, which normally will decrease the strength and increase the ductility. Thus, the ultimate strength of the specimen annealed at $8 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ decreases and the ultimate elongation increases slightly (Fig. 16). For the specimen annealed at $8 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , the quick increase of the ductility may mainly attributed to the increase of γ phase fraction. When the annealing temperature reaches $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ,$ κ- carbide dissolves during annealing, and there is only some boundary κ-carbide of very small size formed during quenching and distributed along some large α grains (Figs. 6(d) and $1 0 ( \mathsf { a } ) ,$ . The ultimate elongation is 36.6%, and the ductility of the specimen is the best of all the annealed specimens. The ductility of the specimen annealed at $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ would be better if no boundary κ-carbide is formed during quenching. The work hardening rate in the early stage is higher in the specimen annealed at 850 °C and decreases quickly to be lower than the specimen annealed at $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the later stage (Fig. 15(b)). The difference in the deformation behavior between α phase and γ phase can be seen from the SEM fractograph of the specimen annealed at $8 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (Fig. 17(a) and (b)) and $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } C$ (Fig. 17(c) and (d)). From the size of the fracture structure, the ductile fracture area is thought to be mainly caused by γ phase, while the quasi-fracture area is thought to be mainly caused by α phase.

When the annealing temperature is over $9 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } C ,$ the ultimate strength and ultimate tensile strength continuously decrease with the increase of annealing temperature (Fig. 16(a)). The phase fractions of α phase and γ phase change only a little in a narrow range (Fig. 14), which should not be the main reason to cause such a large change in the ultimate strength and ductility. Although the boundary κ-carbide formed during quenching is believed to be detrimental to ductility, it exists in all the specimen annealed at or over $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } .$ The average grain size changes of α phase and γ phase shown in Fig. 9 are believed to be the main reason for the change of tensile properties when annealing temperature is over $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ According to Hall–Petch relation [48,49], the strength of the steel will stronger with smaller grain size. For the annealing temperatures of $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and $9 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , although the $\alpha _ { 1 }$ that mainly distributed among γ grains is even smaller in size than that in the specimen annealed at $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ the $\alpha _ { 2 }$ that mainly distributed with banded shape and the γ grains in the specimen annealed at $9 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ are much larger in size, and as a result, the ultimate tensile strength and ultimate elongation of specimen annealed at $9 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ are lower than those of the specimen annealed at $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . When the annealing temperature is over $9 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ the grain size of both α phase and γ phase increases with the increase of temperature, and naturally, the yield strength, ultimate tensile strength and ultimate elongation will decrease with the increase of temperature. The SEM fractograph (Fig. 17(e) and(f)) shows that the ductile fracture area has already lessened and becomes not apparent in the specimen annealed at $1 0 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ Additionally, in the specimen annealed at 1200 °C (Figs. 6(h) and $1 0 ( { \mathsf { d } } ) )$ , the needle-like or plate-like particles in some large α grains will undoubtedly promote cracking.

It should be noted that the κ-carbide in the Fe–18Mn–10Al–1.2C, Fe–18Mn–10Al-–0.8C and Fe– 12Mn–8.0Al-0.8C is dispersed in the austenite matrix, which usually promotes planar glide during deformation and contributes to strength. The ultimate strength and ultimate elongation of the present steel are not as good as the other listed steels. In Table 2, it can be easily noticed that the ultimate elongations of the steels containing α phase (Fe– 20Mn–9Al–0.6C, Fe–18Mn–10Al–0.8C and Fe–12Mn–8.0Al–0.8C) are lower than those of the steels containing no α phase (Fe–28Mn–10Al– 1.0C and Fe–18Mn–10Al–1.2C). The ultimate elongation is lowest among the steels in Table 2. Considering the highest α phase fraction in the present steel among the steels, the boundary κ-carbide between some large α grains, and the inhomogeneous microstructure, it is understandable why the ultimate elongation of the present steel is lowest among the steels. There is nearly no κ-carbide dispersed in the γ matrix of the present steel, and the ultimate strength of the present steel is lower than that of Fe–18Mn–10Al–0.8C and Fe–12Mn–8.0Al–0.8C. Since Mn content in the present steel is designed to be low, the C content may need to be increased to improve the mechanical properties. According to the comparison between Fe–18Mn–10Al–1.2C and Fe– 18Mn–10Al–0.8C investigated by Ding et al., the loss of strength by the reduction of Mn content may be compensated by addition of C content. In addition, tensile properties of the present steel are affected more easily by heat treatment temperature than Fe–20Mn–9Al–0.6C [25]. Anyhow, the investigation of the tensile properties and microstructure of the present steel, Fe–10Mn–10Al–0.7C, is important for the future designs and investigations of Mn-lean Fe–Mn–Al–C steels.

# 5. Conclusions

In the present study, a Mn-lean Fe–Mn–Al–C low-density steel with the chemical composition Fe–10Mn–10Al–0.7C (wt.%) is annealed at the temperature range 700 °C to 1200 °C for 1 h. The microstructure evolution during annealing and the relationship between tensile properties and microstructure of the annealed specimens are investigated.

(1) The density of the steel is 6.80 g/cm3 , about 13% lower than that of pure iron. The largest product of strength and ductility, 31 GPa%, is achieved at the annealing temperature of $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ,$ when the ultimate strength, yield strength, and ultimate elongation are 855 MPa, 651 MPa and 36.6%, respectively.
(2) The phases presented in the annealed steel mainly include ferrite (α), austenite (γ) and κ-carbide (κ). The dissolution temperature of κ-carbide is between 850 °C and 900 °C. When annealed at 700 ${ } ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } } ,$ γ phase transforms to κ-carbide with lamellar morphology and α phase by eutectoid reaction. With the increase of temperature, the γ phase fraction first increases, and reaches largest, about 63% at around 1000 °C, and then decreases slightly. The boundary κ-carbide formed during quenching exists in all the specimens annealed at the range 900 °C to $1 2 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ Generally, the α grains and γ grains grow up quickly when the annealing temperature is over 900 °C.
(3) The ultimate strength of the annealed specimens has a decreasing trend with the increase of temperature, while the ultimate elongation first increases until anneal temperature reaches 900 ${ } ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C } ,$ and then decreases with the continued increase of temperature. The change of ultimate strength is sharp, so the window for controlling the tensile properties is relatively small.
(4) At the annealing temperature between $7 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 900 °C, the ductility of the specimens is mainly controlled by the fraction of γ phase. Although the κ-carbide can contribute to the strength, microcracks preferred to initiate around κ-carbide, which is detrimental to the tensile properties. At the annealing temperature between $9 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and 1200 °C, since the change of phase fractions is very moderate, the grain sizes, instead of phase fraction

change, have a significant effect on the tensile properties. The ultimate strength and ultimate elongation both decrease with the increase of grain sizes when increasing the annealing temperature.
