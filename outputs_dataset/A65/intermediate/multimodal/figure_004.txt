{"image_type": "microscopy_optical", "description": "This figure presents five optical micrographs (a-e) illustrating the microstructural characteristics of hot-rolled Fe-Al lightweight steel alloys produced via different melting routes. The images compare air induction melting (AIM), air induction melting with flux cover (AIMFC), and vacuum induction melting (VIM) for alloys containing 7 wt.% and 9 wt.% aluminum. Subfigure (a) shows the coarse-grained microstructure of AIM-processed Fe-7Al, while (b) displays the AIMFC Fe-7Al alloy with similar equiaxed grain morphology. Subfigure (c) reveals the AIMFC Fe-9Al alloy exhibiting coarse grains with visible microcracks attributed to residual hydrogen embrittlement and poor hot workability. Subfigure (d) presents the VIM Fe-7Al alloy with comparable coarse grain structure, and (e) shows the VIM Fe-9Al alloy with coarse equiaxed grains but free of microcracks, indicating excellent hot workability. All alloys display annealed microstructures with grain sizes ranging from approximately 186 μm to 228 μm. The key finding demonstrates that AIMFC produces grain sizes comparable to VIM, validating the flux-cover technique as a cost-effective alternative for Fe-Al alloys, though high aluminum content (9 wt.%) requires stringent hydrogen control to prevent thermomechanical processing defects.", "confidence": "high"}
