# Material System
- **Alloy Designations**: Fe-7Al, Fe-9Al (wt.%) disordered bcc solid-solution alloys; Fe-7Al-C alloys with nominal carbon additions of 0.02, 0.15, 0.35, 0.5, and 1.0 wt.% (actual measured carbon for base alloy: 0.012 wt.% C).
- **Phases Present**: Disordered bcc α-Fe(Al) solid solution; Fe₃Al ordered phase (detected via XRD in low-carbon variants); Fe₃AlC₀.₅ perovskite-type carbide precipitate (forms at carbon contents ≥0.15 wt.%).
- **Specimen Forms**: Ingots (80 mm diameter), hot-forged, hot-rolled to 12 mm plate, cold-rolled sheets/foils, annealed condition.

# Processing Route and Variables
- **Melting Techniques Compared**:
  - Air Induction Melting (AIM) — led to gas porosity (hydrogen) in high-Al melts; porosity increased with Al content, rendering Fe-9Al ingots entirely unsound.
  - Vacuum Induction Melting (VIM) — produced sound, porosity-free ingots for both Fe-7Al and Fe-9Al by eliminating atmospheric water vapor interaction.
  - **Air Induction Melting with Flux Cover (AIMFC)** — the developed cost-effective alternative: an alumina-lined crucible (MgO/Al₂O₃ lining, 150 mm diameter, 300 mm total height) with a 2–4 mm thick molten slag layer (Al₂O₃-CaO-MgO-CaF₂ system) isolating the melt (240 mm depth) from atmosphere. Iron melted first, preheated flux added, aluminum pieces plunged into bath held at 1900 K for 2 min; pouring via tundish into split cast iron molds (80 mm diameter). Aluminum recovery: 95–98%.
  - Crucible rationale: Thermodynamic data (Figure 2) show Al can reduce MgO above ≈1600°C (1873 K operating temperature), making MgO crucibles unsuitable; alumina lining or Al₂O₃-based flux avoids magnesium contamination.
- **Thermomechanical Processing (applicable to all routes)**: Hot forging → hot rolling → annealing → cold rolling (to sheet/foil). Fe-7Al (AIMFC and VIM) exhibited excellent cold workability; Fe-9Al (AIMFC) exhibited microcracking during hot working due to residual hydrogen embrittlement, while Fe-9Al (VIM) processed without such defects.
- **Compositional Variables**: Carbon content in Fe-7Al systematically varied from 0.02 (actual 0.012) to 1.0 wt.% C.

# Microstructure and Phase Evolution
- **Base Fe-7Al (AIM, AIMFC, VIM)**: Single-phase coarse equiaxed α-Fe(Al) grains. Optical micrographs of hot-rolled plates show comparable grain sizes across all three melting routes (grains ranging approximately 186–228 μm).
- **Fe-9Al (VIM)**: Coarse equiaxed α-Fe(Al) grains free of microcracks. Fe-9Al (AIMFC) shows similar coarse grains but with visible microcracks attributed to hydrogen embrittlement and poor hot workability (Figure 4c).
- **Effect of Carbon (Fe-7Al-C, AIM processed, hot-rolled)**:
  - 0.012 wt.% C: Single-phase α-Fe(Al) solid solution; no precipitates visible; XRD shows only Fe₃Al-type reflections indexed as (111), (220), (400), (422), (440) (Figure 6a).
  - 0.15 wt.% C: Initial Fe₃AlC₀.₅ precipitation appears, predominantly at grain boundaries. XRD confirms additional perovskite carbide peaks at ~44–45° (200) and ~82–83° (311) coexisting with matrix peaks (Figure 6b).
  - 0.35–1.0 wt.% C: Progressive increase in precipitate volume fraction. At 0.35 wt.% C, precipitates appear both at grain boundaries and within grains. At 0.5 wt.% C, a banded/lamellar alignment of precipitates develops. At 1.0 wt.% C, a high volume fraction of Fe₃AlC₀.₅ perovskite precipitates is distributed throughout the matrix (Figure 5b–e). XRD confirms the same Fe₃AlC₀.₅ peaks for all alloys 0.35–1.0 wt.% C.

# Processing-Structure-Property Chain
- **AIMFC vs. VIM (Fe-7Al)**: The AIMFC process produces sound, porosity-free ingots with coarse equiaxed grain structure essentially identical to VIM material. Subsequent cold-rolling to thin sheets and foils is successful (Figure 3), and room-temperature tensile properties of AIMFC Fe-7Al are comparable to those of VIM Fe-7Al. This validates AIMFC as a cost-effective substitute for VIM in Fe-7Al lightweight steel.
- **Al content (7 vs. 9 wt.%) under AIMFC**: Fe-7Al AIMFC exhibits good hot workability and room-temperature ductility. Fe-9Al AIMFC exhibits microcracking during hot working and poor hot workability due to residual hydrogen; VIM is required to obtain sound Fe-9Al product. This demonstrates that higher aluminum content exacerbates hydrogen susceptibility during air melting, even with flux cover.
- **Carbon content (Fe-7Al-C)**: Increasing carbon from 0.012 to 1.0 wt.% causes a transition from a single-phase disordered bcc solid solution to a two-phase structure with increasing volume fraction of Fe₃AlC₀.₅ perovskite precipitates. This microstructural evolution directly increases strength (solid-solution + precipitation hardening) while decreasing tensile ductility.

# Mechanistic Interpretation
- **Hydrogen porosity in AIM**: Aluminum in the melt reacts with atmospheric water vapor, introducing hydrogen into the molten metal; upon solidification, hydrogen solubility drops sharply, forming gas porosity. The flux cover acts as a physical barrier to H₂O, suppressing hydrogen pickup.
- **Environmental embrittlement of Fe-9Al**: Al-rich Fe-Al alloys are susceptible to hydrogen embrittlement; residual hydrogen in AIMFC Fe-9Al causes cracking during thermomechanical processing, whereas VIM eliminates this source of hydrogen.
- **Strengthening by carbon**: Carbon in solid solution provides solid-solution strengthening for the α-Fe(Al) matrix. Above the solubility limit (~0.15 wt.% C), carbon forms hard perovskite-type Fe₃AlC₀.₅ precipitates, introducing precipitation hardening. The progressive increase in precipitate volume fraction with carbon content explains the monotonic increase in strength and hardness and the corresponding loss of ductility.

# Key Quantitative Findings
- **Aluminum recovery in AIMFC**: 95–98%.
- **Melt superheat**: Bath held at 1900 K for 2 min after Al addition.
- **Grain size range (hot-rolled Fe-7Al/Fe-9Al)**: Approximately 186–228 μm across AIM, AIMFC, VIM routes (coarse equiaxed grains).
- **Phase identification**: Fe₃Al (disordered solid solution) at low C; Fe₃AlC₀.₅ perovskite-type carbide detected at C ≥ 0.15 wt.% via additional XRD reflections at 2θ ≈ 44–45° and 82–83° (Cu-Kα).
- **Tensile property trend (Fe-7Al-C)**: Increasing carbon content from 0.012 to 1.0 wt.% enhances ultimate tensile strength (UTS) and yield strength (YS) while reducing tensile elongation; exact values are noted in referenced tensile tables within the paper (specific numerical values not provided in visual_evidence or core_facts).

# Visual Evidence
- **Figure 1 (AIMFC schematic)**: Documents the experimental setup of the AIMFC process, showing the alumina-lined crucible, molten slag layer (2–4 mm) acting as a diffusion barrier, and pouring configuration into split cast iron molds. Supports the claim that flux cover prevents atmospheric water vapor interaction and hydrogen pickup.
- **Figure 2 (Thermodynamic chart)**: Plots standard free energy of formation (ΔF°) vs. temperature for MgO and Al₂O₃. Demonstrates that Al₂O₃ is more stable than MgO above ~1600°C, meaning aluminum in Fe-Al melts can reduce MgO crucible linings at processing temperatures (~1873 K). Justifies the use of Al₂O₃-based linings/fluxes to avoid magnesium contamination.
- **Figure 3 (Cold-rolled product photographs)**: Shows flat plates/sheets and cylindrical foil specimens of AIMFC-processed Fe-7Al. Provides macroscopic evidence that this cost-effective route yields material with sufficient ductility for cold rolling into thin gauges, comparable to VIM material.
- **Figure 4 (Optical micrographs — melting route comparison)**: Compares hot-rolled microstructures of Fe-7Al and Fe-9Al via AIM, AIMFC, and VIM. Shows coarse equiaxed grains for all sound variants; specifically reveals (c) microcracks in AIMFC Fe-9Al vs. (e) crack-free VIM Fe-9Al, linking melting route and hydrogen control to hot workability.
- **Figure 5 (Optical micrographs — carbon effect)**: Shows microstructural evolution in hot-rolled AIM Fe-7Al with C from 0.012 to 1.0 wt.%. Documents progressive precipitate formation: none at 0.012% C; grain-boundary precipitates at 0.15%; intragranular precipitates at 0.35%; banded structure at 0.5%; high volume fraction perovskite at 1.0%. Directly links increasing carbon to increased Fe₃AlC₀.₅ precipitation.
- **Figure 6 (XRD patterns)**: Confirms phase constitution. Pattern (a) for Fe-7Al-0.012C shows only Fe₃Al peaks (single-phase solid solution). Pattern (b) for Fe-7Al-0.15C shows additional Fe₃AlC₀.₅ perovskite carbide peaks; caption states these peaks persist for all 0.35–1.0 wt.% C alloys. Links carbon content to precipitation of strengthening phase.
