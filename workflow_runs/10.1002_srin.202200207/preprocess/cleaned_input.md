# Optimization of Process Conditions for Ultralightweight Steel with More Than 13 wt% of Al and 5 wt% of Cr

Kyeong-Won Kim, Sung-Dae Kim, Seong-Jun Park, Hyungkwon Park, Joonoh Moon, Hyun-UK Hong, and Chang-Hoon Lee*

An ultralightweight Fe–30Mn–13.2Al–1.6C–5Cr steel, which contains more than 13 wt% of Al and thereby reduces the density by 20%, is developed. The ultralightweight steel, which is very brittle due to high Al content, is fabricated by optimizing hot rolling and heat treatment conditions. Hot rolling is conducted after soaking at the temperature range of 1100–1200 °C for 2 h. The ultralightweight steel is hot-rolled successfully after soaking at 1100 °C, whereas specimens soaked at 1150 and 1200 °C are intergranularly cracked after hot rolling, resulting from coarse grain and a large fraction and size of ferrite, which is transformed to ordered ${ \mathsf { D O } } _ { 3 }$ phase during cooling, at the grain boundaries. In homogenization heat treatment, water quenching and air cooling are performed, respectively, after holding at $1 0 5 0 ^ { \circ } C$ for 2 h. The air-cooled steel has inferior tensile property due to the formation of brittle ordered ${ \mathsf { D O } } _ { 3 }$ phase at grain boundaries. Meanwhile, the water-quenched steel shows an excellent tensile property, which is attributed to a uniform microstructure comprising austenite, fine κ-carbide in austenite, and a very small fraction of the ordered ${ \mathsf { D O } } _ { 3 }$ phase.

A variety of advanced high strength steels (AHSS) such as dual-phase (DP) steel, transformation induced plasticity (TRIP) steel, twinning-induced plasticity (TWIP) steel, and lightweight steel have been developed over a long period of time, and the weight of automobiles has been decreased by reducing the thickness or size of automobile parts using these AHSSs.[4–7] In particular, much attention on the lightweight steels to have a large amount of Al has been paid as a material that can significantly contribute to the weight reduction of vehicles due to high strength as well as low density.

Among lightweight steels to have various microstructures and properties depending on alloy compositions, many researches on austenitic Fe–Mn–Al–C

lightweight steels have been conducted due to their excellent tensile properties.[8–16] In the case of the austenitic Fe–Mn–Al–C steels to contain relatively high Al content, κ-carbide of (Fe, Mn)3AlC type, ferrite, and ordered phases such as B2 or DO3 at grain boundaries, which are brittle phases, can be easily formed, resulting in that it is hard to fabricate the lightweight steels containing more than 13 wt% of Al, due to fracture during casting and hot rolling.[17–31] Accordingly, it was hard to find previous articles showing success in fabricating ultralightweight steels containing more than 13 wt% of Al, and most of researches on lightweight steels are limited to 12 wt% or less of Al content.

In this study, it was aimed to manufacture ultralightweight Fe–Mn–Al–C steel containing more than 13 wt% of Al which can reduce 20% of density by suppressing the formation of brittle coarse κ-carbide, ferrite, and ordered phases at grain boundaries through the addition of Cr and optimization of hot rolling and heat treatment conditions.

In this study, it was possible to develop the ultralightweight Fe–30Mn–13.2Al–1.6C–5Cr steel, density of which is $6 . { \overset { \vartriangle } { 3 0 } } \ g \ c m ^ { \stackrel { \textstyle - } { - } 3 } ,$ , having uniform microstructure with austenite fine κ-carbide inside austenite very small fraction of ordered $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 }$ phase and excellent tensile property with 1048 MPa of tensile strength

# 4. Experimental Section

An ultralightweight steel with Fe–30Mn–13.2Al–1.6C–5Cr (wt%) was designed. The Al content was increased to 13.2 wt% to reduce the density by 20%. The measured density of this ultralightweight steel is $6 . 3 0 \ g \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 3 }$ . To prevent the formation of ferrite due to the increase in Al which is ferrite stabilizing element, Mn and C, austenite stabilizing elements, were increased to 30 and 1.6 wt%, respectively. Cr (5 wt%) was added to the Fe–30Mn–13.2Al–1.6C steel to suppress κ-carbide formation due to the increase in Al and C. In the previous $\mathsf { s t u d y } , ^ { [ 2 8 ] }$ it was confirmed that the addition of a certain amount of Cr retards the formation of κ-carbide and thereby increased slightly fraction of austenite due to enrichment of C in austenite.

The alloy-designed ultralightweight steel was prepared in a vacuum induction melting furnace. The 5 kg ingots with 30 mm thickness were hot-rolled to plates with 7 mm thickness and heat-treated for homogenization. The ingots were soaked at three temperatures (1100, 1150, 1200 °C) for 2 h, followed by hot rolling at a temperature higher than $1 0 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ and air cooling. The hot-rolled plates were homogenized 1050 °C for 2 h, and then cooled by air cooling and water quenching to room temperature, respectively. Homogenization temperature, 1050 °C, was selected to dissolve κ-carbide as well as to minimize grain coarsen-$\mathrm { i n g . } ^ { [ 1 6 ] }$ Microstructures were identified by SEM (JSM-7001F, JEOL), EBSD

(NordlysNano EBSD detector and AZTEC software, Oxford), TEM (JEM-2100F, JEOL), and XRD (DMAX-2500, Rigaku). Specimens for SEM were mechanically polished and etched using a 40% nital solution (40 mL $\mathsf { H N O } _ { 3 } + \mathsf { l O O }$ mL ethyl alcohol). Specimens for EBSD were chemomechanically polished with colloidal silica without etching. XRD specimen was prepared as block with 15 mm 12 mm 2 mm after mechanical polishing. XRD measurement was performed with Cu Kα radiation, 40 kV and 200 mA. The diffraction patterns were obtained in step scan mode, $2 \theta = 2 0 ^ { \circ } - 8 0 ^ { \circ }$ with a step of $\dot { 0 . 0 2 ^ { \circ } }$ and 2 s step1 . TEM specimens were prepared as discs with 3 mm in diameter and mechanically polished to 80 μm in thickness and then electrochemically polished in a twin-jet polishing apparatus using a solution (10% perchloric acid þ 90% methyl alcohol). Tensile properties were evaluated in accordance of ASTM E8. All tensile tests were conducted with subsize tensile specimens (2 mm in thickness, 6 mm in width, 25 mm in gauge length, and 100 mm in total length) at a cross head speed of 2 mm min1 at room temperature using universal tensile test machine (INSTRON 5882, INSTRON).
