# 1. Introduction

Fe–Mn–Al–C steels with reduced specific strength (strength/ weight ratio) have attracted significant attention in recent years, driven by the demand for reduction in the energy consumption in the automobile industry.[1–5] Several studies have demonstrated that the presence of Al in Fe–Mn–Al–C steels may modify the deformation mechanisms and alter the mechanical properties of the materials.[6–8]

Among the low density steels, high manganese triplex steels characterized by dispersion of nano-sized κ-carbides in the austenite matrix and ferrite are characterized by superior strength because of effective solid solution and precipitation hardening. Yield strength of 730 MPa, ultimate tensile strength of 1000 MPa, and total elongation of up to 50% was observed in a triplex Fe–28Mn–12Al–1C steel.[1] More recently, it was noted that a decrease in austenite grain size led to higher strength, carbon-content in austenite had significant influence on tensile properties, and ferrite fraction had a positive effect on strength when it’s content was less than 5% in high Mn triplex low density steels.[2]

High yield strength is of particular relevance in the automotive sector for anti-intrusion assembly.[9] Triplex low Mn–Al–C steels with different concentrations of Al were studied and the mechanism associated with the increase in yield strength in Fe–Mn–Al–C steels is elucidated and quantified.

# 2. Experimental Section

The four experimental steels contained Fe-( 26%)Mn-( 1%)C in wt%. Al- contents were 2.59, 5.84, 9.92, and 11.58%, are referred as 3Al, 6Al, 10Al, and 12Al, respectively. The steels were prepared by induction melting, heated at 1150 C and subsequently rolled at the initial rolling temperature of 1050 C to strips of 3 mm thick with a total reduction of 85%. Tensile specimens prepared from the strips were solution-treated at 1100 C for 1 h and quenched in water. Tensile tests were carried out at room temperature using a universal testing machine (SANSCMT5000) at an initial strain rate of 103 s1 . Microhardness tests were performed using a FM-700 tester with a weight of 5 kg. Specimens for optical microscopy were prepared via standard metallography procedure and etched with a solution consisting of 1 ml hydrochloric acid, 100 ml methanol, and 4 g picric acid. Thin foils for TEM were prepared by twin-jet electropolishing using 30% nitric acid in methanol at 30 C.

Al is a ferrite stabilizer such that the ferrite appears when the Al-content reaches a certain amount in the Fe–Mn–Al–C alloy system. Furthermore, the excessive amount of Al can lead to the formation of κ carbides.[10] The increase in Al-content affects the mechanical properties of Fe–Mn–Al–C steels in several ways: 1) solid solution hardening, 2) grain boundary strengthening, 3) precipitation hardening associated with the formation of fine κ precipitates, and 4) strengthening from fine ferrite grains. To quantitatively evaluate the effect of different strengthening mechanisms, the aforementioned aspects were individually considered.

The effect of solid solution hardening can be expressed by the following equation:

$$
\sigma_ {s} = \sigma_ {s 0} + k _ {c} c ^ {m} \tag {1}
$$

where $\sigma _ { s }$ is the yield strength corresponding to solid solution, $\sigma _ { s 0 }$ is the yield strength of pure austenite, c represents the Al-content in the solid solution in austenite, and $k _ { \mathrm { c } }$ and m are constants. Generally, m is taken as 1 when the solid solution hardening in a material is not significant.

Considering that austenite grain size was similar for 3Al and 6Al steels, the increase in yield strength of 6Al steel can be entirely related to the solid solution hardening effect. The coefficient, $k _ { c }$ is defined as follows:

$$
k _ {c} = \frac {\sigma (6 \mathrm{Al}) - \sigma (3 \mathrm{Al})}{C (6 \mathrm{Al}) - C (3 \mathrm{Al})} = \frac {3 7 8 - 3 2 1}{5 . 8 4 - 2 . 5 9} = 1 7. 5 4 \mathrm{MPa/wt\%Al}
$$

Thus, the value of $\sigma _ { s 0 }$ calculated was 275.57 MPa.

The yield strength of 10Al steel was 470 MPa, whereas the solid solution hardening contribution in 10Al steel was calculated to be 174 MPa and the total value of 449.57 MPa was obtained using Equation 1. Given that the grain size of 10Al steel was similar to 3Al and 6Al steels, the difference between the measured and calculated yield strength of 20.43 MPa, was because of limited precipitates in 10Al steel.

In reality, the assessment of different contribution to strengthening mechanisms here is only applicable to yield strength of materials. It was noted that the strain hardening behavior is quite different for the investigated steels.[5] Both 3Al and 6Al steels exhibited obvious strain hardening, which may be attributed to TWIP (twinning-induced plasticity) effect,[18] especially in the case of former. With increase in Al concentration, the stacking fault energy increases and it is difficult for dissociation of dislocations to occur. Therefore, TWIP effect is unlikely to be the deformation mechanism. Instead, the formation of microbands[6] or refinement of dynamic slip bands[19] dominates. Gutirttez-Urrutia and Raabe[7] investigated the influence of Al-content and precipitation on the mechanical behavior of austenitic Fe–30.5Mn–2.1/ 8Al–1.2C steels and associated the strain hardening behavior of the alloy to precipitation of shearable nano-sized carbides and their role in the development of planar dislocation substructures.[20] When the Al concentration increases to a certain amount, a large fraction of carbides form and “glide plane softening”appears.[21] Thus, no remarkable strain hardening in 12Al steel occurs.

# 4. Conclusions

In summary, the role of Al is not only that of an alloying element to reduce the density of the alloy, but also facilitates the microstructural design to obtain the desired mechanical properties. Additionally, alloying with Al up to 12 wt% in steel induces effective grain refinement, triplex microstructure, and combination of solid solution strengthening with consequent increase in the yield strength of 12Al steel. Based on the quantitative assessment of different strengthening mechanisms, the precipitation strengthening effect of nanosized precipitates is of utmost significance, being 1.78 times of the solution hardening effect.
