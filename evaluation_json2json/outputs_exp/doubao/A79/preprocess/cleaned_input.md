# Evaluation of the density of steels

# Ulrich Bohnenkamp and Rolf Sandström

Data on physical properties of steels have been collected from the open literature and put into a database. The influence of composition on the density of steels has been analyzed. An overview over former studies is given. The steels have been investigated by regression analysis in two groups, i) ferritic and low alloy steels, and ii) austenitic steels. For ferritic steels two models are provided. The first model is based on the assumption that all C is bound in cementite and other solutes are insoluble in cementite. The second model employs the result of a thermodynamic analysis where the amount of cementite and the solubilities in ferrite and cementite were determined with computational thermodynamics. The non-linear effect of Cr and Mn in cementite was computed and regression analysis of the effect of solutes on the density of ferrite was performed. For Ti-stabilized austenitic steels, the amount of TiC and the solubilities were assessed in a thermodynamic analysis. The effect of solutes on the density of austenite was studied by regression analysis. For estimations of the density of steels containing components that are not covered by the regression analysis, the regression coefficients can be supplemented with literature data or theoretically determined values. The results obtained by the present regression analysis are: Cu and Mo increase the density of ferritic steels, and C, Cr, Mn, S, Si, and V decrease it. TiC, C, Cr, Mn, N, Si, and Ti reduce the density of austenitic steels and Cu, Co, Mo, and Ni increase it.

Berechnung der Dichte von Stählen. Aus der Literatur wurden Daten über physikalische Eigenschaften von Stählen gesammelt und in eine Datenbank eingegeben. Der Einfluß der chemischen Zusammensetzung auf die Dichte von Stählen wurde untersucht. Es wird ein Überblick über frühere Studien gegeben. Die Stähle wurden mit Regressionsanalyse in zwei Gruppen untersucht, erstens ferritische und niedriglegierte Stähle und zweitens austenitische Stähle. Für ferritische Stähle standen zwei Modelle zur Verfügung: Das erste Modell basierte auf der Annahme daß der gesamte Kohlenstoff in Zementit gebunden sei und andere Elemente in Zementit unlöslich seien. Das andere Modell benutzt die Ergebnisse aus einer thermodynamischen Analyse. Der Anteil von Zementit und die Löslichkeiten in Ferrit und Zementit wurden mit der Methode der rechnergestützen Thermodynamik bestimmt. Der nichtlineare Einfluß von Cr und Mn in Zementit wurde berücksichtigt und eine Regressionsanalyse über den Einfluß von löslichen Elementen auf die Dichte von Ferrit durchgeführt. Für Ti-stabilisierte austenitische Stähle wurden der TiC-Gehalt und die Löslichkeiten in einer thermodynamischen Analyse berechnet. Der Effekt von löslichen Elementen auf die Dichte von Austenit wurde mit der Regressionsanalyse untersucht. Für eine Abschätzung der Dichte von Stählen, die nicht von der hier durchgeführten Regressionsanalyse erfaßte Komponenten enthalten, können die Regressionskoeffizienten mit Literaturdaten oder theoretisch bestimmten Werten ergänzt werden. Die Resultate dieser Regressionsalaysen lauten: Cu und Mo erhöhen die Dichte von ferritischen Stählen, wohingegen die Elemente C, Cr, Mn, S, Si und V sie verringern. TiC, C, Cr, Mn, N, Si und Ti vermindern die Dichte von austenitischen Stählen und Cu, Co, Mo und Ni vergrößern sie.

As a basis for an attempt in materials design, physical property data of steels have been investigated. These properties are basic knowledge for such an attempt. In the present paper the density of steels is covered.

The density of steels as a function of composition and microstructure is needed for example in attempts to explain variations in the elastic modulus due to the influence of the lattice parameter. The small variations of the density of ferritic steels, about $\pm1\%$ , become important in theories of solid solution strengthening. For the determination of the porosity of sintered steels by density-measurements the pore-free density often is not known and has to be estimated. Computations on instationary thermal problems are sensitive to the value of the density used. The density changes together with composition and microstructure along a path through a weldment, so exact values can not be determined experimentally.

The purpose of this paper is to give an overview over existing results and to present models for the prediction of the density of steels.

# Former studies

The effect of solid solutes in bcc iron on the density $\rho$ and the lattice parameter $a$ of binary alloys has been studied. Gale found that P decreases $a$ linearly with increasing concentration [1]. Hornbogen confirmed this result [2]. Takeuchi et al. found that Be and Si decrease $a$ whereas Al, Ti, V, Ge, and Mo raise $a$ [3]. Zwell et al. showed that Ni increases $a$ to an atomic mass fraction of about 15 %, then reduces $a$ [4]. Zwell et al. found that Mn decreases $\rho$ linearly with increasing concentration [5]. Zwell and Wriedt showed that Ti decreases $\rho$ [6]. Leslie gave an overview over the effect of dilute substitutional solid solutions in fcc iron on mechanical and physical properties, including $\rho$ [7]. Zwell et al. found a linear dependence of $a$ and $\rho$ of bcc iron on an atomic mass fraction of up to 10 % of several elements: Co has no effect on $\rho$ , Cr decreases $\rho$ , Re, Ir, Pt, Ru, and Rh increase $\rho$ [8]. For an overview of these results see table 1.

The following are studies on production-type steels, where weaker influences of most elements on $\rho$ remain covered by elements with strong effect: Fink et al. gave the temperature-dependence in scatterbands for twelve low alloy, high chromium and austenitic steels in the different steel-classes [9]. Richter covered several classes of steels with in total 52 iron-alloys [10]. The focus was on the temperature dependence. Some influences of composition and microstructure are visible in the graphs but not further investigated. Richter et al. compared their measurements of the nonlinear effect of Si-concentration on $a$ with a collection of data from literature. $\rho$ is shown to decrease linearly with an atomic mass fraction of Si up to $5\%$ ; for higher concentrations $\rho$ is influenced by the cooling rate after annealing [11]. Later, Richter graphically determined the most important factors for variations of $\rho$ with composition [12]. Cr and Al decrease the density of bcc iron. Si decreases the density of austenitic chrome-nickel-steels. For several melts of two heat-resistant low alloy steels, Richter stated that the influence of changing composition on the physical properties including $\rho$ dominates over changes in microstructure [13]. He studied the influence of composition separately for each steel quality. The regression results show the reduction in $\rho$ by addition of Cr and Si. In a later study on two austenitic steels Richter provided a similar amount of data but did not perform a regression analysis [14]. He showed that the density of nickel alloyed steels is increased with the Ni-content [15].

The effects of various types of oxides have been studied [17; 18]. These effects are significant for sintered steels. The density of cementite has been computed from lattice-parameter data [18; 19]. The effect of C on the density of martensite, respectively, $\alpha$ -iron+Fe3C has been studied in [18] and the result has been checked versus experimental data in [20]. The same regression coefficients for solutes (not C) in bcc and fcc iron have been used in [21] but this was changed in a later paper of the same author [22]. The temperature dependence has been investigated [19; 21; 22].

# Data collection

Data from the open literature have been collected. The data for production-type steels from the papers discussed above have been used, among others. The data for binary alloys have been used for comparison only. Data on properties of steels often are given with respect to the standard compositions. Then, a higher accuracy than two decimals (if $\rho$ is given in g/cm $^3$ ) is not adequate. Using these data for the present computations was unsuccessful. So only data that included the exact composition for each sample could be considered. Richter gave a maximum experimental error for the density of $\pm 0.01\%$ [13; 14].

$$
\rho_ {x} = \rho_ {\mathrm{Fe}} + \sum_ {i} q _ {i} C _ {i} ^ {\mathrm{wt}} (x) \tag {2}

If $\rho_{a}$ or $\rho_{g}$ was used, the effect of different phases was computed by:

$$
\rho_ {\text { steel }} = \sum_ {j} \rho_ {j} F _ {j} ^ {\text { vol }} \tag {3}
$$

where $\rho_{j}$ - density of phase $j$ and $F_{j}^{\mathrm{vol}}$ - volume fraction of phase $j$ in the steel.

In the present analysis the following regression formula was used:

$$
\frac {1}{\rho_ {x}} = \sum_ {i} r _ {i} C _ {i} ^ {\mathrm{wt}} (x) \tag {4}
$$

where $r_i$ is the regression coefficient of element $i$ and $r_i \cong 1 / \rho_i$ for substitutional solutes [23].

Ferritic steels. Model I. Regression formula (4) with x = steel was used. With the assumption that carbon is insoluble in ferrite and the solubilities of other elements in cementite are zero, the regression coefficient found for carbon reflects the amount of cementite in ferritic steels. Since 1 kg C combines with 13.949 kg Fe to 14.949 kg Fe $_{3}$ C,

$$
r _ {\mathrm{C}} = \left(\frac {1 4 . 9 4 9}{\rho_ {\mathrm{Fe} _ {3} \mathrm{C}}} - \frac {1 3 . 9 4 9}{\rho_ {\mathrm{Fe}}}\right) = 1. 7 5 0 \cdot 1 0 ^ {- 4} \frac {\mathrm{m} ^ {3}}{\mathrm{kg}} \tag {5}
$$

is the theoretical value for the regression coefficient $r_{C}$ of C in equation (4).

Model II. For the second model a thermodynamic analysis was performed. The amount of cementite and the solubilities of the elements in cementite and ferrite were computed for the equilibrium either at the temperature of the heat treatment or the temperature at which all austenite was transformed, whatever temperature was lower [24]. The heat-resistant steels known to form carbide-sequences in this study were all at the beginning of that sequence, which means that only cementite is present. The non-linear influence of Cr and Mn on the density of cementite was computed by interpolation of the data given in [25]. The influence of other elements in cementite was neglected. Regression analysis according to equation (4) with $x =$ ferrite was then performed.

Austenitic steels. A stable fcc structure at room temperature is only formed if Ni or other austenite stabilizing elements like C, N and Mn are added to the alloy in a sufficient amount. For that reason the density or lattice parameter of pure $\gamma$ -iron at room temperature has to be estimated by extrapolation from alloy series. Bendick et al. have collected literature data including contradicting estimates of the lattice parameter of $\gamma$ -iron and studied the anomalous thermal expansion of fcc ferrous alloys in the paramagnetic temperature range [26], table 4.

The austenitic steels investigated were single phase or austenite + TiC. A thermodynamic analysis was performed. The TiC-content and the solubilities in TiC and austenite were calculated [24]. Regression analysis by equation (4) with x = austenite was performed, including the mass content of TiC as a variable. Solutes in TiC were neglected.

# Results

Ferritic and low alloy steels. 44 batches were studied. Table 1 gives general information about the elements present in this investigation, including the density.

Model I. The coefficients $r_{j} \simeq 1/\rho_{i}$ as explained by equation (4) with x = steel for the assumption of zero solubility in cementite and zero solubility of C in ferrite are given in table 2. The deviation between density and apparent density and the relative standard error for 95% probability are given. Density and apparent density match each other within the statistical error with the exception of Si (strong deviation) and Mn. The coefficient found for Mn deviates with $6\sigma$ (usual criteria for an outlier: deviation >3σ).

Al, Nb, P, and Ti are insignificant in the amounts present in this study. Cu and Mo increase $\rho_{steel}$ ; C, Cr, Mn, Ni, S, Si, and V reduce $\rho_{steel}$ . The value of $R^{2}$ equals 0.987. The maximum deviation is 5.9 kg/m $^{3}$ ; the average deviation is 2.0 kg/m $^{3}$ .

If the regression coefficient for only one single element in substitutional solution is replaced with the theoretical coefficient a useful model is obtained. This is illustrated for Mo, $r_{Mo} = l/r_{Mo} R^{2}$ equals 0.976. The model predicts the density of the steels with a maximum deviation of 11.7 kg/m $^{3}$ and an average deviation of 3.3 kg/m $^{3}$ ; the result is reasonable. Although the mass content of Mo in the study is maximum 1.1 % and 0.4 % on average, neglecting it gives a model with $R^{2} = 0.94$ , a maximum deviation of 14.4 kg/m $^{3}$ and an average deviation of 5.6 kg/m $^{3}$ . Neglecting an element in equation (2) means to set $q_{i} = 0$ , in equations (1) and (4) it means $r_{i} = r_{Fe}!$ Replacing $r_{i}$ with $l/\rho_{i}$ for other substitutional solutes leads to models with similar accuracy. So, for substitutional solutes where no regression coefficient is determined theoretical or literature values can and should be used to get reasonable estimations of the density.

Model II. The result for the case where the solubilities of Cr and Mn in cementite were computed with Thermo-Calc is given in table 3. Figure 1 shows the inverse of the regression coefficients $r_i$ (apparent density) with error-bars for 95% probability compared to the densities of the elements and literature values. Al, Nb, P, and Ti are insignificant in the amounts present in this study. Cu and Mo increase $\rho_{\text{ferrite}}$ ; C, Cr, Mn, Ni, S, Si, and V reduce $\rho_{\text{ferrite}}$ . The model has $R^2 = 0.994$ , a maximum deviation of 4.2 kg/m $^3$ and an average deviation of 1.4 kg/m $^3$ . The literature values match the present regression results closely for Cr, Cu, Fe, Mn, Mo (some deviation), Ni, S, and Si; the value for V deviates. The elemental densities match the regression result for Cr, Cu, Fe, Mn, Mo (some deviation), and S. Deviations are found for Ni, Si, and V.

The density of cementite in kg/m $^{3}$ was computed as:

$$
\rho_ {\text { cementite }} = (7 7 4 2 - 6 8 7 \cdot C _ {\mathrm{Cr}} ^ {\mathrm{wt}}) \tag {6}
$$

for $C_{Mn}^{wt}$ in cementite above 0.09, below $\rho_{cem} = 7680$ kg/m $^{3}$ constant, or

$$
\rho_ {\text { cementite }} = (7 7 7 0 - 7 4 1 \cdot C _ {\mathrm{Mn}} ^ {\mathrm{wt}}) \tag {7}
$$

for $C_{\mathrm{Mn}}^{\mathrm{wt}}$ in cementite above 0.12 (there were no mixed cases), figure 2.

Austenitic steels. 64 batches of steel have been studied by use of equation (4) with x = austenite, including the mass content of TiC as a variable. Solutes in TiC were neglected. The results are shown in table 4 and figure 3. TiC, C, Cr, Mn, N, Si, and Ti reduce $\rho_{austenite}$ and Co, Cu, Mo, and Ni increase $\rho_{austenite}$ . The value of $R^{2}$ is 0.975. The maximum deviation is 10.4 kg/m $^{3}$ , the average deviation is 2.6 kg/m $^{3}$ .

The apparent densities agree with the elemental densities within the statistical error for TiC, C, Co, Cr, Mn, Mo, Ni, and Ti. They deviate somewhat for Cu and significantly for Si. The literature data agree with the apparent densities within the statistical error for Co, Cr, Cu, Mn, Mo, and Ni. They deviate for N and Si.

# Discussion

The use of regression formula (4) gives a better correlation than equations (1) or (2). For substitutional solutes the apparent densities are close to the elemental densities. This means that substitutional solutes do not strongly affect the iron lattice. The reason for this can be seen in the nature of metallic bonds. On the other hand, atoms that form covalent bonds are bound with preferred orientation to their neighbouring atoms, which strongly affects the gross volume of the atoms.

Elements, which in pure state have another structure than bcc show a systematic deviation of their apparent density from ideal behaviour when they are in solid solution with bcc iron; the same is true for solutes in austenite with other than fcc structure. Changes in the coordination number affect the atomic diameter. Zwell et al. calculated an increase in density of 5 – 8 % for fcc-iron at room temperature compared to bcc and assumed the same relation for Ni in bcc-iron [4]. This would explain that Ni appears with a lower density in bcc-iron than ideal in both literature and these results. The determination of volume size factors [16] seems to be problematic for many solutes since the deviation from ideal behaviour lies within the statistical error, tables 2-4.

For ferritic and low alloy steels, the deviation from ideal behaviour found for Si can be explained by covalent bonds; the present value agrees with literature data. Mn lowers the Curie temperature of Fe and has an anomalous non-linear effect on the density. Its coefficient found in model I shows significant deviation from both ideal and literature values. However, when the effect of Mn in cementite is calculated separately in model II, the coefficient found for Mn solved in ferrite agrees with literature data.

The coefficient found for V in model I is reasonable but shows a significant deviation in model II. V appeared with an average mass content of 0.08 % in the investigated steels. Low concentration means low concentration gradients thus diffusion processes are slow. The thermodynamic analysis assumes thermodynamic equilibrium but it is not sure that this is achieved for elements present in such small amounts. Additionally, the effect of elements other than Cr and Mn in cementite is neglected due to lack of data. For some elements with a strong affinity to C the use of the overall mass content in the steels for determination of a coefficient might be a better approximation. Finally, the assumption that the carbide precipitation is at the beginning of the carbide sequence and only the two phases ferrite and cementite are present might not be fulfilled for all V-containing steels. Information about the microstructure was not available for the steels under investigation.

# Conclusions

Literature data on the physical properties of steels have been collected. The influence of the composition on density of steels was studied by regression analysis, the effect of TiC, respectively, cementite was computed separately. An overview over former studies is given. The steels were investigated in two groups, ferritic together with low alloy steels, and austenitic steels.

For an estimation of the density of ferritic or austenitic steels, equation (4) with the regression coefficients $r_{i}$ in table 2, respectively, table 4 can be used. For improved accuracy for ferritic steels and for Ti-stabilized austenitic steels, a thermodynamic analysis has to be performed. Then, for ferritic steels the density of cementite has to be computed by equations (6) and (7) and the density of ferrite by equation (4) using the coefficients $r_{i}$ in table 3.

In the case that no regression data are available for one element the density of steels can be estimated with reasonable accuracy by use of a literature value or, for substitutional solutes, a coefficient obtained from the density of the pure element.

The results obtained by this regression analysis are:

\- the density of ferritic steels is increased by the addition of Cu and Mo and is decreased by C (cementite), Cr, Mn, S, Si, and V;

\- the density of austenitic steels is reduced by addition of C, Cr, Mn, N, Si, Ti, and TiC and increased by Co, Cu, Mo, and Ni.
