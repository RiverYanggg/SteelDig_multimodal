# A B S T R A C T

Fe-Mn-Al-C low-density steels have gained widely attention in recent years due to its low density and superior mechanical properties. This study investigated the effects of grain size on oxide film formation and corrosion behavior. Low-density steels with various grain sizes (8.7–32 μm) were prepared by adjusting the solution treatment temperature between 950 ℃ and 1150 ℃. The results indicate that as the solution treatment temperature increases, the grain size increases, and the grain boundary density decreases. The fine-grained sample (S950) rapidly forms an Al-rich oxide film. The film thickness reaches 25.2 ± 1.2 nm, with a low defect density (0.68 ×1021/cm3 and 0.26 ×1021/cm3 ) and the lowest corrosion rate (0.160 mm/y). Conversely, the coarsegrained sample (S1150), characterized by a grain size of 32 μm, forms a thinner oxide film $( 1 8 \pm 0 . 9$ nm) with a reduced Al content (90.17 at% to 69.91 at%) and a higher defect density, and the corrosion rate increases by 43 %. Further analysis reveals that smaller grain sizes and high-density grain boundaries promote the preferential dissolution of elements such as Al at the grain boundaries. This drives the formation of a uniform and stable oxide film. The coarse-grained structure results in discontinuous oxide film growth due to sparse grain boundaries, which increases the Fe/Mn content, and diminishes the corrosion resistance of steels.

# 1. Introduction

Fe-Mn-Al-C low-density steels (LDSs) have emerged as promising materials for automotive and marine industry because of their lower density and outstanding mechanical properties[1–6]. These characteristics are mainly attributed to the high Mn and Al contents in LDSs [7–10], which effectively support the demand for lightweight and high-performance steel.

Moreover, prolonged service duration places greater demands on corrosion resistance, which imposes limitations on the long-term operational safety of LDSs. A higher Mn content in steel results in an increased proportion of weakly protective Mn oxides in the corrosion product film, which deteriorates the corrosion resistance of the steel [11–14]. In contrast, increasing the Al content in the LDSs reduces the corrosion current density by promoting the formation of a dense $\mathrm { { A l } _ { 2 } \mathrm { { O } _ { 3 } } }$ layer and an Al(OH) film on the steel surface. This enhances the compactness of the film, and ultimately lowers the corrosion rate[11, 15–19]. Several studies have shown that microalloying is an effective way to improve the corrosion resistance of LDSs. The addition of elements such as Cr, Mo, and Ni promotes the enrichment of beneficial elements (e.g., Al, Cr, Mo) within the passive film, increases its thickness, and reduces defect density, thereby improving the stability and pitting resistance of the films. Table 1 summarizes the corresponding studies[12,13,20–25].

In summary, previous studies concerning the corrosion resistance of LDSs have primarily concentrated on enhancing oxide film density and stability through compositional optimization via alloying elements. On the other hand, microalloying increases the smelting cost and promotes the precipitate formation, thus posing a challenge to the uniformity of the structure. Consequently, an alternative strategy involving composition preservation coupled with microstructure optimization through heat treatment has emerged as a promising approach for corrosion resistance improvement. In recent years, some research has focused on the critical role of grain size in the corrosion process. Kim et al. [26] investigated the initial oxidation behavior of austenitic stainless steel and observed the formation of a double-layer oxide structure near grain boundaries, whereas nonprotective oxides predominantly formed within the interior of the grains. Grain refinement was shown to suppress the formation of rapidly growing spinel oxides, thereby enhancing oxidation resistance. Nnoka et al. [27] reported that grain refinement increased passivation coverage by approximately 40 %, with protective oxide layers preferentially forming along grain boundaries. Mohamed et al. [28] observed thicker oxide films at high-angle random GBs than at adjacent grains. This suggests greater anodic activity at the grain boundaries, which facilitates denser oxide formation. Li et al. [29] further demonstrated that an increased fraction of special grain boundaries in weathering bridge steel significantly improved corrosion resistance. Additionally, Shi et al. [30] found that during the initial stage of oxidation, GBs with relatively lower potentials typically act as anodic regions and undergo dissolution. An appropriate GB density helped homogenize the anodic oxidation current density and made the film growth more uniform.

Although these studies have established the influence of grain size on oxide film characteristics in alloys such as stainless steel, they have mainly focused on the electrochemical behavior or film thickness variations within conventional alloy systems. In the Fe-Mn-Al-C LDSs system, numerous studies[23,31–33] have investigated the corrosion behavior of alloys with comparable compositions after similar heat treatment (solution+aging). These studies predominantly attribute variations in corrosion resistance to precipitate characteristics. For example, the formation of precipitates such as carbides not only depletes the Al element in the matrix, but also intensifies the microgalvanic coupling between phases. This phenomenon promotes the formation of corrosion nucleation sites and accelerates the corrosion process. Furthermore, the segregation of precipitates at GBs induces preferential corrosion of GBs, which reduces the overall corrosion resistance. In addition, some studies[22] pointed out that κ-carbide and D0 precipitates frequently serve as the initiation sites for corrosion, reducing the pitting resistance of LDSs. The distribution and morphology of the precipitates significantly influence corrosion resistance. However, a comparison of these references[23,31–33] reveals that the reported grain sizes differ significantly (9.6 μm-50.2 μm), and the corresponding pitting potentials also vary considerably (-215 mV\~-480 mV) under similar heat treatment conditions. This phenomenon remains inadequately explained in current literature. Similar phenomena were also found in other LDS investigations[1,22,34,35]. Therefore, in addition to the effect of precipitates, the grain size evolution during solution treatment may constitute a critical factor governing the corrosion resistance of LDSs.

Therefore, in this study, the interference of precipitates was eliminated and Fe-Mn-Al-C LDSs with a gradient distribution of grain sizes (8.7–32 μm) were prepared via solution treatment, and the effects of the grain size and GB density on evolution of the Al-enrich oxide film were investigated. The microstructural characteristics of samples with varying grain sizes were systematically characterized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) techniques. The corrosion resistance of the LDSs was comprehensively assessed through electrochemical testing and immersion experiments. Furthermore, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) and Auger electron spectroscopy (AES) were employed to analyze the composition and structure of the oxide film. Finally, this study reveals the effect of grain size on the compositional distribution and formation behavior of Al-rich oxide films on LDSs. We found that grain refinement increased the Al content in the oxide film and improved the film uniformity. This discovery not only enriches the understanding of the surface oxidation behavior of LDSs, but also provides new ideas and theoretical support for improving the corrosion resistance of materials through microstructure regulation.

# 2. Experimental methods

# 2.1. Material preparation

The Fe-Mn-Al-C LDSs (including 30.34 % Mn, 8.93 % Al, and 0.9 % C) were prepared via a vacuum melting furnace. The steel ingot was homogenized at 1200 ℃ for 2 h, hot rolled at 1200–950 ℃ into slabs with a thickness of 20 mm, and quenched in water. The samples were subsequently subjected to solution treatment at 950 ◦C, 1050 ◦C, and 1150 ◦C for 2 h with water quenching to room temperature. For convenience, the original samples without solution treatment and the samples with different grain sizes obtained after solution treatment were labeled as S0, S950, S1050, and S1150, respectively.

# 2.2. Microstructural characterization

Samples with a size of 10 mm× 10 mm× 3 mm were sectioned from the heat-treated steel slab along the rolling direction. The surfaces were sequentially ground with SiC abrasive paper and mirror-polished with a diamond suspension. After being etched with 4 % nitric acid, the microstructure of the LDSs was analyzed by a JSM 7200 F SEM and matched energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS). X-ray diffraction (XRD) and EBSD were used to investigate the crystal structure and phase composition of the LDSs. The scanning range of the XRD (using Cu Kα radiation) test was 30–90◦ with a scan rate of 4◦/min. The samples for the EBSD test were electrolytically polished in a solution of 10 % CH COOH and 90 % C H OH, and EBSD data processing was performed via AZtecCrystal software. The transmission electron microscopy (TEM) morphology and diffraction patterns of the LDSs were observed using FEI Tecnai G2 F20. The samples for TEM were prepared by double jet electropolishing using a mixture of 10 % perchloric acid and 90 % ethanol. The potential difference between the GB and the steel substrate was measured using a Bruker MultiMode8 Scanning Kelvin Probe Force Microscope (SKPFM) equipped with a PFQNE AL Kelvin probe.

# 2.3. Electrochemical measurements

For electrochemical testing, several samples with dimensions of $1 0 \times 1 0 \times 3$ mm were prepared. The back of each sample was welded with copper wire and sealed with epoxy resin to leave a working area of $\scriptstyle 1 \ { \mathrm { c m } } ^ { 2 }$ . After polishing with SiC sandpaper, the electrochemical measurements were performed on a Solartron Modulab XM electrochemical workstation. A three-electrode system was adopted, with a platinum sheet as the counter electrode, a saturated calomel electrode as the reference electrode, the prepared LDSs as the working electrode, and the corrosive medium was 3.5 wt% NaCl solution. To ensure consistency in the experimental conditions, pure N2 was bubbled through the solution for 2 h to deoxygenate it before the experiment. The open-circuit potential (OCP) was first measured for 3600 s. The frequency range of electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was from $1 0 ^ { 5 }$ Hz to 0.01 Hz with an excitation voltage of 10 mV. The potentiodynamic polarization curve was tested by scanning from 0.4 V below the OCP to the anodic current density of $\mathbf { \dot { s } } \mathbf { m } \mathbf { A } / \mathbf { c m } ^ { 2 }$ with a scan rate of 0.3333 mV/s. The scanning potential range of the cyclic voltammetry (CV) test was − 1.2 VSCE to − 0.3 VSCE, and the scanning rate was 5 mV/s. All the electrochemical tests were repeated at least three times, and the error was controlled within 5 %. The consistency of the data demonstrated the good repeatability of the experiment.

# 2.4. Immersion test

The corrosion resistance of the LDSs was evaluated through immersion testing in 3.5 wt% NaCl solution. The size of samples was $3 0 \times 2 5 \times 3 \mathrm { m m }$ . The solution was replaced every 7 days, and the immersion period was 30 days. The corrosion rate was calculated by Eq. (1).

$$
\nu = \frac {\left(W _ {0} - W _ {\mathrm{t}}\right) \times 8 . 7 6 \times 1 0 ^ {4}}{\rho S t} \tag {1}
$$

where v represents the corrosion rate, mm/y; $W _ { 0 }$ is the initial weight of the sample, g; $W _ { \mathrm { t } }$ is the weight of the sample after rust removal, g; S is the immersion area, $\mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 } ;$ t is the immersion time, $\mathrm { d } ; \rho$ is the density of the LDSs, $g / { \mathrm { c m } } ^ { 3 }$ .

# 2.5. Oxide film analysis

The composition of the oxide film on the LDSs surface was characterized using XPS and AES. Prior to the constant potential polarization test, the samples were cathodically polarized by $\mathrm { \ a p p l y i n g { \Sigma } } - 1 . 0 \mathrm { \ V } _ { \mathrm { S C E } }$ for 300 s to remove the air-formed oxide film, and then the samples were polarized $\mathbf { a t } - 0 . 5 \mathrm { V } _ { \mathrm { S C E } }$ for 7200 s to form the oxide film on the surface. After polarization, the samples were immediately rinsed with deionized water, dried with anhydrous ethanol, and then vacuum-sealed to prevent surface oxidation. XPS was conducted using a ThermoFisher ESCALAB 250Xi XPS instrument with an Al Kα anode target at a power of 72 W. The spectra were corrected using the standard carbon peak (284.8 eV) and analysed using Avantage software. The AES measurements were performed with a ULVAC-PHI PHI-700 nanometer scanning Auger probe operating at an accelerating voltage of 5 kV. The sputtering rate was 3.6 nm/min (relative to the thermally oxidized $\mathrm { { S i O } } _ { 2 }$ film on the surface of single crystal Si), and the collection area for the Auger electron signal was $7 0 \times 7 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ . Mott-Schottky (M-S) analysis was used to investigate the semiconductor properties of the oxide film on the LDSs surface. The testing potential ranged from − 0.5 VSCE to − 1.1 VSCE, and the scan step was 30 mV/s.

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Microstructure analysis

The main phase composition of the LDSs was analyzed by XRD, and the diffraction patterns are shown in Fig. 1. The diffraction peaks reveal a single-phase face-centered cubic (FCC) austenitic structure, as confirmed by the presence of (111), (200), (220) and (311) peaks. These results indicate that the solution treatment preserves the austenitic matrix while modifying the relative peak intensities. Notably, the (111) peak intensity significantly increases following solution treatment, whereas the (220) peak intensity correspondingly decreases.

Fig. 2 presents the microstructure images of the LDSs after different solution temperatures. The microstructures of all the samples exhibit a single-phase austenitic structure. The grain size of the S0 sample without solution treatment is uneven, featuring both large deformed grains and fragmented small grains resulting from thermal deformation during rolling (Fig. 2a). With increasing solution treatment temperature, the grain size gradually increases, as shown in Fig. 2b-d.

The crystallographic characteristics of the LDSs were investigated through EBSD, and the results are shown in ${ \mathrm { F i g . ~ } } 3 ,$ and Fig. 4 summarizes the average grain size, kernel average misorientation (KAM) value, and GB density for all samples. The inverse pole figure (IPF) maps in Fig. 3bd reveal that LDSs do not exhibit significant preferred orientations after solution treatment and that the crystal structure orientations have a random crystallographic orientation distribution. Fig. 3a indicates that the S0 sample has a nonuniform grain size distribution, and some small grains are distributed at the interface of large grains. Solution treatment effectively homogenizes the grain size, as evidenced in Fig. 3b-d, and the grain size gradually increases with temperature. The maximum average grain size of 32.0 μm is achieved at the highest solution temperature of 1150 ℃ (Fig. 4a-d).

The KAM mapping was employed to qualitatively evaluate the distribution of local residual stresses within the materials, where elevated KAM values correspond to increased residual stress accumulation and consequently higher corrosion susceptibility[36]. Fig. $3 a _ { 1 } – \mathbf { d } _ { 1 }$ present the KAM distributions for the samples. The S0 sample exhibits the most pronounced residual stress, with an average KAM value of 1.62 as shown in Fig. 4e. After solution treatment, the sub-grain boundaries undergo dynamic recrystallization, reducing the residual stress in the S950, S1050, and S1150 samples to an average KAM value of approximately 0.4. The GB distribution analysis in Fig. 3a -d reveals that the S0 sample contains a substantial population of low-angle grain boundaries (LAGBs, 2–10◦). After solution treatment, the S950, S1050, and S1150 samples exhibit complete recrystallization[37], characterized by abundant annealing twin boundaries (ATBs, Σ3, 60◦) and high-angle grain boundaries (HAGBs, >10◦) within equiaxed austenite grains. Fig. 4e indicates that the S950 sample has the maximum densities of both ATBs and HAGBs, which are 0.146 and 0.114 $\mu \mathrm { m } / \mu \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ , respectively. These boundary densities progressively decrease with increasing solution temperature, with the S1150 sample reaching minimum values of 0.040 and 0.019 μm/μm2 , respectively.

Fig. 2. Microstructure of the LDSs after solution treatment: (a) S0, (b) S950, (c) S1050, (d) S1150.

TEM characterization was conducted to investigate the microstructural characteristics of the LDSs, and the results are shown in Fig. 5. The bright-field TEM image in Fig. 5a, along with the corresponding highresolution TEM (HRTEM) image and fast Fourier transform (FFT) analysis (Fig. 5a ), confirms the presence of numerous κ-carbides (indicated by yellow arrows) with diameters ranging from 5 to 10 nm in the S0 sample. Fig. 5a reveals that the matrix of the LDSs is predominantly austenitic, and contains a high density of irregularly distributed dislocations in the S0 sample. As shown in Fig. 5b, the dislocation density decreases after solution treatment. Notably, dislocation tangles are observed adjacent to the ATBs, suggesting that the abundant ATBs effectively hinder dislocation movement.

# 3.2. Electrochemical properties

Fig. 6 presents the potentiodynamic polarization curves and corresponding corrosion current densities of the LDSs in 3.5 wt% NaCl solution. As shown in Fig. 6a, the cathodic polarization curves exhibit nearly identical behavior across all the samples nearly overlap, primarily involving the oxygen absorption reaction. This indicates that the change in grain size caused by solution treatment has a negligible influence on the cathodic reaction. The anodic polarization process reveals three distinct regions. In the potential range of − $0 . 9 1 \mathrm { V } _ { \mathrm { S C E } } \tan { 0 . 6 7 \mathrm { V } _ { \mathrm { S C E } } } ,$ , the current density rises with increasing potential, which corresponds to the

active dissolution region. From $- 0 . 6 7 \mathrm { V } _ { \mathrm { S C E } } \mathrm { t } 0 \mathrm { ~ } { - 0 . 5 8 \mathrm { V } _ { \mathrm { S C E } } }$ , the current density decreases, and the electrode reaction is in an unstable state, which indicates the surface oxide film formation. Subsequently, with the increase of potential, the current density remains relatively constant. This may be related to the formation of an oxide film with certain protective capabilities, and a similar passive phenomenon occurs. A breakdown current density $( { i _ { \mathrm { b r e } } } )$ derived from the polarization curves is shown in Fig. 6b. The lower breakdown current density indicates better corrosion resistance[38,39]. Notably, the error bars in this figure represent the standard deviation range of the tests. The error values of all the samples are controlled within 5 %, and the experimental data have good repeatability and reliability. The S0 sample without solution treatment has the highest $i _ { \mathrm { b r e } } ~ ( 9 . 9 2 ~ \mu \mathrm { A } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 } )$ , and is relatively more prone to corrosion. The $\dot { \imath } _ { \mathrm { b r e } }$ of the fine-grained sample (S950) is 2.72 $\mathsf { \mu } \mathsf { A / c m } ^ { 2 } ,$ , which is lower than that of the coarse-grained sample (S1150) at 8.84 $\mu \mathrm { A } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 }$ , and it has higher corrosion resistance.

The surface corrosion morphology of the LDSs after potentiodynamic polarization to 5 ${ \mathrm { m A / c m } } ^ { 2 }$ is shown in Fig. 7. According to Fig. 7a, the S0 sample exhibits extensive corrosion damage, characterized by numerous deep pits resulting from elevated surface residual stress and a heterogeneous GB distribution (Fig. 3). After solution treatment at ${ 9 5 0 } ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ,$ , the LDS shows slight pitting corrosion (Fig. 7b), with smaller pit sizes, indicating superior corrosion resistance. However, as the grain size increases (Fig. 7c-d), the number and size of corrosion pits in the S1050 and S1150 samples increase, and corrosion expansion occurs on some pit walls, and the corrosion resistance decreases.

Fig. 8 shows the EIS results of the LDSs in 3.5 wt% NaCl solution. The Nyquist plots in Fig. 8a reveals that all the samples display a single capacitive arc without Warburg impedance. This behavior suggests that the electrode reactions are predominantly controlled by electrochemical reactions. The arc radius of the capacitance represents the resistance to corrosion, where a larger capacitive arc radius corresponds to enhanced corrosion resistance[40]. The S0 sample without solution treatment displays the smallest capacitive arc radius, which demonstrates its greater susceptibility to corrosion. In contrast, the S950 sample has the largest capacitive arc radius, which indicates that it has the highest resistance and the lowest corrosion tendency.

Fig. 3. EBSD images of the LDSs: (a-a2), (b-b2), (c-c2), and (d-d2) are IPF, KAM, and GB distribution maps of S0, S950, S1050, and S1150 samples, respectively.

The EIS data of the LDSs were fitted via an appropriate equivalent circuit model. According to the Bode phase plot in Fig. 8c, the EIS results exhibit the superposition of two times constants, with a maximum phase angle between $6 5 ^ { \circ }$ and $7 5 ^ { \circ }$ at intermediate frequencies, characteristic of typical nonideal capacitive responses[41,42]. This deviation from the ideal capacitance arises from electrode surface porosity, inhomogeneity, and relaxation effects[43]. Consequently, the constant phase angle element Q is used in the equivalent circuit instead of the ideal capacitance C to account for these nonideal characteristics[44,45]. On the basis of the above analysis, the equivalent circuit diagram in Fig. 8a is employed for EIS fitting, where $R _ { s }$ is the solution resistance, $R _ { 1 }$ and $Q _ { 1 }$ are the diffusion resistance and constant phase angle element of ions at the solution/film interface, respectively, and $R _ { 2 }$ and $Q _ { 2 }$ are the oxide film resistance and constant phase angle element, respectively. The impedance of the two constant phase elements (CPEs), Q and $Q _ { 2 } ,$ in the equivalent circuit is defined as[46,47]:

$$
Z _ {\mathrm{CPE}} = \frac {1}{Q (j \omega) ^ {\mathrm{n}}} \tag {2}
$$

where Q is the admittance of the CPE, j is the imaginary unit, ω is the angular frequency, and n is the dispersion index. When $0 . 5 < \mathtt { n } < 1$ , the CPE represents a nonideal capacitor, and when n = 1, the CPE represents an ideal capacitor.

Fig. 8a-c demonstrate excellent agreement between the experimental data and fitted curves. This finding indicates that the equivalent circuit is appropriate and accurately reflects the electrochemical behavior of the system. The polarization resistance $R _ { \mathrm { p } } \left( R _ { \mathrm { p } } { = } R _ { 1 } { + } R _ { 2 } \right)$ is used to evaluate the corrosion resistance of materials[48]. The fitting results are shown in Table 2. Comparative analysis reveals that the S950 sample exhibits the highest $R _ { \mathrm { p } } ( 1 2 4 8 6 \Omega { \cdot } \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 } )$ , which is approximately double that of the S0 sample (6036 Ω⋅cm2 ). Additionally, the $R _ { \mathrm { p } }$ values progressively decrease with increasing solution temperature. This trend is further supported by the Bode magnitude plots in Fig. 8b. The magnitude corresponding to 0.01 Hz can be used to evaluate the corrosion resistance of materials. Higher values correspond to better corrosion resistance[44]. The S950 sample clearly has the highest magnitude at this frequency, whereas the S0 sample exhibits the lowest magnitude. These findings are consistent with the results obtained from the potentiodynamic polarization results, collectively demonstrating the superior corrosion resistance of the S950 sample.

To quantitatively assess the corrosion protection ability of the oxide films of samples with different grain sizes, the protection efficiency (PE) of the oxide film was calculated from the $R _ { 2 }$ values via Eq. (3)[53,54]:

$$
P E = \left(1 - \frac {R _ {2} ^ {0}}{R _ {2} ^ {c}}\right) \times 100 \% \tag{3}
$$

where $R _ { 2 } ^ { 0 }$ is the interfacial resistance before film formation, and $R _ { 2 } ^ { \mathrm { c } }$ is the resistance of the oxide film after film formation. The calculation results show that the fine-grained sample (S950) has a greater PE (30.75 %) compared to the coarse-grained S1150 sample (17.95 %), confirming that grain refinement enhances the protective properties of oxide film. This improvement can be attributed to the high GB density facilitating the formation of an Al-rich oxide film. Notably, although the PE has improved, the overall PE values remain relatively low, likely due to insufficient oxide film compactness and localized structural defects.

The oxide film formed on the surface of LDSs exhibits semiconductor characteristics. Oxide films with different compositions and structures have varying carrier concentrations (defect densities), which directly influence the corrosion resistance. The semiconductor behavior of the oxide film can be quantitatively evaluated through space charge layer capacitance measurements and the relationship curve of the electrode potential (Mott-Schottky curve), with the equation as follows[55,56]:

$$
\frac {1}{C _ {\mathrm{H}} ^ {2}} + \frac {1}{C _ {\mathrm{SC}} ^ {2}} = \pm \frac {2}{\varepsilon \varepsilon_ {0} \mathrm{eN}} \left(E - E _ {\mathrm{fb}} - \frac {\mathrm{k} T}{\mathrm{e}}\right) \tag {4}
$$

where $C _ { \mathrm { H } }$ is the Helmholtz double layer capacitance, C is the space charge layer of the oxide film, “+ ” is the n-type semiconductor, $^ { 6 6 } - ^ { 9 9 }$ is the p-type semiconductor, e is the electron charge $( 1 . 6 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 9 } \mathrm { C } ) ,$ ε is the relative dielectric constant of the semiconductor, which is $1 5 . 6 , \varepsilon _ { 0 }$ is the dielectric constant of vacuum, which is $8 . 8 5 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 2 } \ \mathrm { F / m }$ , N is the carrier density (where $N _ { \mathrm { D } }$ is the donor density corresponding to the ntype semiconductor and $N _ { \mathrm { A } }$ is the acceptor density corresponding to the p-type semiconductor), k is the Boltzmann constant, T is the absolute temperature, E is the applied potential, and $E _ { \mathrm { f b } }$ is the flat band potential. Given the significantly lower magnitude of space charge capacitance than that of the Helmholtz capacitance, Eq. (4) can be simplified to Eqs. (5) and (6).

In summary, both the oxide film thickness and Al content tend to decrease with increasing grain size. Notably, compared with the other samples, the S950 sample forms a uniform, dense, and thick Al-rich oxide film.

With increasing solution temperature, the diffusion coefficient k increases exponentially, which enhances the atomic mobility at elevated temperatures. This facilitates the migration of $\mathbf { G B } ,$ where large grains progressively swallow small grains, thereby accelerating grain growth. # 4.3. Effects of grain size on oxide film formation and corrosion resistance of the LDSs

The corrosion resistance of the LDSs exhibits a progressive decline with increasing grain size and decreasing GB density, with the S950 sample displaying superior corrosion resistance. This variation in corrosion resistance stems principally from the characteristics of the oxide film on the LDSs, which are predominantly governed by the chemical composition and microstructure of the steel[85–87]. Nevertheless, experimental evidence and analytical results indicate that grain size and GB density constitute the primary distinguishing features among current LDSs. Therefore, the difference in corrosion behavior can be reasonably attributed to changes in the oxide film properties resulting from grain size variation.

Previous studies[88–92] reported that GB segregation of alloying elements such as Cr and V significantly influences the corrosion resistance of the material. These studies suggest that elemental segregation at GBs may either promote the formation of stable passive films or lead to precipitates that serve as pitting initiation sites, thereby affecting corrosion performance. In contrast, the present study reveals a relatively homogeneous elemental distribution without noticeable GB segregation. The oxide film formation mechanism in this case appears to be predominantly governed by the intrinsic structural characteristics of GBs, which act as inherent surface defects of the crystal. Specifically, the crystallographic misorientation between adjacent grains causes substantial atomic disorder at HAGBs, which generates structural defects that include lattice distortion, vacancy accumulation, and coordination deficiencies[93,94]. These defects thermodynamically increase the interfacial free energy at GBs to levels substantially higher than those in grain interiors, thereby enhancing their susceptibility to anodic dissolution in electrochemical systems[95–98]. From a kinetic perspective, the weaker binding energy of boundary atoms decreases the activation energy required for metal ion dissolution from the crystal lattice. The structural disorder coupled with vacancy accumulation creates short-circuit diffusion pathways, which significantly promote the dissolution activity of GBs in corrosive environments[99–102]. When selective anodic dissolution initiates at GB, the local metal ion concentration rises sharply while the interfacial $\mathtt { p H }$ value declines. This acidification effect narrows the thermodynamic passive region and lowers the critical energy barrier for oxide film nucleation[100,103]. Concurrently, GBs serve as continuous permeation networks that facilitate both the inward migration of water molecules and the outward diffusion of metal cations. The synergistic interaction of these processes within GB defect regions enables rapid nucleation and growth of oxide films[104, 105]. Consequently, even without elemental segregation, GBs govern both the initial dissolution behavior and subsequent oxide film formation due to their higher interfacial free energy and lower dissolution barriers.

Fig. 17 illustrates the dissolution behavior and surface potential distribution of LDSs with varying grain size during the initial stage of corrosion. SEM images of the S950 and S1150 samples (Fig. 17a-b) demonstrate preferential grain boundary dissolution during the initial stage of corrosion, resulting in the formation of continuous shallow etching grooves, whereas the grain interiors exhibit comparatively less dissolution. The surface potential distribution results in Fig. 17c-e demonstrate that the ATBs exhibit a higher potential than the matrix, while the HAGBs show the lowest potential. This potential distribution indicates that the HAGBs have the most active electrochemical reactions during the initial stage of corrosion and undergo preferential anodic dissolution. This process creates favorable conditions for the rapid oxidation of reactive elements such as Al[106,107], promoting the formation of an Al-rich oxide film. Notably, localized dissolution occurs at certain ATBs, as shown in $\mathrm { F i g . 1 7 , }$ , which is attributable to the approximately 30 mV potential difference between ATBs and the matrix. Despite their inherent stability, this potential disparity facilitates microgalvanic couples at the interface of the ATBs and the matrix, which establishes anodic regions in the adjacent matrix and induces metal dissolution extending along the twin boundary. The uniform distribution of ATBs contributes to maintaining the structural continuity and composition homogeneity of the oxide film in corrosive environments and promotes uniform oxide film growth across the entire grain surface.

Conversely, larger grain sizes correspond to lower GB densities, resulting in a sparsely distributed GB region that diminishes the number of reaction sites for oxide film formation during the initial stage of corrosion. This reduction in active sites results in a slower film formation rate and produces a more discontinuous oxide layer. Additionally, an increasing grain size correlates with a decreased Al content within the oxide film, producing a thinner and compositionally heterogeneous film exhibiting higher density of defects (Mott-Schottky results, Fig. 12). These defective regions may become channels for the infiltration of corrosive media, potentially leading to localized film breakdown and subsequent corrosion initiation[32,108,109].

The growth process of the oxide film has good repeatability and reproducibility in repeated experiments.

The Al content in the surface oxide films of the different LDSs significantly differed, as shown in Figs. 14 and 15. Notably, the S0 sample without solution treatment retains a substantial amount of κ-carbide, which limits the solid solubility of Al and yields the lowest Al content in its oxide film. Fig. 19a-b present the cyclic voltammetry curves of the LDSs in 3.5 wt% NaCl solution. The peak observed in the range $\mathbf { o f _ { \alpha } } - 0 . 8 1 \mathrm { ~ V } _ { \mathrm { S C E } } { \sim } { - 0 . 7 7 \mathrm { ~ V } _ { \mathrm { S C E } } }$ corresponds to Al oxidation, whereas the oxidation peak near approximately $- 0 . 5 6 \mathrm { V } _ { \mathrm { S C E } }$ represents Fe oxidation, possibly involving concurrent minor Mn oxidation.

In the first cycle (Fig. 19a), a distinct Al oxidation peak appears at approximately − 0.81 $\mathrm { V } _ { \mathrm { S C E } } ,$ with the S950 sample exhibiting the highest peak current density. This observation suggests the superior oxidation activity of surface Al in the S950 sample, which facilitates its rapid participation in initial oxide film formation. Comparatively, the Al oxidation peaks of the S1050 and S1150 samples are noticeably weaker, which demonstrates lower Al oxidation reactivity on their surfaces. Furthermore, quantitative analysis of the Al oxidation peak region during the first cycle reveals that the charge density of the S950 sample reaches 2.55 $\times \dot { 1 } 0 ^ { - 5 } \mathrm { C / c m } ^ { 2 }$ , which is higher than that of the S1050 $( 1 . 8 0 \times 1 0 ^ { - 5 } \mathrm { C / c m ^ { 2 } } )$ and S1150 (1.66 $\times 1 0 ^ { - 5 } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 } )$ samples. This indicates that the oxidation reaction of Al in the S950 is more complete in the initial stage, which promotes the rapid formation of an Al-rich oxide film. Notably, the characteristic Fe oxidation peak near − 0.56 $\mathrm { V } _ { \mathrm { S C E } }$ is absent in the first cycle. This result has been consistently confirmed through repeated experiments (see Supporting Information Fig. S1). This phenomenon arises because preferential oxidation and rapid film formation of Al coat the Fe surface with an Al-rich oxide layer, thereby restricting reaction sites and suppressing Fe oxidation, which prevents formation of a detectable current peak during the first cycle.

In the second cycle (Fig. 19b), a distinct Fe oxidation peak emerges at approximately − 0.56 V alongside the persistent Al oxidation peak. The S950 sample displays the lowest peak intensity in this potential region. This result suggests the presence of a dense oxide film on the surface, which significantly inhibits the subsequent oxidation of Fe and Mn. Conversely, the S1050 and S1150 samples demonstrate substantially enhanced anodic peaks at this position, suggesting that the surface film is incompletely formed and that Fe and Mn continue to participate in the film formation process. The XPS results further confirm that the oxide film of the S950 sample contains a higher Al content coupled with reduced Fe and Mn concentrations compared to the other samples. This difference stems from the fine-grained structure and high GB density of the S950, which facilitate the preferential dissolution of Al at the GBs. Consequently, a continuous and dense Al-rich oxide film rapidly forms during the initial stages, effectively impinging subsequent Fe and Mn oxidation processes.

This selective dissolution accelerates nucleation and growth of an Al-enrich oxide film, whereas the uniformly distributed ATBs promote homogeneous film formation, ultimately yields a denser, more continuous protective layer. Schottky analysis results further confirm that the oxide film on the S950 sample surface primarily consists of Al and exhibits a uniform compositional distribution and appropriate thickness. The film also contains a low density of defects, which effectively impedes the invasion of corrosive media and confers superior corrosion resistance. Conversely, the coarse-grained S1050 and S1150 samples have a reduced GB density and sparse boundary distribution, which retard initial oxide film formation and reduce uniform nucleation sites, as shown in Fig. $2 0 \mathbf { b } \mathbf { - } \mathbf { b } _ { 3 } .$ Consequently, these samples develop thinner oxide films with diminished Al content and elevated Fe/Mn concentrations. This results in a structurally porous oxide layer prone to crack formation and other defects. These defective areas serve as infiltration channels for corrosive media, which reduces the protective efficacy of the oxide film, and ultimately degrades the corrosion resistance of LDSs.

# 5. Conclusions

This study investigates the influence of grain size variations induced by solution treatment on the microstructure and corrosion resistance of Fe-Mn-Al-C LDSs. The main conclusions are summarized as follows:

(1) Increasing the solution temperature promotes complete recrystallization in LDSs, and the microstructure contains abundant ATBs and HAGBs. The grain size increases progressively from 8.7 μm to 32 μm, while the HAGBs density decreases from 0.114 μm/ $\mathrm { \mu m } ^ { 2 }$ to 0.019 μm/μm2 .
(2) Electrochemical measurements indicate that the corrosion resistance of LDSs is negatively correlated with grain size. The finegrained sample (S950) has the lowest $i _ { \mathrm { b r e } } ~ ( 2 . 7 2 ~ \mu \mathrm { A } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 } )$ and the highest $R _ { \mathrm { p } } ( 1 2 4 8 6 \Omega { \cdot } \mathrm { c m } ^ { 2 } )$ . Its corrosion rate is 0.160 mm/y,

which is 43 % lower than that of the coarse-grained sample (S1150), and excellent corrosion resistance is exhibited.
(3) The oxide film of LDSs exhibits duplex semiconductor characteristics of n-type and p-type. Both the $N _ { \mathrm { A } }$ and $N _ { \mathrm { D } }$ values increase with increasing grain size. The $N _ { \mathrm { A } }$ and $N _ { \mathrm { D } }$ of the S950 sample are $0 . 6 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 3 }$ and $0 . 2 6 \times 1 0 ^ { 2 1 } / \mathrm { c m } ^ { 3 }$ , respectively, which are significantly lower than those of the S1150 sample. This suggests that finer grains reduce defects within the oxide film. XPS analysis further reveals that grain coarsening reduces the Al content in the film from 90.17 at% to 69.91 at%, while increasing the Fe and Mn contents. Therefore, the stability of the film is reduced.
(4) This study reveals the mechanism of the effects of grain size on Al-rich oxide film formation and corrosion resistance in LDSs. These results indicate that metallic elements preferentially dissolve and form oxide films at the GBs. The change in grain size directly affects the formation rate and growth behavior of oxide films. The HAGBs provide numerous nucleation sites for the growth of the oxide film. A continuous and stable Al-rich oxide film forms quickly during the initial stage of corrosion, significantly enhancing the corrosion resistance. Conversely, coarsegrained microstructures impede oxide film growth, reduce the Al content, and degrade the film density and stability.
