Subfigure (a) shows the bright-field TEM image of the sample deformed at -196 °C, with its corresponding selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern (zone axis Z.A. [110]) inset. Dislocations and mechanical twins formed on {111} plane along <112> direction are clearly observed in (a), confirmed by the split diffraction spots in SAED. Subfigure (b) shows the TEM image of the sample deformed at -77 °C, where dense microbands aligned along traces of {111} slip planes and high-density dislocations are found, with no mechanical twins detected. The observation reveals that decreasing deformation temperature and corresponding stacking fault energy change the dominant deformation mechanism from planar glide forming microbands at -77 °C to mechanical twinning at -196 °C. Both microstructures act as barriers for dislocation motion, contributing to the high work-hardening rate and excellent low-temperature mechanical properties of the steel.",
