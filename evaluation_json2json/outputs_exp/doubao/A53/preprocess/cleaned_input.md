# High Temperature Oxidation of Iron-Manganese-Aluminum Based Alloys

P. R. S. Jackson\* and G. R. Wallwork\*

Received August 1, 1983; revised January 26, 1984

The isothermal oxidation resistance of high purity iron–manganese–aluminum alloys containing from 0 to 40% manganese and from 0 to 15% aluminum was investigated at 600, 800, and 1000°C in pure oxygen at a pressure of 200 torr for periods up to 100 hr. They were subsequently examined using SEM and metallographic techniques, and an oxide map showing the alloy structure and general oxidation behavior at 800°C was produced. Scales formed on alloys which contain insufficient aluminum to form protective alumina have structures which depend largely upon the concentration of manganese in the alloy. Alloys which contain more than 7.5% manganese form manganese rich scales, whereas alloys which contain lower levels of manganese form scales that are composed almost entirely of the oxides of iron. Small manganese oxide nodules grow through the alumina scales which form on alloys containing in excess of 9% aluminum. The most oxidation resistant alloys, having compositions within the range Fe–(5–10)% Mn–(6–10)% Al, develop continuous protective alumina scales and are totally ferritic. Austenite is detrimental to the oxidation resistance of duplex alloys as it promotes the breakdown of preexisting alumina scales and the growth of bulky manganese rich oxides. Small additions of chromium are beneficial and reduce the concentration of aluminum required to form protective alumina scales.

KEY WORDS: Fe–Mn–Al alloys; nodules; alumina scales; manganese oxides.

The need for alloys which resist degradation by oxidation at high temperatures has resulted in the development of a multitude of alloys, almost

The largest group of alloys and by far the major users of chromium are the austenitic stainless steels, which generally contain more than 15% chromium. The search for cheaper alloys having similar mechanical properties and requiring less chromium led to research on alloys belonging to the Fe–Mn–Al system, which rely on aluminum for the formation of protective scales. Effort has been devoted primarily toward the development of austenitic alloys and knowledge of their mechanical properties.

From a recent review paper by the authors, $^{1}$ it was apparent that a lack of knowledge exists concerning the oxidation behavior of alloys belonging to the Fe–Mn–Al system. Information regarding their resistance to oxidation and in particular information regarding scale morphologies and the composition of scales as functions of alloy composition is extremely limited. The literature indicates that ferritic alloys, and especially those which contain small additions of chromium, may have far greater potential for development of oxidation resistant alloys. In order to understand the oxidation behavior and the complex nature of the scales which may form during oxidation of ternary alloys, a preliminary investigation into the oxidation of simpler alloys belonging to the binary Fe–Mn system was made. $^{2}$ Alloys were chosen to complement the ternary alloys discussed here. By so doing, the information necessary to facilitate the identification of scale components and enable a classification of the scales formed on Fe–Mn–Al alloys could be obtained.

# EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURE

Approximately 50, Fe–Mn–Al and several additional alloys containing chromium were prepared from high purity materials using the technique of levitation melting. Disc shaped specimens in the as-cast condition approximately 7 mm in diameter and 1 mm in thickness were individually oxidized under static conditions at 800°C in oxygen at a pressure of 200 torr. Kinetics of oxidation of all alloys oxidized for 24 hr were recorded continuously using a Cahn R. G. electrobalance, and the specimens were weighed before and after oxidation. Two selected alloys were also oxidized for 100 hr at 600, 800, and 1000°C to determine the effects of temperature and time upon oxidation behavior.

Specimens were then examined using the scanning electron microscope (SEM), and pertinent morphological features were qualitatively analyzed using EDAX. After surface examination of the scales, specimens were mounted on edge, sectioned, and metallographically polished to 0–1 $\mu$ m for examination of the scales and alloy structures using optical, SEM, EDAX, X-ray, and electron-probe microanalysis. A number of alloys were also heated to the temperature of oxidation for varying times, quenched, and metallographically examined to confirm the presence of the alloy phases observed in the specimens oxidized at $800^{\circ}$ C.

# Oxidation of Alloys Containing Chromium

Preliminary tests showed that small additions of chromium improved the oxidation resistance of Fe–Mn–Al alloys, hence a series of experiments were performed to study its effect. The compositions and weight gains of alloys oxidized for 24 and 100 hr at 800°C are shown in Table III. Alloys which contained less than 5% aluminum were subject to breakaway and relatively high weight gains unless alloyed with relatively high levels of chromium, for example, Fe–10Mn–4Al–12Cr. Manganese concentrations of the order of 15% also result in breakaway and high weight gains.

Of the alloys oxidized for 24 hr only Fe–10Mn–4Al–12Cr, Fe–5Mn–5Al–5Cr, and Fe–7.5Mn–5Al–5Cr formed thin protective alumina scales. After 100 hr all alloys showed signs of scale degradation. With the exception of Fe–10Mn–4Al–12Cr, alloys which contained less than 5% aluminum formed hollow convoluted or wrinkled oxide mounds (Fig. 15a). Similar scale morphologies were observed on alloys which contained 5% aluminum and less than 5% chromium. Numerous very small manganese rich oxide nodules were observed on alloys which formed hollow oxide mounds. These can be seen in Fig. 15(b) in conjunction with manganese oxide threads which were observed on this alloy only. Where spallation occurred, the substrate contained voids, and remnants of the small oxide nodules appeared as internal oxides. Duplex alloys formed scales which were of two types. # DISCUSSION

# Iron-Manganese-Aluminum Alloys

The addition of aluminum to iron-manganese alloys prevents the formation of the oxides FeO and MnO due to the formation at the metal-oxide interface of oxides with much lower dissociation pressures. Consequently, the formation of alumina or aluminate spinels at the metal-oxide interface prevents the formation of (Fe, Mn)O as a scale component in iron-manganese-aluminum alloys. The solubility of iron and manganese in alumina is very low; however, alumina may react with FeO and MnO to form the aluminate spinels $\mathrm{FeAl_2O_4}$ and $\mathrm{MnAl_2O_4}$ . Scales formed on iron-manganese-aluminum alloys are similar to those formed on binary iron-manganese alloys except for the absence of (Fe, Mn)O and the presence of alumina or aluminate spinels at the metal-oxide interface. In lieu of the above and of phase determinations made previously in an oxidation study of the binary Fe-Mn system, $^2$ the structures and most of the phases present in scales formed on ternary alloys at $800^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ were identified using optical micrographs and X-ray images, despite the small cross-sectional dimensions of the scales.

It was assumed that the alloy phases observed after 24 hr oxidation were similar in composition and proportion to the equilibrium phases and that the phase boundaries did not markedly shift with longer heat treatment times. On the basis of the small changes that were observed in the structures of the alloys Fe–10Mn–6Al and Fe–20Mn–8Al after 24 and 100 hr heat treatments and the similarity of the phase diagram determined by Schmatz $^{3}$ for 760°C, it is considered that the phase boundaries lie close to those at equilibrium. By superimposing the oxidation behavior of the ternary alloys upon the isothermal section of the phase diagram obtained in this way, the oxidation behavior at 800°C can be represented by way of an “oxide map” (Fig. 3) which shows alloy composition regions based on the observed scale morphologies.

# Region I

Alloys in this region were those which contained insufficient aluminum to form continuous alumina scales. Alloys which contained moderate levels of aluminum had scale morphologies which consisted of alumina interspersed with bulky oxide nodules. Less aluminum would undoubtedly have led to the formation of thick stratified scales with structures similar to those of nodules. Nodules formed on alloys which contained less than 7.5% manganese were similar to those formed on binary Fe–Al alloys. They were much larger than those formed on higher manganese alloys and were composed mainly of an outer layer of $Fe_{2}O_{3}$ and an inner layer of $(\mathrm{Fe}, \mathrm{Mn})_{3}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ .

Aluminum rich phases, either $Al_{2}O_{3}$ or aluminate spinels, $(\mathrm{Fe},\mathrm{Mn})\mathrm{Al}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ , were also present either as particles or thin layers at the metal-oxide interface. A high degree of manganese enrichment on the surface of the external $Fe_{2}O_{3}$ layers on Fe–5Mn–6Al and Fe–7.5Mn–5Al and the low solubility of manganese oxides in $Fe_{2}O_{3}$ indicated the presence of an unknown phase. Closer examination of the scales revealed a grey phase $(\mathrm{Mn}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{3})$ present as particles at the gas-oxide interface and within the $Fe_{2}O_{3}$ layer on the Fe–7.5Mn–5Al alloy (Fig. 4).

Nodules formed on alloys which contained more than 7.5% manganese were much smaller in comparison and were composed of distinct, complete external grey layers of $Mn_{2}O_{3}$ above layers of $Fe_{2}O_{3}$ and $(\mathrm{Fe},\mathrm{Mn})_{3}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ . Alumina or aluminate spinels were also observed as layers or as particles at the metal-oxide interface. The formation of both large and small nodules on Fe–7.5Mn–5Al indicates a transition from iron rich to manganese rich scales at approximately 7.5% manganese.

The proportions of the individual phases in the structures of the large oxide nodules were reflected in the oxidation kinetics, and when breakaway occurred, the thickness of the $Fe_{2}O_{3}$ layer was relatively small compared with that when healing occurred. Enhanced $Fe_{2}O_{3}$ formation may occur when alumina or aluminate particles form at the metal-oxide interface and partially or completely isolate the $(\mathrm{Fe},\mathrm{Mn})_{3}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ layer from the metal. In the case of partial isolation, the overall growth rate of the $(\mathrm{Fe},\mathrm{Mn})_{3}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ layer via outward diffusion of cations is retarded; however, the growth rate of $Fe_{2}O_{3}$ remains unaltered. The net result is an overall increase in the ratio of the thicknesses of $Fe_{2}O_{3}$ to $(\mathrm{Fe},\mathrm{Mn})_{3}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ . Further nodule growth is prevented and total conversion to $Fe_{2}O_{3}$ occurs if the $(\mathrm{Fe},\mathrm{Mn})_{3}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ layer becomes completely isolated by growth of a layer of alumina or aluminate; nodules consisting almost entirely of $Fe_{2}O_{3}$ with a small amount of $Al_{2}O_{3}$ or aluminate have been observed on a binary Fe–5Al alloy oxidized at 900°C by Saegusa and Lee $^{5}$ and on binary alloys containing 3, 4 and 6% aluminum oxidized at $800^{\circ}$ C by Tomas. $^{7}$ Irregular metal-oxide interfaces observed on some nodules appeared to form as a result of intermittent breakaway and healing of the metal-oxide interface.

Structurally, the major differences between the different nodule types were the presence of continuous layers of $Mn_{2}O_{3}$ and alumina or aluminate in small nodules, as opposed to the semi- or noncontinuous layers of both phases in the large nodules. The structures of the nodules observed at $800^{\circ}C$ are shown schematically in Fig. 17. The absence of $(\mathrm{Fe},\mathrm{Mn})_{3}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ from the smaller nodules was probably due to the formation of a continuous layer of alumina or aluminate at the metal-oxide interface. Although no analyses were made, its greater thickness compared to that of alumina probably indicates an aluminate spinel. The presence of healing layers in small nodules and their absence in large ones can be explained if the growth rate of the large nodules was sufficiently high to prevent the formation of a continuous layer of aluminate. As the rate of oxidation of manganese is considerably lower than that of iron, $^{1}$ it is likely that formation of a continuous layer of $Mn_{2}O_{3}$ at the outer surface may retard the rate of ionic diffusion sufficiently to allow a healing layer of $Al_{2}O_{3}$ or aluminate to form at the base of the nodule and minimize further growth. The minima in Fig. 2(a) which occur at alloy compositions containing between 5% and 10% manganese are considered largely due to the elimination of large iron oxide nodules and their replacement with small manganese rich nodules and oxides. This transition was directly observed on Fe–7.5Mn–5Al, which formed both large and small nodules.

# Region II

Alloys in this region were mainly duplex, except for Fe–30Mn–9Al, which had a triplex $\alpha + \gamma + (\beta\mathrm{Mn})$ structure. At 800°C alloys from within the two phased region of the oxice map in Fig. 3 generally showed poor oxidation resistance, and the resulting scale morphologies were of two types. Alloys which contained relatively high proportions of ferrite formed alumina with bulky manganese rich oxides and oxide threads (Fig. 10a), which grew above grain boundaries and at distinct orientations on grain surfaces (Fig. 8a). Morphologies of this type were always directly related to the substrate structure and appeared as in Fig. 8(b). On alloys which contained higher proportions of austenite, the scale morphologies were generally bulky manganese rich oxides and wormlike oxide threads, which often appeared to have grown through the alumina (Fig. 10b). Morphologies of this type were also related to the structure of the substrate, and layers of ferrite which formed secondarily were frequently observed at the substrate surface as in Fig. 9.

The oxide map shows that primary ferrite always contains very much higher levels of aluminum than does the austenite. Primary ferrite formed on alloys from this region is considered to be oxidation resistant, since it forms thin alumina scales and contains levels of aluminum as high or higher than oxidation resistant ferritic alloys from region III. Austenite within the structure of these alloys, however, contains at most 7% or 8% aluminum, and as other workers have shown, this level is insufficient for the formation of protective alumina scales on austenitic iron-manganese-aluminum alloys. In contrast to the primary ferrite, ferrite formed secondarily by depletion of manganese from austenite, contains, at most, the amount of aluminum present in the austenite. For alloys containing less than 30% manganese, this amount is below that required to form protective alumina scales as the composition of the secondary ferrite lies within region I of the oxide map. Alloys which contain more than 30% manganese can form secondary ferrite with compositions in region III of the oxide map; however, they contain significant proportions of the brittle $\beta$ manganese phase.

Krishnan and Prakash $^{6}$ found that duplex alloys which contained between 15% and 27% manganese were far less oxidation resistant than the ferritic alloys at temperatures above 700°C, and they attributed the lower oxidation resistance to the presence of discontinuities in the protective scale caused by the presence of the second phase. Descriptions of the scale structures of the high manganese alloys at 600 and 700°C $^{7}$ are consistent with the results of the present investigation. The presence of austenite in alloys with compositions within this region is responsible for their poor oxidation resistance, and thus the true boundary between protective and nonprotective scales follows the $\alpha-(\alpha+\gamma)$ phase boundary for alloys which contain in excess of about 12% manganese. As resistant alloys must be totally ferritic, carbon and other austenitizing elements are considered to be detrimental to the oxidation resistance unless combined with other elements to prevent the formation of austenite.

Wormlike threads of oxide which appeared to grow through alumina scales and on bulky oxides (Fig. 10) were associated with the presence of austenite beneath. Enrichment of aluminum in the outer layer of the bulky oxides was often observed and probably indicates that bulky oxide growth followed the formation of oxide threads. A schematic representation of the stages of growth considered to be involved is shown in Fig. 18.

The structures of the bulky oxides that formed on alloys in this region appeared to depend upon the concentration of manganese rather than aluminum in the alloy. Alloys which contained less than about 20% manganese had very similar structures to the small bulky oxide nodules which formed on alloys containing at least 7.5% Mn from region I of the oxide map. Alloys with more than approximately 20% manganese formed bulky oxides highly enriched in manganese; these alloys contained no $Fe_{2}O_{3}$ , and the phases appeared similar to the manganese rich phases reported to form on a binary Fe-40Mn alloy. $^{2}$ Typical structures of such oxides are vastly different from those of scales formed on binary iron-aluminum alloys. They are composed predominantly of manganese oxides; however, their modes of growth appear generally similar to those of nodules on the binary alloys. The different structures observed on Fe-25Mn-7Al (Fig. 19a) are considered to contain the phases $Mn_{2}O_{3}$ , $Mn_{3}O_{4}$ , $(\mathrm{Fe}, \mathrm{Mn})_{3}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ , and either $(\mathrm{Fe}, \mathrm{Mn})\mathrm{Al}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ or $Al_{2}O_{3}$ , and are shown schematically in Fig. 19(b). Whether formation of the phases $Mn_{3}O_{4}$ and $(\mathrm{Fe}, \mathrm{Mn})_{3}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ does occur appears to depend on the enrichment of manganese and the growth of $(\mathrm{Fe}, \mathrm{Mn})\mathrm{Al}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ at the metal-oxide interface.

The phase $(\mathrm{Fe}, \mathrm{Mn})_{3}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ was usually found associated with apparently incomplete healing layers of alumina or aluminate. Disappearance of $(\mathrm{Fe}, \mathrm{Mn})_{3}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ from the scales was associated with continuous or near-continuous aluminate layers.

The magnitudes of weight gains within this region of alloys oxidized at $800^{\circ}$ C increased with increasing manganese and decreased with increasing aluminum. Since large increases in weight gain are due to growth of oxides other than alumina, that is, bulky manganese rich oxides, the large increases in weight gain observed for alloys which contained more than about 10% manganese are directly attributable to the relative proportion of austenite in the structure. Frequently, breakaway and healing kinetics were observed. However, no direct correlation could be made with the breakdown of alumina and growth of bulky oxides above austenite precipitates. Breakaway often occurred at relatively high weight gains, which indicated that breakthrough and growth of bulky oxides above the initially formed alumina had already begun. Healing kinetics may be attributed to the formation of alumina or aluminates at the metal-oxide interface, thus reducing the rate of outward diffusion of cations across the scales.

The alloy Fe–20Mn–6Al formed a ferrite surface layer during oxidation at $800^{\circ}$ C (Fig. 9) and oxidized rapidly with a weight gain after 20 hr in excess of 1 mg. The moderate oxidation resistance reported for austenitic alloys in the literature, $^{1}$ with weight gains typically of the order of 1 mg per day, may be attributed to the formation of bulky manganese rich scales and healing layers of aluminate or alumina beneath. The structures of the scales formed on austenitic alloys may thus be very similar to those of the bulky oxides described.

In addition to oxidation for 24 hr at 800°C, the alloys Fe–10Mn–6Al and Fe–20Mn–8Al were also oxidized for 100 hr at 600, 800, and 1000°C. The morphologies and structures of scales formed at 800°C were similar to those formed after 24 hr, which indicates that little change in the boundary for protective scale formation at 800°C occurs with time. This suggests that most alloys which are oxidation resistant for 24 hr are also resistant for much longer periods. At 600°C both alloys formed bulky manganese rich oxides with structures of the type discussed previously, which indicates that higher levels of aluminum are required to form protective scales at lower temperatures. Greater proportions of austenite were present in the structure of Fe–20Mn–8Al implying a shift in the $\alpha-(\alpha+\gamma)$ phase boundary toward higher aluminum compositions as the temperature is decreased. At 1000°C both alloys were ferritic and formed protective convoluted alumina scales, indicating that the $\alpha-(\alpha+\gamma)$ phase boundary and the boundary for protective scale formation shifts to lower aluminum concentrations as the temperatures of oxidation increases. These shifts in the phase boundary are in accord with the results of Schmatz $^{3}$ and Shvedov and Goretskii. $^{8}$ In the case of ferritic alloys the amount of aluminum required to form protective alumina also decreased with temperature in a similar manner. For binary iron–aluminum alloys $^{9}$ and ternary iron–nickel–aluminum alloys, $^{10}$ this trend has been correlated with the temperature dependent rate of diffusion of aluminum in ferrite.

Scales formed on Fe–20Mn–8Al at 1000°C spalled readily on cooling, possibly due to large areas of detached scale having few points of contact with the substrate. X-ray images (not presented) showed that the two layered duplex scales formed on both alloys at 1000°C were composed almost entirely of the oxides of manganese and aluminum. The inner layer was considered to be alumina, whilst the outer layer, enriched in manganese and aluminum, was probably a solid solution of $Mn_{3}O_{4}$ and $Al_{2}O_{3}$ . At temperatures above 950°C, the only oxide of manganese in equilibrium with alumina is $Mn_{3}O_{4}$ , which contains in excess of 30 wt.% $Al_{2}O_{3}$ at 1000°C. $^{1}$

# Region III

Alloys within this region were totally ferritic and were characterized by very adherent continuous protective scales, which were not penetrated by nodules or other bulky oxides. Extremely low 24 hr weight gains, typically of the order of 0.05 mg cm $^{-2}$ for most alloys which contained less than 15% manganese, indicated the formation of alumina scales. The relatively high weight gain for the alloy Fe–15Mn–8Al (0.116 mg cm $^{-2}$ ) is considered to result from the large number of small oxide protrusions which covered the entire surface. Their composition was not determined; however, they are possibly remnants of nucleii formed during the early stages of scale growth, in which case they may be expected to be composed of the higher oxides of manganese and iron, or of the aluminate spinels formed by reaction of MnO and FeO with alumina.

From the work of Tomaszewicz and Wallwork, $^{11}$ manganese additions were apparently detrimental to the oxidation resistance of binary iron–aluminum alloys. The oxide map in Fig. 3 shows that all alloys in this system which contain less than 6% aluminum lack oxidation resistance and lie in regions I or II. For alloys which contained higher levels of aluminum, the addition of manganese was found to be beneficial, and alloys from region III were highly oxidation resistant. The optimum alloys were considered to contain between 5% and 10% manganese as this concentration resulted in the lowest weight gain for any group of alloys having the same aluminum concentration.

The upper boundary of region III is an approximate boundary between alloy compositions which form continuous protective alumina scales and alloys which form alumina scales penetrated by small oxide nodules. The lower boundary of region III indicates that manganese as a ternary alloy addition reduces slightly the concentration of aluminum required to form protective external alumina scales. As additions of chromium to Fe–Al alloys result in initially formed scales composed predominantly of $Cr_{2}O_{3}$ and $Al_{2}O_{3}$ , $^{12}$ it is also possible that preferential oxidation of manganese in Fe–Mn–Al alloys could result in initially formed scales composed of manganese oxides and alumina rather than iron oxides and alumina. The lower oxide dissociation pressure of manganese oxide rich scales at the metal interface would be expected to favor the transition from internal to external oxidation $^{13}$ at lower alloy aluminum concentrations.

# Region IV

Alloys in this region formed alumina scales and small manganese-rich oxide nodules, which penetrated into the substrate. Despite the small size of nodules formed on the surface of alloys containing less than 15% manganese, EDAX analysis showed that they also were extremely rich in manganese. Although their structures were not determined, it is likely that they were composed predominantly of the higher oxides of manganese with $(\mathrm{Fe}, \mathrm{Mn})\mathrm{Al}_{2}\mathrm{O}_{4}$ at the metal-oxide interface.

A characteristic feature of alloys which contained high levels of aluminum was the spallation which occurred on cooling. Examination of spalled areas revealed a high density of nodules and voids in the substrate. Most voids were smooth and rounded in contrast to voids formed on alloys from region II, which were mainly angular (Fig. 11a). Voids formed on all alloys which contained more than 25% manganese were smooth and rounded, regardless of the aluminum concentration in the alloy (Fig. 11b). Formation of similar smooth rounded voids beneath spalled $Cr_{2}O_{3}$ scales on a binary iron–chromium alloy $^{14}$ has been attributed to either evaporation or surface diffusion of chromium. Likewise, if diffusion of manganese vapor across voids does occur as proposed for a binary Fe–Mn alloy containing 40% Mn, $^{2}$ the growth of small manganese oxide nodules may be explained as follows.

Nodule nucleation occurs by reaction of oxygen with manganese vapor at flaws or defects in the alumina scale which lie directly above voids in the substrate. Nodule growth then proceeds almost exclusively by the reaction of oxygen with manganese vapor in the void. This is because the vapor pressure of manganese is many orders of magnitude larger than that of either iron or aluminum. Continued growth is mainly by the outward diffusion of $Mn^{2+}$ ions since $Mn_{2}O_{3}$ , $Mn_{3}O_{4}$ , and MnO are all p-type semiconductors. Inward growth of the nodule occurs by a mechanism involving short-circuit diffusion of oxygen, and “filling in” of the void with manganese oxide rather than iron oxide results. When the void has become filled with manganese oxide, rapid internal oxidation of aluminum in the substrate leads to the formation of a healing layer of $Al_{2}O_{3}$ or (Fe, Mn) $Al_{2}O_{4}$ at the nodule-alloy interface, and any further growth occurs at a much reduced rate via diffusion through this layer. The stages involved are shown schematically in Fig. 20. The observation of numerous smooth rounded voids “filled” or “partially filled” with manganese oxide, on the surface of Fe–30Mn–12Al (Fig. 11c), supports such a mechanism.

The steady increase in 24 hr weight gains with increasing aluminum for alloys containing in excess of about 9% aluminum (Fig. 2b) is undoubtedly due to the increased number of such small manganese oxide nodules. If their growth is related to the formation of voids in the substrate, then void formation appears to be related to the concentration of aluminum in the alloy.

# Alloys Containing Chromium

Breakaway during oxidation of the quaternary Fe–Mn–Al–Cr alloys can be directly attributed to the growth of hollow oxide mounds and convoluted scales. Comparison of the hollow oxide mounds with convoluted scales formed on the duplex alloy Fe–15Mn–5Al–5Cr revealed almost identical scale structures, except that the scale on the duplex alloy was more uneven in thickness (Fig. 16a). Bulky oxides formed above austenite plates on Fe–10Mn–4Al–5Cr were almost certainly highly enriched in manganese as were those formed on duplex ternary alloys. Formation of a totally ferritic substrate surface layer and areas of manganese enrichment in the scale of Fe–15Mn–5Al–5Cr indicates that bulky manganese oxides formed initially above precipitated austenite. The compositions and structures of the convoluted scales were not determined. However, their greater thickness than alumina scales and their high degree of enrichment in aluminum suggests that they were composed of the higher oxides of iron, manganese, and chromium with alumina or aluminate spinel at the scale base.

The morphological features of scales formed on chromium containing alloys were in many respects similar to those formed on ternary Fe–Mn–Al alloys. The presence of small manganese rich nodules often associated with areas of spalling alumina containing angular voids and internal oxides is consistent with the mechanism of nodule growth proposed for ternary alloys containing high levels of aluminum. In view of the relatively low concentrations of aluminum in these alloys, it appears that growth of small manganese oxide nodules on both ternary and quaternary alloys is related to the presence of voids in the substrate rather than to the concentration of aluminum in the alloy.

Growth of manganese oxide threads on Fe–Mn–Al alloys was attributed to the presence of austenite below the alumina scale. Austenite was not present in the structure of Fe–7.5Mn–4Al–5Cr; however, oxide threads were present after 100 hr (Fig. 15b). Aluminum depletion of the substrate due to formation of an alumina scale may have been sufficient to form austenite, as the alloy composition was extremely close to the $\alpha-(\alpha+\gamma)$ phase boundary at 800°C.

Oxidation resistant quaternary alloys contained significantly higher manganese concentrations than similar resistant quaternary alloys reported previously. $^{15}$ Perkins $^{16}$ found that alloys with compositions within the range Fe-(2.5–5)% Mn-(8–10)% Al-(5–15)% Cr afforded optimum resistance to cyclic oxidation at 1800°F (982°C); however, alloys containing less than 8% aluminum and alloys containing 5–20% manganese were not investigated. The poor cyclic oxidation resistance of alloys which contained 20–30% manganese and the associated high weight losses were attributed to vaporization of manganese from the alloy. From the observation of nonadherent highly convoluted scales during isothermal oxidation of Fe–15Mn–5Al–5Cr at 800°C, it is considered that the high weight losses were more probably due to repeated growth and spallation of the scale during cycling. It has been shown that Fe–Cr–Al alloys containing 5% chromium require 5% aluminum to form highly protective scales at 800°C. $^{7}$

# CONCLUSIONS

It has been shown that certain alloys which belong to the iron-manganese-aluminum system oxidize to form alumina scales, which are highly protective at 600, 800, and $1000^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . An oxide map which shows the alloy structure and general oxidation behavior as a function of alloy composition has been produced for the temperature of $800^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . The structures of the scales on alloys which contained insufficient aluminum to form protective alumina depended largely upon the manganese concentration in the alloy. Alloys which contained less than $7.5\%$ manganese formed scales composed of alumina and large iron oxide nodules, which were enriched in manganese at the outer surface. Alloys containing more than $7.5\%$ manganese were characterized by the formation of alumina and much smaller nodules or oxides, which were highly enriched in manganese. High levels of aluminum led to the growth of very small manganese oxide nodules and alumina scales which were prone to spalling. Totally ferritic alloys with compositions in the range $\mathrm{Fe - (5 - 10)\% Mn - (6 - 10)\% Al}$ were highly resistant to oxidation and were considered worthy of further development. Small additions of chromium were beneficial to these alloys and enabled the concentration of aluminum necessary for protective scale formation to be reduced without loss of oxidation resistance. The formation of austenite in ferritic alloys designed to resist high temperature oxidation must be avoided as austenite facilitates the breakdown of preexisting alumina scales and the subsequent growth of bulky manganese rich oxides.
