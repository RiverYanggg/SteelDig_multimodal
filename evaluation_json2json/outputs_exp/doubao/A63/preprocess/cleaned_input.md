# A B S T R A C T

Heterostructures, particularly dual-phase heterostructures, often lead to hetero-interfacial stress concentration, and ultimately premature failure, though they benefit from heterogeneous deformation. Addressing the challenge of strain localization and hetero-interfacial cracking, a single-phase heterogeneous grain structure is designed through tuning zirconium (Zr) content and employing precise thermo-mechanical treatment. As Zr content increases to 0.50 wt %, a single-phase bimodal grain structure is successfully engineered, including soft coarse grain zones (\~5 μm in size and 65 % in area) and hard fine grain zones (\~1 μm in size and 35 % in area). Such bimodal heterostructure is mainly ascribed to the retarding effect of recrystallization, due to the drag effect of Zr atoms and Zener pinning of ZrC. The synergistic effects of microstructural heterogeneity and ZrC precipitation enhance the strengths. Furthermore, multistage deformation mechanisms, involving heterogeneous deformationinduced hardening, dislocation slip and nanotwinning, enable the strain hardening capability and ductility.

# 1. Introduction

For structural alloys used in engineering applications, the simultaneous enhancement of strength and ductility remains a critical challenge. Recently, the Fe-Mn-Al-C-based steels have grained considerable attention, due to the desirable strength-ductility synergy and lightness [1–3]. To further enhance their mechanical properties, utilizing incoherent B2 intermetallic compounds has been a vital approach through addition of Ni [4,5]. However, such precipitation strengthening effect usually leads to a significant enhancement of strengths accompanied by a ductility loss, namely strength-ductility trade-off [6].

Recent development on alloys with heterostructues has a great potential to breakthrough such trade-off, thereby leading to various heterostructures introduced by different approaches including muti-phase structures [7,8], gradient grains [9], multi-modal grain structures [7–9]. For these heterostructures, the extra strengthening and hardening depends on the difference in hardness between the soft and hard regions, which is usually referred as heterogeneous deformation-induced (HDI) effect [7,8]. However, the pronounced disparity between soft and hard zones (e.g., hardness and elastic modulus) often induces the interfacial strain localization and premature failure, such as different dual-phase systems including FCC/B2 dual-phase steels [10], and FCC/L21 medium entropy alloys [11,12]. In comparison to these dual-phase alloys, heterogeneous grain structures usually exhibit hardness difference and an identical elastic modulus between soft and hard zones [13,14], owing to the single-phase structure. Such microstructural heterogeneities can effective mitigate strain localization via reducing disparity between hard and soft zones. Thus, introducing a single-phase heterogeneous grain structure is a promising pathway to enhancing the strength and ductility.

Following this line of thought, we introduce heterostructure grain structure via a combination of thermo-mechanical treatment (TMT) and addition of Zr. A typical single-phase heterostructure grain structure including coarse grains and fine grains is successfully designed via tuning Zr content. Such heterostructure effectively provides an extra strengthening and hardening effect, thereby further enhancing the strength and strain hardening capability.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Material preparation

Three Fe-Mn-Al-C low density steels were melted using a vacuum arc-melt furnace, and their chemical compositions are Fe-25Mn-7Al-0.9C-0Zr, Fe-25Mn-7Al-0.9C-0.01Zr and Fe-25Mn-7Al-0.9C-0.50Zr (wt. %), which are referred to as Zr0, Zr0.01 and Zr0.50 steels, respectively. For the melting process, the raw materials (>99.8 wt % pure) were placed into the water-cooled copper crucible. Then, the furnace was evacuated to a high vacuum level (the pressure below $1 0 ^ { - 6 }$ bar) to avoid gas contamination including oxygen (O), nitrogen (N) and hydrogen (H). Additionally, introducing high-purity argon (Ar) into the furnace chamber (a pressure below 1 bar) prevents the oxidation and contamination. Repeating melting was conducted at least five times to further promote the uniformly chemical compositions and reduce the gas contamination. Such melting process including high-purity raw materials, a high vacuum level and high-purity argon can effectively achieve the average content of O (12–89 ppm [15]), N (\~18 ppm [16]) and H (\~2 ppm [17])during the steelmaking process. These as-cast steels were subjected to homogenization (1200 ◦C for 2 h), and Fig. S1 reveals that the oxygen segregate to grain boundaries does not occur. Then, hot-rolling with a thickness reduction of 40 %, followed by water quenching. These hot-rolled plates were subjected to cold rolling process with an 80 % reduction. Then, the Zr-alloyed cold-rolled plates were annealed at 900 ◦C for 5 min, followed by water-quenching.

# 2.2. Mechanical testing and materials characterization

For the room temperature tensile experiments, flat dog-bone-shaped samples with a gauge length of 14 mm and width of 4 mm were prepared from the cold-rolling plate along rolling direction (RD). The uniaxial tensile testing was conducted using Instron 5892, and the experimental data were recorded via a contact extensometer at a strain rate of $1 0 ^ { - 3 }$ $s ^ { - 1 }$ . To reveal the local strain evolution of different microstructures, a quasi-in-situ tensile testing with EBSD and $\mu { - } \mathrm { D I C }$ analysis, was conducted. Specifically, the sample surface was prepared by mechanical grinding and polishing. Then, a monolayer of nanoscale $\mathrm { S i O } _ { 2 }$ particles was dispersed on the polished sample surface. The sample was then subjected to a quasi in-situ tensile deformation using the Instron 5892 universal testing machine. Further, the deformed sample surface with nanoscale $\mathrm { S i O _ { 2 } }$ was captured using a scanning electron microscope (SEM, Hitachi SU7000). These images were taken at the global strain level of 0 %, 2 % and $8 \ : \% ,$ respectively, and then were analyzed using VIC-2D software to obtain local strain fields at different strain levels. Additionally, backscatter electron imaging (BSE) and electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) analysis were carried out using SEM equipped with an EBSD detector (accelerating voltage: 30 kV, step size: 500 nm). For transmission electron microscopy (TEM) observations, TEM foils were prepared by twin-jet electro-polishing with a mixture of 5 % perchloric acid and 95 % ethanol (vol%). The TEM/high-resolution TEM (HRTEM) images and selected area electron diffraction (SAED) patterns were carried out by JEOL F200 operated at 200 kV.

# 3.2. Tensile deformation behavior

Fig. 4a shows the engineering stress-strain curves of the Fe-Mn-Al-C low density steels with different Zr content. In Zr0 steel, its yield strength is \~435 MPa, the ultimate tensile strength is 820 MPa, and fracture ductility is 52 %. As Zr content increased to 0.01 wt %, the strengths of the Zr0.01 steel are slightly increased, i.e., yield strength: \~450 MPa, ultimate tensile strength: \~832 MPa, while its fracture ductility is increased to 55 %. With further increasing of Zr content, the Zr0.50 steel exhibits a reasonable strength-ductility combination, i.e., yield strength: \~505 MPa, ultimate tensile strength: \~855 MPa and fracture ductility: 58 %. Similarly, a high true ultimate tensile strength and true strain is observed in the Zr0.50 steel. This suggests that introducing heterostructure in Fe-Mn-Al-C low-density steels through addition of Zr can enhance the mechanical properties. The microscopic fracture morphologies of these fractured Fe-Mn-Al-C low density steels are shown in Fig. 4b–d. For the Zr0 steel, its facture surface is mainly composed of small dimples and cleavage faces. As the Zr content increasing, the dimples are obviously larger and deeper than those in Zr0 steel, and the density is also increased.

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Formation mechanism of the heterogeneous grain structure

In general, heterogeneous grain structures, e.g., bimodal recrystallized grains (CG/FG zones) [9], recrystallized and non-recrystallized zones [13], are designed through introducing partial recrystallization. The TMT, i.e., severe cold-rolling and various annealing conditions, is a significant approach for the partial recrystallization. In present work, a typical TMT treatment including 80 % cold-rolling and short-time annealing $( 9 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 min) was applied in the Fe-Mn-Al-C steels. However, the Zr0 steel exhibits a homogeneous grain structure after the TMT treatment. Interestingly, the combination of TMT treatment and Zr addition leads to a heterogeneous grain structure including CG and FG zones. As increasing Zr content, the fraction of FG zones is obviously increased (Fig. 3). Thus, it is necessary to reveal the formation mechanism of heterogeneous grain structure in Zr0.50 steel.

It is well accepted that recrystallization is driven by the stored energy of cold-rolled deformation, i.e., the high density of dislocations and shear bands. Such driving force $( F _ { \mathrm { r e c } } )$ is decreased by the retarding forces including solute drag $( P _ { s } )$ and Zener pinning $( P _ { \mathrm { z } } ) _ { \mathrm { : } }$ , which is expressed by Ref. [21]:

$$
F _ {\mathrm{rec}} = - \frac {\gamma_ {\mathrm{gr}}}{R _ {\mathrm{rec}}} + \frac {\gamma_ {\mathrm{sub}}}{R _ {\mathrm{sub}}} + c _ {\mathrm{d}} G b ^ {2} \left(\rho_ {\mathrm{def}} - \rho_ {\mathrm{rec}}\right) - P _ {\mathrm{s}} - P _ {\mathrm{z}} \tag {1}
$$

where $\gamma _ { \mathrm { g r } }$ and $\gamma _ { \mathrm { s u b } }$ are boundary energy of subgrains and boundary energy of a high angle grain boundary, $R _ { \mathrm { r e c } }$ and $R _ { \mathrm { s u b } }$ are sizes of recrystallized grains and subgrains, $c _ { \mathrm { d } }$ is a constant (0.5), ρdef and $\rho _ { \mathrm { r e c } }$ are the dislocation densities in the deformed and recrystallized grains, G is the shear modulus, b is Burger's vector.

For the annealed Zr0.50 steel, Zr elements are mainly distributed in FCC matrix, except for precipitation of ZrC. Based on Equation (1), Zr in solid solution and ZrC precipitation exhibit a retarding effect of recrystallization. Specifically, these Zr elements distributed in FCC matrix serve as solute atoms, which usually diffuses to the vicinity of defects including grain boundaries, shear bands and dislocations. During annealing process, the accumulation of these Zr atoms in turn impedes the movement of grain boundaries and recovery of shear bands and dislocations. Such drag effect from Zr atoms leads to a high energy barrier of recrystallization of cold-rolled microstructure during annealing [11,22], thereby inducing the partial recrystallization. Further, according to the thermodynamic calculation (Fig. 7), the fraction of ZrC particles is \~0.6 % after annealing at 900 ◦C. These nanoscale ZrC particles also inhibit the migration of grain boundaries during recrystallization through a typical Zener pinning effect. The Zener pinning force is usually related to the average size of precipitates and fraction of precipitates [23]. Compared to the Zr0 and Zr0.01 steels, the fraction of ZrC carbides in Zr0.50 steel is highest. Based on Fig. 6d, the size of ZrC in the Zr0.50 steel is only 30 nm. As a result, these nanoscale ZrC carbides in the Zr0.50 steel can lead to a desirable Zener pinning force, thereby playing a pivotal role of the partial recrystallization. Additionally, the micro shear bands caused by the heavy cold-rolling deformation are the preferred sites for the nucleation of recrystallization $[ 2 4 , 2 5 ]$ , which provides the enough driving force to formation of fine grains. As a result, the synergetic effects of drag effect of Zr atoms, Zener pinning of ZrC and TMT treatment promote the heterogeneous grain structure in the Zr0.50 steel.

# 4.2. Effect of heterogeneous grain structure on enhancing strengthductility synergy

The tensile results show that the Zr0.50 steel exhibits a desirable mechanical property compared to the Zr0 and Zr0.01 steels. For the yield strength, the bimodal heterogeneous grain structure induces an extra strengthening contribution (i.e., HDI strengthening), apart from traditional strengthening mechanisms, e.g., solid-solution strengthening, grain boundary strengthening and precipitation strengthening. In Zr0.50 steel with a bimodal grain structure (FG and CG zones), the contributions for yield strength including grain boundary strengthening and HDI strengthening can be expressed as [9]:

$$
\sigma_ {G B - H D I} = f _ {F G} \sigma_ {F G} + \left(f _ {C G} - f _ {C G, b}\right) \sigma_ {C G} + f _ {C G, b} \sigma_ {C G, b} \tag {2}
$$

where $f _ { \mathrm { F G } }$ and $f _ { \mathrm { C G } }$ are fractions of FGs and $\mathbf { C G s } ,$ respectively, $f _ { \mathrm { C G , b } }$ is the fraction of those CGs adjacent to FGs, which is \~37 % based on EBSD result (Fig. 2). σFG and σCG can be estimated using Hall-Petch equation [26]:

$$
\sigma = \sigma_ {0} + k d ^ {- 1 / 2} \tag {3}
$$

where $\sigma _ { 0 }$ is the fraction stress (\~97 MPa [10]), k values for coarse and 0 fine FCC grains are 14.55 MPa mm $^ { 1 / 2 }$ and 9.1 MPa mm1/2, respectively [10].

$\mathfrak { c } _ { \mathrm { C G } , \textbf { b } }$ is the back stress strengthened CGs adjacent to FGs, which is expressed as [9]:

$$
\sigma_ {C G, b} = \sigma_ {0} + \sqrt {\frac {2 k ^ {2}}{d _ {C G} d _ {F G}} l _ {H b a r} + \sigma_ {C G} ^ {2}} \tag {4}
$$

where $l _ { \mathrm { H b a r } }$ is the width of hetero-boundary affected region, which is calculated by $[ 1 - \left( \sigma _ { C G } - \sigma _ { F G } \right) ^ { 2 } ] d _ { C G }$ . Based on Fig. 3, the $l _ { \mathrm { H b a r } }$ is estimated to be 2.45 μm. Based on Equations (2)–(4), the strengthening contributions from grain boundary and HDI are \~300 MPa.

In present study, the precipitation of nanoscale ZrC carbides can promote the Orowan precipitation strengthening [27,28]:

$$
\sigma_ {p} = \left(\frac {0 . 5 3 8 G b f ^ {1 / 2}}{a}\right) \ln \left(\frac {a}{2 b}\right) \tag {5}
$$

Where $f$ is the fraction of precipitates and a is the average size of precipitates. Based on Figs. 6 and 7, the average size and fraction of ZrC carbides are 30 nm and 0.006, respectively. The precipitation strengthening of ZrC carbides is calculated to be 108 MPa. As a result, the high yield strength in the Zr0.50 steel originates from grain boundary strengthening, HDI strengthening and precipitation strengthening.

Except for the enhanced yield strength, the Zr0.50 steel exhibits a remarkable strain hardening capability and fracture ductility, as shown in Fig. 4a. For the heterogeneous grain structure, a high density of GNDs is generated during the elasto-plastic co-deformation, as evidenced in Fig. 5b. As the global strain increasing, the co-deformation of soft CG zones and hard FG zones occurs, which further promotes the compatible deformation and induces additional strain hardening, which is expressed as [29]:

$$
\frac {d \tau}{d \varepsilon} = \alpha G b \frac {d \left(\sqrt {\rho_ {s} + \rho_ {G}}\right)}{d \varepsilon} \tag {6}
$$

$$
\frac {d \tau}{d \varepsilon} = \left(\frac {d \tau}{d \varepsilon}\right) _ {\text { dis }} + \left(\frac {d \tau}{d \varepsilon}\right) _ {\text { HDI }} \tag {7}
$$

where ε and τ are strain and shear stress, respectively. ρs and ρG are the density of the statistically stored dislocations (SSDs) and GNDs, respectively. According to Equations (6) and (7), the density of GNDs is increased in the Zr0.50 steel with heterogeneous grain structures, which in turn assist an extra hardening capability, i.e., HDI hardening effect, and thereby leads to manifestation of HDI ductility [8].

Further, a high density of deformation twins can be successfully introduced during tensile deformation, apart from the dislocations. It is reported that formation of deformation twinning is related to the critical stress for twinning. The critical stress for twining in FCC-structured alloys is described as [29,30]:

$$
\sigma_ {t w} = M \left(\frac {S F E}{b} + \frac {G b}{D}\right) \tag {8}
$$

where M is the Taylor factor (3.06), SFE is the stacking faults energy, b is Burgers' vector (0.147 nm) and D is the grain size (Fig. 3). In present work, the SFE of FCC matrix in Zr0.50 steel is estimated to be \~55 mJ/ m2 (Supplementary text S1). Thus, the critical twinning stress of the Zr0.50 steel is \~1.14 GPa, which is obviously lower than its true ultimate tensile strength (the inset in Fig. 4a). Such high strengthening ef fect promotes the formation of deformation twinning, thereby enabling the further strain hardening capability. Thus, the synergic effects of HDI hardening and deformation twins provide a reasonable strain hardening rate, which further improves the fracture ductility.

# 5. Conclusion

In present study, a single-phase heterogeneous grain structure, containing coarse grains with \~5 μm in size and 65 % in area and fine grains with \~1 μm in size and 35 % in area, is successfully engineered in a Fe-Mn-Al-C low density steel. As the Zr content increases to 0.50 wt %, the drag effect of Zr atoms and Zener pinning of ZrC carbides is obvious enhanced, which can retard the recrystallization process. Such retarding recrystallization in turn assists the formation of bimodal grain structure. The heterogeneous grain structure has yielded desirable enhancement in strength-ductility synergy, in comparison to the Fe-Mn-Al-C steels without Zr addition or with 0.01 wt % Zr addition. The significant strengthening effect is mainly attributed to the grain boundary strengthening, HDI strengthening and precipitation strengthening. For the enhanced strain hardening capability and fracture ductility, the deformation mechanisms are dominated by cooperating slip bands, HDI hardening and deformation twins.
