# Material System
Low-density austenite-based duplex steels, Fe-20Mn-9Al-1.2C-xCr (x = 0, 3, 6 wt.%), produced by near-rapid solidification. The alloys are denoted as 0Cr, 3Cr, and 6Cr steel strips. The microstructure consists of γ-austenite (fcc) matrix with δ-ferrite (bcc) and nano-scale ordered precipitates: κ-carbide ((Fe,Mn)₃AlCₓ, perovskite structure) in γ-austenite, and DO₃ ordered phase ((Fe,Mn)₃Al) in δ-ferrite.

# Processing Route and Variables
- Near-rapid solidification: Centrifugal casting under argon atmosphere into a copper mold rotating at 600 rpm; estimated cooling rate range ~1–1×10⁴ K/s.
- As-cast annealing: 600 °C for 3 h (referred to as annealed steel strips).
- Stress relief annealing prior to cold rolling: 400 °C for 1 h.
- Cold rolling: Multipass reduction of 20% in thickness to produce CR20% strips.
- Post-rolling annealing: CR20% strips annealed at 600 °C for 3 h (CR20% + annealed condition).

# Microstructure and Phase Evolution
- **As-cast condition**: All samples exhibit a dual-phase microstructure of γ-austenite matrix and irregularly shaped δ-ferrite particles. α-ferrite (δ) volume fraction increases systematically with Cr addition: ~3% (0Cr) → ~12% (3Cr) → ~18% (6Cr) (SEM, Fig. 2). Cr behaves as a ferrite stabilizer in this alloy system.
- **Annealing (600 °C, 3 h)**: δ-ferrite volume fraction further increases to ~6% (0Cr), ~14% (3Cr), ~24% (6Cr). X-ray diffraction (XRD) shows that the double-peak feature between 43.5°–45.0° (overlapping (110)δ and (220)DO₃ reflections) transforms into a single DO₃ superlattice peak after annealing, indicating complete ordering of δ-ferrite (Fig. 1). Annealing also promotes κ-carbide precipitation in 0Cr and 3Cr, as evidenced by subtle superlattice reflections in XRD.
- **κ-carbide precipitation in as-cast austenite**: TEM dark-field imaging using (001) superlattice reflection reveals abundant, uniformly distributed κ-carbide nanoparticles in 0Cr: volume fraction 3%, average size ~6 nm, lattice parameter 0.387 nm. In 3Cr, κ-carbides are present but reduced; in 6Cr they are barely visible. Cr expands Al and C solid solubility in austenite, suppressing κ-carbide precipitation.
- **DO₃ ordered phase in δ-ferrite**: Dark-field TEM images (using (002) superlattice reflection) show densely distributed DO₃ particles. Size in 0Cr: 26 ± 3 nm; in 3Cr: ~30 ± 3 nm.
- **Cold-rolled (CR20%) microstructure**: δ-ferrite volume fraction increases from ~5% (0Cr) to ~18% (6Cr). XRD shows distinct (110)δ and (220)DO₃ peaks in the 6Cr sample. Deformation in austenite is characterized by {111} planar slip bands, whose spacing decreases significantly with Cr content: 371 ± 47 nm (0Cr) → 100 ± 15 nm (3Cr) → 45 ± 13 nm (6Cr) (TEM, bright-field images with [110] zone axis SADP).
- **Post-rolling annealing (CR20% + 600 °C, 3 h)**: Annealing does not significantly alter δ-ferrite morphology or volume fraction. κ-carbides reprecipitate primarily at dislocation entanglements within the retained slip-band structure. In 0Cr: κ-carbide volume fraction ~7%, size ~8 nm. In 3Cr: 4% volume fraction, ~4 nm. In 6Cr: κ-carbides are too fine to be resolved in dark-field TEM.

# Processing-Structure-Property Chain
## As-cast and annealed condition (before cold rolling)
- **0Cr annealed strips** exhibit highest yield strength (YS): ~967 MPa (annealed) vs ~836 MPa (as-cast). The high strength is attributed to coherent nano-scale κ-carbide precipitation in γ-austenite, which provides substantial precipitation strengthening. κ-carbides have orientation relationship [110]γ//[110]κ and are fully coherent with the matrix.
- **3Cr and 6Cr annealed strips**: As Cr content increases, YS decreases in the as-cast condition, consistent with reduced κ-carbide volume fraction. However, ultimate tensile strength (UTS) and total elongation (TE) are not compromised: 6Cr achieves UTS ~1000 MPa with ~38% elongation. Cr addition suppresses κ-carbide precipitation but increases δ-ferrite, shifting strengthening contributions.
- **Strain hardening behavior (annealed)**: The 0Cr steel displays a characteristic double-hump strain hardening rate (SHR) vs true strain curve. The first hump is linked to progressive slip-band refinement; the second hump to microband formation at higher strain. Cr-containing steels exhibit gradually decreasing SHR without pronounced humps.

## Cold-rolled condition (CR20%)
- After 20% cold rolling, **Cr-containing steels (3Cr, 6Cr) show higher strength** than 0Cr steel. CR20% 3Cr and 6Cr reach UTS ~1270 MPa, vs ~1150 MPa for 0Cr (engineering stress–strain curves). This is the reverse of the as-cast/annealed ranking, where 0Cr had higher yield strength.
- **Strain hardening rate**: Cr addition elevates the work hardening rate of CR20% strips. The dense, finely spaced {111} planar slip bands formed during rolling (spacing decreasing from 371 nm in 0Cr to 45 nm in 6Cr) provide strong dislocation storage capacity and act as a primary strengthening mechanism.
- **Post-rolling annealing**: CR20%+annealed strips show limited yield strength increase despite κ-carbide reprecipitation because pre-existing slip-band structures are retained and annealing does not eliminate these deformation features. Annealing causes a decrease in early-stage SHR for 0Cr and 3Cr compared to their CR20% state, but overall strength levels remain higher than as-cast annealed counterparts due to retained work hardening substructure.

# Mechanistic Interpretation
- **κ-carbide suppression by Cr**: Cr expands the solid solubility of Al and C in γ-austenite, reducing the chemical driving force for κ-carbide precipitation. This directly lowers the as-cast/annealed yield strength (removal of precipitation strengthening) but does not degrade strain hardening capacity, as deformation-induced slip-band formation takes over.
- **Double hardening mechanism**: In annealed 0Cr steel, the distinct double-hump SHR arises from a sequential activation of slip-band refinement (early stage, mediated by κ-carbide shearing and planar glide) followed by microband formation (at larger strains). κ-carbide controls early-stage strain hardening rate. In Cr-containing steels, scarce κ-carbide reduces early SHR, and slip-band formation becomes the dominant hardening mechanism.
- **Enhanced strength in cold-rolled Cr-containing steels**: Higher δ-ferrite content and larger pre-existing slip-band densities (smaller spacing) in 3Cr and 6Cr provide abundant obstacles to dislocation motion. TEM of tensile-fractured samples reveals that slip bands in 6Cr are more closely spaced than in 0Cr; scattered dislocations entangle and agglomerate between bands, generating higher dislocation density and stronger work hardening.
- **Deformation features at high strain**: Slip bands extend through low-angle grain boundaries (misorientation ~2.56° in 6Cr) creating small steps, indicating that these boundaries are penetrable during deformation. Microband formation is promoted in Cr-containing steels, while planar slip bands remain dominant in 0Cr. At the γ/δ interface, dislocation entanglements form due to deformation incompatibility between the two phases.

- **TEM of 0Cr, 3Cr, 6Cr as-cast (Figs. 3–5 corresponding to figure_006/007/008)**: BF/DF/SADP images systematically document κ-carbide and DO₃ precipitation. Provides direct evidence that Cr suppresses κ-carbide but leaves DO₃ ordering intact.
