Subfigures (a) and (b) display engineering stress-strain curves for the CR20% and CR20%+annealed conditions, respectively, with y-axis ranging from 0 to 1400 MPa and x-axis showing engineering strain up to 30%. Subfigures (c) and (d) present the corresponding true stress-strain curves (solid lines, left y-axis, 0-1600 MPa) alongside strain hardening rate curves (dashed lines, right y-axis, 0-4000 MPa) plotted against true strain (0-0.25). The curves demonstrate that Cr addition significantly increases both ultimate tensile strength and strain hardening capacity, with 3Cr and 6Cr alloys reaching approximately 1270 MPa compared to ~1150 MPa for the 0Cr steel. The strain hardening rate analysis reveals that Cr elevates the strain hardening rate of CR20% strips, while annealing causes a clear decrease in early-stage strain hardening rates for the 0Cr and 3Cr alloys. These results support the conclusion that high-density slip bands introduced during cold rolling serve as the primary strengthening mechanism, while subsequent annealing promotes κ-carbide precipitation at dislocation entanglements without eliminating the pre-existing deformation microstructures.", "confidence": "high"}
