# Material System  
The investigated alloy system is a family of austenite-based Fe–Mn–Al–C low-density steels with nominal base composition Fe–30Mn–8Al–1C (all in wt%) and systematically varied Cu additions of 0, 1, and 3 wt%. The alloys are designated 0Cu, 1Cu, and 3Cu, respectively. The metallurgical state includes a single-phase γ-austenite (FCC) matrix, κ-carbides, and MC-type NbC carbides, with Cu present either as a solid-solution element or as elemental Cu-enriched grain-boundary precipitates, depending on the Cu content.

# Processing Route and Variables  
All three steels were produced by vacuum induction melting followed by homogenization at 1200 °C for 2 h, multipass hot rolling performed above 850 °C, and air cooling to room temperature. The sole intentionally varied processing variable is the Cu content: 0, 1, and 3 wt%. Corrosion testing was conducted in a 3.5 wt% NaCl solution at room temperature.

# Microstructure and Phase Evolution  
**Grain structure**  
- Optical microscopy with image grey segmentation yields ASTM grain size numbers of 10.2 (0Cu), 9.5 (1Cu), and 9.6 (3Cu). Equivalent diameter distributions show the 0Cu sample has a finer grain structure peaked near 7–8 µm, while 1Cu and 3Cu samples coarsen, with distributions peaking at 13–14 µm and 8–9 µm, respectively. Cu promotes austenite nucleation and growth, leading to a larger average grain size in Cu-containing steels.  

**Carbide evolution**  
- In the 0Cu sample, NbC (MC-type) carbides with an average size of 0.52 µm and a count of 1740 particles are distributed on austenite grain surfaces.  
- The 1Cu addition refines carbides slightly to 0.51 µm while markedly increasing the carbide number to 2377. EPMA maps show Nb and C co-localization at these particles in the 1Cu sample, confirming their NbC identity; slight Cu enrichment is detected along grain boundaries.  
- At 3Cu, the carbide number drops sharply to 876, with average size coarsening to 0.61 µm. EPMA reveals strong Cu segregation along grain boundaries as elemental Cu particles, and some white particles are identified as elemental Cu rather than NbC. Excessive Cu thus hinders carbon diffusion, reduces the carbide population, and causes Cu accumulation at boundaries rather than intragranular carbide refinement.  

**Cu distribution (TEM‑EDS)**  
- In the 3Cu alloy, TEM‑EDS mapping explicitly shows Cu particle aggregation at grain boundaries, while Al, C, Cr, and Fe are homogeneously distributed. Nb and Mo display sparse localized concentrations consistent with MC carbides.  

**Dislocation substructure around carbides (TEM)**  
- In all compositions (0Cu, 1Cu, 3Cu), HRTEM and inverse FFT images reveal lattice distortions and edge dislocations at the carbide–austenite interface. These high-strain-energy interfacial sites are identified as preferential anodic locations for pitting initiation.

# Processing–Structure–Property Chain  
- **Processing**: Increasing Cu content from 0 to 1 and 3 wt% under identical homogenization and hot-rolling conditions.  
- **Structure**: Cu initially (1Cu) refines and multiplies NbC carbides on grain surfaces while coarsening austenite grains; at 3Cu, Cu largely migrates to grain boundaries as elemental Cu, sharply reducing the carbide number and causing heterogeneous boundary segregation.  
- **Electrochemical properties (3.5 wt% NaCl)**:  
  - Potentiodynamic polarization: 0Cu shows the most positive corrosion potential (~ –0.38 V vs SCE) and the widest passive region; increasing Cu shifts the pitting potential in the negative direction, narrows the passive range, and raises the corrosion current density.  
  - EIS: Nyquist plots reveal depressed capacitive semicircles; the charge transfer resistance Rct decreases from ~5796 Ω·cm² (0Cu) to ~2062 Ω·cm² (3Cu). Bode plots show that the low‑frequency impedance modulus |Z| drops from ~6000 Ω·cm² (0Cu) to lower values as Cu increases, while the phase-angle peak shifts to higher frequencies with reduced magnitude, indicating progressive deterioration of passive film stability.  
  - Mott‑Schottky: Donor density ND increases from 0.81×10¹⁸ cm⁻³ (0Cu) → 1.37×10¹⁸ cm⁻³ (1Cu) → 1.45×10¹⁸ cm⁻³ (3Cu), and acceptor density NA rises from 2.62×10¹⁸ → 3.40×10¹⁸ → 6.07×10¹⁸ cm⁻³, demonstrating that Cu alloying raises the charge carrier concentration in the passive film, consistent with higher susceptibility to pitting.  
- **Immersion corrosion (5 days, 3.5 wt% NaCl)**:  
  - Weight-loss curves and average corrosion rates: 0Cu, 0.0546 mm·a⁻¹; 1Cu, ≈0.081 mm·a⁻¹; 3Cu, ≈0.092 mm·a⁻¹.  
  - Morphology evolution: 0Cu develops isolated pits that nucleate predominantly at carbide particles and expand over time. 1Cu exhibits chain‑like connected pits along grain boundaries, characteristic of intergranular corrosion. 3Cu displays a more uniform network of fine corrosion products decorating grain boundaries, accompanied by dispersed pitting, consistent with enhanced microgalvanic activity.  
- **Passive film composition (XPS after potentiostatic polarization at –0.3 V vs Ag–AgCl for 7200 s)**:  
  - 0Cu passive film contains 35.5 % MnO, 30 % MnO₂, 10.9 % Al₂O₃, 4.8 % Fe₃O₄, 7.4 % Fe₂O₃, 5.9 % FeOOH, and 5.5 % metallic Al.  
  - 3Cu passive film composition shifts to 43.5 % MnO, 24.6 % MnO₂, 8.1 % Al₂O₃, 4.3 % Fe₃O₄, 6.7 % Fe₂O₃, 5.4 % FeOOH, plus 1.1 % Cu₂O and 1 % CuO.  
  - After 5‑day immersion, total Mn oxides increase from ~47 % (0Cu) to 77 % (3Cu), while protective Al₂O₃ drops from 38.2 % to 16.3 %. The increasing proportion of Mn oxides and the reduction of Al₂O₃ correlate with accelerated corrosion and diminished passive protection.

# Mechanistic Interpretation  
**0Cu – carbide‑induced pitting:** NbC carbides attached to austenite grain surfaces create lattice‑distorted regions with high dislocation density that act as anodic sites, leading to local pitting in chloride solution. The passive film is relatively enriched in protective Fe and Al oxides, yielding the lowest corrosion rate.  
**1Cu – pitting and incipient intergranular attack:** Moderate Cu addition refines and multiplies carbides, introducing a high number density of galvanic micro‑cells between the nobler carbides and the surrounding austenite matrix. This generates numerous pits. Slight Cu enrichment at grain boundaries begins to contribute to intergranular corrosion propagation, evident as chain‑like pit alignment.  
**3Cu – synergistic microgalvanic corrosion:** Excessive Cu does not fully dissolve but accumulates as elemental Cu particles at grain boundaries. SKPFM shows increased Volta potential differences between grain‑boundary regions and the austenite matrix (1.2 mV for 3Cu vs 0.9 mV for carbide‑matrix in 0Cu). Cu‑enriched boundaries act as cathodes while adjacent austenite dissolves anodically, establishing a synergistic microgalvanic corrosion mechanism together with remaining carbides. This leads to severe intergranular attack, a passive film enriched in Mn oxides and depleted in Al₂O₃, and the highest corrosion rate.

# Key Quantitative Findings  
- ASTM grain size: 10.2 (0Cu), 9.5 (1Cu), 9.6 (3Cu).  
- Carbide count and mean size: 0Cu: 1740 / 0.52 µm; 1Cu: 2377 / 0.51 µm; 3Cu: 876 / 0.61 µm.  
- Corrosion rate: 0Cu 0.0546 mm·a⁻¹, 1Cu ≈0.081 mm·a⁻¹, 3Cu ≈0.092 mm·a⁻¹.  
- Rct (EIS): ≈5796 Ω·cm² (0Cu) → ~2062 Ω·cm² (3Cu).  
- ND: 0.81×10¹⁸ cm⁻³ (0Cu) → 1.45×10¹⁸ cm⁻³ (3Cu); NA: 2.62×10¹⁸ cm⁻³ → 6.07×10¹⁸ cm⁻³.  
- Passive film oxide proportion shift (potentiostatic): Al₂O₃ decreases from 10.9 % (0Cu) to 8.1 % (3Cu); Cu₂O + CuO appear only in 3Cu (2.1 %). After 5‑day immersion: Al₂O₃ drops from 38.2 % to 16.3 %, total Mn oxides rise from 47 % to 77 %.  
- SKPFM Volta potential difference: 0.9 mV (carbide‑matrix, 0Cu) vs 1.2 mV (grain‑boundary‑matrix, 3Cu).

# Visual Evidence  
- **Figure 1 (SEM + XRD):** Shows equiaxed austenite grains with white precipitates. In 0Cu, sparse NbC carbides on grain surfaces; in 1Cu, dense fine particles on surfaces with minimal boundary decoration; in 3Cu, granular white objects enrich at grain boundaries. XRD confirms single‑phase γ‑austenite (FCC) in all three compositions, with no second‑phase diffraction peaks detected.  
- **Figure 2 (OM grain size):** Color‑coded grain‑boundary identification with histograms demonstrates Cu‑induced grain coarsening; ASTM numbers and equivalent diameter shifts quantify the grain‑size evolution.  
- **Figure 3 (EPMA mapping):** In 1Cu, Nb and C co‑localize at white particles (NbC), with weak Cu enrichment at boundaries; in 3Cu, strong Cu segregation along boundaries and identification of some white particles as elemental Cu, not carbides.  
- **Figure 4 (carbide quantification):** Bars and histograms document the non‑monotonic evolution of carbide count and size with Cu content, confirming carbide refinement at 1Cu and severe carbide reduction plus coarsening at 3Cu.  
- **Figure 5 (polarization curves):** Tafel‑type plots show the negative shift of pitting potential, narrowing of passive region, and increase in corrosion current density with Cu addition.  
- **Figure 6 (EIS):** Nyquist arc radius reduction and Bode‑plot trends (lower |Z|, phase‑angle peak shift) provide direct evidence of decreasing passive‑film stability as Cu increases.  
- **Figure 7 (Mott‑Schottky):** Three‑region C⁻² vs V plots quantify the increase in donor and acceptor densities, linking Cu addition to higher defect concentrations in the passive film and enhanced pitting susceptibility.  
- **Figures 8 (corrosion morphology time series):** OM sequences over 5 days contrast isolated pit growth (0Cu), chain‑like intergranular attack (1Cu), and network‑like boundary corrosion with fine products (3Cu), visually corroborating the corrosion‑rate trend.  
- **Figure 9 (weight loss and corrosion rate):** Quantitative plots confirm the progressive increase in weight loss and corrosion rate from 0Cu to 3Cu.  
- **Figure 10 (AFM/SKPFM):** Topography and Volta potential maps plus line profiles show a larger potential difference between grain boundaries and matrix in 3Cu (1.2 mV) compared to carbide‑matrix contrast in 0Cu (0.9 mV), supporting the microgalvanic mechanism.  
- **Figures 11–13 (TEM, 0Cu–3Cu):** Bright‑field, SAED, dark‑field, HRTEM, inverse FFT, and EDS data confirm the austenitic matrix, the presence of NbC and κ‑carbides, and the lattice distortions/edge dislocations at carbide–matrix interfaces that serve as anodic pitting sites in all three steels.  
- **Figure 14 (TEM‑EDS of 3Cu grain boundary):** Cu elemental map shows explicit Cu particle aggregation at grain boundaries, while Al, C, Cr, Fe are homogeneous and Nb, Mo appear locally, substantiating the Cu‑enrichment model.  
- **Figures 15, 16, 17 (XPS passive film and corrosion products):** Deconvoluted Fe 2p, Mn 2p, Al 2p, O 1s, and Cu 2p spectra plus stacked bar charts quantify the shift in oxide composition—particularly the loss of Al₂O₃ and gain of Mn oxides—that explains the degradation of passive protection with Cu addition.  
- **Figure 18 (corrosion schematic):** Summarizes the mechanistic model: for 0Cu, carbide‑induced pitting; for 1Cu, refined carbides create multiple galvanic pits; for 3Cu, Cu‑enriched grain boundaries synergize with carbides to cause severe microgalvanic intergranular corrosion.
