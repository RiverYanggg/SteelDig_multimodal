# A B S T R A C T

The microstructure and mechanical properties of Fe-35Mn-xAl-0.1C (x = 4, 6, 8, 10) low-density steels were studied using scanning electron microscopy (SEM), transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and tensile machine. It is found that the microstructure evolves with the increasing of Al content from a single austenite phase steel for 4 %Al, to a ferrite-based duplex steel for 8 %Al, but nearly a fully ferrite phase steel with 10 %Al. As the Al content is increased from 4 wt % to 8 wt %, the yield strength and tensile strength are significantly increased from 239 MPa to 552 MPa–455 MPa and 740 MPa, respectively, while the uniform elongation is only slightly decreased from 38.5 % to 31.0 %. However, as the Al content is increased up to 10 %, the yield strength is gradually increased to 585 MPa, but the tensile elongation is sharply decreased down to about 4.6 %. Based on the thorough microstructure examination of the dislocation type and dislocation pattern and the microstructure evolution during tensile deformation, it is concluded that the excellent combination of the mechanical property of the 8Al steel is realized by the activation of the edge-typed dislocation and dynamic slip band refinement in the ferrite grain mitigated by the surrounded austenite grains, which enhanced the strain hardening rate significantly. This study not only reveals a new ductility-enhancing mechanism of ferrite + austenite duplex steel, but also provides a new route for designing BCC based low-density steel with high strength and high ductility.

# 1. Introduction

As one of the new generation materials, the lightweight steel has been paid much more attention over the past two decades due to its excellent combination of mechanical properties and low density [1–6]. The lightweight steels mainly include austenitic low-density steel with high carbon, high manganese, and high aluminum contents, the duplex low-density steel with relatively low contents of carbon, manganese, and aluminum, and the ferritic low-density steel with the lowest contents of carbon, manganese, and aluminum [2]. Al-alloyed low-density steels exhibit an attractive combination of yield strength, tensile strength, elongation and work-hardening capacity [2,7–9]. The excellent mechanical properties of high stacking fault energy (SFE) austenitic low-density steel are attributed to the planar glide of dislocations and the dynamic slip band refinement (DSBR) mechanism [10–13]. The planar glide of dislocations in high stacking fault energy austenite originates from the “slip plane softening” effect caused by dislocation shearing of ordered domains [12].

In ferritic low-density steel, plastic instability exists and is induced by the introduction of B2 and DO ordered domains [14–16]. It was found that the dislocations gradually transitioned from single mixed dislocations to paired screw dislocations with the increase of Al content in Fe-Al ferritic low-density steel [14,16], which was associated with low-energy screw dislocations [14] and high Peierls-Nabarro stresses [16]. In traditional fully body-centered cubic (BCC) alloys, the gliding velocities of edge dislocations are typically much higher than those of screw dislocations due to the low Peierls-Nabarro stresses [17–19]. As a result, edge dislocation segments are readily annihilated during deformation, and screw dislocations survive in the deformed microstructure [20,21]. However, edge dislocations are observed in multi-component BCC phases, such as titanium alloys [22] and high-entropy alloys [20, 21]. In these alloys, the significant increase of Peierls-Nabarro stress for edge dislocation glide, caused by substantial local atomic volume mismatch or ordered domains, may effectively impede dislocation motion and consequently promote extensive dislocation multiplication [20]. However, edge dislocations in ordered ferrite have not been reported to date in ferritic matrix lightweight steels.

The research on duplex low-density steels primarily focused on austenite-based duplex low-density steels [23–25]. The introduction of B2 and $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 }$ ordered phases into high-aluminum ferrite can bring a significant enhancement in yield strength, but simultaneously lead to an increase in brittleness [15,23,24,26,27]. It was found that B2 and DO3 ordered domains appear in Fe-Al alloys during the cooling process when the Al content exceeds 6.2 % [28,29]. In the Fe21Mn10Al1C5Ni lightweight steel, dislocations within the B2 particles exhibit planar glide characteristics, which may be attributed to the dislocation shearing of $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 }$ nanoparticles [24]. Furthermore, planar slip of dislocations associated with the shearing of ordered structures has also been observed in titanium alloys [30,31] and high-entropy alloys [32]. Therefore, the dislocation deformation characteristics of ferrite will be significantly influenced by the interactions between dislocations and ordered phases. However, the systematic work in this aspect has not yet been conducted to investigate the work hardening mechanisms of high-aluminum ferritic matrix-based lightweight steels.

To address this issue of both dislocation patterns and work hardening behaviors, Fe35MnxAl0.1C (x = 4, 6, 8, 10 wt%) low-density steels were designed. Among them, Fe35Mn4Al0.1C is an austenitic lightweight steel, Fe35Mn6Al0.1C is an austenite based duplex lightweight steel, and Fe35Mn8Al0.1C and Fe35Mn10Al0.1C are ferrite based duplex lightweight steels. Tensile deformations were carried out to reveal the microstructural effects on the work hardening behaviors and mechanical properties of the designed steels. And both scanning electron microscope (SEM) and transmission electron microscope (TEM) were applied to reveal the microstructure evolution in terms of dislocation patterns of the designed steel during deformation process. The effects of microstructure on the dislocation type, dislocation pattern, work hardening behavior and mechanical property will be discussed, which is expected to give a thorough understanding on the evolution of dislocation pattern and the work hardening behavior of the ferrite-based duplex lightweight steel.

# 2. Materials and experimental details

Four ingots with nominal compositions of Fe-35Mn-xAl-1C-0.1Nb-0.1V-1Mo-1W (x = 4, 6, 8, 10 wt%) were prepared via vacuum induction melting under a high-purity Ar atmosphere. The as-cast ingots were homogenized at 1150 ◦C for 4 h, and subsequently hot-forged into rods with a diameter of 16.5 mm. The bars were solution-treated at $1 0 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ $^ { \mathrm { f o r ~ 2 ~ h , } }$ followed by oil quenching, to achieve a microstructure with nearly equiaxed grains. For simplicity, the four solution-treated steels are hereafter referred to as 4Al, 6Al, 8Al, and 10Al steels, respectively. The compositions of the solution-treated samples were examined by the wet-chemical analysis, and the results were shown in Table 1. The compositions of austenite phase and ferrite phase of the designed steels were investigated using the electron probe microanalysis (EPMA). The carbon content in austenite was calculated using the chemical composition lever rule [1,24]. The densities of the investigated steels, measured using the principle of Archimedes, are 7.42, 7.18, 6.99, and $6 . 8 4 ~ \mathrm { g ~ c m } ^ { - 3 }$ , respectively.

$$
\gamma_ {S F E} = 2 \rho \Delta G ^ {\gamma \rightarrow \varepsilon} + 2 \sigma \tag {3}
$$

where $\rho$ is the molar surface density along {111} planes, $\Delta G ^ { \gamma \to \varepsilon }$ is the molar Gibbs energy of the phase transformation from γ to ε, and σ is the interfacial energy per unit area of the $\gamma / \varepsilon$ phase boundary. The calculated $\gamma _ { S F E }$ was recorded in Table 2 by using thermodynamic functions and parameters listed in literature [35].

The microstructure was characterized in details by electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) in a scanning electron microscope (JSM-7800 F) and transmission electron microscopes (TEMs) operated at 200 kV (FEI-Tecnai G2 F20 and FEI talos F200X). The original data obtained from EBSD with a scanning step size of 1 μm was analyzed by the HKL channel 5 software. Tensile testing was performed on standard specimens (5 mm in diameter, 25 mm in gauge length) machined from waterquenched steel rods, with the tensile axis oriented parallel to the rod axis. The stress-strain responses and corresponding strain hardening rates of solution-treated specimens were determined with a strain rate of $1 . 0 \times 1 0 ^ { - 3 } \ : s ^ { - 1 }$ at room temperature using an MTS tensile machine. At least three tensile specimens were tested to fracture to ensure experimental reproducibility. Interrupted tensile tests were conducted to engineering strains of 3 %, 10 %, 20 ${ \% } ,$ and 28 % to investigate the microstructural evolution and to elucidate the deformation mechanisms.

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Initial microstructures

The microstructures of the 4Al, 6Al, 8Al, and 10Al steels after solution treatment are shown by the phase distribution maps in Fig. 1(a–d). EBSD analysis indicates that 4Al steel is a single-phase face-centered cubic $( { \mathrm { F C C } } ) \ Y$ austenite lightweight steel, 6Al steel is an austenite-based duplex lightweight steel, and 8Al and 10Al steels are ferrite-based duplex lightweight steels. It also shows that the microstructures with quasi-equiaxed grains are obtained under the solution treatment conditions. It can be observed that the austenite grains contain numerous annealing twin boundaries (represented by white lines).

Fig. 1(e–h) shows the selected area diffraction patterns (SADPs) of 4Al, 6Al, 8Al, and 10Al steels along the [100]γ zone axis of the austenite grains, where obvious satellite diffraction spots can be seen near the γ diffraction spots. The satellite diffraction spots near the $\gamma$ diffraction spots are related to the concentration fluctuations of Al and C atoms [11, 36], indicating that spinodal decomposition occurred during quenching. Very weak diffraction spots are identified at the {110} positions in 6Al, 8Al, and 10Al steels. In previous studies, this kind of ordered structure is revealed to be an $\mathrm { L } 1 _ { 2 }$ type short-range ordered crystal structure [11,37]. It can be seen that the intensity of these satellite diffraction spots decreases with increasing of the nominal Al content, which is revealed to be an $\mathbf { L } 1 _ { 2 }$ type short-range ordered crystal structure.

Fig. 1(i–k, m) shows the selected area diffraction patterns (SADPs) of 6Al, 8Al and 10Al steels along the $[ 1 1 0 ] _ { \alpha }$ zone axis of the ferrite grains, where distinct superlattice diffraction spots are observed. When h, k and l (Miller indices) are all even, the diffraction spots belong to both B2 and $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 } .$ When h, k and l are all odd, the diffraction spots belong only to $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 }$ $[ 1 1 , 1 6 ]$ . Fig. 1(j–l, n) presents the $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 }$ dark-field (DF) images acquired from the {111} and $\{ 0 0 2 \} _ { \mathrm { D O 3 } }$ diffraction spots in the same field of view for the 6Al, 8Al and 10Al steels, respectively. The green arrows show that the dark-field image acquired from the $\{ 0 0 2 \} _ { \mathrm { D O 3 } }$ reflection exhibits a lower intensity or approximate intensity than that obtained from the $\{ 1 1 1 \} _ { \mathrm { D O 3 } }$ reflection, indicating the coexistence of main $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 }$ domains in the ferritic matrix. The yellow arrows indicate that the dark-field image acquired from the $\{ 0 0 2 \} _ { \mathrm { D O 3 } }$ reflection exhibits a higher intensity than that obtained from the {111} reflection, indicating the coexistence of $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 }$ domains and B2 domains within the ferritic matrix. Therefore, $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 }$ ordered domains coexist within both the B2 ordered domains and the ferrite matrix [23]. The maximum sizes of $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 }$ ordered domains in the 6Al, 8Al, and 10Al steels are estimated to be approximately 2.0 nm, 2.1 nm, and 19 nm, respectively. # 3.2. Mechanical properties

The typical tensile engineering stress-strain curves and mechanical properties of 4Al, 6Al, 8Al, and 10Al steels are shown in Table 3 and Fig. 3(a and b). It can be observed that as the Al content increases from 4 wt% to 8 wt%, the yield strength and tensile strength increase from 239 MPa to 552 MPa–455 MPa and 740 MPa, respectively, while the uniform elongation only decreases from 38 % to 31 %. However, as the Al content increased up to 10 %, the yield strength is increased to 585 MPa, but the tensile elongation is only about 4.6 %, exhibiting the brittle characteristics. As demonstrated in Section 3.1, the degradation in plasticity of the 10Al steel can be attributed to the coarsening of $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 }$ ordered domains [15] and the formation of 18R martensite [42].

Fig. 3 (c, d) presents the true stress-strain curves and the corresponding strain hardening curves for the 4Al, 6Al, 8Al, and 10Al steels. It can be seen that the 4Al and 6Al steels exhibit typical three-stage strain hardening characteristics, labeled as A, D and E, whereas the 8Al steel displays five distinct strain hardening stages, labeled as A, B, C, D and $\mathrm { E } ,$ which is similar to the strain hardening behavior exhibited by highcarbon, high-aluminum austenitic lightweight steels. This indicates that the deformation mechanism of the designed 8Al ferrite-based duplex lightweight steels differs from that of conventional ferrite-

# 3.3. Deformed microstructure characteristics

The microstructure of the uniform deformation zone of the experimental steels after tensile deformation is shown in Fig. 4. Due to the plastic instability of the 10Al steel, the microstructures after fracture at \~3 % engineering strain are shown in Fig. 4(g–i). It can be observed that the deformation mechanisms of austenite $( \mathrm { F i g } , 4 ( \mathbf { a } , \mathbf { b } , \mathbf { d } , \mathbf { e } , \mathbf { g } ) )$ and ferrite (Fig. 4(c–f, h, i)) are distinctly different. The microstructure of the austenite after tensile deformation is characterized by dislocation cell blocks regardless of the aluminum content, as shown in Fig. 4(a, b, d), which is common in low-carbon or low-aluminum lightweight steels [1, 45,46]. Additionally, a small number of deformation twins are observed in 8Al steel (Fig. 4(e)), which is associated with the localization of stress [1,47]. Fig. 4 (g) shows that the 10Al steel exhibits complex planar slip characteristics during the early deformation stage of austenite, which is similar to the deformation structure of the 6Al steel reported in our previous study [1]. The difference in dislocation deformation structure between this kind of low carbon austenitic lightweight steel and the traditional high carbon austenitic lightweight steel may be associated with the carbon induced suppression of cross-slip [46] and the development of the ordered structure [11].

It is very interesting that the dislocations in ferrite exhibit distinct planar slip characteristics, as shown in Fig. 4(c–f, h, i), which is quite rare in BCC structures [20,21]. It can be observed that the ferrite dislocation structure in 8Al steel exhibits a more pronounced planar slip characteristic compared with that in 6Al steel, indicating that the increase in Al content may promote planar slip of ferrite dislocations. However, the increase in Al content also makes the 10Al steel particularly brittle. Fig. 4(h, i) shows that only a small number of long straight dislocations are activated in the ferrite matrix. These long straight dislocations are identified as screw dislocations based on the discussion in Section 4.1. Brittle fracture caused by the activation of only a few long straight screw dislocations has also been observed in the TiZrHfNb0.4 BCC high-entropy alloy [20]. The planar slip of dislocations in ferrite will be discussed in detail in Section 4.2.

When the engineering strain reaches 3 %, planar dislocation slip along the slip planes and distinct cross-slip are observed in the austenite $\left( \mathrm { F i g . ~ 5 ~ ( a ) } \right)$ , while the ferrite is primarily characterized by a planar dislocation structure (Fig. 5 (b)). As the strain further increases to 10 %, cross-slip of dislocations in austenite becomes more pronounced (Fig. 5(d)), and the slip bands composed of dislocations in ferrite continue to evolve (Fig. 5(e)). When the engineering strain further increases to 20 %, the dislocation cell blocks (Fig. 5(f)) and dislocation cells (Fig. 5(g)) are formed in the austenite, and the slip bands in the ferrite are further refined (Fig. 5(h)). # 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Identification of dislocation types in ferrite of 8Al and 10Al steel

There are significant differences in the micro-deformation behavior in ferrite of 10Al steel and 8Al steel, which are closely related to the activation of different types of dislocations. Here, the two-beam diffraction mode has been employed to investigate the types of dislocations in ferrite of 10Al steel and 8Al steel. To better observe the dislocations, the samples are pre-deformed to the 3 % engineering strain. Fig. 7(a–f) presents the dislocation structures in ferrite of 10Al steel using the different g diffraction vectors near the [111] zone axis.

To identify the Burgers vector b of the ferrite, the g⋅b analyses are conducted. When g = [10 1] and $g = [ 1 \overline { { 1 } } 0 ]$ operating vector, some long straight dislocations systematically lose contrast (Fig. 7(a and b)), whereas when $g = [ 0 \mathrm { ~ \overline { { 1 } } ~ }$ 1] operating vector, all dislocations are visible (Fig. 7(c)). It can be concluded that the Burgers vector b is determined to be a/2[111] by the $g \cdot b = 0$ analysis of the ferrite in 10Al steel, as shown in Fig. 7(a–c). Similarly, the Burgers vector of dislocations in ferrite of 8Al steel is also analyzed near the [111] zone axis, as shown in Fig. 7 (g–l). Some dislocations systematically lost contrast under the [0 1 1] and [1 10] operating vector conditions. Subsequently, the Burgers vector b is also determined to be a/2[111] through the g⋅b = 0 analysis of the ferrite in 8Al steel.

Fig. 7(a–l) shows that the dislocation structures are quite different although the Burgers vectors of dislocations in ferrite of both 10Al steel and 8Al steel are of the a/2 < 111 > type. A predominant fraction of the dislocations in the ferrite of 10Al steel are identified as long straight screw dislocations, and their distribution is non-uniform, as shown in Fig. 7(a–f). These long straight screw dislocations are commonly observed in Fe-Al ordered alloys [14–16,48], which can be attributed at least partially to the significant energy difference between edge and screw dislocations, where the energy of screw dislocations is notably lower than that of edge dislocations [14].

Comparing with the long straight screw dislocations in 10Al steel, a substantial number of edge dislocations and mixed dislocations with near-edge character are uniformly distributed in the ferrite phase of 8Al steel. This homogeneous distribution of dislocations can effectively mitigate brittle fracture caused by stress concentration. It is reported in conventional body-centered cubic (BCC) alloys, the gliding velocities of edge dislocations are typically much higher than those of screw dislocations [18,19], owing to the lower resistance stress. Consequently, the edge segments are more readily annihilated within BCC alloys, leaving screw dislocations as the predominant type in the deformed microstructures [21]. However, edge dislocations have also been reported in the BCC-structured Mo-Nb-Ti multi-principal element alloy (MPEA) [49] and Ti-Zr-Hf-Nb high-entropy alloy (HEA) [20]. The local chemical fluctuations in these alloys result in relatively high glide resistance for edge dislocations on the glide plane, making it less likely for edge dislocations to annihilate. Similarly, B2 and DO ordered domains increase the resistance stress to the glide of edge dislocations in the ferrite of 8Al steel, which reduces the glide velocity of the edge dislocations and consequently leads to a significant multiplication of edge dislocations in 8Al steel.

# 4.2. The planar slip of dislocations in ferrite

In body-centered cubic (BCC) materials, screw dislocations typically exhibit pronounced cross-slip behavior due to their non-planar glide characteristics [52], which is fundamentally linked to the asymmetric core structure of screw dislocations and their high Peierls-Nabarro stress [53,54]. Interestingly, both edge and screw dislocations in 8Al steel and 10Al steel show distinct planar slip, as illustrated in Figs. 5 and 7. Studies demonstrate that the interaction between dislocations and ordered structures can lead to planar slip of dislocations, as observed in titanium alloys [30,31], FCC-structured Fe-Mn-Al-C alloys [12,13], and BCC-structured TiZrHfNbTa0.2 high-entropy alloys (HEAs) [32].

# 4.3. Strain hardening behavior of 8Al steel

Fig. 3(d) reveals that 8Al steel exhibits multi-stage strain hardening, which is associated with the dynamic slip band refinement mechanism in ferrite. In stage A, the movement of dislocations leads to a sharp decrease in the strain hardening rate. In stage B, a reduction in the strain hardening rate is caused by the slip plane softening effect induced by dislocation shearing of the $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 }$ long-range ordered domains [13]. In stage $\mathrm { C } ,$ the passing stress generated by slip bands of stored dislocations has been demonstrated to be the primary contributor to strain hardening [12]. With the increase of strain, the number of slip bands increases, the spacing between slip bands decreases, and the passing stress rises, which leads to an increase in the strain hardening rate. In stage D, with the continued increase of strain, the slip bands become nearly saturated, and cross-slip of dislocations is activated, which leads to a decrease in the strain hardening rate. In stage $\mathrm { E } ,$ localization of stress results in a rapid decline in the strain hardening rate and leads to plastic failure.

The deformation structures of ferrite and austenite in 8Al steel are dominated by dislocations until a small amount of deformation twins are activated in austenite near the maximum force. Therefore, the excellent strain hardening performance of 8Al steel is attributed to the refinement of ferrite slip bands and austenite dislocation cells. In the subsequent calculations, the flow stress of 8Al steel is estimated using the average spacing of ferrite slip bands and the average dislocation cell size of austenite. In dislocation-mediated plastic materials, the total flow stress $\sigma _ { t o t }$ can be expressed by the following equation [57,58]:

$$
\sigma_ {t o t} = \sigma_ {0} + \sigma (\varepsilon) \tag {5}
$$

where $\sigma _ { 0 }$ represents the deformation-independent strengthening contribution, i.e., the yield stress, and the strain hardening term σ(ε) characterizes the evolution of flow stress during plastic deformation. The strain hardening term σ(ε) is composed of two parts, ferrite and austenite, and can be expressed as follows:

$$
\sigma (\varepsilon) = 0. 7 5 7 \sigma_ {\alpha} (\varepsilon) + 0. 2 4 3 \sigma_ {\gamma} (\varepsilon) \tag {6}
$$

where 75.7 % and 24.3 % represent the mass fractions of ferrite (α) and austenite (γ), respectively, and $\sigma _ { \alpha } ( \varepsilon )$ and $\sigma _ { \gamma } ( \varepsilon )$ denote the strain hardening terms for ferritic and austenitic phases during plastic deformation.

The ferrite strain hardening contribution from slip band refinement is determined through passing stress analysis [12,59]:

$$
\sigma_ {(\alpha)} (\varepsilon) = K M G b / D \tag {7}
$$

where K is a geometrical factor, M = 2.8 [56,60] is the Taylor-factor, G = 80.7 GPa [56] is the shear modulus, b = 0.25 nm [56] is the magnitude of the Burgers vector, and D is the mean slip band spacing (Fig. 6).

The austenite strain hardening contribution arising from slip band, dislocation cell block, and dislocation cell refinement is derived from an empirical relationship [45,46,61]:

$$
\sigma_ {(\gamma)} (\varepsilon) = K _ {S B} M _ {S B} G b / D + 0. 8 K _ {D C B} M _ {D C B} G b / D + 0. 2 K _ {D C} M _ {D C} G b / D \tag {8}
$$

Where K is the similitude coefficient, M is the Taylor factor $( M _ { S B } = M _ { D C B }$ = 3.06, M = 2.44) [46], G = 70 GPa [46] denotes the shear modulus, b = 0.25 nm [62] represents the magnitude of the Burgers vector, and D corresponds to the slip band spacing, dislocation cell block size, or dislocation cell size (Fig. 6). The values 0.8 and 0.2 are the area fractions of the dislocation cell blocks and the dislocation cells, respectively.

In order to calculate the coefficient of K in the austenite phase, Fig. 9 (a) presents the relation (8) for the 4Al steel, from which the similitude constant K is obtained as $K _ { S B + D C B } = 2 . 0 3 , K _ { D C } = 2 . 0 2$ . This value is very close to the reported K values of 2.0–2.9 for austenitic stainless steel [63, 64]. The austenite phases in 4Al and 8Al steels possess similar compositional systems and deformation structures. Studies [45,46] have shown that the similarity coefficient K essentially remains unchanged with variations in C and Al content for high manganese steels with similar deformation structures. Therefore, the similarity constant of austenite in 8Al steel is approximated by that in 4Al steel.
