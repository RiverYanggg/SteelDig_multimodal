Material System  
The study examines three hot-rolled austenitic low-density steels (LDS) with systematic compositional variations to isolate the role of the B2 ordered Fe(Ni)Al intermetallic phase. The alloys are:

- Ni-free LDS: Fe‑28Mn‑9Al‑0.9C (wt.%, assumed nominal). This alloy serves as the reference and contains a fully austenitic γ matrix with fine, intragranular κ‑carbides (L1₂ ordered (Fe,Mn)₃AlC). No B2 phase is present.
- Ni‑containing high‑Mn LDS: Fe‑28Mn‑9Al‑5Ni‑0.9C. The addition of 5 wt% Ni preserves the high Mn content while introducing the B2 intermetallic alongside κ‑carbides in the γ matrix.
- Ni‑containing low‑Mn LDS: Fe‑15Mn‑9Al‑5Ni‑0.9C. The Mn level is reduced to 15 wt%, resulting in coarser κ‑carbides and a pronounced B2 phase that appears in two morphologies: bamboo‑like stringers along the rolling direction and equiaxed polygonal grains.

Processing Route and Variables  
All three alloys were processed by hot rolling. Subsequent deformation was applied during room‑temperature tensile testing and nanoindentation. The main process‑relevant variables that differentiate the steels are the alloy compositions—specifically the manganese and nickel contents—which directly control the stability and fraction of the B2 phase and the size of κ‑carbides.

Microstructure and Phase Evolution  
- In the Ni‑free LDS, the microstructure consists of equiaxed γ grains containing a high density of fine, coherent κ‑carbides (L1₂). No intermetallic B2 particles are observed.  
- The Ni‑containing high‑Mn LDS retains a γ‑austenite matrix but now also contains both κ‑carbides (coarser than in the Ni‑free alloy) and discrete B2 Fe(Ni)Al particles distributed throughout the matrix. The presence of Ni stabilizes the B2 ordering.  
- The Ni‑containing low‑Mn LDS exhibits a similar γ‑matrix but with even coarser κ‑carbides. The B2 phase is more abundant and adopts a mixed morphology: bamboo‑like stringers elongated along the hot‑rolling direction and polygonal B2 grains, indicating a stronger tendency for B2 formation at lower Mn content.  
- Orientation relationships and automated crystal orientation mapping (ACOM) in transmission electron microscopy reveal that B2 and γ phases share specific crystallographic alignment, facilitating slip transfer during deformation.

Processing–Structure–Property Chain  
The compositional changes (processing variable) directly modify the phase constitution and morphology (structure), which in turn dictates the mechanical response (properties):  
1. **Strength** – The introduction of the hard, ordered B2 intermetallic increases the tensile strength of the Ni‑containing alloys relative to the Ni‑free LDS. Nanoindentation measurements show that B2 regions exhibit higher hardness and display distinct “pop‑in” events indicative of dislocation nucleation barriers.  
2. **Ductility** – Total tensile elongation is reduced in the B2‑bearing steels compared to the Ni‑free alloy. The B2 phase, being brittle at room temperature, serves as a site for early crack initiation, especially at the interfaces with the γ matrix.  
3. **Strain hardening** – The Ni‑free LDS develops a uniform, high‑density dislocation substructure within the γ grains, leading to sustained strain hardening. In the B2‑containing steels, strain hardening is influenced by the co‑deformation of the ductile γ and the harder B2. Slip transfer from B2 to γ and vice versa governs local strain accumulation; dislocation cells form preferentially in the γ regions adjacent to B2 particles, while the B2 phase itself shows limited dislocation activity and eventually cracks, causing a deviation in the hardening rate.  
4. **Nanoindentation response** – Load–displacement curves from nanoindentation reveal that the B2 phase exhibits pop‑in bursts, attributed to incipient plasticity events, whereas the γ matrix shows a more continuous deformation. The pop‑in load gives a measure of the critical stress for dislocation nucleation in the intermetallic.

Mechanistic Interpretation  
The central mechanism identified is **co‑deformation controlled by slip transfer** between the ductile γ matrix and the ordered B2 intermetallic. Crystallographic orientation relationships (determined via ACOM‑TEM) enable easy glide transmission at certain B2/γ interfaces, allowing the two phases to deform together up to the yield point. Beyond yield, strain incompatibility accumulates because the B2 phase has limited slip systems and high Peierls stress, leading to stress concentration at the interfaces. This triggers cracking of the B2 particles and accelerates the formation of geometrically necessary dislocations in the adjacent γ regions. The evolution of the dislocation substructure—from planar arrays to dense cell structures near B2 particles—explains the differences in strain hardening behavior among the alloys. In the Ni‑free steel, dislocation glide is more homogeneous, resulting in higher uniform elongation.
