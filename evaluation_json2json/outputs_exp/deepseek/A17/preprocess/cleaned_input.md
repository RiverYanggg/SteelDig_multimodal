# ABSTRACT

The present study deals with the synthesis of a novel $\mathrm { F e _ { 4 0 } M n _ { 1 9 } N i _ { 1 5 } A l _ { 1 5 } S i _ { 1 0 } C _ { 1 } }$ low-density high entropy steel (HES) processed through mechanical alloying (MA) and spark plasma sintering (SPS). The elemental powder was milled for 35 h, leading to the formation of multiphase structures i.e., BCC phase $( \mathsf { a } = 0 . 2 8 6$ nm; ${ \mathrm { c I 2 } } ) , \tau$ -brass type (a = 0.872 nm; cI52) and partially ordered B2-type $( \mathbf { a } = 0 . 2 9 0$ nm; cP2) phases with the trace amount of Si. The milled powder was found to be thermally stable up $\mathbf { t o } \sim 5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C } ,$ , however, the formation of Fe5Si3-type phase was evident at \~520 ◦C. The spark plasma sintered (SPSed) sample was able to retain the BCC phase (a = 0.286 nm), γ-brass type $( \mathbf { a } = 0 . 8 7 2$ nm), B2 $( \mathsf { a } = 0 . 2 8 9$ nm) along with the formation of $\mathrm { F e } _ { 5 } \mathrm { S i } _ { 3 }$ type silicide phase ${ \mathfrak { a } } =$ b = 0.667 nm, c = 0.468 nm; hP16). These SPSed HES sample was found to have low density $( \sim 6 . 4 9 \pm 0 . 3 \mathrm { ~ g ~ }$ cm− 3 ), high microhardness (\~7.8 ± 0.3 GPa) and good compressive strength (\~2046 ± 160 MPa) with an appreciable ductility of \~19%. The enhanced mechanical properties of the SPSed sample can be attributed to dual-phase microstructure i.e., BCC and B2 along with finer nano-sized silicide precipitates leading to dominant strengthening mechanisms (i.e., grain-boundary and dislocation strengthening). The experimental findings on phase evolution, thermal stability and mechanical properties of non-equiatomic HES were correlated with the various thermodynamic parameters, CALPHAD modelling and strengthening mechanisms. The significance of the present study is to design a high entropy steel containing the dual phase microstructures i.e., BCC as major phase and B2 as secondary phase, for giving rise to high mechanical strength and high temperature stability.

# 1. Introduction

The thrust for development of advanced materials having a synergy between strength and ductility has been evolving in the modern era of industrialization. The urge for high strength advanced materials led to the emergence of high entropy alloys (HEAs). The HEAs are usually disordered single-phase solid solution consisting of five or more major alloying elements in equiatomic or near-equiatomic compositions [1]. HEAs have allured the materials industries across the world due to their excellent mechanical and functional properties, and attracted attention from the research community [2]. These HEAs can be sub-categorized into various classes based on the types of alloying elements, microstructure and/or phases formed $\mathrm { i . e . , }$ , eutectic [3], non-equiatomic [4], precipitation strengthened [5], interstitial [6] HEAs, TRIP and TWIP HEAs [7,8], high entropy intermetallics [9], high entropy nanoparticles [10], and high entropy steels [11].

The HEAs are mainly prepared through liquid metallurgy route $\mathrm { i . e . , }$ vacuum arc melting or vacuum induction melting. However, this route has some limitations owing to porosity, segregation, difference in melting point and density among thealloying elements. Powder metallurgy route $( \mathrm { i . e . , }$ mechanical alloying (MA) followed by sintering.) have overcome these problems and also easily produced the nanocrystalline materials with homogenous solid solution [12]. In addition, this technique can expand the solid solubility even in the immiscible alloys [13].

Spark plasma sintering (SPS) has become a popular technique for consolidation of mechanically alloyed powders. The high heating rate during SPS can be attributed to the generation of high-power current (pulsed). The samples consolidated by SPS can effectively retain the nanocrystalline features [14]. A few researchers revealed the nanocrystalline nature of FCC phase along with $\mathrm { C r } _ { 7 } \mathrm { C } _ { 3 }$ in CrFeCoNi and CrMnFeCoNi HEAs processed by mechanical alloying (MA) and SPS [15]. These HEAs based composites’ mechanical properties were almost similar to those of a few advanced composites prepared by powder metallurgical processing $\mathrm { e . g . , C u { - S i C , A l _ { 2 } O _ { 3 } { - } W C { - } C O \ [ 1 6 , 1 7 ] } }$ .

The structural material used for high temperature applications ought to have phase stability up to higher temperatures. Therefore, it is of paramount importance to establish the thermal stability of these HEAs to ascertain two aspects i.e., phase transformation and the retention of nanosized structure. The CrFeCoNi HEA has revealed the high resistance to grain growth even at long duration of heat treatment at $9 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 600 h [15]. Shivam et al. [18] synthesized AlCoCrFeMnNi HEA by mechano-chemical process and illustrated that the powder sample was thermally stable up to $5 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and afterwards encountered diffusional transformation to form minor fraction of intermetallics along with the parent phase at room temperature.

The use of carbon as an important interstitial alloying element has strong effect on the phase constitution and mechanical properties on steels, Ni-based and Co-based superalloys [19]. The addition of carbon in $\mathrm { F e _ { 4 0 . 4 } N i _ { 1 1 . 3 } M n _ { 3 4 . 8 } A l _ { 7 . 5 } C r _ { 6 } }$ [20], $\mathrm { F e } _ { 4 0 } \mathrm { M n } _ { 4 0 } \mathrm { C o } _ { 1 0 } \mathrm { C r } _ { 1 0 }$ [21], FeCoNiAl [22], Ni CoCuFeCr V $( x = 0 . 2 5$ , 0.5 mol) [23], FeCoNiCu [24], TiFeCoNiAl [25] and $\mathrm { C r _ { 2 0 } M n _ { 1 0 } F e _ { 3 5 } N i _ { 3 5 } }$ [26] HEAs showed excellent mechanical properties at room and high temperatures. Raabe et al. [27] studied the FeMnAl–C and FeMnAlSi–C high entropy steel having FCC structure and exhibiting good tensile ductility with nominal strength [27]. They explained the formation of different phases by varying the C and Mn content in the non equiatomic FeMnAlSi–C high entropy steel. The effect of C and Mn in the phase formation of FeMnAlSi–C high entropy steel was shown efrom multiphase to single phase FCC solid solution. Raabe and co-workers illustrated the formation of FCC structure strengthened by addition of Al and Si due to solid solution strengthening [27].

In the view of above, the present investigation envisages the preparation of a non-equiatomic low-density Fe Mn Ni Al Si C high entropy steel by MA and SPS possessing a multi-phase microstructure consisting of mainly BCC and B2 phases that may have greater strength with appreciable amount of ductility. The alloying behavior, thermal stability, microstructural and chemical analysis, mechanical properties (i.e., microhardness and compressive strength) during MA and SPS have been studied systematically. Further, the mechanical properties of these HES were understood with the help of various strengthening mechanisms.

# 2. Experimental details

The starting elemental powder of Fe, Ni, Mn, Al, Si and C (99.2% pure) was taken in appropriate proportion of $\mathrm { F e _ { 4 0 } M n _ { 1 9 } N i _ { 1 5 } A l _ { 1 5 } S i _ { 1 0 } C _ { 1 } }$ 一 (at. %) (Fe-46.00%, Mn-21.50%, Ni-18.13%, Al-8.34%, Si-5.78%, C-0.25%; wt. %) and was milled in planetary ball mill (Model no. PM 400/ 2, Retsch) at the speed of 200 rpm. The ball to powder ratio was taken as 10:1. The milling was carried out in the tungsten carbide (WC) vials and with WC balls. To avoid oxidation, toluene was taken as a process control reagent during milling. To understand the phase evolution during milling, 2–3 g of milled powder was taken after every 5 h of milling. The milling was stopped intermittently for 20 min after every 1 h for avoiding any overheating. The 35 h milled powder was consolidated by spark plasma sintering (Model no. Dr. Sinter SCM 1050, Japan) at a temperature of ${ 9 0 0 } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ pressure of 50 MPa, and heating rate of 100 ◦C. min− 1 with a holding time of 15 min. The diameter and thickness of the spark plasma sintered sample (SPSed) was \~30 mm and \~9 mm, respectively. The pellet was cleaned with the help of polisher to remove the graphite layer. The wire EDM machine (Medha enterprises, model no. EXPRESSCUT EX-2530) was used to cut the pellet into many pieces of dimensions (diameter = 4 mm and $\mathrm { h e i g h t } = 8$ mm) for further characterization and evaluation of mechanical properties. The consolidated pellet was found to have a relative density of \~99%, which was calcualted using the following [28]:

$$
\rho_ {\mathrm{RD}} = \frac {\rho_ {\mathrm{exp}}}{\rho_ {\mathrm{th}}} \times 1 0 0 \tag {1}
$$

where, $\rho _ { \mathrm { R D } }$ is relative density (%), ρth is therotecial density computed by the rule of mixture (6.55 g/cc), and $\rho _ { \mathrm { e x p } }$ is experimental density calculated by the Archmides principle [29]. The experiment was repeated six times to calculate the average experimental density $( 6 . 4 9 \pm 0 . 3 \mathrm { g / c c } )$ .

The differential scanning calorimeter (DSC) (Netzsch, DSC404 Pegasus) was used to study the thermal stability of milled powder. The 35 h milled powder was heated up to 1000 ◦C with 20 K/min of heating rate under the nitrogen (N ) atomsphere. 35 h milled powder were encapsulated in a quartz tube and back filled with argon atmosphere for annealing it at higher temperatures. For better understanding of the thermal stability of this high entropy steel, 35 h milled sample was annealed for 1 h at diferent temperatures $\mathrm { ( i . e . , ~ } 4 0 0 \mathrm { ~ \textdegree C } , \mathrm { ~ } 5 0 0 \mathrm { ~ \textdegree C } , \mathrm { ~ } 6 0 0 \mathrm { ~ \textdegree C }$ and $8 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ followed by water quenching. The alloying behavior during milling and phase formation after the SPS was ascertained by using high resolution X-Ray diffraction (XRD) (EMPYREAN, PANALYTICAL) with a Co-Kα source (operating voltage:40 kV; operating current:40 mA) with the scan rate of 5◦ min− 1 . The XRD analysis of the annealed sample was done in the Rigaku X-Ray diffractometer (Cu- Kα source) operating at voltage of 40 kV and 15 mA of current with the scan rate of $5 ^ { \circ } \operatorname* { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ . The microstructural feature, chemical analysis and mapping of the milled powder at different milling time was examined using Evo 18 Carl Zeiss scanning electron microscope (SEM), and field emission scanning electron microscopee (FE-SEM) (FEI model no. Nova Nano SEM 450), both equipped with EDS detector. The microstructural study of the SPSed pellet was done with the help of FE-SEM (model no. Nova Nano SEM 450). The elemental analysis of the SPSed sample was performed by the EDS detector attached with SEM. For microstructural features, the SPSed samples were systematically polished and chemically etched with the 5% nitric acid and 95% methanol solution (Nital). For 35 h milled sample, structural analysis was done with the help of transmission electron microscope (TEM) (TECNAI $\mathrm { G } ^ { 2 } \mathrm { T } 2 0$ , FEI) operating at 200 kV. Electron Probe Micro Analyzer (EPMA) (CAMECA SXFive) was used for BSE imaging, quantitative point analysis and X-ray elemental mapping of the SPSed sample. The SPSed samples were carbon coated with 20 nm layer using LEICA-EM ACE200 instrument. Wavelength-dispersive spectrometry (WDS), LaB6 filament and TAP, PET, LIF, LPET and LTAP crystals were used at a voltage of 15 kV and current of 10 nA for the analyses. The acquisition, data processing and routine calibration were carried out using SxSAB version 6.1 and SX-Results software of CAMECA.

The XRD data of the milled samples, annealed and SPSed pellet were refined using Rietveld refinement method in the general structural analysis system (GSAS-II) [30] to determine the structural (lattice parameter) and microstructural (crystallite size and microstrain) parameters. The background was fitted with the Chebyschev-I background function with 10 coefficients. The peak profiles were fitted from broadening of Lorentzian and Gaussian component of pseudo-Voigt profile function. The phase fraction was determined using histogram scale factor. The average crystallite size and isotropic microstrain was based on uniaxial model [30]. The least-square method was used in order to minimize the difference between the experimental and observed data to achieve the acceptable goodness of fit (GOF) factor [31]. The dislocation density [32] was calculated from the equation,

$$
\rho_ {\mathrm{dd}} = \frac {\left(3 \sqrt {2 \pi} <   \varepsilon^ {2} > ^ {\frac {1}{2}}\right)}{\mathrm{tb}} \tag {2}
$$

where, ρdd is dislocation density, t is crystallite size in nm, b is Burger’s vecto $\scriptstyle { \frac { } { } } = { \frac { \sqrt { 3 } } { 2 } }$ a for BCC, a = lattice parameter in nm, ε = microstrain. The microstrain [33] was calculated using equation,

$$
\beta = 4 \varepsilon \tan \theta \tag {3}
$$

where, β is FWHM and θ is Bragg angle.

The mechanical properties $\mathrm { i . e . , }$ microhardness and uniaxial compression test of the SPSed samples were done using the $\mathrm { { M H T } ^ { 3 } }$ (Make: Anton Paar) instrumented microhardness tester and 100 kN Universal Testing Machine (Make: Instron; model no. 5982) respectively. The microhardness test was performed with the load of 5000 mN. The dwell time of 20 s, approach speed of 80 μm/min, retract speed of 16 μm/min, loading and unloading rate of 10,000 mN/min, respevtively. The Vickers diamond indentator was used and atleast six measurements were done to measure the microhardness. To determine the microhardness and elastic modulus of the SPSed pellets, Oliver-Pharr method was used [34]. The compression test was done at 25 ◦C with the sample dimension of diameter (4 mm) to height (8 mm) ratio of 1:2 and at crosshead speed of 0.5 mm/min with the strain rate of $1 0 ^ { - 3 } s ^ { - 1 }$ . The test was repeated three times to evaluate the average ultimate compressive strength.

$$

The crystallite size and lattice strain of the milled samples were calculated using the Rietveld refinement method with the help of GSAS-II (Table 2). The crystallite size was found to vary as a function of milling (Table 2). The crystallite size was found to be tuned in the range of \~13–17 nm and \~10–13 nm for BCC and γ-brass type phase respectively. The crystallite size for Si decreased from 20 nm to 14 nm as milling extended from 25 h to 35 h. The lattice strain increased for all the phases as the milling time progressed. The increase in the lattice strain with milling is due to the atomic size difference between the individual elements, mechanical deformation in the powder and the increase in the fraction of grain boundary. Peak broadening and the decrease in diffraction peaks intensity with the milling are evident from Fig. 1(a). The reason is due to the lattice distortion leading to the induced lattice strain and extra grain refinement. The usual characteristics of the milling process and the combination of both the phenomenon contributes to the peak broadening. There is a slight change in the amount of phase fraction for the BCC and γ-brass type structure. The dislocation density shows the negligible change with the milling time and the values are displayed in Table 2.

# 4. Discussion

# 4.1. Alloying behavior and microstructure

The phase formation during milling and after spark plasma sintering was correlated with the thermodynamic parameters. Zhang et al. [42] proposed the three parameters for the prediction of disordered solid solution formation in the HEAs i.e., high configurational entropy $( \Delta S _ { \mathrm { c o n f } } ) ,$ , mixing enthalpy $\left( \Delta \mathrm { H } _ { \mathrm { m i x } } \right)$ and the size mismatch (δ). The $\Delta S _ { \mathrm { c o n f } } ,$ ΔHmix and δ parameters were calculated based upon the Boltzmann hypothesis, Miedema model and Zhang’s approach respectively [38,42]. The calculated values of thermodynamic parameter for the non-equiatomic high entropy steel and the criteria to form the solid solution are listed in Table 6. The values of the various parameter are well within the range of random solid solution formation criteria. Guo et al. [43] proposed some more parameters i.e., electronegativity (χ), valence electron concentration (VEC) and Ω for the prediction of solid solution formation in the multicomponent alloys. The VEC is used to predict the FCC/BCC or both the phase formation in the HEAs. The VEC is well within the range of dual-phase formation, and it forms the BCC type structure in the milled and SPSed sample of the present alloy. AlxCoCrCuFeNi (x = 0.45, 1, 2.5, 5 at. %) HEAs phase formation were discussed on the basis of mixing enthalpy, configurational entropy and size mismatch [44]. It was stated that the Al increased the B2 phase formation due to the high mixing enthalpy in the HEAs. It is also mentioned that the enthalpy of mixing in the HEAs system was preferred to form the ordering or clustering (formations of intermetallic compound) [45]. Shivam et. al [46], discussed the phase formation in the non-equiatomic $\mathrm { F e } _ { 4 0 } \mathrm { C r } _ { 2 5 } \mathrm { N i } _ { 1 5 } \mathrm { A l } _ { 1 5 } \mathrm { C o } _ { 5 }$ medium entropy alloy in terms of binary mixing enthalphy and configurational entropy, which has formed the BCC and B2 structure. The B2 phase formation in the alloy was correlated with the binary enthalphy of mixing in Ni–Al which is the highest among the other pairs. In present alloy, the binary enthalpy between the Al–Fe, Al–Ni are highly negative. The values of the various possible pair of binary enthalphy are mention in the Table 1. To retain the solid solution in the HEAs at room temperature, the configurational entropy is not sufficient. The partially ordered B2-type structure in the alloy was formed due to the binary negative enthalpy. This parameters are well within the solid solution formation criteria. The criteria proposed for the prediction of the disordered solid solution in the HEAs are based upon the results of the reported HEAs. However, some of the reported HEAs suggested that the Zhang’s criteria were neither necessary nor sufficient condition to predict the formation of disordered solid solution in the HEAs. This can only provide the basic idea to predict the phase formation criteria of disordered solid solution. Other than the thermodynamic parameters, the processing tenchique and atomic percentage of the indiviual elements also play an important role in the phase formation of the HEAs [45].

# 4.2. Thermal stability

The phase evolution of the non-equiatomic HES at higher temperature are observed at ${ } ^ { 2 5 } \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ such as BCC + γ-brass $\mathrm { t y p e } + { \bf B } \boldsymbol { 2 } + { \bf S } \mathrm { i }$ (undissolved). The alloy is thermally stable up $\mathbf { t o } \sim 5 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C } .$ . At 420 ◦C, the phases observed to be stable are BCC + γ-brass type + B2-type. But the phase fraction of the γ-brass type structure increased and some peaks were evolved. This type of transformation was also seen in the nonequiatomic $\mathrm { F e } _ { 4 0 } \mathrm { C r } _ { 2 5 } \mathrm { N i } _ { 1 5 } \mathrm { A l } _ { 1 5 } \mathrm { C o } _ { 5 }$ medium entropy alloy [46] and quasicrystals $[ 2 9 , 4 8 , 4 9 ] .$ . At ${ 5 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } } ,$ the phases are found to be BCC + γ-brass type + B2 and $\mathrm { F e } _ { 5 } \mathrm { { S i } _ { 3 } }$ type silicide. The silicide formation was also seen in the previously studied HEAs i.e., non-equiatomic FeMnNiTiAlSi–C [35], FeMnNiCrAlSi–C [11], AlCoCrFeNiSi [50] and AlCrFeMgNi [51] HEAs fabricated using the powder metallurgy route. The phase transformations occurred at higher temperature were due to the formation of metastable solid solution phase during milling. $\mathrm { F e _ { 3 6 } M n _ { 2 1 } C r _ { 1 8 } N i _ { 1 5 } A l _ { 1 0 } }$ high entropy alloy, the phases formed was BCC and B2. The alloy showed thermal stability of B2 phase at different heat treatment of the as-cast samples. The good thermal stability of the B2 phase was due to the strong bonding between the Ni–Al atoms and sluggish diffusion effect in the high entropy alloys [46,52]. In the present alloy, the retention of the BCC and B2 phase after the various annealing condition and sintering was due to the strong bonding between the pairs of Fe–Al and Ni–Al, and sluggish diffusion effect. The formation of the silicide was due to the undissolved Si remaining after milling, and tendency of Fe and Mn to react with Si. The binary enthalpy of mixing between Fe–Si and Mn–Si is highly negative.

# 4.3. Mechaincal properties and strengthening mechanisms

The measured yield stress of the SPSed sample of the non-equiatomic HES is found to be 2046 ± 160 MPa and it is the combination of the frictional stress and the various strengthening mechanisms including grain-boundary, precipitates, dislocation and solid solution strengthening. The total yield stress can be expressed as:

$$
\sigma_ {\mathrm{y}} = \sigma_ {\mathrm{o}} + \Delta \sigma_ {\mathrm{gb}} + \Delta \sigma_ {\mathrm{p}} + \Delta \sigma_ {\mathrm{d}} + \Delta \sigma_ {\mathrm{ss}} \tag {5}
$$

where, $\sigma _ { 0 }$ is frictional stress, Δσgb is grain boundary strengthening, $\Delta \sigma _ { \mathrm { p } }$ is precipitate strengthening, Δσd is dislocation strengthening and $\Delta \sigma _ { \mathrm { s s } }$ is solid solution strengthening. The frictional stress is taken as 120 MPa for BCC and B2, respectively [53]. The grain boundary strengthening was calculated using the Hall-Petch relation [35] as,

$$
\Delta \sigma_ {g b} = K _ {o} d ^ {- 1 / 2} \tag {6}
$$

where, $\mathrm { K } _ { 0 }$ is the constant related to the material and value is taken as 574 MPa/μm1/2 for BCC and partially ordered B2-type respectively [54] and d represents the grain size. The value of the grain size for BCC and B2-type was found to be 0.28 μm and 0.22 μm, respectively. The value for grain boundary strengthening was evaluated to be 1085 MPa and 1224 MPa for BCC and B2-type, respectively.

The effect of the silicide and the B2-type precipitate on the strength of the high entropy steel was calculated using the equation [35] as:

$$
\Delta \sigma_ {p} = \frac {0 . 4 M G b}{\pi \lambda} \frac {\ln \left(\frac {2 \bar {r}}{b}\right)}{\sqrt {1 - v}} \tag {7}
$$

where, M is the Taylor factor (2.73) [53], G is the shear modulus, calculated using the rule of mixture and its value was found to be 75.34 GPa, υ is the Poisson’s ratio (0.3) [35], b is the Burgers vector $( { \textstyle { \frac { \sqrt { 3 } } { 2 } } } a ) _ { : }$ , a is the lattice parameter for BCC, λ is the spacing between the precipitates, calculated using the equation as, $\begin{array} { r } { \lambda = 2 \bar { r } \bigg ( \sqrt { \frac { \pi } { 4 f } } - 1 \bigg ) } \end{array}$ , f is the volume fraction of the precipitates (8.4% for Fe5Si3-type and 34% for B2-type), r is the average radius (circular cross section) of the spherical precipitates, calculated using the equation as, $\begin{array} { r } { \overline { { r } } = \sqrt { \frac { 2 } { 3 } } r } \end{array}$ and r is the average radius of the precipitates (102 nm for $\mathrm { F e } _ { 5 } \mathrm { S i } _ { 3 ^ { - } } \mathrm { t y p e }$ and 220 nm for B2-type). The value of the dispersion strengthening for BCC phase was found to be 54 MPa and 111 MPa for silicide and B2-type, respectively and for B2-type was found to be 55 MPa for silicides.

The contribution of the dislocation strengthening was calculated using the Bailey-Hirsch relationship [35] as:

$$
\Delta \sigma_ {d} = M \alpha G b \sqrt {\rho_ {d d}} \tag {8}
$$

where, α is the constant and taken as 0.2 for BCC and B2-type [35], respectively, b is the Burgers vector and ρdd is the dislocation density, calculated using equation (2) and the value was taken as 4.46 $\times \ 1 0 ^ { 1 5 }$ $\mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 }$ and $3 . 3 6 \times \dot { 1 } 0 ^ { 1 5 } \mathrm { ~ m } ^ { - 2 }$ , for BCC and B2-type, respectively. The strength value was found to be 687 MPa and 590 MPa for BCC and B2-type respectively. The solid solution strengthening was calculated using the Fleischer model [35] as:

$$
\Delta \sigma_ {s s} = \frac {M G \varepsilon_ {f}}{7 0 0} c ^ {1 / 2} \tag {9}
$$

where, c is the atomic percentage of the solute element and $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { f } }$ is the relation parameter and given as, $\begin{array} { r l r } { \varepsilon _ { f } = } & { { } } & { \left| \frac { \varepsilon _ { G } } { 1 + 0 . 5 | \varepsilon _ { G } | } - 3 \varepsilon _ { a } \right| ^ { 3 / 2 } } \end{array}$ , ε is the modulus misfit $\textstyle { \bigl ( } { \frac { 1 } { G } } { \frac { d G } { d c } } { \bigr ) }$ and $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { a } }$ is the atomic size misfit $( { \textstyle { \frac { 1 } { a } } } { \frac { d a } { d c } } ) . { \mathrm { A l } }$ was taken as solute element for both, the BCC and B2-type phases as Al has highest atomic radius as compared to the other elements and the atomic percent was taken as 13.45 and 36.55. The strength value was found to be 106 MPa and 177 MPa for BCC and B2-type, respectively.

The strengthening mechanism based calculation was done on the assumption that the phase fraction of the BCC and B2-type was 100%. The phase fraction of BCC and B2-type in the SPSed sample of the high entropy steel was calculated as 56.0% and 34.0%, respectively. Therefore, the phase fraction of the BCC and B2-type for the calculation of the strengthening mechanism was taken as 62.0% and 38.0%, respectively. The contribution of the various strengthening mechanism is mentioned in Table 9. The grain boundary and dislocation strengthening are dominant strengthening mechanisms. The individual contribution of different strengthening mechanisms are as follows: grain boundary (52.55%), dislocation (30.00%), precipitation (5.70%) and solid solution strengthening (6.14%). The calculated theoretical strength and the measured strength of the alloy are in close agreement.

It was observed that the addition of the Si promotes the formation of BCC and silicides phases with Cr, Fe, Ni and Ti in the HEAs. The mechanical properties $\mathrm { i . e . , }$ , hardness and compressive strength was increased with the addition of the silicon in the reported HEAs [55]. $\mathrm { A l } _ { 0 . 3 } \mathrm { C o C r F e N i S i } _ { x }$ showed the phase transition from FCC to BCC/ordered B2 phase as x increases (0 to 1). The tensile strength and hardness of the HEAs increased without any decrease in the tensile ductility by 63% and 143 Hv to 863 Hv as silicon content increased (x = 0–1 at. %) [56]. Wang et al. [57] reported that the formation of $\mathbf { M } _ { 5 } \mathbf { S i } _ { 3 }$ type silicide in the HEAs enhanced the strength and plasticity. The strength and plasticity both increased with the small content of Si. However, as Si increases, the strength also increases but plasticity decreases in the HEAs. In $\mathrm { A l _ { 0 . 2 } C o _ { 1 . 5 } C r F e N i _ { 1 . 5 } T i _ { 0 . 5 } }$ HEAs, the silicide formation plays a crucial role to strengthen the alloy and it increases the hardness and compressive strength of the alloys [58]. In the non-equiatomic FeMnNiCrAlSi–C high entropy steel, which has formed the FCC and BCC dual-phase structure with the carbide and silicide intermetallic compound, showed the hardness value of 5.86 GPa [11]. In contrast to previous work on the high entropy steel, the present non-equiatomic high entropy steel has showed the high hardness value of 7.8 GPa. In the present alloy, the in-situ formation of the silicide has played a crucial role to strengthen the alloy $\mathrm { i . e . , }$ microhardness and compressive strength with good amount of ductility.
