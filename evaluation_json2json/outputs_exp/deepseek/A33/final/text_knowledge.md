## Material System

- Novel austenite–ferrite duplex low‑density steel based on Fe‑30.35Mn‑12.60Al‑1.32C‑4.83Cr (wt%).
- Phases present: austenite (γ, FCC), granular α ferrite, banded δ ferrite, nanosized κ‑carbide precipitates, Cr₇C₃.
- Measured ultralow density: 6.36 g cm⁻³.

## Processing Route and Variables

1.  Homogenisation at 1200 °C for 2 h.
2.  Hot rolling followed by solution treatment at 1050 °C for 0.5 h.
3.  Cold rolling reduction from 6 mm to 3 mm (50% thickness reduction).
4.  Intercritical annealing between 600 °C and 1050 °C for 1–5 min, followed by water quenching.
5.  Mechanical testing performed along rolling direction (RD) and transverse direction (TD).

Key process variants discussed:
- DP950‑1/5: annealed at 950 °C for 1 min or 5 min.
- DP1000‑1/5: annealed at 1000 °C for 1 min or 5 min.
- DP1050‑1: annealed at 1050 °C for 1 min.
- DP1000‑5‑TD: same as DP1000‑5 but tested in transverse direction.

## Microstructure and Phase Evolution

### Cold‑rolled condition (CR)
- Deformed austenite containing shear bands; ferrite (BCC) and austenite (FCC) are present.

### Annealing at 950 °C (DP950‑5)
- Partial recrystallisation (54.8% recrystallised fraction); shear bands still visible.
- High ferrite fraction (36 % total; granular α = 23.8 %, banded δ = 12.1 %).
- Coarse Cr₇C₃ carbides at grain boundaries.
- Intergranular κ‑carbides (2–3 µm).
- Ductility is limited (~5 % total elongation).

### Annealing at 1000 °C (DP1000‑5, RD)
- Fully recrystallised austenite matrix (95.2 % fraction, average grain size 7.40 ± 5.13 µm).
- Reduced ferrite content (granular α 4.1 %, banded δ 9.3 %).
- High‑density intragranular κ‑carbide nanoparticles within austenite (radius 0.86 ± 0.20 nm, volume fraction 1.56 %).
- Cr₇C₃ carbides dissolved.
- Annealing twins present.

### Annealing at 1050 °C (DP1050‑1)
- Larger recrystallised austenite grains (9.65 ± 8.17 µm).
- Minimal ferrite (granular α 2.7 %, banded δ 4.6 %).
- Complete dissolution of both Cr₇C₃ and κ‑carbide precipitates.

### Phase‑transformation sequence with temperature (XRD, 600–1050 °C)
- β‑Mn forms at 700–900 °C.
- Cr₇C₃ and κ‑carbides precipitate at 950–1000 °C and dissolve at 1050 °C.

## Processing‑Structure‑Property Chain

1.  **Cold rolling** introduces high stored energy and shear bands in austenite, providing driving force for recrystallisation.
2.  **Annealing at 950 °C** yields insufficient recrystallisation (54.8 %) and retains a high volume fraction of brittle intergranular Cr₇C₃ and coarse κ‑carbides; consequence: very low ductility (~5 % TE).
3.  **Annealing at 1000 °C for 5 min (DP1000‑5)** achieves full recrystallisation of austenite, dissolves embrittling grain‑boundary carbides, and precipitates a high density of coherent intragranular κ‑carbide nanoparticles (radius 0.86 nm, Vf = 1.56 %). The retained banded δ ferrite (9.3 %) is aligned parallel to RD.
    - **Tensile property (RD)**: UTS ≈1250 MPa, total elongation ≈41.4 %.
    - **Specific performance**: Specific ultimate tensile strength × total elongation exceeds 8 GPa·g⁻¹·cm³·%.
4.  **Annealing at 1050 °C for 1 min (DP1050‑1)** causes dissolution of κ‑carbides and ferrite‑content reduction, resulting in lower strength but higher elongation (≈47.5 % TE).
5.  **Anisotropy**: When tested in the transverse direction (DP1000‑5‑TD), the δ‑ferrite bands are oriented perpendicular to the tensile axis. Ductility drops to ≈18.1 % TE, while strength remains high.

## Mechanistic Interpretation

### Strengthening mechanisms (DP1000‑5)
- Grain refinement of recrystallised austenite (7.40 µm) provides Hall‑Petch strengthening.
- Nanosized κ‑carbides (0.86 nm radius) act as effective intragranular obstacles, raising flow stress via Orowan‑type and coherency strengthening.
- Dual‑phase structure with soft δ‑ferrite bands contributes to composite strengthening without embrittlement.

### Ductility and crack‑deflection mechanism (RD vs. TD)
- **RD loading (δ bands || tensile axis)**  
  - Cracks nucleate at α/γ interfaces and propagate along the normal direction.  
  - Upon encountering soft δ‑ferrite bands aligned parallel to the loading direction, cracks are deflected along the RD and blunted within the ferrite.  
  - This mechanism delays catastrophic failure and leads to high total elongation (41.4 %).
- **TD loading (δ bands ⊥ tensile axis)**  
  - Cracks nucleate at δ/γ interfaces and propagate along the TD.  
  - The perpendicular δ‑ferrite bands create high decohesive stresses at interfaces, enabling cracks to rapidly link across the specimen thickness, causing early failure and low ductility (18.1 % TE).

### Role of δ ferrite
- Inhibits austenite grain growth during recrystallisation (grain‑refinement effect).  
- Acts as crack‑deflection medium when oriented parallel to the load, improving toughness.  
- Lowers overall density.

### Contrast with single‑phase austenitic lightweight steels
Without the ferrite phase, grain‑growth control is less effective, and intrinsic crack‑deflection mechanisms are absent, typically resulting in inferior specific strength‑ductility synergy (DP1000‑5 outperforms conventional austenitic Fe‑Mn‑Al‑C steels and benchmark materials such as 2000‑series Al alloys and Ti‑6Al‑4V on an Ashby‑type SUTS‑TE map).

## Key Quantitative Findings

### Composition and density
- Density: 6.36 g cm⁻³.

### DP1000‑5 (optimised condition, RD)
- Austenite fraction: 95.2 %.
- Austenite grain size: 7.40 ± 5.13 µm.
- Ferrite fractions: granular α 4.1 %, banded δ 9.3 %.
- κ‑carbide nanoparticle radius: 0.86 ± 0.20 nm, volume fraction 1.56 %.
- Tensile: UTS ≈1250 MPa, total elongation ≈41.4 %.
- Specific performance: SUTS × TE > 8 GPa·g⁻¹·cm³·%.

### DP950‑5
- Recrystallised fraction: 54.8 %.
- Total ferrite: 36 % (α 23.8 %, δ 12.1 %).
- Total elongation: ≈5 %.

### DP1050‑1
- Austenite grain size: 9.65 ± 8.17 µm.
- Ferrite fractions: α 2.7 %, δ 4.6 %.
- Total elongation: ≈47.5 % (strength lower than DP1000‑5).

### DP1000‑5‑TD
- Total elongation: ≈18.1 %.

### Comparative benchmarks
- DP1000‑5 and DP1050‑1 exceed the SUTS × TE envelope of 2000‑series Al alloys, Ti‑6Al‑4V, conventional duplex steels, and previous Fe‑Mn‑Al‑C austenitic steels.
