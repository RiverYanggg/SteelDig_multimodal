Material System
----------------

-   **Alloy:** Fe–8.03Mn–6.10Al–0.4C (wt%) low‑density duplex steel.
-   **Primary phases:** δ‑ferrite, austenite (γ), martensite (α', including twin martensite), needle‑like α‑ferrite, equiaxed α‑ferrite.

Processing Route and Variables
------------------------------

-   **Homogenization:** 1050 °C for 2 h.
-   **Reheating:** 1200 °C for 2 h.
-   **Hot rolling:** 900–1100 °C, seven passes to 4 mm sheet.
-   **Critical processing split – cooling path after hot rolling:**
    *   **Air cooling (AC):** rapid cooling to ambient temperature.
    *   **Furnace cooling (FC):** slow cooling in furnace.
-   **Post‑cooling annealing:** 800 °C, 900 °C, or 1000 °C for 30 min (yielding designations AC‑A800/900/1000 and FC‑A800/900/1000).

Microstructure and Phase Evolution
----------------------------------

### As‑hot‑rolled condition (Figure 2, Figure 8)

-   **AC sample:** banded morphology along rolling direction; δ‑ferrite bands alternating with (γ + α*) bands, where α* is martensite in prior austenite. Contains **26 vol% martensite** and **42 vol% austenite**. EBSD reveals higher kernel average misorientation (KAM) in austenite (0.48°) than in FC (0.43°), indicating greater geometrically necessary dislocation (GND) density from martensitic transformation.
-   **FC sample:** δ‑ferrite + austenite (γ) bands; only **2.8 vol% martensite**, **51 vol% austenite**. Lower defect density and less internal stress.

### Annealed microstructures (Figure 3, Figure 4, Figure 6, Figure 7, Figure 8)

-   **AC‑annealed (800‑1000 °C):**  
    - Martensite partially reverses to austenite and degrades into needle‑like α‑ferrite (Figure 6, Figure 8).  
    - Duplex microstructure: δ‑ferrite, needle‑like α‑ferrite embedded in austenite bands, no retained martensite.  
    - Needle‑like α‑ferrite refines austenite grains to average sizes of **3.5 µm (800 °C), 4.2 µm (900 °C), 5.8 µm (1000 °C)**.  
    - With increasing annealing temperature, low‑angle grain boundary length in δ‑ferrite decreases (from 4351.1 µm to 1634.3 µm), indicating recovery.  
-   **FC‑annealed (800‑1000 °C):**  
    - Banded morphology persists: δ‑ferrite, austenite, and small fractions of martensite. Contains fine equiaxed α‑ferrite but no needle‑like morphology.  
    - Austenite grain sizes significantly larger: **9.3 µm (800 °C), 8.1 µm (900 °C), 10.1 µm (1000 °C)**.  
    - Coarse grains and lower austenite stability lead to partial martensitic transformation during final cooling.

Processing–Structure–Property Chain
-----------------------------------

### Tensile properties (Figure 9, Figure 10)

-   **AC‑annealed samples:**
    *   AC‑A800: yield strength ~513 MPa, elongation 56.9%.
    *   AC‑A900: yield strength ~427 MPa, elongation 39.5%.
    *   AC‑A1000: yield strength ~427 MPa, elongation 31.1%.
    *   Work‑hardening rate shows three stages: Stage I (rapid drop), Stage II (slow decrease), Stage III (recovery at higher strains) – the Stage III recovery is linked to the TRIP effect.
-   **FC‑annealed samples:**
    *   FC‑A800: yield strength ~419 MPa, elongation 19.0%.
    *   FC‑A900: yield strength ~267 MPa, elongation 15.4%.
    *   FC‑A1000: yield strength ~267 MPa, elongation 10.6%.
    *   Work‑hardening rate exhibits only Stages I and II, followed by abrupt brittle fracture without necking.

### Austenite stability and TRIP evidence (Figure 5)

-   XRD‑measured austenite volume fractions before and after tensile deformation:
    *   **AC800:** 58.6% → 23.7% (Δ ≈ 34.9 vol%)
    *   **AC900:** 61.4% → 44.4% (Δ ≈ 17.0 vol%)
    *   **AC1000:** 57.1% → 46.4% (Δ ≈ 10.7 vol%)
    *   Substantial deformation‑induced austenite consumption confirms active TRIP effect.
-   **FC samples:** much smaller changes; e.g., FC800 62% → 56.5%, indicating higher mechanical stability of austenite and limited TRIP. The presence of brittle martensite leads to early crack nucleation.

### Fracture micromechanisms (Figure 11)

-   **AC‑A800 fracture surface:** dimple‑dominated ductile fracture with some secondary cracking.
-   **FC‑A800 fracture surface:** cleavage facets and extensive cracking along the rolling direction, characteristic of brittle delamination.

Mechanistic Interpretation
--------------------------

-   **Air cooling path:** produces a high fraction of martensite in the as‑rolled state. During annealing, martensite reversely transforms to austenite and degenerates into needle‑like α‑ferrite. The needle‑like ferrite refines austenite grains (3.5‑5.8 µm) and, together with elemental partitioning, chemically stabilizes the austenite. The refined, metastable austenite undergoes pronounced TRIP during tensile straining, yielding Stage III work‑hardening rate recovery and an excellent strength‑ductility balance (elongations >30%).
-   **Furnace cooling path:** as‑rolled microstructure is largely martensite‑free. Annealing leads to grain growth (8.1‑10.1 µm) and only slight formation of equiaxed α‑ferrite. The austenite remains too stable or, when partially transformed, the co‑existing martensite acts as a brittle phase. Stress concentrates at martensite–austenite interfaces, initiating cleavage cracks along the banded structure, causing premature brittle fracture with elongations ≤19%.
