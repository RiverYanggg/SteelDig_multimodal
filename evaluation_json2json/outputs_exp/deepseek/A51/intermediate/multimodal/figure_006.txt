Subfigure (a) shows the microstructure after 5 min annealing, revealing tempered martensite, austenite, and δ-ferrite, with obvious twin crystal structures within the martensite. Subfigure (b) displays the microstructure after 15 min annealing, where the prior martensite has partially degenerated into needle-like α-ferrites that inherit the morphology of the parent martensite. The images demonstrate that with increasing annealing time, the high-density dislocations in twin martensite provide nucleation sites for ferritic transformation, facilitating the formation of needle-like α-ferrite that refines the austenite grains. Concurrently, the film austenite located between the α-ferrite and/or prior martensite becomes thickened with prolonged annealing. This microstructural modification, specifically the embedding of needle-like α-ferrite refining the austenite grain size to approximately 3.5 μm, enhances both the chemical and mechanical stability of austenite by partitioning carbon and manganese into the austenite phase. The figure supports the core conclusion that controlled annealing of air-cooled samples produces a duplex microstructure of ferrite and austenite without martensite, which is crucial for achieving a superior strength-ductility match through the transformation-induced plasticity (TRIP) effect.", "confidence": "high"}
