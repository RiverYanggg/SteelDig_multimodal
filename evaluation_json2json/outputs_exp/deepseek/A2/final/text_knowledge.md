# Material System

Fe–Al polycrystalline alloys covering 0 to 40 at.% Al: nominally pure Fe, disordered Fe–Al solid solutions (<20 at.% Al), Fe₃Al intermetallic with DO₃ order, and FeAl intermetallic with B2 order. Binary Fe–Al alloys were studied alongside Fe–Al–B variants containing ~0.1 at.% B. All materials were in the as-cast and annealed condition with coarse, thermally relaxed grain sizes.

# Processing Route and Variables

The primary processing variable is the aluminum content from nearly 0 to 40 at.%. Alloys were prepared by induction melting followed by slow cooling from the melt, resulting in an as-cast and annealed coarse-grained condition. The slow cooling protocol was designed to attain B2 order with moderate (weak) DO₃ order in the 25–30 at.% Al range. Room-temperature mechanical testing was conducted in compression at a strain rate of 3×10⁻⁴ s⁻¹ to plastic strains of 2–3% for TEM dislocation structure analysis. Work hardening rate (WHR) was evaluated between 1 and 1.5% plastic strain.

# Microstructure and Phase Evolution

With increasing Al content, the phase and order state evolve systematically: disordered bcc solid solution (<20 at.% Al) → short-range ordered structure → B2 long-range order with coexisting weak DO₃ order (25–30 at.% Al) → dominant B2 order with high antiphase boundary energy (>30 at.% Al). In Fe–25% Al, thermal APBs from coarse B2 domains are visible by TEM via 100 superlattice reflection dark-field imaging.

The dislocation substructure transforms accordingly:
- Fe–15% Al: single (1/2)⟨111⟩ dislocations in loose cellular distributions with incipient cell boundaries and dipole debris (Figure 5).
- Fe–20% Al: single curved (1/2)⟨111⟩ dislocations with preferred screw orientations, edge dipoles, and small prismatic loops; some near-screw segments appear loosely paired (Figures 6, 7).
- Fe–25% Al: near-screw dislocations arranged as loosely coupled pairs connected by weak APBI faults; dislocations remain individual-like despite weak APB coupling, with abundant edge dipole segments and screw dipoles (Figures 8, 9).
- Fe–28% Al (peak WHR composition): all dislocations are superdislocations composed of two (1/2)⟨111⟩ partials trailing APBII faults, predominantly straight near-screw segments on {110} planes, with high-density superdislocation dipoles and sessile ⟨100⟩ edge dislocation locks formed by reactions (Figures 10–13).
- Fe–30% Al and Fe–35% Al: closely spaced superdislocation partials due to high APBI fault energy, straight near-screw segments, abundant edge dipole dipoles dragged from obstacles; residual APBII fringe contrast from destroyed weak DO₃ order (Figures 14–19).

# Processing-Structure-Property Chain

**Al content → Order state → Dislocation character → Work hardening rate:**
1. Fe–(0–20)%Al, disordered/short-range ordered → single (1/2)⟨111⟩ dislocations, loose cells, dipoles → low WHR < 2 GPa.
2. Fe–25%Al, B2 with weak DO₃, low APBI energy (DO₃) ~75–78 mJ/m² and APBII ~65–78 mJ/m² → loosely coupled near-screw dislocation pairs, individual-like glide → moderate WHR.
3. Fe–28%Al, B2 with residual weak DO₃, APBII energy ~65–75 mJ/m² → all dislocations as superdislocations, straight near-screw, high-density superdislocation dipoles + sessile ⟨100⟩ edge locks → sharp WHR peak of ~16 GPa (between 1–1.5% plastic strain).
4. Fe–(30–40)%Al, dominant B2 order, APBI energy rises steeply from ~50 to ~110 mJ/m² (Figure 3, Ref. [19–21]) → closely coupled superdislocation partials → WHR declines from peak to ~6 GPa at 40% Al.

**Yield stress anomaly and ductility dip:** Yield stress peaks at ~650 MPa near 25% Al (solution hardening and short-range order hardening) then declines and partially recovers. Ductility drops overall from ~35% to near-zero at 40% Al but shows a small local peak near 28% Al (Figure 1), coinciding with the WHR maximum while flow stress remains nearly constant—consistent with the Considère criterion for plastic instability.

**Boron microalloying:** Fe–Al–B (~0.1% B) exhibits only a slight increase in WHR compared to binary Fe–Al, without a consistent significant effect (Figure 2).

# Mechanistic Interpretation

The peak work hardening near 28% Al is attributed to two primary factors rather than elastic anisotropy:
1. **Superdislocation dipole hardening:** In the B2 + weak DO₃ regime (25–30% Al), APB energies are insufficient to maintain tightly coupled superdislocations; dislocations glide as loosely coupled pairs, generating high densities of superdislocation dipoles—both edge dipoles trailing from moving screws and screw dipoles. These dipoles act as strong short-range obstacles to subsequent dislocation motion.
2. **Strong Peierls forces on screw dislocations:** The straight near-screw character of all superdislocations reveals high lattice friction (Peierls stress) on screw orientations, which limits cross-slip and recovery, promoting dipole accumulation.
3. **Sessile ⟨100⟩ edge dislocation locks:** Reactions between ⟨111⟩ superdislocations on different {110} planes produce immobile ⟨100⟩ edge dislocations that further obstruct glide, amplifying hardening.

The Zener anisotropy factor A = 2C₄₄/(C₁₁–C₁₂) peaks sharply at ~6.5–7 near 25–30% Al (Figure 4), coinciding with the WHR peak. However, the authors argue that elastic anisotropy is not the direct cause—the dislocation structures (straight screw superdislocations with dipoles) differ fundamentally from those in other elastically anisotropic bcc metals, and the primary hardening mechanism is rooted in superdislocation dipole physics and Peierls forces.

# Key Quantitative Findings

- WHR for binary Fe–Al (1–1.5% plastic strain): <2 GPa below 20% Al; sharp maximum of ~16 GPa at 28–29% Al; decreases to ~6 GPa at 40% Al (Figure 2).
- Yield stress: peak ~650 MPa at ~25% Al (Figure 1).
- Ductility: ~35% at low Al, local peak near 28% Al, ~0% at 40% Al (Figure 1).
- APBI energy (B2): increases from ~28 mJ/m² at 28% Al to ~110 mJ/m² at 36% Al (Figure 3).
- APBI energy (DO₃): decreases from ~78 mJ/m² to ~65 mJ/m² over 25.5–29% Al (Figure 3).
- APBII energy (DO₃): similar range, ~78 mJ/m² to ~65 mJ/m² (Figure 3).
- Zener anisotropy factor: peaks at ~6.5–7 in the 25–30% Al range (Figure 4).
- C₄₄: peaks at ~130 GPa near 25–30% Al; (C₁₁–C₁₂)/2: ~20 GPa minimum in the same range (Figure 4).

# Visual Evidence

**Figure 1 – Yield stress and ductility vs. Al content (0–40%):** Dual-axis plot. Yield stress peaks at ~650 MPa near 25% Al; ductility shows a small peak near 28% Al superimposed on an overall decreasing trend. Supports the anomalous mechanical behavior near the WHR peak composition.

**Figure 2 – Work hardening rate vs. Al content:** WHR (1–1.5% strain) for binary Fe–Al peaks sharply at ~16 GPa at 28–29% Al. Fe–Al–B data show minimal deviation. Directly quantifies the compositional WHR peak.

**Figure 3 – APB energies vs. Al content (25–36%):** APBI(B2) rises sharply with Al; APBI(DO₃) and APBII(DO₃) decrease. Explains transition from loosely coupled (low APB) to closely coupled (high APB) superdislocation configurations.

**Figure 4 – Elastic stiffness constants and Zener anisotropy factor vs. Al content:** C₄₄ peaks, (C₁₁–C₁₂)/2 dips, and A peaks at ~6.5–7 in the 25–30% Al range. Documents elastic anisotropy peak coincident with order onset and WHR peak, though authors downplay its direct mechanistic role.

**Figure 5 – Fe–15%Al, TEM:** Single (1/2)⟨111⟩ dislocations in loose cellular distribution with dipoles and incipient cell walls. Establishes baseline dislocation structure in low-Al disordered alloy.

**Figure 6 – Fe–20%Al, TEM ±g pair:** Curled single dislocations with edge dipoles and small loops confirmed by image displacement. Demonstrates dipole hardening from individual dislocation motion.

**Figure 7 – Fe–20%Al, TEM near [001]:** Straight near-screw single dislocations with edge dipole segments and prismatic loops. Evidence of strong Peierls forces even in disordered alloys.

**Figure 8 – Fe–25%Al, DF + WB TEM pair:** Thermal APBs (B2 domains) and APBI faults from (1/2)⟨111⟩ dislocations; near-screw straight dislocations with loosely coupled pairs. Shows onset of ordered structure and weak APB coupling.

**Figure 9 – Fe–25%Al, TEM g=110 near [001]:** Near-screw dislocations as loosely coupled pairs ('p') and dipoles ('d'). Confirms individual-like dislocation behavior despite order.

**Figures 10–13 – Fe–28%Al (peak WHR), multiple TEM conditions:** High-density straight near-screw superdislocations, superdislocation dipoles, and sessile ⟨100⟩ edge dislocation locks. ±g pairs confirm dipole nature via image displacement; weak-beam imaging resolves reaction dislocations. Directly visualizes the proposed hardening mechanisms at the WHR peak composition.

**Figures 14–16 – Fe–30%Al, TEM:** Straight near-screw superdislocations with closely coupled partials and edge dipoles. Residual APBII fringe contrast indicates destroyed weak DO₃ order. Supports transition to tighter coupling at higher Al.

**Figures 17–19 – Fe–35%Al, TEM and ±g pair:** Abundant straight screw superdislocations and dipole segments, residual APBII fringes. Confirms continued dipole hardening mechanism and order destruction in high-Al B2 alloys.
