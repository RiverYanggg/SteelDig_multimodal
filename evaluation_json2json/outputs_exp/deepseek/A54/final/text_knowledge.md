## Material System
- **Alloy designation**: Fe-7.5Al-0.65C (nominal composition)
- **Actual composition**: Fe-7.54Al-0.65C (wt%), with minor Mn, Si, P, S impurities
- **Phase constituents**: Ferrite matrix (α-Fe) containing dispersed Fe₃AlCₓ carbides (K-phase)
- **Initial condition**: Two-phase microstructure produced by hot forging at 1200 °C followed by homogenization at 1100 °C for 4 h; K-phase particles appear as dark, irregularly shaped precipitates distributed throughout the lighter-etching ferrite matrix (Figure 1)

## Processing Route and Variables
- **Prior processing**: Hot forging at 1200 °C → homogenization heat treatment at 1100 °C for 4 h
- **Sample preparation**: Mechanical polishing to 1200 grit finish, ultrasonic cleaning in acetone
- **Oxidation environment**: Dry air, flow rate 100 mL/min
- **Oxidation temperature levels**: 600, 700, 800, 900 °C
- **Oxidation duration**: 24 h (standard); extended to 96 h for selected 600 °C tests
- **Heating/cooling rate**: 100 °C/min
- **Sample alias**: Fe-7.5Al-0.65C — a single alloy composition investigated across all temperature conditions

## Microstructure and Phase Evolution
- **As-homogenized baseline (Figure 1)**: Ferrite (α-Fe) matrix with uniformly distributed Fe₃AlCₓ (K-phase) carbide precipitates; scale bar 40 μm
- **After 600 °C / 24 h oxidation (Figure 3)**: Dome-shaped oxide nodules protruding from the alloy surface; internal oxidation zones extending into the substrate beneath nodules, characterized by fine internal aluminum oxide precipitates; K-phase carbides still visible in substrate as dark irregular spots
- **After 600 °C / 96 h oxidation (Figure 4)**: Multi-layered oxide structure comprising: (i) outer porous Fe₂O₃ layer, (ii) intermediate (Fe,Al)₂O₃ spinel layer, (iii) internal oxidation zone containing aluminum oxide precipitates beneath the nodule; scale bar 10 μm
- **After 700 °C / 24 h oxidation (Figure 6)**: Multiple surface nodules protruding from the alloy surface; no internal oxidation beneath nodules — a key contrast with 600 °C behavior; EPMA indicates an outer porous iron-oxide layer and an inner aluminum-enriched layer acting as a healing barrier
- **After 900 °C / 24 h oxidation (Figure 7)**: Relatively flat and uniform oxide-metal interface, free of nodules and internal oxidation zones; scale bar 200 μm; two-phase substrate (ferrite + K-phase) visible beneath continuous protective scale

## Processing-Structure-Property Chain
- **Pathway 1 — 600 °C (low-temperature, non-protective regime)**:
  - *Process*: Oxidation at 600 °C in dry air
  - *Structure*: Fe₂O₃/(Fe,Al)₂O₃ nodules + internal aluminum oxide precipitates beneath nodules (Figures 3, 4); three-layer oxide confirmed by EPMA (Figure 5)
  - *Property*: Weight gain curve shows initial parabolic stage followed by dramatic linear increase (Figure 2); breakaway oxidation; non-protective scale
  - *Causal link*: Insufficient Al diffusivity at 600 °C prevents formation of a continuous protective Al₂O₃ scale; inward oxygen diffusion through pores/microcracks leads to internal oxidation
- **Pathway 2 — 700 °C (intermediate regime with healing layer)**:
  - *Process*: Oxidation at 700 °C in dry air
  - *Structure*: Surface nodules present but without internal oxidation (Figure 6); inner aluminum-enriched oxide layer forms beneath each nodule, functioning as a healing barrier
  - *Property*: Highest final weight gain among all temperatures (~1.5 mg cm⁻², Figure 2); rapid initial oxidation followed by plateau
  - *Causal link*: Enhanced Al diffusivity at 700 °C enables formation of an inner Al-rich healing layer that suppresses internal oxidation, but Al flux remains insufficient for a fully continuous protective scale, allowing outward Fe-rich nodule growth
- **Pathway 3 — 800–900 °C (high-temperature, protective regime)**:
  - *Process*: Oxidation at 800 °C and 900 °C in dry air
  - *Structure*: Flat, uniform oxide-metal interface; no nodules; no internal oxidation (Figure 7, 900 °C)
  - *Property*: Significantly lower oxidation rates; 900 °C shows minimum final weight gain (~0.4 mg cm⁻², Figure 2); parabolic kinetics
  - *Causal link*: Sufficient Al diffusivity at elevated temperatures enables establishment of a continuous protective Al₂O₃ scale that suppresses iron cation outward diffusion and eliminates internal oxidation

## Mechanistic Interpretation
- **Aluminum diffusion control**: The transition from internal oxidation (600 °C) to protective scale formation (800–900 °C) is governed by temperature-dependent Al diffusivity in the ferrite matrix. At 600 °C, the Al flux toward the oxidation front is too low to sustain an external Al₂O₃ scale, so oxygen ingress leads to internal Al₂O₃ precipitation beneath Fe-rich nodules.
- **Carbide stability and decarburization**: K-phase (Fe₃AlCₓ) carbides serve as aluminum reservoirs; their thermal stability and dissolution kinetics influence the availability of mobile Al for scale formation. Temperature affects both Al diffusion from the matrix and Al release from dissolving carbides.
- **Nodule growth and healing**: At 700 °C, a partially protective regime emerges: each nodule develops an inner Al-enriched layer that acts as a local diffusion barrier, suppressing internal oxidation beneath it. However, between nodules, insufficient Al coverage allows continued outward Fe diffusion and nodule growth.
- **Protective–non-protective transition**: The oxidation resistance of Fe-7.5Al-0.65C does not decrease monotonically with increasing temperature. Instead, a beneficial transition occurs above ~700–800 °C where Al mobility becomes sufficient to establish a stable, continuous Al₂O₃ scale that passivates the surface.

## Key Quantitative Findings
- **Weight gain at 24 h / 600 °C**: Initial parabolic stage transitioning to linear kinetics (breakaway oxidation, Figure 2)
- **Weight gain at 24 h / 700 °C**: ~1.5 mg cm⁻² — highest among all tested temperatures (Figure 2)
- **Weight gain at 24 h / 900 °C**: ~0.4 mg cm⁻² — lowest among all tested temperatures (Figure 2)
- **EPMA composition across 600 °C / 96 h oxide scale** (Figure 5):
  - Oxide region (0–55 μm from surface): Fe fluctuates between 43 and 67.2 wt%; Al peaks at 14.5 wt% around 40 μm
  - Alloy substrate (>60 μm): stabilizes at 91.8 wt% Fe, 7.5 wt% Al
- **Internal oxidation depth at 600 °C**: Extends into substrate beneath nodules; quantified via cross-sectional metallography (Figures 3, 4, 5)

## Visual Evidence
- **Figure 1 (Optical micrograph, as-homogenized)**: Establishes the baseline two-phase microstructure (ferrite + K-phase carbides) prior to oxidation; serves as reference for all subsequent oxidation-induced microstructural changes.
- **Figure 2 (TGA kinetic curves, 600–900 °C)**: Demonstrates the non-monotonic temperature dependence of oxidation resistance; supports the mechanistic transition from internal oxidation/breakaway kinetics (600 °C) through nodular growth with healing (700 °C) to protective parabolic oxidation (800–900 °C).
- **Figure 3 (Optical cross-section, 600 °C / 24 h)**: Directly visualizes dome-shaped nodules and internal oxidation zones; confirms that at 600 °C, Al flux is insufficient to prevent oxygen ingress and internal Al₂O₃ precipitation.
- **Figure 4 (SEM cross-section, 600 °C / 96 h)**: Reveals the multi-layered oxide architecture (outer Fe₂O₃, middle (Fe,Al)₂O₃ spinel, inner internal oxidation zone); supports the mechanism of inward oxygen diffusion through porous nodules.
- **Figure 5 (EPMA line scan, 600 °C / 96 h)**: Provides quantitative elemental evidence for the three-layer oxide structure; confirms Al enrichment at ~40 μm depth and Fe dominance in outer layers; validates the internal oxidation interpretation.
- **Figure 6 (Optical cross-section, 700 °C / 24 h)**: Shows nodule formation without internal oxidation; supports the "healing layer" mechanism where Al-enriched inner oxide suppresses further oxygen ingress beneath nodules.
- **Figure 7 (Optical cross-section, 900 °C / 24 h)**: Demonstrates a flat, uniform, nodule-free oxide-metal interface; visually confirms the establishment of a continuous protective scale at high temperature, consistent with the lowest weight gain in Figure 2.
