# Transverse tensile and fatigue properties of Fe-Mn-Al-C alloys

M. T. JAHN

Department of Mechanical Engineering, California State University, Long Beach, California 90840, USA

S. C. CHANG, Y. H. HSIAO

Department of Materials Science and Engineering, National Tsing Hua University, Hsinchu, Taiwan

The studies of Fe–Mn–Al (Femnal) alloys have been attractive and significant in the development of new series of stainless steels $[1-4]$ . In industrial applications and in particular the making of structural and machinery components, the mechanical properties of the alloys are most demanding. Banerji $[2]$ reported that the Femnal system possesses yield and tensile strengths about 50% higher than the conventional stainless steels (300 series) with equivalent ductility. Our recent study $[3]$ shows that at an elongation of 40%, the tensile strength of Femnal alloys can easily reach 1033 MPa which is roughly twice the tensile strength of 300 series stainless steels if proper ageing is applied. Because more than 90% of the premature failure of dynamic machinery components is due to fatigue, it is critical that the fatigue behaviour and fatigue mechanisms of Femnal alloys be examined and understood. The tensile properties and fatigue behaviour of three Femnal alloys having various carbon contents leading to different volume fraction-of austenite and ferrite phase, have been investigated by the authors $[5]$ . The stress applied was longitudinal. In this study, transverse stress was applied to the three Femnal alloys for tensile and fatigue tests. The results are compared with those published previously $[5]$ and the mechanisms are discussed.

Three Femnal alloys with different carbon contents were examined in this work. They were Fe-29Mn-9.4Al-1.1C (alloy A), Fe-29Mn-9.1Al-0.6C (alloy B) and Fe-29Mn-9.2Al-0.26C (alloy C). The alloys were prepared by melting appropriate percentages of commercial pure iron, electrolytic manganese, carbon powder and pure aluminium in an induction furnace protected in an argon atmosphere. The ingots were hot forged, homogenized and cold rolled to a thickness of 2 mm. After cold rolling, the plates were solution treated at 1050°C for 1.5 h followed by oil quenching and specimen machining. The details of tensile test, fatigue test and microstructural observation were described in the previous study [5]. However, the stress orientation in this study is transverse which is different from the longitudinal stress applied in previous research [5].

Optical microscopy indicated that after solution treatment, specimen A was fully austenite, specimen B contained 10% ferrite and 90% austenite and specimen C consisted of 55% ferrite and 45% austenite [5]. The results of the tensile tests are summarized in Table I. Each specimen is represented by two letters. The first letter (A, B or C) indicates the alloy type after solution treatment. The second letter indicates the stress direction, L for longitudinal and T for transverse. The data show that the longitudinal specimen possessed higher tensile strength and lower elongation than the transverse specimen for the same type of alloy. However, the differences are not significant. The fatigue S-N curves of alloys AL and CL coincide with the curves of AT and CT. No differences were observed between the fatigue properties of longitudinal specimens and transverse specimens for alloys A and C. The S-N curves of alloy B are shown in Fig. 1 indicating that the fatigue properties of specimen BL are better than specimen BT. It was observed that in alloy B slip bands only occurred in the austenite phase. No slip bands or fatigue cracks were found within the ferrite phase. The fatigue cracks initiated at slip bands and propagated along a direction roughly normal to the applied stress. If the string arrangement of ferrite phase was parallel to the crack propagation direction as in specimens BT, the cracks propagated across the austenite grains, as shown in Fig. 2. If the string arrangement of the ferrite phase was perpendicular to the crack propagation as in specimens BL, it was observed that the crack propagation was impeded by the ferrite phase, as shown in Fig. 3. In this case, it is believed that the ferrite phase serves as a crack arrester. Because the ferrite grains are fine and soft, the blunting effect at the initial stage of fatigue and the intense work hardening at the later stage may lead to the impediment of crack propagation. Because fatigue crack propagation is impeded by the ferrite phase in specimen BL, this specimen is expected to possess better fatigue resistance than specimen BT, which is in agreement with our experimental results.

From the results and discussion mentioned above, the following conclusions were obtained. The tensile properties of the transverse specimen are very close to those of the longitudinal specimen if the specimens possess the same chemical composition. The fatigue behaviour of specimens A and C is independent of specimen orientation. However, the fatigue life of the longitudinal specimen B is much better than that of the transverse specimen B, because the fatigue crack propagation is impeded by the string arrangement of ferrite phase in specimen BL.
