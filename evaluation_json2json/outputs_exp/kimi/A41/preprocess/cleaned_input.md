# A B S T R A C T

This study investigates the bainitic transformation and mechanical performance of five medium-to high-Al steels with varying C contents, alloying elements (Ti, Ni), and austenitizing/austempering parameters. In steels with ≥6 % wt.% Al, the fastest transformation was determined for S1 at $3 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ with a transformation time 1600 s. In contrast, for the S3 steel with 8.08 wt.% Al, the transformation time increased dramatically, ranging from 14,000 to 43,000 s depending on the austempering temperature. This significant delay is attributed to the presence of excessively stabilized retained austenite, resulting from C-enrichment around polygonal ferrite. For S5, a lower C (0.60 wt.%) and moderate Al (3.92 wt.%) steel, the transformation kinetics were the fastest (700-1000 s), and reduced impingement was observed in the microstructure. Additionally, lenticular martensite near the Ms temperature further accelerated the transformation at lower austempering temperature. Regarding mechanical properties, the ultimate tensile strength (UTS) ranged from 1315 MPa to 1600 MPa. Ti-micro-alloyed S4 achieved the highest average total elongation (10 %) under optimal heat treatment. Yield strength $\mathrm { ( R _ { p 0 . 2 } ) }$ trends generally matched the UTS, except for S3 at low austempering temperature. Microstructural precipitate analysis confirmed the primary formation of $( \mathrm { F e M ) _ { x } C _ { y } }$ and TiMoC. MatCalc simulations supported these findings, indicating primary precipitation above austempering temperatures at the austenitizing and cooling stages. Overall, the findings highlight the pivotal role of alloy composition and microstructural features in governing bainitic transformation and the resulting strength–ductility response.

# 1. Introduction

The development of advanced steels with high strength, ductility, and toughness remains a key objective in materials engineering, particularly for demanding applications such as the automotive industry. Bainitic steels are especially attractive due to their promising mechanical properties [1–4]. On the other hand, vehicle weight reduction is a critical factor in improving fuel efficiency and reducing emis sions. In this regard, the low-density steel (LDS) concept, typically Fe–Mn–C-based alloys alloyed with lightweight elements such as Al and/or Si, has gained increasing attention as they offer reduced density without compromising strength [5–11]. Using Al has been reported to reduce the density of steel by approximately 1.3 % per 1 wt.% addition [5,7,10]. Adding more than 9 wt.% Al can lead to embrittlement due to ordering and environmental effects [8]. Moreover, Al is known for suppressing carbide precipitation, stabilizing ferrite, and accelerating bainitic transformation [1,2,6,9,12,13]. Furthermore, Al plays a significant role in refining bainitic lamellae during low-temperature austempering, aiding in the design of nano-bainitic (nB) steels: According to the study of Hu on the nano-bainitic (nB) steels, the addition of only 3.4 wt.% Al resulted in an ultimate tensile strength (UTS) of around 2 GPa and 15 % total elongation (El) while also reducing the density down to 5 % [12]. García-Mateos et al. demonstrated that in high-carbon steels (0.80 wt.%) alloyed with approximately 1.6 wt.% Si and 1.0 wt.% Al, a UTS (2 GPa), and an El of 10 % were achieved. This was attributed to an increased amount and enhanced stability of retained austenite (γr), promoted by the improved strain-hardening effect [14]. In the study of [15], approximately 2 GPa of UTS and 9 % El were achieved in nB steel containing around 3 wt.% Al. The role of Al in solid solution strengthening within iron matrices has been a subject of debate. Some studies classify Al as a potent solid solution strengthener [10,16], while others report minimal strengthening effects [17,18]. These contradictions suggest that the synergistic interactions between Al and other alloying elements, such as C and Mn, play a crucial role in determining the mechanical properties of these steels. In the conventional steels, the addition of Al suppresses cementite precipitation by decreasing both the activity and diffusivity of C in austenite, while promoting the formation of short-range order through the ordering of Al and C in the solid solution. Short-range order generates the strain energy necessary for spinodal decomposition, ultimately leading to the formation of precipitates [5]. As a known ferrite stabilizer, Al has been shown in several studies to accelerate the bainitic transformation $[ 2 , 6 , 9 , 1 5 , 1 9 ]$ . The potential synergistic effects of C–Al and further microalloying elements in bainitic steels remain an open topic for further investigation.

This study investigates the transformation kinetics and mechanical properties of high C and Al-containing nB-steels, with a focus on clarifying the microscopic mechanisms that underlie these properties. Precipitation kinetics simulations are used as a complementary tool in this investigation. The novelty of this work lies in the systematic investigation of transformation behavior and mechanical performance in nanobainitic steels with relatively high C–Al contents, a compositional range that remains insufficiently studied in the literature, given its relevance for low-density, high-strength applications.

# 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Alloy design

In this study, the investigated steels were specifically designed to minimize the density and bainitic transformation time (Bt) via Al, which was a primary objective in the development of nB steels. For steels S1, S2, S3, and S4, incorporating Al at levels of 5.8–8 wt.% and limiting Mn content to 1 wt.% helps stabilize the ferrite phase, thereby accelerating the transformation process. However, this approach also has the drawback of raising the martensite start (Ms) temperature, reducing the size of the austenite single-phase region, and shifting it to higher temperatures and C contents. To address this, a high C content of 0.9–1.0 wt.% is required to achieve full austenitization without polygonal ferrite $( \alpha _ { \mathsf { p } } )$ phases and to maintain a Ms temperature around $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ Si is added to suppress the carbide precipitation, but its content is kept low at 0.3 wt.% since Al also performs this role. Additionally, Mo is included to enhance hardenability, while micro-alloying with B is utilized to further improve hardenability only for S1, S2, S3, and S4 without negatively impacting the bainitic transformation. In Steel S4, a slightly lower Al content was utilized to suppress ${ \mathfrak { X } } _ { \mathrm { p } }$ formation, while a reduced C content was employed to accelerate the bainitic transformation and mitigate brittleness. Additionally, Ti microalloying was applied to enhance bainitic transformation and improve mechanical properties. To prevent the potential formation of ${ \mathfrak { d } } _ { \mathfrak { p } }$ due to the decrease in C content, 0.78 % Ni was added to the composition. Alloy S5 is designed with a lower alloying concept. This alloy contains 0.6 % C and 3.92 % Al. The addition of Ni promotes an $\alpha _ { \mathrm { p } } .$ free microstructure without compromising the bainitic transformation kinetics or reducing the resulting strength [20,21], in contrast to the commonly used Mn. Ni content was minimized to 0.78 % due to its high cost and austenite stabilization effect in the steel S4 and S5. Chemical compositions of the steel grades are given in Table 1.

# 2.3. Microscopic methods

A comprehensive set of characterization techniques was employed to evaluate the microstructure, phase composition, and precipitation behavior of the investigated steels. These included light optical microscopy (LOM), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), energy-dispersive Xray spectroscopy (EDX), electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD), X-ray diffraction (XRD) and precipitation modeling using MatCalc. The specific sample preparation procedures and analysis methodologies for each technique are detailed below.

For LOM (Carl Zeiss AG, Jena, Germany), samples were etched with 6 % Nital solution to reveal microstructural features, particularly due to the high Al content. First, $1 0 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ was established as the benchmark temperature $\left( \mathrm { T _ { A } } \right)$ for all steels, and at this condition the overall ferrite distribution was inspected after applying black-and-white contrast in ImageJ. Quantitative image analysis of the primary $\alpha _ { \sf p }$ volume fraction and spatial distribution was performed only for steel $^ { \mathbf { \boldsymbol { S 3 } } } \mathrm { , }$ , selected due to its higher polygonal ferrite content, using ImageJ (v1.53, NIH, Bethesda, MD, USA) on light-optical micrographs $( 2 0 0 ~ \times ~ )$ acquired from five distinct regions. Subsequently, gold sputter coating was applied to etched samples to enable SEM analysis (Carl Zeiss $\mathbf { A G } ,$ Jena, Germany) and EDX (Oxford Instruments, Abingdon, UK). SEM micrographs were utilized to determine bainite thickness with the aid of ImageJ image analysis software. For each condition, 100 measurements were conducted from 10 SEM images per specimen, with 10 measurements taken per image. The true lamellar thickness, Lt(t), was calculated by first measuring the mean linear intercept, Lt, perpendicular to the length of the bainitic lamellae. As Lt cannot directly represent the actual thickness of the lamellae, a stereological correction was applied using the relationship $L t ( t ) = 2 \mathrm { L t } / \pi .$ This method is specified in the study of [24], and also used in these studies [15,25].

MatCalc was used to calculate the phase transformations and precipitation kinetics (Time-Temperature Precipitation (TTP) diagrams). The equilibrium calculations were performed using the standard step calculation method. The synergy of existing phases fcc, bcc, liquid, κ-carbide, hcp carbide, AlN, TiCN–TiMoC system was considered for both equilibrium and kinetic calculations. $\mathrm { T i - M o - C , ( F e , M ) _ { x } C _ { y } }$ carbide systems were kinetically simulated by a precipitation kinetics model according to the heat treatment defined for bainitic transformation. The modeling was based on a heterogeneous nucleation theory, considering only dislocation and grain boundary sites and ortho-equilibrium transformation. Volumetric misfit was also considered in the simulations due to possible lattice parameter mismatches between the investigated carbides and the ferritic/austenitic matrix, which can notably influence nucleation energy and growth kinetics. κ-carbides were considered as fixed phase fractions due to the consideration of that κ-carbides form with the spinodal decomposition [26–28], hence inappropriate for classical nucleation theory.

XRD measurements were performed on a Malvern Panalytical Empyrean diffractometer, equipped with a Cu X-ray tube, operated at 40 kV/40 mA with a PIXcel 1D detector at the TU Clausthal Institute of Non-Metallic Materials on mechanically grinded and polished samples. The diffractometer was operated at a voltage of 40 kV with Cu Kα radiation. The volume fraction of retained austenite $( V _ { \gamma } )$ and micro-strains (for austenite: $\varepsilon \gamma ,$ for bainitic ferrite: εα) were determined using the Rietveld refinement method, which relies on the integrated intensity of XRD peaks, following the guidelines of ASTM E975-13 [29]. Due to the high alloying concept, the presence of precipitates and intermetallic phases was expected to enhance peak intensity because they can be embedded, particularly within the fcc crystals. # 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Determination of $T _ { A }$ and Ms temperatures

This section quantifies austenitization and extracts useable $\mathrm { T _ { A } }$ values by integrating dilatometry, LOM, and MatCalc equilibrium phase diagram, explicitly addressing the broadened dissolution signals in high-C–Al steels that obscure a unique $\mathrm { T _ { A } , }$ and Ms values and guiding the chosen optimized heat-treatment conditions.

In this high C–Al alloy set, overlapping carbide-dissolution sequences tend to broaden the RLC–Temperature (T) signal and prevented a single sharp break from marking the austenitizing temperature $\mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { A } } .$ An operational $\mathrm { T _ { A } }$ was therefore taken from the differentiated curves as the temperature at which the first-derivative peak decayed and linearity of the base curve was first restored. Using this criterion, approximate $\mathrm { T _ { A } }$ values were obtained as $1 0 6 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \left( \mathrm { S } 1 \right)$ , 1031 ◦C (S2), a broadened transition without a unique $\mathrm { T _ { A } }$ for S3, $1 0 6 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \ ( \mathrm { S } 4 )$ , and ${ \sim } 9 0 0 \ { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (S5) as shown in Fig. 3. To disentangle concurrent effects and to estimate the carbide-dissolution temperature (Tcd) and the ferrite-free $\mathrm { T _ { A } , }$ MatCalc was employed as an assisting tool (see Fig. 4). The simulations indicated ferrite-free $\mathrm { T _ { A } }$ values of 991 ◦C (S1), 929 ◦C (S2), 1177 ◦C (S3), 1016 ◦C (S4), and 986 ◦C (S5) and predicted complete dissolution of MC-type (Fe, $\mathrm { M ) _ { x } C _ { y } }$ carbides around $1 1 0 3 - 1 1 0 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for S1–S2 and near $1 1 3 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for S4. Guided by these considerations, $1 0 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ was first assessed as a benchmark temperature for all steels to reach the $\alpha _ { \mathrm { p } } .$ -free microstructure (for LOM images see Fig. 3). At 1050 ◦C, S1 displayed a dual-phase structure with ${ \mathfrak { d } } _ { { \mathfrak { p } } } ,$ whereas S2 appeared $\mathfrak { a } _ { \mathfrak { p } ^ { - } } \mathrm { f r e e } _ { \mathrm { i } }$ , consistent with its lower derivative-based $\mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { A } } .$ S3 exhibited extensive ${ \mathfrak { d } } _ { \mathfrak { p } }$ at $1 0 5 0 ^ { \circ } ($ (area fraction $\sim 1 4 . 6 \% )$ , and ${ \mathfrak { d } } _ { \mathfrak { p } }$ persisted even at $1 2 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (area fraction: \~8.2 %), in line with the simulated requirement ${ \bf o f } \sim 1 1 7 7 ^ { \circ } { \bf C }$ for approaching a ferrite-free state and with the known ferrite-stabilizing effect of very high Al; a single experimental $\mathrm { T _ { A } }$ could therefore not be assigned to S3. S4 contained a visibly higher amount of ${ \mathfrak { d } } _ { \mathfrak { p } }$ at $1 0 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , but only a trace level (≲ 1 %) at $1 1 5 0 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ consistent with the derivative-based $\mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { A } } .$ Similarly, S5 showed more prominent ${ \mathfrak { d } } _ { \mathfrak { p } }$ at $1 0 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and only a very small residual fraction (≲ 1 %) a $: 1 1 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ while the simulations placed Tcd near ${ \sim } 7 7 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and a ferrite-free $\mathrm { T _ { A } }$ around $9 8 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . Taken together, the three data streams (dilatometry, simulation, and LOM) point to $1 1 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ as an optimized austenitizing temperature for S1, S2, S4, and ${ \mathsf { S } } 5 ,$ where carbide dissolution is advanced, chemical homogenization is improved, and ${ \mathfrak { d } } _ { \mathfrak { p } }$ is suppressed to negligible or non-existing levels. For $^ { S 3 , }$ a higher schedule of $1 2 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ was required to minimize ${ \mathfrak { X } } _ { \mathrm { p } }$ and to approach the simulated ferrite-free field, although a fully $\alpha _ { \mathrm { p } } .$ -free condition could not be realized within the practical window. Additionally, the rationale for these applied temperatures follows directly from the above synthesis. At substantially lower $\mathrm { T _ { A } , }$ incomplete dissolution and carbon heterogeneity would be retained, which are known to increase scatter in mechanical response and to disturb bainitic transformation through particle-assisted impingement.

Consequently, to enable consistent comparison among steels with varying $\mathrm { T _ { A } , }$ a fixed austenitization temperature of $1 1 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ was applied for alloys S1, S2, S4, and S5 in this study. This approach ensures that observed differences in bainitic transformation behavior are primarily attributable to alloy composition and $\mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { B } } ,$ rather than variations in prior austenite conditions, Only for the alloy with the highest Al content (8 wt. %) (S3), 1220 ◦C represents the most effective compromise available for the minimum $\alpha _ { \sf p }$ level and maximum austenitizing. Where relevant (e.g., S4), an additional high-temperature schedule (e.g., 1250 ◦C) can further dissolve nanoscale MC/TiC precipitate and enhance austenite homogenization.

The measured values were $2 7 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ( \mathrm { S } 1 ) , 2 7 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ( \mathrm { S } 2 ) , 2 5 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ( \mathrm { S } 3 )$ , 310 ◦C (S4) after austenitizing at $1 1 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $3 0 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ after austenitizing at $1 2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , and 331 ◦C (S5) (see $\mathrm { F i g } . 5 ) .$ The order of magnitudes conforms to the established inverse dependence of Ms on the carbon content of austenite, with secondary influences from substitutional solutes (Al tending to raise Ms; Mn, Ni, and Mo tending to suppress it)[30–34].

In S1–S3, the relatively low Ms values indicate austenite that is effectively carbon-enriched after austenitization; the lowest Ms in S3 is consistent with extended κ-carbide (see Fig. 4 (c)) dissolution together with ferrite retention that facilitates local carbon partitioning into austenite despite the Ms-raising tendency of its very high Al content. In S4, Ms decreased only marginally from $3 1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C } \left( 1 2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C } \right.$ austenitization) to $3 0 5 ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C } \left( 1 1 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C } \right.$ austenitization). In S4, Ms changes only marginally with austenitizing temperature (305 ◦C at $1 1 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ versus $3 1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at $1 2 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ; \Delta \colon 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ) ,$ , which is within experimental scatter. No clear effect of the Ti microalloying addition on Ms was evident (Fig. 5 (d)). In S5, the relatively high Ms (331 ◦C) reflects the lower effective carbon in austenite under the chosen condition, in agreement with the continueddissolution signature observed in the heating curve and with its lower overall alloying. Overall, the Ms trends are internally consistent with the alloy chemistries and the austenitization analysis, and they support the heat-treatment strategy: austempering temperatures were chosen slightly above the measured Ms values to promote bainitic transformation while suppressing martensite.

S5 steel, characterized by an Ms of 331 ◦C, contains relatively low Cand Al contents (0.60 wt.% C and 3.92 wt.% Al, respectively). The incubation period was completed within 10 s, followed by a rapid bainitic transformation. Complete bainitic transformation was observed at $3 3 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ after 700 s and at 365 ◦C after 1000 s during austempering. According to the work of [15], steels containing approximately 5 wt.% Al (with 0.67–0.94 wt.% C) exhibited incubation times between 10 and 30 s and a 2000–6000 s transformation time. In contrast, steels with approximately 3 wt.% Al (with 0.77–0.95 wt.% C) showed incubation times ranging from 100 to 800 s and a 2900–9000 s transformation time. In comparison to these alloys, S5 steel with 3.92 wt.% Al may be considered to represent a near-stoichiometric composition that provides optimal transformation kinetics. In addition to Al content, the relatively lower C concentration (0.60 wt.%) in S5 also contributes to the acceleration of the bainitic transformation. Carbon is known to stabilize austenite and delay the transformation, whereas its reduction facilitates faster transformation of bainite. This is consistent with the observed shorter transformation times in S5 compared to other alloys with higher carbon levels, Additionally, the presence of 0.78 wt.% Ni did not adversely affect the bainitic transformation kinetics. Similar to the S4 steel, the ${ \mathfrak { d } } _ { \mathfrak { p } }$ content was maintained at a minimal level due to the austenite-stabilizing effect of Ni, without any detrimental impact on the transformation behavior.

Nonetheless, its UTS (1360–1470 MPa) remained lower than those of S1 and S2 under equivalent thermal conditions. However, due to the higher El in S4, its PSE was in fact comparable or even superior to that of S1 and S2, indicating that S4 offers a better strength–ductility balance despite its lower carbon content (0.87 wt.%).

Steel S5 exhibited UTS ranged from 1315 MPa with 8.7 % El at 365 ◦C (PSE: 11,450 MPa⋅%) to 1530 MPa with 7.0 % El at 335 ◦C (PSE: 10,710 MPa⋅%). In this context, the UTS–El performance of the S5 steel can be positioned intermediate between steels containing 3 wt.% and 5 wt.% $\mathbf { A l } ,$ and possessing comparatively higher C contents (ranging from 0.67 wt.% to 0.94 wt.%).

In summary, within the tested window: (i) $\mathrm { { T _ { B } } }$ primarily tunes the UTS–El trade-off via plate thickness and retained-austenite stability; (ii) the 8.08 wt.% Al design is detrimental to ductility at lower TB; (iii) C variations near 1 wt.% are secondary to Al level in setting that balance; and (iv) Ti + Ni alloying in S4 compensates for lower C by elevating El and thus PSE, offering the best overall balance among the studied steels.

# 3.4.3. Discussion of ${ \geq } 6$ % Al-alloyed steels (S1, S2, S3)

Fe3C is known as a ductility-decreasing precipitate due to its brittle nature, which reduces the steel matrix’s ability to undergo plastic deformation by acting as a stress concentrator that promotes crack initiation and propagation, resulting in lower elongation [2,37]. However, in the study of [38] on the high-C steel, it was demonstrated that the accelerated nucleation of Fe3C precipitates improved ductility. In other studies were shown that controlling the particle size and achieving a spherical morphology of Fe C can enhance ductility [39,40]. In our research, it can be claimed that accelerated nucleation of and spherical nature of these cementite-like $( \mathrm { F e , M ) _ { x } C _ { y } }$ precipitates showed no micro-void formation (see Fig. 9), hence evaluated as having negligible effect on the mechanical properties. The presence of Si within the Fe C crystal, as revealed by EDX analysis see Fig. 9 (j), can be interpreted as the result of entrapment within the lattice due to rapid cooling, since under equilibrium conditions, Si is not expected to dissolve in Fe3C, hence have suppressing effect on them [2]. Furthermore, the existence of Mo in these precipitates can have enhanced their stability, thereby inhibiting void formation.

The observed particles exhibit a rounded morphology inconsistent with spinodal decomposition, indicating a nucleation-driven formation mechanism. Given the apparent absence of significant Al within the particles, their composition is more consistent with cementite-like (Fe, $\mathbf { M } ) _ { \mathbf { x } } \mathbf { C } _ { \mathbf { y } }$ carbides modified by the presence of M = Si, Mo. These carbides are located near the γr–bainitic ferrite phase boundaries and were only detectable due to their localized brightness under SEM, despite spatial resolution limitations during EDX analysis. The observation of these precipitates was achieved for steels S1, S2, and S3 as seen in Fig. 9.

E21-type stoichiometric perovskite κ-carbide was not directly examined microscopically in this study. The absence of observable κ-carbide structures is attributed to the limited Mn content (≈1 wt.%), which is insufficient to stabilize such phases. As a result, no plate-like morphology was detected, and the remaining κ-carbides are presumed to exist in the matrix as nanoscale, embedded, non-stoichiometric $\mathrm { L } 1 _ { 2 ^ { - } }$ type structures.

At $\mathrm { { T _ { B } } }$ of 350 ◦C, the standard deviation Lt(t) increased, indicating the presence of bainitic structures with a broader range of thicknesses, such as nB Type 1 and Type 2, alongside blocky γr regions located between them (see Fig. 9). This structural phenomenon is particularly pronounced in the S1, S2, and S3 steels (see Table 3 for the SD difference between the temperatures of $\mathrm { T _ { B } ) }$ . This heterogeneity promotes the formation of interlocked bainitic morphologies consisting of different bainite types. For S4, this correlation is lower, and it is negligible for S5. This indicates that the lower alloying concept suppresses variative Lt(t). This can be considered as an El improving factor as shown in aforementioned Fig. 7.

For steel S3, a modified austenitization approach was adopted due to the unavoidable presence of ${ \mathfrak { d } } _ { \mathfrak { p } }$ (8.2 %). The ferrite plates are named based on their morphology, as their α or δ nature remains unclear. The structures are observed to be occasionally idiomorphic but predominantly allotriomorphic (see LOM in Fig. 9(h) and (i)).

Although ${ \mathfrak { d } } _ { \mathfrak { p } }$ can potentially serve as nucleation sites for bainitic transformation, thereby accelerating transformation kinetics [41], the low C solubility of pre-existing ${ \mathfrak { d } } _ { \mathfrak { p } }$ leads to significant C-partitioning into the adjacent austenite, resulting in C-enrichment [42]. This partitioning locally stabilizes the adjacent austenite, thereby inhibiting austenite–bainite transformation by increasing the energy barrier around ${ \mathfrak { d } } _ { \mathfrak { p } }$ plates [43,44]. Consequently, the lack of sufficient driving force for the austenite – bainitic ferrite displacive transformation leads to the partially decomposed sluggish γr in some micro-regions (see Fig. 9 (f)) [45]. Indeed, while initial nucleation was observed at α –austenite interfaces (see Fig. 9 (h), (i)), the bainite growth could not be propagated and stalled at many of these sites, resulting in transformation-free regions that required significantly longer holding times to achieve complete bainitic transformation. This behavior is particularly evident in the LOM images at $2 7 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 25,200 s (see LOM Fig. 9 (h)), where the transformation remained incomplete; consequently, a prolonged duration of 43,200 s was required to achieve near-complete transformation (see Fig. 9 (i)). Furthermore, at ${ 2 7 0 } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ where the Lt(t) is reduced from 58 nm to 40 nm, the higher average εγ (0.33 %) compared to the ferritic phases (0.58 %) (see Table 3). The significantly higher ε compared to the adjacent ferritic phases suggests that dislocation accumulation is particularly pronounced in the metastable γr, leading to the formation of highly brittle phase boundaries and potential stress spiking regions. These factors explain the severe drop in El at that parameter.

# 3.4.4. Discussion of Ti-micro-alloyed steel (S4)

In the study of [46,47], TiMoC exhibits favorable wettability characteristics with the steel matrix in reinforced austenitic high-Mn steels, contributing to enhanced interfacial bonding. Despite a reduction in El, approximately 16 % in the reinforced variant compared to 35 % in conventional steels, the change in UTS and $\mathrm { R } _ { \mathrm { p } 0 . 2 }$ was negligible. These results are intrinsic to the powder metallurgy processing route employed. On the other hand, in our study, the self-nucleated TiMoC complex phase was expected to exhibit superior thermodynamic and structural stability relative to discrete TiC and MoC precipitate systems according to the findings of [48]. Within the S4 steel design framework, incorporation of TiMoC was used to mitigate the formation of $( \mathrm { F e , M ) _ { x } C _ { y } , }$ MoC, and probably κ-carbide phases commonly exists in analogous alloy systems. Additionally, facilitating refined microstructural evolution would help transformation kinetics be accelerated, while the TiMoC architecture further enables controlled coarsening, offering potential improvements in El. These precipitates accelerate bainite nucleation by providing initial nucleation sites for bainite sheaves, leading to a shorter incubation time compared to steels S1, S2, and S3. However, due to the impingement of bainite sheaves (see Fig. 10) caused by an increased number of various phase boundaries [49–51], the progression of bainite transformation after the incubation period was gradually suppressed in S4 compared to S1 and S2 (see Fig. 6).

Nonetheless, the S4 steel exhibited the best average and maximum El of 10 and 12 % at $\mathrm { T _ { A } }$ 1150 ◦C and $\mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { B } } \colon 3 5 0 \ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , which are slightly higher than S1and S2 as aforementioned (see Fig. 7). This shows that the suppression of El was not determined due to the existence of TiMoC on the phase and grain boundaries.

As expected, $( \mathrm { F e , M ) _ { x } C _ { y } }$ and MoC precipitates were less present in the final microstructure compared to steels S1, S2 and $^ { S 3 , }$ due to the suppresing effect of TiMoC. The longitudinal alignment of TiMoC precipitates indicates their formation during the cooling phase after casting, followed by deformation during subsequent hot forming, indicating that secondary precipitation played a negligible role (Fig. 10 (a), (b), (c)). These findings are compatible with the results of TTP (see Fig. 8 (c)). To achieve the desired microstructure through the precipitation of Ti–Mo carbides along with a controlled amount of free TiC, an austenitization temperature of $1 2 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ was selected. This temperature was also specifically chosen because it suppressed partially the impingement associated with the extremely small prior austenite grain sizes observed at 1150 ${ } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ thereby facilitating relatively faster phase transformations. Furthermore, a primary characteristic of Ti–Mo–C complex carbides is the absence of micro-defects within the microstructure (Fig. 10 (a), (b), (c)). As illustrated by the EDX mapping, a depletion of Fe was detected within the complex carbides, further confirming their composition (see Fig. 10 (d)).

# 3.4.5. Discussion of relatively low C–Al alloyed steel (S5)

In S5, the moderate Al–C level together with a small Ni addition promotes very rapid bainitic transformation when austempered slightly above Ms, yielding a predominantly bainitic microstructure with filmlike γr and only isolated secondary constituents (see Fig. 8 (d)). The EBSD maps substantiate this fast kinetics by revealing a fine, wellconnected bainitic ferrite network with minimal impingement. The IPF maps (Fig. 11 (d), (e)) for $\mathrm { { T _ { B } } }$ at $3 3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ show narrow packets of bainitic ferrite laths with small, scattered lenticular martensite (labelled M). The packets exhibit intra-packet orientation clustering, as expected for laths sharing a parent variant, separated by frequent highangle packet/block boundaries. The corresponding KAM maps (Fig. 11 (g), (h)) display bands of elevated local misorientation along lath/ packet boundaries and at sheaf tips; in the martensite-focused field (Fig. 11 (h)) local KAM hot-spots occur adjacent to M, consistent with the high density of geometrically necessary dislocations generated by low-temperature displacive growth. Although dilatometry gave Ms of 331 ◦C, the sporadic M plates at 335 ◦C (Ms+4 K) are compatible with stress-assisted transformation and local thermal/compositional heterogeneities, i.e. Ms is distributed at the microscale. XRD at this condition indicates the highest micro-strain in austenite (εγ: 0.51 %), slightly above ferrite (εα: 0.45 %), and a lower retained-austenite fraction (19 %) (Table 3). These features explain the higher strength but reduced ductility measured at 335 ◦C (UTS: 1530 MPa, El: 7 %): finer plates and concentrated defect fields raise strength, while the lower γr content limits TRIP-assisted strain hardening.

The IPF map (Fig. 11 (f)) for at $\mathrm { { T _ { B } } }$ of $3 6 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ reveals slightly coarser bainitic packets and a trace of allotriomorphic ferrite (labelled F), while martensite is absent. The global KAM field (Fig. 11 (i)) is marginally higher on average than at 335 ◦C (mean KAM \~ 0.905◦ vs 0.825◦; median 0.789◦ vs 0.692), whereas local KAM maxima are more pronounced near M at 335 ◦C. KAM is step-size sensitive; the maps were acquired at different magnifications $( 2 5 \mathbf { k } \times \mathbf { v s } 2 0 \mathbf { k } \times \mathbf { ) } .$ , so small differences in absolute values should be interpreted with this measurement sensitivity in mind. XRD corroborates the microstructural evolution. The retainedaustenite fraction increases to \~26 % and the phase micro-strains converge (εγ: 0.42 %, εα: 0.43 %), consistent with reduced lattice curvature at the higher transformation temperature and improved deformation uniformity. The mechanical response follows accordingly (UTS: 1315 MPa, El: 8.7 %).

# 4. Conclusion

This work systematically compares bainitic transformation kinetics and tensile behavior across five low-density Al–C steels with varied C–Al levels and Ti microalloying, supported by LOM, SEM /EDX, XRD, EBSD, and MatCalc simulations. The conclusions are as follows.
