{"image_type": "microscopy_sem", "description": "This figure presents a series of SEM micrographs illustrating the microstructural evolution of Fe–10Mn–10Al–0.7C low-density steel annealed at temperatures from 700 °C to 1200 °C for 1 h. Subfigures (a) through (g) display the RD–TD section, while (h) shows the RD–ND section. At 700 °C (a), the γ phase has almost completely decomposed into a lamellar eutectoid mixture of α-ferrite and κ-carbide, with continuous κ-carbide decorating α grain boundaries. At 800 °C (b) and 850 °C (c), the γ fraction increases while κ-carbide coarsens toward a globular morphology and its overall fraction decreases. Between 900 °C (d) and 1100 °C (g), no obvious κ-carbide is visible in the matrix; only trace boundary κ-carbide forms along some α/α grain boundaries during quenching, accompanied by significant grain growth of both α and γ phases. At 1200 °C (h), the microstructure consists of very large equiaxed α and γ grains, with caption-reported needle-like or plate-like FCC particles inside some large α grains. The progressive dissolution of κ-carbide between 850 and 900 °C and the consequent coarsening of the duplex α+γ microstructure above 900 °C are central to understanding the loss of precipitation strengthening and the resulting tensile property trends.", "confidence": "high"}
