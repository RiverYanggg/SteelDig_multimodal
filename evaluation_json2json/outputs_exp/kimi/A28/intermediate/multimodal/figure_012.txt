  "description": "Figure 11 consists of scanning electron microscopy (SEM) micrographs showing the regions immediately beneath the fracture surfaces of Fe–20Mn–6Al–0.6C–0.15Si austenitic low-density steel after Charpy impact testing at (a) −196 °C, (b) −77 °C, and (c) room temperature (RT). Yellow arrows indicate planar glide traces—aligned slip bands or deformation markings—in the near-fracture microstructure. At −196 °C, planar glide is scarce and restricted to isolated regions, whereas at −77 °C these features become more numerous and pronounced. At RT, planar glide is most extensive and densely distributed. This temperature dependence demonstrates that although reduced stacking fault energy favors glide planarity during low-strain-rate tensile deformation, the higher activation energy required for slip at cryogenic temperatures inhibits planar glide activation under high-strain-rate impact conditions. As a result, the paucity of planar glide barriers at −196 °C offers less resistance to crack propagation, explaining the experimentally observed poor cryogenic impact toughness despite the steel’s increased tensile ductility at lower temperatures.
