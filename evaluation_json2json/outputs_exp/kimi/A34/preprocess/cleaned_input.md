# a b s t r a c t

The hot deformation behavior of low-density high-strength FeeMneAleC steel at T  900 e1150 -C and $\dot { \varepsilon } = 0 . 0 1 \mathrm { - } 1 0 { \ s } ^ { - 1 }$ is studied using the Gleeble-3500 thermo-mechanical simulator. The rheological stress curve characteristics of the steel are analyzed through experimental data and the dynamic recrystallization activation energy is calculated. The critical strain/stress, peak strain/stress and steady-state strain/stress models of dynamic recrystallization are characterized by introducing the dimensionless parameter Zener-Hollomon. The microstructure evolution is analyzed and the dynamic recrystallization state diagram of the steel is drawn. The results indicated that the steel is a negative temperature-sensitive and a positive strain rate-sensitive material. The dynamic recrystallization activation energy is 669.844 kJ mol1 . With the decrease of ε\_ or the increase of T, the Z parameter decreases gradually and the dynamic recrystallization behavior is more likely to occur; however, its grain size increases accordingly. The fine and uniform grain structure can be obtained by hot working in the range of 950e1000 -C/0.01e0.1 s1 and 1050 e1100 $^ { \circ } { \bf C } / 1 { - } 1 0 \ { \bf s } ^ { - 1 }$ . Finally, the prediction model for the dynamic recrystallization average grain size is established, and the correlation coefficient (R) of the prediction model is 0.996; therefore, the model has a high prediction accuracy.

# 1. Introduction

With the rapid development of the automobile industry, automotive lightweight materials have become an important research topic. Among them, FeeMneAleC steel has good corrosion resistance, low density and excellent comprehensive mechanical properties, having great application prospect in the fields of weight reduction, collision safety and the complex working conditions of the automobiles structure [1e4]. In addition, thermoplastic deformation can effectively refine the grain size and improve the properties of forgings and microstructure. In the field of actual production, the microstructure is primarily controlled by way of thermal machining. Dynamic recrystallization (DRX) is beneficial to grain refinement and homogenization and is an important deformation mechanism to improve the microstructure and properties within the hot working process [5e7]. Therefore, in-depth studies on the microstructure evolution and DRX behavior of FeeMneAleC steel are of great significance for the formulation and optimization of the hot forging process of the steel.

At present, reports on FeeMneAleC steel primarily focus on smelting, heat treatment and mechanical properties. For example, Moon et al. [8] studied Fee30Mn-10.5Al-1.1C-(0e5) Mo steel. It was found that Mo-enriched $\mathtt { M } _ { 6 } \mathtt { C }$ and ${ \bf M } _ { 2 3 } { \bf C } _ { 6 }$ carbides precipitate during the solution treatment when the Mo content is higher than 4%, inhibiting the growth of the austenite grains during the solution treatment. Liu et al. [9] heated Fee11Mne10Al-1.25C steel and cooled the steel via three different methods. It was found that the distribution of -carbide in the samples after intense cooling was more unikform than that of the samples after air and water cooling; this, the corresponding mechanical properties were superior. However, the research reports on the hot deformation behavior of low-density high-strength FeeMneAleC steel are not thorough enough. In this paper, the microstructure evolution law and DRX behavior of the steel were studied using a thermal simulation compression experiment and metallographic observation. The flow stress curve characteristics of the steel were analyzed through the experimental data, and the dynamic recrystallization activation energy and Z parameters were calculated. The critical strain conditions for DRX were determined based on the Cingara-McQueen equation [10]. Functional relationships between rheological stress curve characteristic values (critical strain/stress, peak strain/ stress and steady-state strain/stress) and the dimensionless parameter Zener-Hollomon [11] were established. A dynamic recrystallization state diagram was drawn and a dynamic recrystallization average grain size prediction model was constructed. The research results provide theoretical support for the determination of the hot forging processes parameters as well as the numerical simulation of the hot forming of lowdensity high-strength FeeMneAleC steel.

# 2. Materials and methods

The material used in the experiment is FeeMneAleC steel and its main chemical composition (mass fraction, %) is: 1.46 C, 25.14 Mn, 10 Al, 0.053 Nb and the rest is Fe. The corresponding initial microstructure is shown in Fig. 1. The forging billet was processed into a cylindrical sample of F 8 mm 12 mm, and the perpendicularity of both ends of the samples was ensured. The isothermal constant strain rate compression experiments were carried out with the Gleeble-3500 thermo-mechanical simulator. The experimental temperatures were 900, 950, 1000, 1050, 1100 and $1 1 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ , and the strain rates were 0.01, 0.1, 1 and $1 0 \ { \mathsf { s } } ^ { - 1 }$ , respectively. The maximum compression was 60% (corresponding to the maximum true strain 0.92). The mica foils with high-temperature lubricants were affixed to the two end faces of the sample to reduce the friction and the non-uniformity of deformation. The sample was heated to 1200 -C at a rate of ${ 1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } } \mathrm { C } / { \mathsf { s } }$ , then homogenized by holding for 120 s, and finally cooled to the set T at a rate of 5 -C/s. The sample was for 15 s at the set T before compression, so the temperature difference between the inside and outside of the sample was the same. After compression, the sample was sprayed with water to cool the steel down. Figure 2 shows the experimental process. The compressed sample was split in half along the axial axis, and after sample preparation, grinding, polishing and etching, the microstructure images were collected using a LEXT OLS4000 laser confocal microscope. The etching solution was: 30% nitric acid alcohol solution (volume fraction). Through the Gleeble-3500 thermomechanical simulator, the isothermal constant strain rate thermal compression experiments of the same FeeMneAleC steel batch with the same composition can be confirmed under the same experimental conditions in order to check the reproducibility of the proposed system.

# 3. Results and discussion

# 3.1. Rheological behavior analysis

Fig. 3 (a) and (b) show the rheological stress curves of FeeMneAleC steel at different T (900e1150 -C) and ε\_ (0.01e10 $\mathsf { \pmb { s } } ^ { - 1 } )$ . Fig. 3 (a) and (b) show that the rheological stress behavior of the steel is primarily influenced by T and ε\_. The decrease of ε\_or the increase of T will result in the decrease of the rheological stress, indicating that the steel is a negative temperature-sensitive and positive strain rate-sensitive material. The former is primarily due to the accelerated grain boundary mobility of the dynamic recrystallization at a higher T. Furthermore, high temperatures are beneficial in strengthening the softening effect and reducing the dislocation slip resistance. The latter is primarily due to the lower ε\_ providing more time to promote DRX and dynamic recovery (DRV). This leads to the work hardening effect being less than the softening effect of the material, showing that the rheological stress decreases with the decrease of ε\_. In addition, peak stress is an important basis for estimating maximum deformation load, die selection and equipment checking in actual production. Fig. 3 (c) and (d) are 3D peak stress maps of $\mathrm { F e { \mathrm { - } } M n { \mathrm { - } } A l { \mathrm { - } } C }$ steel under different T and ε\_. Fig. 3 (c) and (d) show that the peak stress of this steel is sensitive to ε\_ and T during hot deformation. At the low ε\_ (0.01 $\mathsf { \pmb { s } } ^ { - 1 } )$ , the peak stress decreased from 305.4 MPa at $9 0 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to 53.8 MPa at $1 1 5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ a reduction of 82%. When the ε\_ is high (10 $\mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } ) _ { : }$ , the peak stress decreases by 73% within the same temperature range, indicating that ε\_ has a greater influence on the peak stress. The main reason is that the deformation time of the material is shortened with the increase of ε\_, the dislocation propagation rate is reduced, the DRV and DRX are insufficient, the softening effect is weak and the effect of work hardening is intensified, so the peak stress increases [12,13]. The same calculation can be obtained, in the case of a constant ε\_, the higher the T, the lower the peak stress, indicating that the peak stress is more sensitive to T. This is because the higher the T, the higher the thermal activation energy of the material, the faster the grain boundary mobility, the better the recrystallization and the lower the slip resistance, the stronger the softening effect and the lower the peak stress $[ 1 4 , 1 5 ]$ . In summary, the steel can reduce the peak stress by reducing ε\_ or increasing T during hot deformation. The above experimental phenomena have reproducibility.

# 3.2. Determination of dynamic recrystallization activation energy and Z parameter

The low-density high-strength steel underwent a greater degree of DRX during the hot deformation. The corresponding rheological stress curve has an obvious stress peak; thus, there is a peak stress/strain $( \sigma _ { \mathrm { p } } / \varepsilon _ { \mathrm { p } } ) .$ . Since the thermal deformation process of the metals was controlled by hot activation, the relationship between $\sigma _ { \mathrm { { p } } } , \mathrm { { T } }$ and ε\_ can be established by the Arrhenius hyperbolic sine function equation in order to determine the dynamic recrystallization activation energy:

$$
\dot {\varepsilon} = A \left[ \sinh \left(\alpha \sigma_ {p}\right) \right] ^ {n} \exp \left(- \frac {Q _ {p}}{R T}\right) \tag {1}
$$

where $Q _ { \mathrm { p } }$ is the activation energy for dynamic recrystallization (J\$mol1 ); R is the gas constant (8.314 J\$(mol\$K)1 ); ε\_ is the strain rate $( \mathsf { s } ^ { - 1 } ) ; \sigma _ { \mathsf { p } }$ is the peak stress (MPa); T is the temperature $( \mathbb { K } ) ; A ,$ a and n are all material constants, where the stress level factor a is determined by the two constants (n1 and b) [16,17], that is $\alpha = \beta / n _ { 1 } = ( \mathtt { d } \mathtt { l n } \dot { \varepsilon } / \mathtt { d } \sigma ) / ( \mathtt { d } \mathtt { l n } \dot { \varepsilon } / \mathtt { d } \ln \sigma ) = 0 . 0 0 5 9 9$ .

According to the linear fitting, the average slopes of the curves in Fig. 4 (a) and (b) are $k _ { 1 } = 5 . 1 3 5$ and $k _ { 2 } = 1 5 . 6 9 0$ , respectively. Furthermore, the dynamic recrystallization activation energy of $\mathrm { F e { \mathrm { - } } M n { \mathrm { - } } A l { \mathrm { - } } C }$ steel is 669.844 kJ $\mathrm { m o l } ^ { - 1 }$ according to formula (2):

$$
Q _ {p} = 1 0 0 0 \cdot R \left(\frac {\partial \ln \dot {\varepsilon}}{\partial \ln [ \sinh (\alpha \sigma_ {p}) ]}\right) _ {T} \left(\frac {\partial \ln [ \sinh (\alpha \sigma_ {p}) ]}{\partial (1 0 0 0 / T)}\right) _ {\dot {\varepsilon}} = 1 0 0 0 \cdot R k _ {1} k _ {2} \tag {2}
$$

The parallelism of the fitted straight line in Fig. 4 is not good, indicating that the deformation conditions have a significant effect on the dynamic recrystallization activation energy. Therefore, the calculation results of the activation energy for dynamic recrystallization above are only the average value within the experimental range of this paper. Zener and Hollomon [11] studied the constitutive relationship of steel and found that rheological stress depends on temperature and strain rate. Therefore, they proposed the Zener-Hollomon (Z) parameter model to characterize the comprehensive influence of T and ε\_ on the rheological stress.

$$
Z = \dot {\varepsilon} \exp \left(Q _ {p} / R T\right) \tag {3}
$$

The temperature compensated strain rate function (Z parameter formula (3)) has been applied in many studies. Singh et al. [18] studied the dynamic recrystallization of a high Nb-containing cast -TiAl based Tie45Ale8Nbe2Cr-0.2B alloy gduring hot compressive deformation and analyzed the Zener-Hollomon parameter modulated recrystallization mechanisms. Wang et al. [19] conducted the constitutive analysis of as-cast 40CrNiMo alloy steel through isothermal compression experiments and established the Arrhenius-type constitutive equation with the Zener-Hollomon parameter, having excellent accuracy. Detrois et al. [20] studied the hot deformation behavior of a Ni-based superalloy and simulated the flow stress from the experimental data using the Zener-Hollomon parameter.

According to the calculated dynamic recrystallization activation energy and formula (3), the Z parameters of FeeMneAleC steel under different T and ε\_ can be obtained, as shown in Table 1. Table 1 shows that the Z parameter increases with the increase of ε\_ or the decrease of T, which is the same as that of the flow stress. The above experimental phenomena have reproducibility. The introduction of the Zener-Hollomon parameter helps construct the functional relationship between the characteristic value (critical strain/ stress $( \varepsilon _ { \mathrm { c } } / \sigma _ { \mathrm { c } } ) _ { }$ , peak strain/stress $( \varepsilon _ { \mathrm { p } } / \sigma _ { \mathrm { p } } )$ and steady-state strain/ stress $\left( \varepsilon _ { \mathsf { s } } / \sigma _ { \mathsf { s } } \right) )$ of the flow stress curve and the Z parameter in the future. This also provides a practical basis for further studying the influence of hot deformation parameters (temperature and strain rate) on $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { c } } / \sigma _ { \mathrm { c } } , \varepsilon _ { \mathrm { p } } / \sigma _ { \mathrm { p } }$ and $\varepsilon _ { s } / \sigma _ { s }$ .

# 3.3. The relationship between the characteristic value of flow stress curve and Z parameter and the dynamic recrystallization state diagram

The flow stress curve of the DRX behavior of low-density highstrength steel can be described by three sets of characteristic values, namely critical strain/stress $( \varepsilon _ { \mathrm { { c } } } / \sigma _ { \mathrm { { c } } } )$ , peak strain/stress $( \varepsilon _ { \mathrm { p } } / \sigma _ { \mathrm { p } } )$ and steady-state strain/stress $( \varepsilon _ { s } / \sigma _ { s } )$ , where $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { { C } } }$ and $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { s } }$ represent the strain required to initiate and complete dynamic recrystallization, respectively. The $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { { p } } } / \sigma _ { \mathrm { { p } } }$ and $\varepsilon _ { s } / \sigma _ { s }$ here can be obtained directly from the rheological stress curve, while $\varepsilon _ { \scriptscriptstyle \mathrm { C } } / \sigma _ { \scriptscriptstyle \mathrm { C } }$ can be obtained only through data processing. In this regard, the $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { { C } } }$ of DRX was determined by the mathematical derivation of the Cingara-McQueen equation. The formula is shown in (4):

The work hardening rate q expression can be derived by the partial differentiation of formula (4), that is:

$$
\theta = \partial \sigma / \partial \varepsilon = C \sigma (1 / \varepsilon - 1 / \varepsilon_ {\mathrm{p}}) \tag {6}
$$

Poliak and Jonas [21] concluded that when dynamic recrystallization occurred, the $\theta - \sigma$ curve would present a characteristic inflection point, that is, $- \ \partial ^ { 2 } \theta / \partial \sigma ^ { 2 } = 0 ,$ , the inflection point was the critical point of dynamic recrystallization. Using the properties of partial derivatives to formula (6), the following relationship can be derived:

$$
\partial \theta / \partial \sigma = \partial \ln \theta / \partial \varepsilon \tag {7}
$$

It is seen that there must be an inflection point corresponding to the $\theta - \sigma$ curve in the ln $\theta - \varepsilon { \mathrm { ~ c u r v e } } _ { \mathrm { ~ } }$ , that is, $\partial ^ { 2 } { \mathrm { l } n } \theta / \partial \varepsilon ^ { 2 } = 0 .$ . In this regard, by taking the natural logarithm of v vformula (7), and performing the second partial derivative:

$$
\frac {\partial}{\partial \varepsilon} \left(- \frac {\partial \ln \theta}{\partial \varepsilon}\right) = 0 \tag {8}
$$

The mathematical expression of the $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { { C } } }$ of DRX can be obtained:

$$
\frac {\varepsilon_ {\mathrm{c}}}{\varepsilon_ {\mathrm{p}}} = \frac {\sqrt {1 - C} - (1 - C)}{C} \tag {9}
$$

According to the C values obtained under different T and ${ \dot { \varepsilon } } ,$ the corresponding $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { { C } } }$ can be calculated via formula (9) and the $\sigma _ { \mathrm { { C } } }$ corresponding to the $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { { C } } }$ can be calculated based on the flow stress curve. The functional relationship between the required $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { { C } } }$ and the $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { { p } } }$ is $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { c } } / \varepsilon _ { \mathrm { p } } \approx 0 . 4 5 2$ . This result is similar to the conclusions found within the literature [22,23] that use the inflection point on the $\theta - \sigma$ curve to identify the dynamic recrystallization $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { { C } } }$ of a low-density high-strength steel, indicating that the method is reliable in solving the critical strain of the steel.

To evaluate the selected model, the average relative error (MRE) was considered an unbiased statistic for confirming the credibility of the model.

During the dynamic recrystallization of the material, the grain size constantly changes under the driving force. On the one hand, the grain growth is promoted under the action of temperature and plastic strain and the driving force has a positive effect on the grain size. On the other hand, dynamic recrystallization refines the microstructure inside the material and the driving force is a counter-driving force, which inhibits the grain growth [27e29]. This may cause errors in the dynamic recrystallization average grain size prediction model. In addition, there were some statistical errors. It provides a reliable theoretical basis for the prediction of the dynamic recrystallized grain size of the steel after hot forging.

# 4. Conclusion and prospect

# 4.1. Conclusion

(1) FeeMneAleC steel is a negative temperature-sensitive and positive strain rate-sensitive material, and its rheological stress increases with the increase of $\dot { \varepsilon } \_ \mathrm { o r }$ the decrease of T. During hot deformation, the peak stress of the steel can be reduced by decreasing ε\_ or increasing T.
(2) Based on the Arrhenius hyperbolic sine function equation, the dynamic recrystallization activation energy of the steel was calculated as 669.844 kJ $\mathrm { m o l } ^ { - 1 }$ . Through the introduction of the dimensionless parameter Zener-Hollomon, the $\varepsilon _ { \mathrm { c } } / \sigma _ { \mathrm { c } } , \varepsilon _ { \mathrm { p } } / \sigma _ { \mathrm { p } }$ and $\varepsilon _ { s } / \sigma _ { s }$ models of dynamic recrystallization were established, and the state diagram of DRX was drawn. The results show that with the increase of the Z parameter, the initiation and completion of the dynamic recrystallization behavior of the steel becomes more difficult.

(3) The low T and high ε\_ are advantageous to obtain fine dynamic recrystallization grains, but they are not sufficient. With the decrease of ε\_ and the increase of T, full dynamic recrystallization of the material can be effectively induced and grain growth can also be caused. It is considered by comprehensive analysis that the fine and uniform grain structure can be obtained by hot working in the range of 950e1000 -C/0.01e0.1 s1 and 1050e1100 -C/1e10 s1 .
