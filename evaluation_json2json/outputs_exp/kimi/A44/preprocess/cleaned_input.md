# a b s t r a c t

The feasibility of significant weight reduction in conjunction with the superior strength-ductility combination makes Fe-Mn-Al-C-based steels the candidate material for automotive and structural applications. The alloy chemistry and processing conditions influence the microstructure in low-density steels (LDS); however, their role in deformation behaviour is not fully understood. In the present study, three different austenitic LDS grades viz. Fe-28Mn-9Al-0.9C, Fe-28Mn-9Al-5Ni-0.9C and Fe-15Mn-9Al-5Ni-0.9C alloys in hot-rolled condition were used for evaluating the role of secondary phases, especially B2 ordered Fe(Ni)Al phase on deformation characteristics. Ni-free LDS microstructure consisted of the ??-matrix with fine ??-carbides, whereas Ni containing low Mn alloy possessed coarser ??-carbides along with B2 in both bamboo-like stringer and polygonal particle morphology in the ??-matrix. The B2 containing alloy exhibited higher strength with reduced tensile ductility than the B2-free alloy. Hardness of the ??-matrix and B2 phase were similar with B2 grains exhibiting distinct pop-in events in the nanoindentation curves indicating incipient plasticity. The ??-matrix and B2 co-deform near yield, and a favourable orientation relationship (OR) between the ??-matrix and B2 facilitated easy slip transfer, while the non-favourable OR controlled ductility by strain accumulation and B2-cracking. The differences in the strain hardening behaviour of B2-free and B2 containing alloys were elucidated based on the changes in dislocation substructure evolution examined by an automated crystal orientation mapping in electron microscopy.

# 1. Introduction

The demand for lightweight, high-strength structural materials has seen an exponential rise in recent times. The reduction in overall weight has become an essential criterion for several applications, especially in automotive and aerospace sectors, with a thrust to improve fuel efficiency and bring down emission levels. There have been significant developments in producing advanced high strength steels (AHSS) exhibiting a reasonable ductility necessary for forming various structural components. In this context, at present, there exists three generations of AHSS [1–3]. The 3rd generation AHSS are still under the nascent stage of research, whereas the 2nd generation AHSS have already gained importance to replace the 1st generation steels in specific structural components. Amongst the 2nd generation AHSS, Fe-Mn-Al-C based steel grades are popularly termed as either low-density steels (LDS) or lightweight steels with induced plasticity (L-IP). These alloys offer superior mechanical properties and extraordinary weight reductions because of alloy chemistry and complex strengthening mechanisms [4–6]. Based on the constitution of phases, LDS are classified as ferritic, austenitic, duplex, or triplex. Austenite (??) based LDS alloys have four major microstructural features: (i) the ??-matrix, (ii) $\mathbf { L } 1 _ { 2 }$ ordered $\mathrm { ( F e M n ) } _ { 3 } \mathrm { A l C }$ (??-carbide) particles, (iii) B2 ordered Fe(Ni)Al type intermetallic compounds, and (iv) $\mathrm { D O } _ { 3 }$ ordered $\mathsf { F e } _ { 3 } \mathbf { A l }$ precipitates [7–9]. The formation of these features depends entirely on alloy chemistry and thermomechanical processing conditions. Based on the size, shape, and site of occurrence, the morphology of B2 can be classified as (i) elongated cylindrical shapes called stringers which exist across several ??-grains, (ii) intergranular spherical (200 nm – 1 ??m) at the recrystallised ??-grain boundaries, and (iii) intragranular precipitates (50 – 300 nm) formed within the shear bands in the deformed ??-grains [10]. It has also been reported that nickel addition would improve the strength of LDS alloys through the formation of Fe(Ni)Al type B2 phase [10].

Exhaustive literature is available on deformation behaviour and microstructural evolution in B2-free LDS [11–20]. However, limited studies exist which focus on understanding the influence of B2, with contrasting observations, on deformation behaviour of B2-containing LDS [21,22].
