The two micrographs act as a comparative set: reversing the sign of g causes dipole pairs (marked d) to change their relative dislocation contrast and position, confirming their dipole character, while non-dipole pairs (marked p) show no such reversal, indicating identical Burgers vectors. The images reveal straight, near-screw superdislocations composed of two (1/2)<111> partials gliding on {110} planes and trailing APBII faults. Many superdislocations are arranged in dipole configurations (d), while other parallel pairs (p) are straight edge dislocations with identical Burgers vectors produced by reactions between glissile <111> superdislocations. These observations support the central conclusion that the anomalous work-hardening peak in Fe–28% Al arises from strong superdislocation dipole locks and sessile <100> edge dislocation locks on {001} planes that block further room-temperature superdislocation motion.", "confidence": "high"}
